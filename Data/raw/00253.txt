<p><b>Gloria Stewart</b>, known as <b>Gloria Stuart</b>, (July 4, 1910 – September 26, 2010) was an American actress and artist. Stuart began her acting career in the theater. In the 1930s and 1940s, she performed in little theater and summer stock on both coasts. Her career in the movies spanned from 1932 to 2004—with a twenty-nine year break. Her iconic film role was the 100-year-old Old Rose in <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a>'s Academy Award-winning film <i><a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">Titanic</a></i>. In recognition of this work, she was nominated for an Academy Award as Best Supporting Actress. Receiving her nomination at eighty-seven, she is the <a href="/wiki/List_of_oldest_and_youngest_Academy_Award_winners_and_nominees" title="List of oldest and youngest Academy Award winners and nominees">oldest person ever nominated for an Academy Award for acting</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-obit_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-obit-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life_.281910.E2.80.931931.29">Early life (1910–1931)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life (1910–1931)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:James_Cagney_and_Gloria_Stuart_in_Here_Comes_the_Navy_trailer.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="237" data-file-width="269" height="194" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/c8/James_Cagney_and_Gloria_Stuart_in_Here_Comes_the_Navy_trailer.jpg/220px-James_Cagney_and_Gloria_Stuart_in_Here_Comes_the_Navy_trailer.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c8/James_Cagney_and_Gloria_Stuart_in_Here_Comes_the_Navy_trailer.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/c8/James_Cagney_and_Gloria_Stuart_in_Here_Comes_the_Navy_trailer.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

Here with <a href="/wiki/James_Cagney" title="James Cagney">James Cagney</a> from the 1934 film <i><a href="/wiki/Here_Comes_the_Navy" title="Here Comes the Navy">Here Comes the Navy</a></i>.</div>

<p>Stuart was born Gloria Stewart <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> at 11:00 p.m. on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fourth_of_July" title="Fourth of July">Fourth of July</a> at home in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Santa_Monica" title="Santa Monica">Santa Monica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson19996_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson19996-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>A<span>]</span></a></sup> Stuart's father, Frank Stewart, born in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Washington_state" title="Washington state">Washington state</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> was an attorney representing The Six Companies, Chinese tongs in San Francisco. Stuart's brother, Frank Jr.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> was born eleven months later. In two years, their brother Thomas was born, but he died of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spinal_meningitis" title="Spinal meningitis">spinal meningitis</a> aged three years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson19996_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson19996-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When Stuart was nine years old, her father was fatally injured when hit by a car.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199911_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199911-8"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Hard-pressed to support two small children, her mother soon accepted the proposal of local businessman Fred J. Finch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>B<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199911-12_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199911-12-10"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Stuart went through school as Gloria Fae Finch.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Since her parents did not give her a middle name, she often adopted one (sometimes it was Frances, the feminine of Frank, her father's name).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Acting_and_writing">Acting and writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Acting and writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At <a href="/wiki/Santa_Monica_High_School" title="Santa Monica High School">Santa Monica High School</a>, Stuart acted in plays, had the lead in her senior class play, <i><a href="/wiki/Ferenc_Moln%C3%A1r" title="Ferenc Molnár">The Swan</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> She loved writing as much as acting, and spent her last two summers in high school taking short story and poetry writing classes<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199913_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199913-13"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> and working as a cub reporter for the <i>Santa Monica Outlook</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199920_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199920-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/University_of_California_at_Berkeley" title="University of California at Berkeley">University of California at Berkeley</a>, Stuart majored in philosophy and drama, appeared in more plays, worked on the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Daily_Californian" title="Daily Californian">Daily Californian</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hello.2C_p._8_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hello.2C_p._8-15"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> contributed to the campus literary journal, <i>Occident</i> and posed as an artist's model. It was at Berkeley that she began signing her name, Gloria <i>Stuart</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>C<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_Bohemian_life_in_Carmel_.281930.E2.80.931932.29">A Bohemian life in Carmel (1930–1932)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: A Bohemian life in Carmel (1930–1932)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At the end of her junior year, in June, 1930, Stuart married Blair Gordon Newell,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199923_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199923-17"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> a young sculptor who apprenticed with <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Stackpole" title="Ralph Stackpole">Ralph Stackpole</a> on the facade of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/San_Francisco_Stock_Exchange" title="San Francisco Stock Exchange">San Francisco Stock Exchange</a> building.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199918_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199918-18"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The Newells moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Carmel-by-the-Sea" title="Carmel-by-the-Sea">Carmel-by-the-Sea</a> where there was a stimulating community of artists and movers and shakers such as <a href="/wiki/Ansel_Adams" title="Ansel Adams">Ansel Adams</a>, <a href="/wiki/Edward_Weston" title="Edward Weston">Edward Weston</a>, <a href="/wiki/Robinson_Jeffers" title="Robinson Jeffers">Robinson Jeffers</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lincoln_Steffens" title="Lincoln Steffens">Lincoln Steffens</a> and his wife <a href="/wiki/Ella_Winter" title="Ella Winter">Ella Winter</a>.</p>
<p>Stuart acted at the <a href="/wiki/Theatre_of_the_Golden_Bough" title="Theatre of the Golden Bough">Theatre of the Golden Bough</a> and worked many jobs on <i>The Carmelite</i> newspaper. In her spare time she hand-sewed aprons, patchwork pillows and tea linens and created bouquets of dried flowers for a tea shop where she also waited on tables when needed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199936_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199936-19"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Newell laid brick, chopped and stacked wood, taught sculpture and woodworking, and managed a <a href="/wiki/Miniature_golf" title="Miniature golf">miniature golf</a> course. They lived in a shack in the middle of a wood yard as night watchmen.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199931-37_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199931-37-20"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hollywood--first_pass_.281932.E2.80.931939.29">Hollywood--first pass (1932–1939)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Hollywood--first pass (1932–1939)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:WAMBAS_Baby_Stars_1932.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="295" data-file-width="450" height="144" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/WAMBAS_Baby_Stars_1932.jpg/220px-WAMBAS_Baby_Stars_1932.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/WAMBAS_Baby_Stars_1932.jpg/330px-WAMBAS_Baby_Stars_1932.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/9/99/WAMBAS_Baby_Stars_1932.jpg/440px-WAMBAS_Baby_Stars_1932.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:WAMBAS_Baby_Stars_1932.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Gloria Stuart, fourth from left, top row, with the rest of the 1932 <a href="/wiki/WAMPAS_Baby_Stars" title="WAMPAS Baby Stars">WAMPAS Baby Stars</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Stuart's theater work in Carmel brought her to the attention of Gilmor Brown's private theater, The Playbox, in Pasadena. She was invited there to appear as Masha in Chekhov's <i>The Seagull</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> Opening night, casting directors from Paramount and Universal were in the audience. Both came backstage to arrange a screen test, both studios claimed her. Finally the studios flipped a coin and Universal won the toss.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hello.2C_p._8_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-Hello.2C_p._8-15"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Stuart considered herself a serious actress in theater but she and Newell "were stony broke, living hand to mouth" so she decided to sign a contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199940_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199940-22"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Stuart does not mention it in her book, but the Internet Movie Database includes her with thirty other players in a slapstick comedy, <i>The Cohens and Kellys in Hollywood, A Behind-the-Scenes Farce</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Produced by Universal in the spring of 1932, this is likely Gloria Stuart's first appearance before the camera. Stuart actually began her movie career by playing an ingénue confronting her father's mistress in <i>Street of Women</i>, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pre-Code" title="Pre-Code">Pre-Code</a> fallen-women film. Stuart's second turn, again playing the ingénue, was in a football-hero movie, <i>The All-American</i>.</p>
<p>In early December, 1932, the Western Association of Motion Picture Advertisers announced that Gloria Stuart was one of fifteen new movie actresses "Most Likely to Succeed"—she was a WAMPAS Baby Star. Ginger Rogers, Mary Carlisle, Eleanor Holm were among the others.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Stuart's career advanced when English director <a href="/wiki/James_Whale" title="James Whale">James Whale</a> chose her for the glamor role in his ensemble cast (<a href="/wiki/Boris_Karloff" title="Boris Karloff">Boris Karloff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Melvyn_Douglas" title="Melvyn Douglas">Melvyn Douglas</a>, <a href="/wiki/Charles_Laughton" title="Charles Laughton">Charles Laughton</a>, <a href="/wiki/Lilian_Bond" title="Lilian Bond">Lilian Bond</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ernest_Thesiger" title="Ernest Thesiger">Ernest Thesiger</a>, <a href="/wiki/Eva_Moore" title="Eva Moore">Eva Moore</a> and <a href="/wiki/Raymond_Massey" title="Raymond Massey">Raymond Massey</a>) for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Old_Dark_House" title="The Old Dark House">The Old Dark House</a></i>. The movie became a cult classic.</p>
<p>Stuart was given her first co-starring role by director <a href="/wiki/John_Ford" title="John Ford">John Ford</a> in her next film, <i><a href="/wiki/Air_Mail_(film)" title="Air Mail (film)">Air Mail</a></i>, playing opposite Pat O'Brien and Ralph Bellamy. Of her performance in the movie, the <i>New York Times</i> Mordaunt Hall wrote, "Gloria Stuart, who does so well in <i>The Old Dark House</i>, a picture now at the Rialto, makes the most of the part of the girl..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> That two Gloria Stuart movies were in theaters simultaneously became the rule rather than the exception in her early career. In 1932, her first year, Stuart had four films released, then nine in 1933, six in 1934. In 1935, Stuart was having a baby, so only four movies were released. Six movies followed in 1936. After <i>Air Mail</i>, Mordaunt Hall's notices for Gloria Stuart came down to a few words. <i>Laughter in Hell</i>: "Gloria Stuart appears as Lorraine...";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Sweepings</i>: "...played by the comely Gloria Stuart...";<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Private Jones</i>: "Gloria Stuart is charming..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>James Whale called Stuart back for just one scene in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Kiss_Before_the_Mirror" title="The Kiss Before the Mirror">The Kiss Before the Mirror</a></i>, but the critic Hall wrote, "There are those who may think that it is too bad to introduce as one of the players the dainty Gloria Stuart and have her killed off in the first episode of the narrative. Perhaps it is, but a pretty girl was needed for the part and Mr. Whale obviously did not wish to weaken his production by casting an incompetent actress or an unattractive one for this minor role."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After good notices in <i>The Girl in 419</i>, (Mordaunt Hall mentions "...the pleasing acting of the attractive Gloria Stuart),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> and <i>Secret of the Blue Room</i> ("Miss Stuart gives a pleasing performance."),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> James Whale cast Gloria Stuart opposite Claude Rains in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Invisible_Man_(film)" title="The Invisible Man (film)">The Invisible Man</a></i>. Rains was a celebrated import from the London stage and this was his first Hollywood film. (Mordaunt Hall's review of Stuart's work was a temperate, "Miss Stuart also does well by her role."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup>) <i>The Invisible Man</i> also became a cult favorite.</p>
<p>In 1934, Universal loaned-out Stuart to Warner Brothers for <i><a href="/wiki/Here_Comes_the_Navy" title="Here Comes the Navy">Here Comes the Navy</a></i>. Stuart co-starred with <a href="/wiki/James_Cagney" title="James Cagney">James Cagney</a> and <a href="/wiki/Pat_O%27Brien_(actor)" title="Pat O'Brien (actor)">Pat O'Brien</a>, the first of nine films featuring this male team. <a href="/wiki/Frank_Nugent" title="Frank Nugent">Frank S. Nugent</a> wrote in the <i>New York Times</i>, "Supporting Mr. Cagney--and doing very creditable jobs, too--are Pat O'Brien, Gloria Stuart..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1935, Stuart was cast as Dick Powell's love interest in Busby Berkeley's, <i><a href="/wiki/Gold_Diggers_of_1935" title="Gold Diggers of 1935">Gold Diggers of 1935</a></i>. It was a musical; Stuart did not dance or sing, and the <i>New York Times</i> critic commented: "Nor has Gloria Stuart anything of vast import to contribute in the position usually occupied by Ruby Keeler."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In that same year, Stuart left Universal and joined Twentieth Century-Fox. Her first assignment from the studio head, <a href="/wiki/Darryl_F._Zanuck" title="Darryl F. Zanuck">Darryl F. Zanuck</a>, was in <i><a href="/wiki/Professional_Soldier_(film)" title="Professional Soldier (film)">Professional Soldier</a></i> supporting the child star <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Bartholomew" title="Freddie Bartholomew">Freddie Bartholomew</a> and Academy Award winner <a href="/wiki/Victor_McLaglen" title="Victor McLaglen">Victor McLaglen</a> (the year before, McLaglen won the Oscar for Best Actor in a Leading Role in John Ford's <i>The Informer</i>). Frank S. Nugent noted: "There is a minor romance along the way between Gloria Stuart, the king's noble governess, and Michael Whalen, the professional soldier's part-time assistant, but no one should take it seriously."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1936, John Ford chose Stuart to co-star with Warner Baxter in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Prisoner_of_Shark_Island" title="The Prisoner of Shark Island">The Prisoner of Shark Island</a></i>. Playing the wife of the doctor who treated Lincoln's assassin, Stuart felt privileged to work again with Ford,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199989_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199989-37"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> although the <i>New York Times</i> Frank S. Nugent wrote of Stuart's "...helpful performance..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> In <i>Poor Little Rich Girl</i>, Stuart again was asked to support a child star: this time, <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Temple" title="Shirley Temple">Shirley Temple</a>. Frank S. Nugent: "Listing [Temple's] supporting players hastily, then, before we forget them entirely, we might mention Miss Faye [and] Gloria Stuart…as having been permitted a scene or two while Miss Temple was out freshening her costume."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>For the rest of 1936 and through 1937, Zanuck placed Stuart in movies such as <i>The Girl on the Front Page</i>—Frank S. Nugent's note: "Call it mediocre and extend your sympathies to the cast…"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Reviewing <i>Girl Overboard</i>, Nugent begins, "In the definitive words of the currently popular threnode featured by a frog-voiced radio singer, Universal's 'Girl Overboard'…is 'nuthin' but a nuthin',' and a Class B nuthin' at that."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Lady Escapes,</i> <i>Life Begins in College</i> and <i>Change of Heart</i> did not even merit space in the <i>New York Times</i> movie pages. In 1938, Zanuck again insisted Stuart support Shirley Temple. Of <i>Rebecca of Sunnybrook Farm</i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Variety_(magazine)" title="Variety (magazine)">Variety</a></i> wrote: "Shirley Temple proves she's a great little artist in this one. The rest of it is synthetic and disappointing... More fitting title would be Rebecca of Radio City."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1938, for the fourth time, Stuart was a supporting player to a child star: Jane Withers in <i>Keep Smiling</i>. Stuart but not her performance is noted in the <i>New York Times</i> review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In <i>Time Out for Murder</i>, Stuart's reviewer said she was "…a pretty bill collector".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Then in 1939, the last year in this phase of Stuart's career, in <i>The Three Musketeers,</i> Stuart's billing came after Don Ameche, The Ritz Brothers and Binnie Barnes and again Stuart's work was not reviewed. In <i>Winner Take All</i>, the <i>Times</i> critic wrote, "…the only thing worth seeing in the picture is Tony Martin trying to play a prizefighter. This is positively killing."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>It Could Happen to You</i>, "a quasi-comedy"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> co-starring Stuart Erwin, finished the eight years. Again Stuart is not mentioned.</p>
<p>What did give the actress space in the movie pages the previous November was the story: "Gloria Stuart Quits Fox...Gloria Stuart has terminated her contract with Fox..."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> In fact, Darryl Zanuck did not renew Stuart's contract.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199990_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199990-49"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Arthur_Sheekman_.281933.E2.80.931978.29">Arthur Sheekman (1933–1978)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Arthur Sheekman (1933–1978)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stuart's sculptor husband, Gordon Newell, was unhappy with Hollywood life. He and Stuart separated amicably and divorced.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199947-48_50-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199947-48-50"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1933 on the set of her film <i>Roman Scandals</i>, a comedy starring <a href="/wiki/Eddie_Cantor" title="Eddie Cantor">Eddie Cantor</a>, Stuart met <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Sheekman" title="Arthur Sheekman">Arthur Sheekman</a>, one of the movie's writers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199961_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199961-51"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> They were "instantly attracted to each other".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199961_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199961-51"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> Stuart and Sheekman married in August, 1934<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> and their daughter, Sylvia – named after Princess Sylvia, Stuart’s character in <i>Roman Scandals</i> – was born the following June.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Sheekman began his writing career as a journalist, first in St. Paul then Chicago. When the <a href="/wiki/Marx_Brothers" title="Marx Brothers">Marx Brothers</a> came to Chicago touring in <i>The Cocoanuts</i> in 1926 and then <i>Animal Crackers</i> in 1928,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-54"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> Sheekman interviewed them for his <i>Chicago Times</i> column, "Ahead of the Times." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199983-84_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199983-84-56"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> Groucho Marx urged Sheekman to come to Hollywood to write for the brothers. In 1930, Sheekman made the move. He became Groucho Marx's closest friend and collaborated (often without credit) on Marx Brothers movies such as <i><a href="/wiki/Monkey_Business_(1931_film)" title="Monkey Business (1931 film)">Monkey Business</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Horse_Feathers" title="Horse Feathers">Horse Feathers</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Duck_Soup_(1933_film)" title="Duck Soup (1933 film)">Duck Soup</a></i>. Sheekman also worked on Marx’s 1932 radio show, <i>Flywheel, Shyster, and Flywheel</i> and ghost wrote Marx's small book, <i>Beds</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199984_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199984-57"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> After <i>Roman Scandals</i>, Sheekman worked on more comedies for Eddie Cantor as well as for Shirley Temple and <a href="/wiki/Joe_E._Brown" title="Joe E. Brown">Joe E. Brown</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMDBSheekman_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMDBSheekman-58"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Activist_.281933.E2.80.931939.29">Activist (1933–1939)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Activist (1933–1939)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UC_Berkeley" title="UC Berkeley">UC Berkeley</a> when she was seventeen, Stuart wanted to join the <a href="/wiki/Young_Communist_League" title="Young Communist League">Young Communist League</a>. She wrote, "I was told it was for the poor and the oppressed. That appealed to me. But membership wasn't open to anyone under eighteen, so I couldn't join."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199938_59-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199938-59"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> In Carmel, she notes that her friendship with muckraker Lincoln Steffens gave her "...much deeper insight into the abuses of laborers and blue-collar workers and made me ready to work for liberal causes when I got to Hollywood a few years later."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199938_59-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199938-59"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1933 Stuart was one of the first stars to work toward an actors’ union<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199945_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199945-60"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> and was one of thirty-nine new Class A members of the Screen Actors Guild.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> In June, 1936, she helped Paul Muni, Franchot Tone, Ernst Lubitsch, and Oscar Hammerstein II form the Hollywood Anti-Nazi League.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199946_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199946-63"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> That same year she and writer Dorothy Parker helped create the League to Support the Spanish Civil War Orphans.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199946_63-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199946-63"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1938 as a member of the Hollywood Democratic Committee, Stuart was on the Executive Board of the California State Democratic Committee.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199946_63-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199946-63"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Away_from_Hollywood_.281939.E2.80.931943.29">Away from Hollywood (1939–1943)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Away from Hollywood (1939–1943)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_trip_around_the_world_.281939.29">A trip around the world (1939)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: A trip around the world (1939)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Early in 1939, their studio assignments completed, Stuart and Sheekman decided to go around the world before war broke out.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199992_64-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson199992-64"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> For four months, they toured Asia, Egypt and Italy, then landed in France just as France and England declared war on Germany.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999116_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999116-65"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> They appealed to the American consul, asking to stay, Sheekman as a war correspondent, Stuart as a hospital volunteer. The consul refused help, told them they had to go home. They caught the SS <i>President Adams</i>, the last American passenger ship to cross the Atlantic, <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999116-117_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999116-117-66"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> and arrived in New York in September.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_taste_of_theater_.281940.E2.80.931943.29">A taste of theater (1940–1943)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: A taste of theater (1940–1943)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Stuart and Sheekman stayed in New York. Stuart wanted to work in the theater again and Sheekman hoped to write a play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999118_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999118-67"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> Stuart was welcomed into summer stock. In theaters up and down the east coast, she performed in <i>Man and Superman</i>, <i>The Animal Kingdom</i>, <i>The Night of January 16th</i>, <i>Accent on Youth</i>, <i>Route 101</i>, <i>Mr. and Mrs. North,</i> <i>The Pursuit of Happiness</i>, <i>Here Today</i>, <i>Sailor Beware</i> and was Emily to Thornton Wilder’s Stage Manager—under Wilder’s own direction—in <i>Our Town</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999129_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999129-68"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>But for two years, as many readings, lunches and cocktail parties as she attended, no director, producer or writer (not even Sheekman) gave Stuart a role on Broadway. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999130-137_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999130-137-69"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Sheekman worked on his comic play, collaborating with a colleague from newspaper days, Margaret Shane.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999121_70-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999121-70"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> Their <i>Mr. Big</i> opened on Broadway but closed after seven performances.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999136_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999136-71"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> Then Sheekman collaborated with Ruth and Augustus Goetz (years later their play and screenplay, <i>The Heiress</i>, won prizes). <i>Franklin Street</i> closed out of town.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999137-138_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999137-138-72"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Creativity_without_.28much.29_acting_.281943.E2.80.931975.29">Creativity without (much) acting (1943–1975)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Creativity without (much) acting (1943–1975)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After more than three years away, the Sheekmans returned to Hollywood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999138_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999138-73"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>An idea for a movie for Danny Kaye (<a href="/wiki/Wonder_Man" title="Wonder Man">Wonder Man</a>) returned Sheekman to the studio and he was back at work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMDBSheekman_58-1"><a href="#cite_note-IMDBSheekman-58"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>To help with the war effort,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999162_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999162-74"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> Stuart took singing and dancing lessons, then the USO teamed her with actress Hillary Brooke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999158-159_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999158-159-75"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup> The two blonde actresses toured the country, visited hospitals, danced with servicemen in canteens, sold war bonds. Stuart "wanted terribly to volunteer for service overseas with the USO, but Arthur wouldn't hear of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999143_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999143-76"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Stuart asked her former agents to get her work. Her first movie in four years, <i>Here Comes Elmer</i>, was a comedy with music starring Roy Rogers’ wife, Dale Evans. In <i>The Whistler</i>—an early directing credit of the horror specialist, William Castle—Stuart co-starred with Richard Dix. In <i>Enemy of Women</i>, Stuart was seventh in billing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999143_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999143-76"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> Two years later, Stuart took one more role: she wore a redhead's wig in a comedy starring Joan Davis and Jack Oakie. Stuart does not mention <i>She Wrote the Book</i> in her autobiography. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-77"><a href="#cite_note-77"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="D.C3.A9cor_Ltd._.281946.E2.80.931949.29">Décor Ltd. (1946–1949)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Décor Ltd. (1946–1949)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<div class="thumbcaption">

An example of découpage by Gloria Stuart.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Not long after this last movie, Stuart went to New York with Sheekman—Paramount sent him to see the new play <i>Dream Girl</i> wanting him to adapt it for to screen. A friend took Stuart to the studio of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/D%C3%A9coupage" title="Découpage">découpage</a> artist. Drawn to the art form, Stuart thought it could replace acting in her life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999168_78-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999168-78"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> With Sheekman's encouragement, she opened a shop on Los Angeles's decorators’ row, named it Décor, Ltd.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999169_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999169-79"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> Stuart created découpaged lamps, mirrors, tables, chests and other one of a kind <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Objets_d%27art" title="Objets d'art">objets d'art</a></i>. Over the next four years, her work gained attention and her pieces were carried by <a href="/wiki/Lord_%26_Taylor" title="Lord &amp; Taylor">Lord &amp; Taylor</a> in New York, <a href="/wiki/Neiman_Marcus" title="Neiman Marcus">Neiman Marcus</a> in Dallas, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bullock%E2%80%99s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bullock’s (page does not exist)">Bullock’s</a> in Pasadena and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gump%E2%80%99s&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gump’s (page does not exist)">Gump’s</a> in San Francisco. But in time, labor involved in "the fine fine cutting, applying sixteen coats of lacquer" to every piece<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999170_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999170-80"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup> and other costs proved prohibitive and Stuart closed her shop.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="A_house_and_a_garden_.281948.E2.80.931954.29">A house and a garden (1948–1954)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: A house and a garden (1948–1954)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After living in rented spaces for ten years, Stuart and Sheekman bought an old <a href="/wiki/American_Craftsman" title="American Craftsman">craftsman-style house</a> on an acre of ground that wanted tending. In the house, Stuart redesigned the interior, supervised the remodeling, designed all the furniture and had it custom made. In the garden, she planned the landscaping, included a green house for orchids and lath house for grafting fruit trees, spent hours on her knees cultivating and planting. In Stuart’s words, “I became a whirling dervish of creative renovation.”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999171-172_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999171-172-81"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Painting_and_serigraphy_.281954.E2.80.931983.29">Painting and serigraphy (1954–1983)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Painting and serigraphy (1954–1983)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:312px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gloria_Stuart%27s_Watts_Towers-4828-2.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1800" data-file-width="2932" height="190" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Gloria_Stuart%27s_Watts_Towers-4828-2.jpg/310px-Gloria_Stuart%27s_Watts_Towers-4828-2.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Gloria_Stuart%27s_Watts_Towers-4828-2.jpg/465px-Gloria_Stuart%27s_Watts_Towers-4828-2.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a1/Gloria_Stuart%27s_Watts_Towers-4828-2.jpg/620px-Gloria_Stuart%27s_Watts_Towers-4828-2.jpg 2x" width="310"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gloria_Stuart%27s_Watts_Towers-4828-2.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
One of Stuart's Watts Towers, January 1972</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Early in 1954, visiting Paris, Stuart first saw the Impressionist paintings at the <i>Jeu de Paume</i> museum. As when she first saw découpage, Stuart wanted to do it, too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999174_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999174-82"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup> The Sheekmans were on their way to Italy. At the time, American artists living abroad for at least eighteen months paid no taxes on income earned during the residency.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999175_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999175-83"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup> Sheekman was now very successful. In the eight years since returning from New York, he had been on fourteen movies, mostly writing the screenplays. He wanted to try another play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999177_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999177-84"><span>[</span>81<span>]</span></a></sup> For the next eighteen months, Stuart painted and Sheekman worked on his play.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999178_85-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999178-85"><span>[</span>82<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Sheekman's comedy about a sorrowful comic, <i>The Joker</i>, had Tommy Noonan for its star and was booked into The Playhouse Theater in New York to open April 5, 1957. April 1st, it was announced the play was terminating a pre-Broadway tour of three-and-one-half weeks in Washington DC and was "taken off for repairs."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-86"><a href="#cite_note-86"><span>[</span>83<span>]</span></a></sup> Repairs were never made.</p>
<p>Then after seven years of working at her easel every day, Stuart was ready to show her paintings. In September, 1961, <a href="/wiki/Victor_Hammer" title="Victor Hammer">Victor Hammer</a> gave Stuart a debut one-woman show at his Hammer Galleries in New York.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999182_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999182-87"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> Nearly all of her forty canvases sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999182_87-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999182-87"><span>[</span>84<span>]</span></a></sup> In the following years, Stuart exhibited her primitive-style paintings in many shows, including at the Bianchini Gallery in New York, the Simon Patrich Galleries and The Egg and the Eye in Los Angeles, the Galerie du Jonelle in Palm Springs and the Staircase Gallery in Beverly Hills. Stuart’s paintings are in numerous private collections and the permanent collections of the Los Angeles County Museum of Art, the Museum of New Mexico (Santa Fe), the Desert Museum of Palm Springs and the Belhaven Museum (Jackson, Mississippi).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>85<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Stuart had been painting for nearly thirty years when, as she noted in her book, “…the challenges to me of painting as a primitive had been wearing a little thin, and I had become fascinated by the complex art form of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Serigraphy" title="Serigraphy">serigraphy</a>—silk screening." Stuart studied with serigrapher Evelyn Johnson then created vivid serigraphs that are also in private collections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999227_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999227-89"><span>[</span>86<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bonsai_.281970.E2.80.932010.29">Bonsai (1970–2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Bonsai (1970–2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gloria_Stuart-French_black_oak_forest.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="777" data-file-width="787" height="217" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Gloria_Stuart-French_black_oak_forest.jpg/220px-Gloria_Stuart-French_black_oak_forest.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Gloria_Stuart-French_black_oak_forest.jpg/330px-Gloria_Stuart-French_black_oak_forest.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/48/Gloria_Stuart-French_black_oak_forest.jpg/440px-Gloria_Stuart-French_black_oak_forest.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gloria_Stuart-French_black_oak_forest.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
<a href="/wiki/Bonsai" title="Bonsai">Bonsai</a> called "French Black Oak Forest" was created by Gloria Stuart in 1982 after returning from France where she gathered the acorns in the royal forest at Fontainebleau.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In the late 1960s, Sheekman's health began to decline and gradually he showed symptoms of dementia. It was then that Stuart embraced another art form, the art of <a href="/wiki/Bonsai" title="Bonsai">bonsai</a>. She took classes from Frank Nagata, colleague of <a href="/wiki/John_Naka" title="John Naka">John Naka</a>, a bonsai master in Los Angeles,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999191_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999191-90"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup> joined Nagata’s bonsai club, Baiko-En, and became one of the first Anglo members of the California Bonsai Society. Eventually Stuart's collection numbered over one hundred miniature trees.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999191-192_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999191-192-91"><span>[</span>88<span>]</span></a></sup> Some of her trees are in the bonsai collection of the Botanical Gardens of the Huntington Library in San Marino, California.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hollywood.E2.80.94second_pass.E2.80.94and_new_passions_.281975.E2.80.932010.29">Hollywood—second pass—and new passions (1975–2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Hollywood—second pass—and new passions (1975–2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bit_parts_and_Peter_O.E2.80.99Toole_.281975.E2.80.931989.29">Bit parts and Peter O’Toole (1975–1989)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Bit parts and Peter O’Toole (1975–1989)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1975, Stuart decided to return to acting. She got an agent who got her bit parts, mostly in television--"guest appearances" on series such as <i>The Waltons</i> and <i>Murder, She Wrote</i>--Stuart was back before the camera.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999209_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999209-92"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup> Her friend director Nancy Malone gave her a leading role in <i>Merlene of the Movies</i>, a quirky film for television, and other friends gave her parts in their shows. In 1982 came <i>My Favorite Year</i>. Although Stuart's scene lasted moments and she had no lines, she was dancing with Peter O'Toole. She wrote, "It was a great privilege to work with him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999162_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999162-74"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-93"><a href="#cite_note-93"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup> After that, Stuart was in Jack Lemmon's <i>Mass Appeal</i> and Goldie Hawn's <i>Wildcats</i>, then more bits and pieces in television.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-94"><a href="#cite_note-94"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Ward_Ritchie_.281983.E2.80.931996.29">Ward Ritchie (1983–1996)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Ward Ritchie (1983–1996)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Arthur Sheekman died in January, 1978.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-95"><a href="#cite_note-95"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup> Five years later, <a href="/wiki/Ward_Ritchie" title="Ward Ritchie">Ward Ritchie</a>, a close friend of Stuart’s first husband, Gordon Newell, sent Stuart one of his books. Ritchie had become a celebrated printer, book designer and printing historian.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-96"><a href="#cite_note-96"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup> With his commercial Ward Ritchie Press and private Laguna Verde Imprenta press, Ritchie produced distinguished books on the arts, poetry, cookery and the American West. Stuart invited him to dinner and they fell in love. Ritchie was seventy-eight and Stuart seventy-two.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999219-220_97-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999219-220-97"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Imprenta_Glorias_.281984.E2.80.932010.29">Imprenta Glorias (1984–2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Imprenta Glorias (1984–2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>When Stuart first followed Ritchie into his studio and watched him pull a printed page from his 1839 English iron Albion hand press, she wanted to do it, too.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999226_98-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999226-98"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup> After studying typesetting at the Women’s Workshop in Los Angeles, Stuart bought her own hand press, a Vandercook SP15<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999228_99-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999228-99"><span>[</span>96<span>]</span></a></sup> and established her own private press, Imprenta Glorias.</p>
<p>Stuart’s next discovery was the <a href="/wiki/Artist%27s_book" title="Artist's book">Artist’s book</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999230_100-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999230-100"><span>[</span>97<span>]</span></a></sup> She designed the books, wrote the text (often poetry), set the type—carefully selecting the style of type to match the subject--printed the pages, then decorated the pages with water colors, silk screen, découpage or all three. She created large artist’s books and books in miniature. Several of her books took her years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999231_101-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999231-101"><span>[</span>98<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Through Ritchie, Stuart was introduced to prestigious librarians and bibliophiles from San Francisco to Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999244_102-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999244-102"><span>[</span>99<span>]</span></a></sup> Imprenta Glorias books can be found in the <i>Bibliothèque nationale de France</i>, the Huntington Library, J. Paul Getty Museum, the Library of Congress, the Los Angeles Public Library, the Metropolitan Museum of Art, the Morgan Library &amp; Museum, the New York Public Library, the Occidental College Library, the Princeton University library, the UCLA Clark Library, the Victoria and Albert Museum as well as private collections.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999233_103-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999233-103"><span>[</span>100<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Stuart and Ritchie were together for thirteen years until his death from pancreatic cancer in 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999239_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999239-104"><span>[</span>101<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Titanic_.281996.E2.80.931998.29"><i>Titanic</i> (1996–1998)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Titanic (1996–1998)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>May Day, 1996, Stuart came in from printing in her studio and checked her answering machine. "A female voice said she was calling from Lightstorm Entertainment...about a movie to be shot on location, maybe Poland...about the Titanic, directed by James Cameron..." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999249_105-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999249-105"><span>[</span>102<span>]</span></a></sup> The next afternoon, Cameron’s casting director, Mali Finn, came to Stuart’s house "…with her assistant, Emily Schweber, who was carrying a video camera... Mali and I talked while Emily filmed us."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999250_106-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999250-106"><span>[</span>103<span>]</span></a></sup> The next morning, Finn brought over James Cameron and <i>his</i> video camera. Stuart wrote, "I was not the least bit nervous. I knew I would read Old Rose with the sympathy and tenderness that Cameron had intended…"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999251_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999251-107"><span>[</span>104<span>]</span></a></sup> Five days after Stuart's eighty-sixth birthday, Finn phoned again and asked, “Gloria, how would you like to be Old Rose?”<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999254_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999254-108"><span>[</span>105<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Most of Stuart’s filming was completed in Halifax, Nova Scotia, over about three weeks in early summer. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999268_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999268-109"><span>[</span>106<span>]</span></a></sup> But the complex movie, events connected with it and the consequences of Stuart’s new status in Hollywood filled the next year. Stuart filmed and made recordings for several documentaries, did more looping and dubbing for Cameron, received offers of scripts. Stuart wrote, "On April 7, 1997, the publicity blitz for Titanic kicked off… From that point on, the deluge of publicity never stopped."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999278_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999278-110"><span>[</span>107<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_after_Titanic_.281997.E2.80.932010.29">Life after <i>Titanic</i> (1997–2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Life after Titanic (1997–2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Honors_and_perks_of_being_Old_Rose">Honors and perks of being Old Rose</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Honors and perks of being Old Rose">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>December 17th, the Hollywood Foreign Press Association nominated Stuart for "Best Performance by an Actress in a Supporting Role in a Motion Picture—<i>Titanic</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-111"><a href="#cite_note-111"><span>[</span>108<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On February 10, 1998, the Academy Awards nominations were announced on radio and television. Gloria Stuart was among the names for Best Performance by an Actress in A Supporting Role. Stuart remembered, "..the phone began to ring, and I happily thought, 'Finally!'" <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999297_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999297-112"><span>[</span>109<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>March 8, 1998, Stuart’s union, the Screen Actors Guild, awarded Stuart their Founders Award.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-113"><a href="#cite_note-113"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup> Then for Best Performance by an Actress in a Supporting Role, Stuart tied with Kim Basinger (<i>L.A. Confidential</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-114"><a href="#cite_note-114"><span>[</span>111<span>]</span></a></sup> For both awards, Stuart received a standing ovation from her peers. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999302_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999302-115"><span>[</span>112<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The following May, <i>People</i> magazine included Stuart on their list of "The 50 most beautiful people in the World in 1998." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-116"><a href="#cite_note-116"><span>[</span>113<span>]</span></a></sup> Also in May, Stuart was guest of honor at the Great Steamboat Race between the Belle of Louisville and the Delta Queen and then was Grand Marshal of the 1998 Kentucky Derby Festival’s Pegasus Parade.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-117"><a href="#cite_note-117"><span>[</span>114<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Next, Stuart signed a contract with Little, Brown and Company to write her autobiography, <i>I Just Kept Hoping</i>.</p>
<p>In June, Stuart filmed a cameo appearance parodying Old Rose for the Hanson music video <i>River</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-118"><a href="#cite_note-118"><span>[</span>115<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Stuart made her debut at The Hollywood Bowl on July 19, 1998 reading the poem, <i>Standing Stone</i>, Paul McCartney’s oratorio for orchestra and chorus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-119"><a href="#cite_note-119"><span>[</span>116<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Stuart was asked by the producer and star, Kate Capshaw, to join her cast of <i>The Love Letter</i>. Mid-August, Stuart went on location to Rockport, Maine, and filmed her comic role.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999307_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999307-120"><span>[</span>117<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In October 1999, Stuart’s native Santa Monica issued a Commendation signed by the mayor recognizing Gloria Stuart "…for many contributions world-wide and her inspirational message to always keep hoping. Dated this 16th day of October, 1999. Pam O’Connor, Mayor."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-121"><a href="#cite_note-121"><span>[</span>118<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In September, 2000, Stuart unveiled her star on the <a href="/wiki/Hollywood_Walk_of_Fame" title="Hollywood Walk of Fame">Hollywood Walk of Fame</a>. <i>GLORIA STUART</i> inlaid in brass lies in front of the old <a href="/wiki/Pig_%27n_Whistle" title="Pig 'n Whistle">Pig 'n Whistle</a>. The café opened its doors when Stuart was in high school.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-122"><a href="#cite_note-122"><span>[</span>119<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Even though once again reduced to minor roles, Stuart's last two movies were for the respected German director, <a href="/wiki/Wim_Wenders" title="Wim Wenders">Wim Wenders</a>. In 1999 Stuart worked on <i>The Million Dollar Hotel</i> in downtown Los Angeles. In 2004, Wenders found a spot for Stuart in his <i>Land of Plenty</i>, her final film.</p>
<p>On June 19, 2010, Stuart was honored by the Screen Actors Guild for her years of service. At a luncheon, she was presented the Ralph Morgan Award by <i>Titanic</i> co-star <a href="/wiki/Frances_Fisher" title="Frances Fisher">Frances Fisher</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-123"><a href="#cite_note-123"><span>[</span>120<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On July 22, 2010, The Academy of Motion Picture Arts and Sciences honored Stuart’s career with a program featuring film clips and conversations between Stuart and film historian Leonard Maltin, portrait artist Don Bachardy and David S. Zeidberg, the Avery Director of the Huntington Library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-124"><a href="#cite_note-124"><span>[</span>121<span>]</span></a></sup> One thousand people filled the Samuel Goldwyn Theater.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-125"><a href="#cite_note-125"><span>[</span>122<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>From the time Stuart was announced in the <i>Titanic</i> cast, she appeared before the camera for interviews on subjects as diverse as Groucho Marx, Shirley Temple, <a href="/wiki/James_Whale" title="James Whale">James Whale</a>, horror movies and friends <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Isherwood" title="Christopher Isherwood">Christopher Isherwood</a> and <a href="/wiki/Don_Bachardy" title="Don Bachardy">Don Bachardy</a> <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-126"><a href="#cite_note-126"><span>[</span>123<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Coping_with_illness_and_infirmity_.281984.E2.80.932010.29">Coping with illness and infirmity (1984–2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Coping with illness and infirmity (1984–2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Just after Ward Ritchie came back in her life, Stuart discovered she had breast cancer. It was caught early, she had a lumpectomy followed with radiation, then ignored it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999246-247_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEStuartThompson1999246-247-127"><span>[</span>124<span>]</span></a></sup> Stuart’s breast cancer did not return.</p>
<p>But twenty years later, when she was ninety-four, Stuart was diagnosed with lung cancer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-128"><a href="#cite_note-128"><span>[</span>125<span>]</span></a></sup> Stuart had radiation, but in time the cancer returned and Stuart underwent a shorter course of radiation. The malignancy continued to spread, but slowly due to Stuart's age. She lived six years after her initial diagnosis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-129"><a href="#cite_note-129"><span>[</span>126<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="100th_birthday_and_death_.28July_4.2C_1910_.E2.80.93_September_26.2C_2010.29">100th birthday and death (July 4, 1910 – September 26, 2010)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: 100th birthday and death (July 4, 1910 – September 26, 2010)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On the day of Stuart’s 100th birthday, James and Suzy Cameron hosted Stuart’s family and friends at the ACE Gallery in Beverly Hills. There Stuart saw many of her paintings and serigraphs, artist’s books, samples of her découpage and trees from her bonsai collection exhibited in the gallery. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-130"><a href="#cite_note-130"><span>[</span>127<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Stuart’s Baiko-En bonsai club gave her a gala birthday party at the Huntington Library.</p>
<p>Stuart died on the afternoon of September 26, 2010.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-131"><a href="#cite_note-131"><span>[</span>D<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Legacy">Legacy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Gloria_Stuart&amp;action=edit&amp;section=24" title="Edit section: Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time of her death, she had four grandchildren and twelve great-grandchildren.</p>
<p>Stuart's grandson, Benjamin Stuart Thompson, is working on a documentary, <i>The Secret Life of Old Rose</i>. The film explores Stuart's long acting career as well as her career as an artist, fine printer and bonsai enthusiast.</p>
<p>Gloria Stuart's great-granddaughter, Deborah B. Thompson, produced an e-book, <i>Butterfly Summers: A Memoir of Gloria Stuart's Apprentice</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-132"><a href="#cite_note-132"><span>[</span>128<span>]</span></a></sup> Thompson was one of several artists who, over a period of years, assisted Stuart on her masterwork, <i>Gloria Stuart’s Flight of Butterfly Kites</i>. Thompson's observations of Stuart's creative process—and how to print on Stuarts hand press—make it a unique chronicle.</p>
<p>Gloria Stuart's paintings are represented by the Papillon Gallery in Los Angeles and <i>I Dated Oppenheimer</i>, her last miniature artist's book, is represented by Lorson's Books and Prints. Pieces of Gloria Stuart's découpage turn up in antique shops occasionally. Studio photographs signed by Gloria Stuart still circulate on the Internet.</p>
