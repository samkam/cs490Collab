<p><br/>
<i><b>The Lord of the Rings</b></i> is an epic <a href="/wiki/High_fantasy" title="High fantasy">high fantasy</a> novel written by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>, later fitted as a trilogy. The story began as a sequel to Tolkien's earlier fantasy book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">The Hobbit</a></i> and soon developed into a much larger story. It was written in stages between 1937 and 1949, with much of it being written during World War II.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-0"><a href="#cite_note-0">[1]</a></sup> It was originally published in three volumes in 1954 and 1955,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1">[2]</a></sup> and has since been reprinted numerous times and translated into at least 38 different languages,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2">[3]</a></sup> becoming one of the most popular works in twentieth-century literature.
</p>
<figure class="article-thumb tright show-info-icon" style="width: 250px"> <a class="image image-thumbnail" href="http://img2.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20130619012726/lotr/images/1/11/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_First_Copies.jpg"><img alt="The Lord of the Rings First Copies" class="thumbimage " data-image-key="The_Lord_of_the_Rings_First_Copies.jpg" data-image-name="The Lord of the Rings First Copies.jpg" height="177" src="http://img4.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20130619012726/lotr/images/thumb/1/11/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_First_Copies.jpg/250px-The_Lord_of_the_Rings_First_Copies.jpg" width="250"/></a> <figcaption> <a class="sprite info-icon" href="/wiki/File:The_Lord_of_the_Rings_First_Copies.jpg"></a> <p class="caption">First edition copies of Tolkien's <i>The Lord of the Rings</i></p> </figcaption> </figure>
<p>The action in <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> is set in what the author conceived to be the lands of the real Earth, inhabited by humanity but placed in a fictional past, before our history but after the fall of his version of Atlantis, which he calls <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BAmenor" title="NÃºmenor">NÃºmenor</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letters_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-letters-3">[4]</a></sup> Tolkien gave this setting a modern English name, <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth" title="Middle-earth">Middle-earth</a>, a rendering of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Old_English_language" title="Old English language">Old English</a> <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middangeard" title="wikipedia:Middangeard">Middangeard</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4">[5]</a></sup>
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_main_conflict">The main conflict</span></h2>
<p>The story concerns peoples such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hobbit" title="Hobbit">Hobbits</a>, <a href="/wiki/Elves" title="Elves">Elves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Men" title="Men">Men</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dwarves" title="Dwarves">Dwarves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Wizards" title="Wizards">Wizards</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Orcs" title="Orcs">Orcs</a> (called <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Goblin" title="Goblin">goblins</a> in <i>The Hobbit</i>), and centers on the <a href="/wiki/One_Ring" title="One Ring">Ring of Power</a> made by the Dark Lord <a href="/wiki/Sauron" title="Sauron">Sauron</a>. Starting from quiet beginnings in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Shire" title="The Shire">The Shire</a>, the story ranges across Middle-earth and follows the courses of the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Ring" title="War of the Ring">War of the Ring</a>. The main story is followed by six appendices that provide a wealth of historical and linguistic background material,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5">[6]</a></sup> as well as an index listing every character, place, <a href="/wiki/Songs_and_Verses" title="Songs and Verses">song</a>, and sword.
</p><p>Along with Tolkien's other writings, <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> has been subjected to extensive analysis of its literary themes and origins. Although a major work in itself, the story is merely the last movement of a larger <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythological</a> cycle, or legendarium, that Tolkien had worked on for many years since 1917.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6">[7]</a></sup> Influences on this earlier work, and on the story of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, include <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philology" title="wikipedia:Philology">philology</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a> and religion, as well as earlier fantasy works and Tolkien's experiences in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>. <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> in its turn is considered to have had a great impact on modern fantasy, and the impact of Tolkien's works is such that the use of the words "Tolkienian" and "Tolkienesque" have been recorded in the Oxford English Dictionary.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7">[8]</a></sup>
</p><p>The immense and enduring popularity of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> has led to <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth_in_popular_culture" title="Middle-earth in popular culture">numerous references in popular culture</a>, the founding of many societies by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tolkien_fandom" title="Tolkien fandom">fans of Tolkien's works</a>, and a large number of books about Tolkien and his works being published. <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> has inspired (and continues to inspire) short stories, <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth_in_video_games" title="Middle-earth in video games">video games</a>, artworks and musical works (see <a href="/wiki/Works_inspired_by_J._R._R._Tolkien" title="Works inspired by J. R. R. Tolkien">Works inspired by J. R. R. Tolkien</a>). Numerous adaptations of Tolkien's works have been made for a wide range of media. <a href="/wiki/Adaptations_of_The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="Adaptations of The Lord of the Rings">Adaptations of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i></a> in particular have been made for the radio, for the theatre, and for film. The 2001â2003 release of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy" title="The Lord of the Rings film trilogy"><i>Lord of the Rings</i> film trilogy</a> saw a surge of interest in <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> and Tolkien's other works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surge_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-surge-8">[9]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The back story begins thousands of years before the action in the book, with the rise of the eponymous Lord of the Rings, the Dark Lord <a href="/wiki/Sauron" title="Sauron">Sauron</a>, a malevolent incarnated <a href="/wiki/Maia" title="Maia">immortal spiritual being</a> who possessed great supernatural powers and who later became the ruler of the dreaded realm of <a href="/wiki/Mordor" title="Mordor">Mordor</a>. At the end of the <a href="/wiki/First_Age" title="First Age">First Age</a> of Middle-earth, Sauron survived the catastrophic defeat and chaining of his lord, the ultimate <a href="/wiki/Dark_Lord" title="Dark Lord">Dark Lord</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morgoth_Bauglir" title="Morgoth Bauglir">Morgoth Bauglir</a> (who was formerly counted as one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vala" title="Vala">Vala</a>, the angelic <i>Powers</i> of the <a href="/wiki/Arda" title="Arda">world</a>). During the <a href="/wiki/Second_Age" title="Second Age">Second Age</a>, Sauron schemed to gain dominion over Middle-earth. In the disguise as "Annatar" or <i>Lord of Gifts</i>, he aided <a href="/wiki/Celebrimbor" title="Celebrimbor">Celebrimbor</a> and other Elven-smiths of <a href="/wiki/Eregion" title="Eregion">Eregion</a> in the forging of magical rings which confer various powers and effects on their wearers. The most important of these were The Nine, the seven an the three (which he did not touch or know of the three.) called the <i><a href="/wiki/Rings_of_Power" title="Rings of Power">Rings of Power</a></i> or <i>Great Rings</i>.
</p><p>However, he then secretly forged a Great Ring of his own, the <a href="/wiki/One_Ring" title="One Ring">One Ring</a>, by which he planned to enslave the wearers of the other Rings of Power. This plan failed when the Elves became aware of him and took off their Rings. Sauron then launched a war during which he captured sixteen and distributed them to lords and kings of Dwarves and Men; these Rings were known as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seven_rings" title="Seven rings">Seven</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nine_Rings" title="Nine Rings">Nine</a> respectively. The Dwarf-lords proved too tough to enslave although their natural desire for wealth, especially gold, increased; this brought more conflict between them and other races. The Men who possessed the Nine were slowly corrupted over time and eventually became the <i><a href="/wiki/Nazg%C3%BBl" title="NazgÃ»l">NazgÃ»l</a></i> or Ringwraiths, Sauron's most feared servants. The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Three_Rings" title="Three Rings">Three</a> Sauron failed to capture, and remained in the possession of the Elves (who forged these independently). The war ended as the Men of the island-nation of <a href="/wiki/N%C3%BAmenor" title="NÃºmenor">NÃºmenor</a>, a great nation, helped the besieged Elves, and Sauron's forces retreated from the coasts of Eriador. At this time he still held most of Middle-earth, excluding Imladris (<a href="/wiki/Rivendell" title="Rivendell">Rivendell</a>) and the <a href="/wiki/Gulf_of_Lune" title="Gulf of Lune">Gulf of Lune</a>.
</p>

<p>Over 1500 years later, word reaches the current <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kings_of_N%C3%BAmenor" title="Kings of NÃºmenor">Kings of NÃºmenor</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ar-Pharaz%C3%B4n" title="Ar-PharazÃ´n">Ar-PharazÃ´n</a>, that Sauron has been bearing the title "Lord of all Middle-earth". This provoked Ar-PharazÃ´n and gave him an opportunity to show the glory and strength of NÃºmenor. He arrived in Middle-earth with such overwhelming force that Sauron's armies flee at the sight of them. Abandoned by his minions, Sauron surrendered to the NÃºmenÃ³reans, and was taken to NÃºmenor as a "prisoner". Sauron then started to poison the minds of the NÃºmenÃ³reans against the Valar. Thus, Sauron set into motion events that brought about NÃºmenor's destruction. He did this by corrupting the King's mind, telling him that the immortality of the Elves was his to take if he set foot upon the lands of <a href="/wiki/Aman" title="Aman">Aman</a>, the <i>Blessed Realm</i>, where <a href="/wiki/Valinor" title="Valinor">Valinor</a>, the realm of the Valar, was located. With old age on his mind, Ar-PharazÃ´n lead an invasion of Aman and Valinor with the greatest host seen since the <a href="/wiki/War_of_Wrath" title="War of Wrath">end of the First Age</a>. However, upon reaching Aman, he and his army were buried by a landslide, and there they would remain until the <a href="/wiki/Dagor_Dagorath" title="Dagor Dagorath">Final Battle</a> in Tolkien's <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eschatology" title="wikipedia:Eschatology">eschatology</a>. <a href="/wiki/Manw%C3%AB" title="ManwÃ«">ManwÃ«</a>, the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Arda" title="King of Arda">King of Arda</a>, calls upon <a href="/wiki/Eru_Il%C3%BAvatar" title="Eru IlÃºvatar">Eru IlÃºvatar</a> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/God" title="God">God</a>), who opened a great chasm in the sea, destroying NÃºmenor, and removed the Undying Lands from the mortal world. The destruction of NÃºmenor destroyed Sauron's fair and handsome physical body, but his spirit returned to Mordor and assumes a new form â black, burning hot (though he was not on fire), and terrible. 
</p><p>Over 100 years later, he launched an attack against the NÃºmenÃ³rean exiled (the Faithful, who did not join Ar-PharazÃ´n's expedition), who managed to escape to Middle-earth. However, the exiles (led by <a href="/wiki/Elendil" title="Elendil">Elendil</a> and his sons <a href="/wiki/Isildur" title="Isildur">Isildur</a> and <a href="/wiki/An%C3%A1rion" title="AnÃ¡rion">AnÃ¡rion</a>) had time to prepare, and, after forming the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Last_Alliance_of_Elves_and_Men" title="Last Alliance of Elves and Men">Last Alliance of Elves and Men</a> with the Elven-king <a href="/wiki/Gil-galad" title="Gil-galad">Gil-galad</a>, they marched against Mordor, defeated Sauron on the plain of <a href="/wiki/Dagorlad" title="Dagorlad">Dagorlad</a>, and besieged <a href="/wiki/Barad-d%C3%BBr" title="Barad-dÃ»r">Barad-dÃ»r</a>, at which time AnÃ¡rion was slain. After seven years of siege, Sauron himself was ultimately forced to engage in single combat with the leaders. Gil-galad and Elendil perished as they combat Sauron, and Elendil's sword, <a href="/wiki/Narsil" title="Narsil">Narsil</a>, broke beneath him. However, Sauron's body was also overcome and slain,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letters_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-letters-3">[4]</a></sup> and Isildur cut the One Ring from Sauron's hand with the hilt-shard of Narsil, and at this Sauron's spirit flees and does not reappear in his terrible form for many centuries. Isildur was advised to destroy the One Ring by the only way it could be â by casting it into the volcanic <a href="/wiki/Mount_Doom" title="Mount Doom">Mount Doom</a> where it was forged â but he refused, attracted to its beauty and kept it as compensation for the deaths of his father and brother (weregild).
</p><p>So began the <a href="/wiki/Third_Age" title="Third Age">Third Age</a> of Middle-earth. Two years later, while journeying to <a href="/wiki/Rivendell" title="Rivendell">Rivendell</a>, Isildur and his soldiers were ambushed by a band of Orcs at what was eventually called the <a href="/wiki/Disaster_of_the_Gladden_Fields" title="Disaster of the Gladden Fields">Disaster of the Gladden Fields</a>. While the latter were almost all killed, Isildur escaped by putting on the Ring â which made mortal wearers invisible. However, the Ring slipped from his finger while he was swimming in the great River <a href="/wiki/Anduin" title="Anduin">Anduin</a>; he was killed by Orc-arrows and the Ring was lost for two millennia. It was then found by chance by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hobbit" title="Hobbit">hobbit</a> named <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9agol" title="DÃ©agol">DÃ©agol</a>.  His relative and friend<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letters_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-letters-3">[4]</a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sm%C3%A9agol" title="SmÃ©agol">SmÃ©agol</a> strangled him for the Ring and was banished from his home by his maternal grandmother. He fled to the <a href="/wiki/Misty_Mountains" title="Misty Mountains">Misty Mountains</a> where he slowly withered and became a disgusting, slimy creature called <a href="/wiki/Gollum" title="Gollum">Gollum</a>. 
</p><p>In <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">The Hobbit</a>,</i> set 60 years before the events in <i>The Lord of the Rings,</i> Tolkien relates the story of the seemingly accidental finding of the Ring by another hobbit, <a href="/wiki/Bilbo_Baggins" title="Bilbo Baggins">Bilbo Baggins</a>, who takes it to his home, <a href="/wiki/Bag_End" title="Bag End">Bag End</a>. Story-externally, the tale related in <i>The Hobbit</i> was written before <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, and it was only later that the author developed Bilbo's magic ring into the "One Ring." Neither Bilbo nor the wizard, <a href="/wiki/Gandalf" title="Gandalf">Gandalf</a>, are aware at this point that Bilbo's magic ring is the One Ring, forged by the Dark Lord Sauron.
</p>

<p><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> takes up the story about 60 years after the end of <i>The Hobbit.</i> The story begins in the first volume, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring" title="The Fellowship of the Ring">The Fellowship of the Ring</a>,</i> when <a href="/wiki/Frodo_Baggins" title="Frodo Baggins">Frodo Baggins</a>, Bilbo's adoptive heir, came into possession of Bilbo's magic ring. Bilbo's old friend, <a href="/wiki/Gandalf" title="Gandalf">Gandalf the Grey</a>, who got Bilbo involved in the adventures in <i>The Hobbit</i> that led to the discovery of the Ring, discovered that it was in fact the <a href="/wiki/One_Ring" title="One Ring">One Ring</a>, the instrument of Sauron's power and the object for which the Dark Lord has been searching for most of the Third Age, and which corrupted others with desire for it and the power it held.
</p><p>Sauron sent the sinister Ringwraiths, in the guise of riders in black, to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Shire" title="The Shire">the Shire</a>, Frodo's native land, in search of the Ring. Frodo escaped, with the help of his loyal gardener <a href="/wiki/Samwise_Gamgee" title="Samwise Gamgee">Sam Gamgee</a> and three close friends, <a href="/wiki/Meriadoc_Brandybuck" title="Meriadoc Brandybuck">Merry Brandybuck</a>, <a href="/wiki/Peregrin_Took" title="Peregrin Took">Pippin Took</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fredegar_Bolger" title="Fredegar Bolger">Fatty Bolger</a>. While Fatty acted as decoy for the Ringwraiths, Frodo and the others set off to take the Ring to the Elven haven of <a href="/wiki/Rivendell" title="Rivendell">Rivendell</a>. They were aided by the enigmatic <a href="/wiki/Tom_Bombadil" title="Tom Bombadil">Tom Bombadil</a>, who saved them from <a href="/wiki/Old_Man_Willow" title="Old Man Willow">Old Man Willow</a> and took them in for a few days of feasting, rest, and counsel. At the town of <a href="/wiki/Bree" title="Bree">Bree</a>, Frodo's party was joined by a man called "Strider", who was revealed, in a letter left by Gandalf at the <a href="/wiki/The_Prancing_Pony" title="The Prancing Pony">local inn</a> for Frodo, to be <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aragorn" title="Aragorn">Aragorn</a>, the heir to the kingships of <a href="/wiki/Gondor" title="Gondor">Gondor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Arnor" title="Arnor">Arnor</a>, two great realms founded by the NÃºmenÃ³rean exiles. Aragorn led the hobbits to Rivendell on Gandalf's request. However, Frodo was gravely wounded by the leader of the Ringwraiths, though he managed to recover under the care of the <a href="/wiki/Half-elven" title="Half-elven">Half-elven</a> lord <a href="/wiki/Elrond" title="Elrond">Elrond</a>. 
</p><p>In Rivendell, the hobbits also learned that Sauron's forces could only be resisted if Aragorn took up his inheritance and fulfilled an ancient prophecy by wielding the sword <a href="/wiki/Narsil" title="Narsil">AndÃºril</a>, which had been forged anew from the shards of <a href="/wiki/Narsil" title="Narsil">Narsil</a>, the sword that cut the Ring from Sauron's finger in the Second Age. <a href="/wiki/Council_of_Elrond" title="Council of Elrond">A high council</a>, attended by representatives of the major races of Middle-earth; Elves, Dwarves, and Men, and presided over by Elrond, decide that the only course of action that can save Middle-earth is to destroy the Ring by taking it to Mordor and casting it into <a href="/wiki/Mount_Doom" title="Mount Doom">Mount Doom</a>, where it was forged.
</p><p>Frodo volunteered for the task, and a "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fellowship_of_the_Ring_(characters)" title="Fellowship of the Ring (characters)">Fellowship of the Ring</a>" were formed to aid him â consisting of Frodo, his three Hobbit companions, Gandalf, Aragorn, <a href="/wiki/Boromir" title="Boromir">Boromir</a> of Gondor, <a href="/wiki/Gimli" title="Gimli">Gimli</a> the Dwarf, and <a href="/wiki/Legolas" title="Legolas">Legolas</a> the Elf. Their journey took them through the Mines of <a href="/wiki/Moria" title="Moria">Moria</a>, where they were being followed by the wretched creature <a href="/wiki/Gollum" title="Gollum">Gollum</a>, whom Bilbo had met in the Goblin-caves of the Misty Mountains years before. (The full tale of their meeting is told in <i>The Hobbit.</i>) Gollum long possessed the Ring before it passed to Bilbo. Gandalf explained that Gollum belonged to a people "of hobbit-kind" before he came upon the Ring, which corrupted him. A slave to the Ring's evil power, Gollum desperately sought to regain his "Precious." As they proceeded through the Mines, Pippin unintentionally betrayed their presence and the party was attacked by creatures of Sauron. Gandalf battled a giant subterranean demon, the <a href="/wiki/Balrog" title="Balrog">Balrog</a>, and fell into a deep chasm, apparently to his death. Escaping from Moria, the Fellowship, now led by Aragorn, go to the Elven realm of <a href="/wiki/Lothl%C3%B3rien" title="LothlÃ³rien">LothlÃ³rien</a>. Here, the Lady <a href="/wiki/Galadriel" title="Galadriel">Galadriel</a> showed Frodo and Sam visions of the past, present, and future. Frodo also saw the <a href="/wiki/Eye_of_Sauron" title="Eye of Sauron">Eye of Sauron</a>, a metaphysical expression of Sauron himself, and Galadriel was tempted by the Ring. By the end of the first volume, after the Fellowship has travelled along the great River <a href="/wiki/Anduin" title="Anduin">Anduin</a>, Frodo decided to continue the trek to Mordor on his own, largely due to the Ring's growing influence on Boromir; however, the faithful Sam insisted on going with him.
</p><p>In the second volume, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Two_Towers" title="The Two Towers">The Two Towers</a>,</i> a parallel story, told in the first book of the volume, details the exploits of the remaining members of the Fellowship who aided the country of <a href="/wiki/Rohan" title="Rohan">Rohan</a> in its war against the emerging evil of <a href="/wiki/Saruman" title="Saruman">Saruman</a>, leader of the Order of Wizards, who wanted the Ring for himself. At the start of the first book, the Fellowship was further scattered; Merry and Pippin were captured by Sauron and Saruman's orcs, Boromir was mortally wounded defending them, and Aragorn and the others went off in pursuit of their captors. The three met Gandalf, who has returned to Middle-earth as "Gandalf the White": they found out that he slew the Balrog of Moria, and although the battle also proved fatal to Gandalf, he was then sent back and "reborn" as a more imposing figure. At the end of the first book, Gandalf, Aragorn, Legolas and Gimli helped defeat Saruman's armies at the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Hornburg" title="Battle of the Hornburg">Battle of the Hornburg</a> while Saruman himself was cornered by the tree-like <a href="/wiki/Ents" title="Ents">Ents</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Huorns" title="Huorns">Huorns</a>, accompanied by Merry and Pippin, who escaped from captivity, and the two groups' paths crossed.
</p><p>The second book of the volume tells of Frodo and Sam's exploits on the way to Mount Doom. They managed to capture and "tame" Gollum, who then showed them a way to enter Mordor secretly (as opposed to the <a href="/wiki/Black_Gate" title="Black Gate">Black Gate</a>), albeit through the dreaded realm of <a href="/wiki/Minas_Morgul" title="Minas Morgul">Minas Morgul</a>. At the end of the volume, Gollum betrayed Frodo to the great spider, <a href="/wiki/Shelob" title="Shelob">Shelob</a>, and though he survived, he was captured by Orcs. Meanwhile, Sauron launched an all-out military assault upon Middle-earth, with the <a href="/wiki/Witch-king_of_Angmar" title="Witch-king of Angmar">Witch-king</a> (leader of the Ringwraiths) leading a fell host from <a href="/wiki/Minas_Morgul" title="Minas Morgul">Minas Morgul</a> into battle against <a href="/wiki/Gondor" title="Gondor">Gondor</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Ring" title="War of the Ring">War of the Ring</a>.
</p><p>In the third volume, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_King" title="The Return of the King">The Return of the King</a>,</i> the further adventures of Gandalf, Aragorn and company are related in the first book of the volume, while Frodo and Sam's are related in the second, as with <i>The Two Towers.</i> As told in the first book, the Fellowship assisted in the final battles against the armies of Sauron, including the siege of the tower-city of <a href="/wiki/Minas_Tirith" title="Minas Tirith">Minas Tirith</a> in Gondor and the climactic life-or-death battle before the <a href="/wiki/Black_Gate" title="Black Gate">Black Gate</a> of Mordor, where the alliance of Gondor and Rohan fought desperately against Sauron's armies in order to distract him from the Ring, hoping to gain time for Frodo to destroy it.
</p><p>In the second book, Sam rescued Frodo from captivity. After much struggle, they finally reached Mount Doom itself, tailed by Gollum. However, the temptation of the Ring proved too great for Frodo and he claimed it for himself. However, Gollum struggled with him and managed to bite the Ring off. Crazed with triumph, Gollum slipped into the fires of the mountain, and the Ring was destroyed.
</p><p>Finally, Sauron was defeated, and Aragorn was crowned king. However, all was not over, for Saruman managed to escape and <a href="/wiki/Scouring_of_the_Shire" title="Scouring of the Shire">scour the Shire</a> before being overthrown. At the end, Frodo remained wounded in body and spirit and went west accompanied by Bilbo over the Sea to Valinor, where he could find peace.
</p><p>According to Tolkien's <a href="/wiki/Timeline_of_Arda" title="Timeline of Arda">timeline</a>, the events depicted in the story occurred between Bilbo's announcement of his September 22, <a href="/wiki/TA_3001" title="TA 3001">3001</a> <a href="/wiki/Bilbo%27s_Farewell_Birthday_Party" title="Bilbo's Farewell Birthday Party">birthday party</a>, and Sam's re-arrival to Bag End on October 6, <a href="/wiki/TA_3021" title="TA 3021">3021</a>. Most of the events portrayed in the story occur in 3018 and 3019, with Frodo heading out from Bag End on September 23 <a href="/wiki/TA_3018" title="TA 3018">3018</a>, and the destruction of the Ring six months later on <a href="/wiki/March_25" title="March 25">March 25</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/3019" title="3019">3019</a>.
</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Books">Books</span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span></h3>
<table class="infobox" style="text-align: center;">
<tr>
<th style="background: #ccf;"> <strong class="selflink">The Lord of the Rings</strong>
</th></tr>
<tr>
<td> <a href="/wiki/The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring_(novel)" title="The Fellowship of the Ring (novel)">Volume I</a> - <a href="/wiki/The_Two_Towers_(novel)" title="The Two Towers (novel)">Volume II</a> - <a href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_King_(novel)" title="The Return of the King (novel)">Volume III</a>
</td></tr></table>
<p><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> was started as a sequel to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">The Hobbit</a>,</i> a fantasy story that Tolkien had written for, and read to, his children, which was published in 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[10]</a></sup> The popularity of <i>The Hobbit</i> led to demands from his publishers for more stories about Hobbits and <a href="/wiki/Goblins" title="Goblins">goblins</a>, and so that same year, at the age of 45, Tolkien began writing the story that would become <i>The Lord of the Rings.</i> The story would not be finished until 12 years later, in 1949, and it would not be fully published until 1955, by which time Tolkien was 63 years old.
</p><p>Tolkien did not originally intend to write a sequel to <i>The Hobbit,</i> and instead wrote several other children's tales, including <i><a href="/wiki/Roverandom" title="Roverandom">Roverandom</a>.</i> As his main work, Tolkien began to outline the history of <a href="/wiki/Arda" title="Arda">Arda</a>, telling tales of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silmarils" title="Silmarils">Silmarils</a>, and many other stories of how the races and situations that we read about in the Lord of the Rings came to be. Tolkien died before he could complete and put together this work, today known as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Silmarillion" title="The Silmarillion">The Silmarillion</a>,</i> but his son <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Tolkien" title="Christopher Tolkien">Christopher Tolkien</a> edited his father's work, filled in gaps, and published it in 1977.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10">[11]</a></sup> Some Tolkien biographers regard <i>The Silmarillion</i> as the true "work of his heart",<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shippey_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-shippey-11">[12]</a></sup> as it provides the historical and linguistic context for the more popular work and for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Languages_of_Middle-earth" title="Languages of Middle-earth">his constructed languages</a>, and occupied the greater part of Tolkien's time. As a result <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> ended up as the last movement of Tolkien's legendarium and in his own as a "much larger, and I hope also in proportion the best, of the entire cycle".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letters_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-letters-3">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Persuaded by his publishers, he started 'a new Hobbit' in December 1937.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[10]</a></sup> After several false starts, the story of the One Ring soon emerged, and the book mutated from being a <a href="/wiki/Sequel" title="Sequel">sequel</a> to <i>The Hobbit,</i> to being, in theme, more a sequel to the unpublished <i><a href="/wiki/The_Silmarillion" title="The Silmarillion">Silmarillion</a>.</i> The idea of the first chapter (<i>A Long-Expected Party</i>) arrived fully-formed, although the reasons behind Bilbo's disappearance, the significance of the Ring, and the title <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> did not arrive until the spring of 1938.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[10]</a></sup> Originally, he planned to write another story in which Bilbo had used up all his treasure and was looking for another adventure to gain more; however, he remembered the ring and its powers and decided to write about it instead.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[10]</a></sup> He began with Bilbo as the main character but decided that the story was too serious to use the fun-loving hobbit and so Tolkien looked to use a member of Bilbo's family.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[10]</a></sup> He thought about using Bilbo's son, but this generated some difficult questions, such as the whereabouts of his wife and whether he would let his son go into danger. Thus he looked for an alternate character to carry the ring. In Greek legend, it was a hero's nephew that gained the item of power, and so the hobbit Frodo came into existence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>Writing was slow due to Tolkien's perfectionism, and was frequently interrupted by his obligations as an examiner, and other academic duties.<sup class="noprint">[<a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citing_sources" title="wikipedia:Citing sources"><span style="white-space: nowrap;" title="The material in the vicinity of this tag needs references to reliable sources."><font color="red">Source?</font></span></a>]</sup> The first sentence of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">The Hobbit</a></i> was in fact written on a blank page which a student had left on an exam paper which Tolkien was marking â "In a hole in the ground there lived a hobbit."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12">[13]</a></sup> He seems to have abandoned <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> during most of 1943 and only restarted it in April 1944.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[10]</a></sup> This effort was written as a serial for <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Tolkien" title="Christopher Tolkien">Christopher Tolkien</a> and <a href="/wiki/C.S._Lewis" title="C.S. Lewis">C.S. Lewis</a> â the former would be sent copies of chapters as they were written while he was serving in Africa in the Royal Air Force. He made another push in 1946, and showed a copy of the manuscript to his publishers in 1947.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[10]</a></sup> The story was effectively finished the next year, but Tolkien did not finish revising earlier parts of the work until 1949.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[10]</a></sup>
</p><p>A dispute with his publishers, <a href="/wiki/Allen_%26_Unwin" title="Allen &amp; Unwin">Allen &amp; Unwin</a>, led to the book being offered to <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/HarperCollins" title="wikipedia:HarperCollins">HarperCollins</a> in 1950. He intended <i>The Silmarillion</i> (itself largely unrevised at this point) to be published along with <i>The Lord of the Rings,</i> but A&amp;U were unwilling to do this. After his contact at Collins, Milton Waldman, expressed the belief that <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> itself "urgently needed cutting", he eventually demanded that they publish the book in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1952" title="1952">1952</a>. They did not do so, and so Tolkien wrote to Allen and Unwin, saying "I would gladly consider the publication of any part of the stuff."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-genesis_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-genesis-9">[10]</a></sup>
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication">Publication</span></h3>
<p>For publication, due largely to post-war paper shortages, but also to keep the price of the first volume down, the book was divided into three volumes: <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring" title="The Fellowship of the Ring">The Fellowship of the Ring</a></i>: Books I and II, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Two_Towers" title="The Two Towers">The Two Towers</a></i>: Books III and IV, and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_King" title="The Return of the King">The Return of the King</a></i>: Books V and VI plus 6 appendices. Delays in producing appendices, maps and especially indices led to these being published later than originally hoped â on 21 July <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1954" title="1954">1954</a>, 11 November <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1954" title="1954">1954</a> and 20 October <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1955" title="1955">1955</a> respectively in the United Kingdom, slightly later in the United States. <i>The Return of the King</i> was especially delayed. Tolkien, moreover, did not especially like the title <i>The Return of the King</i>, believing it gave away too much of the storyline. He had originally suggested <i><a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Ring" title="War of the Ring">War of the Ring</a>,</i> which was dismissed by his publishers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13">[14]</a></sup>
</p>

<p>The books were published under a 'profit-sharing' arrangement, whereby Tolkien would not receive an advance or royalties until the books had broken even, after which he would take a large share of the profits. An index to the entire 3-volume set at the end of third volume was promised in the first volume. However, this proved impractical to compile in a reasonable timescale. Later, in 1966, four indices, not compiled by Tolkien, were added to <i>The Return of the King.</i> Because the three-volume binding was so widely distributed, the work is often referred to as the <i>Lord of the Rings</i> "trilogy." In a letter to W. H. Auden, Tolkien himself made use of the term "trilogy" for the work (<i><a href="/wiki/The_Letters_of_J._R._R._Tolkien" title="The Letters of J. R. R. Tolkien">The Letters of J. R. R. Tolkien</a></i>, #163) though he did at other times consider this incorrect, as it was written and conceived as a single book (<i>Letters,</i> #149). It is also often called a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novel</a>; however, Tolkien also objected to this term as he viewed it as a romance (<i>Letters,</i> #329; "romance" in this sense refers to a heroic tale).
</p><p>A 1999 (Millennium Edition) British (<a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0261103873">ISBN 0-261-10387-3</a>) 7-volume box set followed the six-book division authored by Tolkien, with the Appendices from the end of <i>The Return of the King</i> bound as a separate volume. The letters of <i>Tolkien</i> appeared on the spines of the boxed set which included a CD. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_history">Publication history</span></h3>
<p>The three parts were first published by <a href="/wiki/Allen_%26_Unwin" title="Allen &amp; Unwin">Allen &amp; Unwin</a> in 1954â1955, several months apart. They have since been reissued many times by multiple publishers, as one, three, six or seven volume sets. The two most common current printings are <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0618343997">ISBN 0-618-34399-7</a> (one-volume) and <a class="internal mw-magiclink-isbn" href="/wiki/Special:BookSources/0618346244">ISBN 0-618-34624-4</a> (three volume set). In the early 1960s, Donald A. Wollheim, <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_fiction" title="wikipedia:Science fiction">science fiction</a> editor of the paperback publisher Ace Books, realized that <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> was not protected in the United States under American copyright law because the US hardcover edition had been bound from pages printed in the United Kingdom, with the original intention being for them to be printed in the British edition. Ace Books proceeded to publish an edition, unauthorized by Tolkien and without royalties to him. Tolkien took issue with this and quickly notified his fans of this objection. Grass-roots pressure from these fans became so great that Ace books withdrew their edition and made a nominal payment to Tolkien, well below what he might have been due in an appropriate publication. However, this poor beginning was overshadowed when an authorized edition followed from Ballantine Books to tremendous commercial success. By the mid-1960s the books, due to their wide exposure on the American public stage, had become a true cultural phenomenon. Also at this time Tolkien undertook various textual revisions to produce a version of the book that would have a valid US copyright. This would later become known as the Second Edition of <i>The Lord of the Rings.</i>
</p><p>The books have been translated, with various degrees of success, into dozens of other languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14">[15]</a></sup> Tolkien, an expert in philology, examined many of these translations, and had comments on each that reflect both the translation process and his work. To aid translators, Tolkien wrote his <i>Guide to the Names in The Lord of the Rings.</i> Because <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> is said to be a translation of the <i><a href="/wiki/Red_Book_of_Westmarch" title="Red Book of Westmarch">Red Book of Westmarch</a>,</i> translators have an unusual degree of freedom when translating <i>The Lord of the Rings.</i> This allows for such translations as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Elf" title="Elf">elf</a> becoming <i>Elb</i> in German â Elb does not carry the connotations of mischief that its English counterpart does and therefore is more true to the work that Tolkien created. In contrast to the usual modern practice, names intended to have a particular meaning in the English version are translated to provide a similar meaning in the target language: in German, for example, the name "Baggins" becomes "Beutlin," containing the word <i>Beutel</i> meaning "bag."
</p><p>In 1990 Recorded Books published an unabridged audio version of the books. They hired British actor <a class="new" href="/wiki/Rob_Inglis?action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Rob Inglis (page does not exist)">Rob Inglis</a>, who had starred in a one man production of <i>The Hobbit</i>, to read. Inglis performs the books verbatim, using distinct voices for each character, and sings all of the songs. Tolkien had written music for some of the songs in the book. For the rest, Inglis, along with director Claudia Howard wrote additional music. The current ISBN is 1402516274.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences">Influences</span></h3>
<p><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> began as a personal exploration by Tolkien of his interests in philology, religion (particularly Roman Catholicism), <a href="/wiki/Fairy_tale" title="Fairy tale">fairy tales</a>, as well as <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse</a> and Celtic <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythology</a>, but it was also crucially influenced by the effects of his military service during <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15">[16]</a></sup> Tolkien detailed his creation to an astounding extent; he created a complete mythology for his realm of Middle-earth, including genealogies of characters, languages, writing systems, calendars and histories. Some of this supplementary material is detailed in the appendices to <i>The Lord of the Rings,</i> and the mythological history woven into a large, Biblically-styled volume entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Silmarillion" title="The Silmarillion">The Silmarillion</a>.</i> However many parts of the world he crafted, as he freely admitted, are influenced by other sources.
</p><p>Tolkien's largest influences in the creation of his world were his Catholic faith and the Bible.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16">[17]</a></sup> Tolkien once described <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> to his friend, the English Jesuit Father Robert Murray, as "<i>a fundamentally religious and Catholic work, unconsciously so at first, but consciously in the revision.</i>"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letters_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-letters-3">[4]</a></sup> There are many theological themes underlying the narrative including the battle of good versus evil, the triumph of humility over pride, and the activity of grace. In addition the saga includes themes which incorporate death and immortality, mercy and pity, resurrection, salvation, repentance, self-sacrifice, free will, justice, fellowship, authority and healing. In addition the Lord's Prayer "And lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from evil" was reportedly present in Tolkien's mind as he described Frodo's struggles against the power of the One Ring.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letters_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-letters-3">[4]</a></sup>
</p><p>Non-Christian religious motifs also had strong influences in Tolkien's Middle-earth. His Ainur, a race of angelic beings who are responsible for <a href="/wiki/Ainulindal%C3%AB" title="AinulindalÃ«">conceptualising the world</a>, includes the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vala" title="Vala">Valar</a>, the pantheon of "gods" who are responsible for the maintenance of everything from skies and seas to dreams and doom, and their servants, the <a href="/wiki/Maiar" title="Maiar">Maiar</a>. The concept of the Valar echoes Greek and Norse mythologies, although the Ainur and <a href="/wiki/Arda" title="Arda">the world</a> itself are all creations of a monotheistic deity â <a href="/wiki/Eru_Il%C3%BAvatar" title="Eru IlÃºvatar">IlÃºvatar or Eru, "The One"</a>. As the external practice of Middle-earth religion is downplayed in <i>The Lord of the Rings,</i> explicit information about them is only given in the different versions of <i>Silmarillion</i> material. However, there remain allusions to this aspect of Tolkien's mythos, including "the Great Enemy" who was Sauron's master and "Elbereth, Queen of Stars" (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Morgoth" title="Morgoth">Morgoth</a> and <a href="/wiki/Varda" title="Varda">Varda</a> respectively, two of the Valar) in the main text, the "Authorities" (referring to the Valar, literally <i>Powers</i>) in the Prologue, and "the One" in Appendix A. Other non-Christian mythological elements can be seen, including other sentient non-humans (Dwarves, Elves, Hobbits and Ents), a "Green Man" (<a href="/wiki/Tom_Bombadil" title="Tom Bombadil">Tom Bombadil</a>), and spirits or ghosts (<a href="/wiki/Barrow-wights" title="Barrow-wights">Barrow-wights</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dead_Men_of_Dunharrow" title="Dead Men of Dunharrow">Oathbreakers</a>).
</p>
<figure class="article-thumb tleft show-info-icon" style="width: 190px"> <a class="image image-thumbnail" href="http://img2.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20070102112551/lotr/images/4/45/Cover_lotr_green_gandalf.jpg"><img alt="Cover lotr green gandalf" class="thumbimage lzy lzyPlcHld " data-image-key="Cover_lotr_green_gandalf.jpg" data-image-name="Cover lotr green gandalf.jpg" data-src="http://img1.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20070102112551/lotr/images/thumb/4/45/Cover_lotr_green_gandalf.jpg/190px-Cover_lotr_green_gandalf.jpg" height="292" onload="if(typeof ImgLzy==='object'){ImgLzy.load(this)}" src="data:image/gif;base64,R0lGODlhAQABAIABAAAAAP///yH5BAEAAAEALAAAAAABAAEAQAICTAEAOw%3D%3D" width="190"><noscript><img alt="Cover lotr green gandalf" class="thumbimage " data-image-key="Cover_lotr_green_gandalf.jpg" data-image-name="Cover lotr green gandalf.jpg" height="292" src="http://img1.wikia.nocookie.net/__cb20070102112551/lotr/images/thumb/4/45/Cover_lotr_green_gandalf.jpg/190px-Cover_lotr_green_gandalf.jpg" width="190"/></noscript></img></a> <figcaption> <a class="sprite info-icon" href="/wiki/File:Cover_lotr_green_gandalf.jpg"></a> <p class="caption">Gandalf the "<a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odin" title="wikipedia:Odin">Odinic</a> wanderer", from a book cover by <a href="/wiki/John_Howe" title="John Howe">John Howe</a>.</p> </figcaption> </figure>
<p>The mythologies from northern Europe are perhaps the best known non-Christian influences on Tolkien. His Elves and Dwarves are by and large based on <a href="/wiki/Norse_mythology" title="Norse mythology">Norse</a> and related Germanic mythologies. <sup title="Needs citation">[<a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citing_sources" title="wikipedia:Citing sources"><i>citationÂ needed</i></a>]</sup> The figure of Gandalf is particularly influenced by the Germanic deity Odin in his incarnation as "the Wanderer", an old man with one eye, a long white beard, a wide brimmed hat, and a staff; Tolkien states that he thinks of Gandalf as an <i>"Odinic wanderer"</i> in a letter of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1946" title="1946">1946</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-letters_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-letters-3">[4]</a></sup> Finnish mythology and more specifically the Finnish national epic <i><a href="/wiki/Kalevala" title="Kalevala">Kalevala</a></i> were also acknowledged by Tolkien as an influence on Middle-earth. <sup title="Needs citation">[<a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citing_sources" title="wikipedia:Citing sources"><i>citationÂ needed</i></a>]</sup> In a similar manner to <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, the <i>Kalevala</i> centres around a magical item of great power, the <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sampo" title="wikipedia:Sampo">Sampo</a>, which bestows great fortune on its owner but never makes its exact nature clear. Like the One Ring, the Sampo is fought over by forces of good and evil, and is ultimately lost to the world as it is destroyed towards the end of the story. In another parallel, the latter work's wizard character <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/V%C3%A4in%C3%A4m%C3%B6inen" title="wikipedia:VÃ¤inÃ¤mÃ¶inen">VÃ¤inÃ¤mÃ¶inen</a> also has many similarities to Gandalf in his immortal origins and wise nature, and both works end with their respective wizard departing on a ship to lands beyond the mortal world. Tolkien also based his <a href="/wiki/Elvish_languages" title="Elvish languages">Elvish language</a> <a href="/wiki/Quenya" title="Quenya">Quenya</a> on Finnish.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17">[18]</a></sup>
</p><p>In addition <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> was crucially influenced by Tolkien's experiences during World War I and his son's during World War II. The central action of the books â a climactic, age-ending war between good and evil â is the central event of many world mythologies, notably Norse, but it is also a clear reference to the well-known description of World War I, which was commonly referred to as "the war to end all wars."
After the publication of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> these influences led to speculation that the One Ring was an allegory for the nuclear bomb.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-la_Hood_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-la_Hood-18">[19]</a></sup> Tolkien, however, repeatedly insisted that his works were not an allegory of any kind. However there is a strong theme of despair in the face of new mechanized warfare that Tolkien himself had experienced in the trenches of World War I. The development of a specially bred Orc army, and the destruction of the environment to aid this, also have modern resonances; and the effects of the Ring on its users evoke the modern literature of drug addiction as much as any historic quest literature.
</p><p>Nevertheless, Tolkien states in the introduction to the books that he disliked allegories and that the story was not one,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19">[20]</a></sup> and it would be irresponsible to dismiss such direct statements on these matters lightly. Tolkien had already completed most of the book, including the ending in its entirety, before the first nuclear bombs were made known to the world at Hiroshima and Nagasaki in August 1945.
</p><p>While Tolkien plausibly denied any specific 'nuclear' reference, it is clear that the Ring has broad applicability to the concept of Absolute Power and its effects, and that the plot hinges on the view that anyone who seeks to gain absolute worldly power will inevitably be corrupted by it. Some also say there is also clear evidence that one of the main subtexts of the story â the passing of a mythical "Golden Age" â was influenced not only by Arthurian legend but also by Tolkien's contemporary anxieties about the growing encroachment of urbanisation and industrialisation into the "traditional" English lifestyle and countryside. The concept of the "ring of power" itself is also present in Plato's Republic and in the story of Gyges' ring (a story often compared to the Book of Job). Many, however, believe Tolkien's most likely source was the Norse tale of <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sigurd_the_Volsung" title="wikipedia:Sigurd the Volsung">Sigurd the Volsung</a>. Some locations and characters were inspired by Tolkien's childhood in <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sarehole" title="wikipedia:Sarehole">Sarehole</a> (then a <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Worcestershire" title="wikipedia:Worcestershire">Worcestershire</a> village, now part of <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Birmingham" title="wikipedia:Birmingham">Birmingham</a>) and Birmingham.
</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span></h3>
<p>Tolkien's work has received mixed reviews since its inception, ranging from terrible to excellent. Recent reviews in various media have been, in a majority, highly positive. On its initial review the <i>Sunday Telegraph</i> felt it was "among the greatest works of imaginative fiction of the twentieth century". The <i>Sunday Times</i> seemed to echo these sentiments when in their review it was stated that "the English-speaking world is divided into those who have read <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> and <i>The Hobbit</i> and those who are going to read them." The New York Herald Tribune also seemed to have an idea of how popular the books would become, writing in its review that they were "destined to outlast our time."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20">[21]</a></sup>
</p><p>Not all original reviews, however, were so kind. <i>New York Times</i> reviewer Judith Shulevitz criticized the "pedantry" of Tolkien's literary style, saying that he "formulated a high-minded belief in the importance of his mission as a literary preservationist, which turns out to be death to literature itself." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21">[22]</a></sup> Critic Richard Jenkyns, writing in <i>The New Republic</i>, criticized a perceived lack of psychological depth. Both the characters and the work itself are, according to Jenkyns, "anemic, and lacking in fiber."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22">[23]</a></sup> Even within Tolkien's social group, <a href="/wiki/The_Inklings" title="The Inklings">The Inklings</a>, reviews were mixed. <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Dyson" title="Hugo Dyson">Hugo Dyson</a> was famously recorded as saying, during one of Tolkien's readings to the group, "Oh no! Not another fucking elf!"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23">[24]</a></sup> However, another Inkling, <a href="/wiki/C.S._Lewis" title="C.S. Lewis">C.S. Lewis</a>, had very different feelings, writing, "here are beauties which pierce like swords or burn like cold iron. Here is a book which will break your heart."
</p><p>Several other authors in the genre, however, seemed to agree more with Dyson than Lewis. Science-fiction author David Brin criticized the books for what he perceived to be their unquestioning devotion to a traditional elitist social structure, their positive depiction of the slaughter of the opposing forces, and their romantic backward-looking worldview.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24">[25]</a></sup> Michael Moorcock, another famous science fiction and fantasy author, is also a fervent detractor of <i>The Lord of the Rings.</i> In his essay, "Epic Pooh," he equates Tolkien's work to <i>Winnie-the-Pooh</i> and criticizes it and similar works for their perceived Merry England point of view.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25">[26]</a></sup> Incidentally, Moorcock met both Tolkien and Lewis in his teens and claims to have liked them personally, even though he does not admire them on artistic grounds.
</p><p>More recently, critical analysis has focused on Tolkien's experiences in the <a href="/wiki/First_World_War" title="First World War">First World War</a>; writers such as John Garth in 'Tolkien and the Great War', Janet Brennan Croft and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Shippey" title="Tom Shippey">Tom Shippey</a> all look in detail at this aspect and compare the imagery, mental landscape and traumas in Lord of the Rings with those experienced by soldiers in the trenches and the history of the Great War. John Carey, formerly Merton Professor of English Literature at Oxford University, speaking in April 2003 on the BBC "Big Read" programme which voted Lord of the Rings "Britain's best-loved book", said that "Tolkien's writing is essentially a species of war literature; not as direct perhaps as Wilfred Owen, or as solid as some, but very, very interesting as that â the most solid reflection on war experiences written up as fantasy."
</p><p><i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, despite not being published in paperback until the 1960s, sold well in hardback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26">[27]</a></sup>
In 1957 it was awarded the <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/International_Fantasy_Award" title="wikipedia:International Fantasy Award">International Fantasy Award</a>. Despite its numerous detractors, the publication of the Ace Books and Ballantine paperbacks helped <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> become immensely popular in the 1960s. The book has remained so ever since, ranking as one of the most popular works of fiction of the twentieth century, judged by both sales and reader surveys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27">[28]</a></sup> In the 2003 "Big Read" survey conducted by the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>, <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> was found to be the "Nation's Best-loved Book". Australians voted <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> "My Favourite Book" in a 2004 survey conducted by the Australian ABC.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28">[29]</a></sup> In a 1999 poll of Amazon.com customers, <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> was judged to be their favourite "book of the millennium".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29">[30]</a></sup> In 2002 Tolkien was voted the ninety-second "greatest Briton" in a poll conducted by the BBC, and in 2004 he was voted thirty-fifth in the SABC3's Great South Africans, the only person to appear in both lists. His popularity is not limited just to the English-speaking world: in a 2004 poll inspired by the UKâs "Big Read" survey, about 250,000 Germans found <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> to be their favourite work of literature.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30">[31]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span></h2>

<p><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> has been adapted for film, radio and stage multiple times.
</p><p>The book has been adapted for radio three times. In 1955 and 1956, the British Broadcasting Company (<a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a>) broadcast <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(1956_radio_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (1956 radio series)">The Lord of the Rings</a></i>, a 12-part radio adaptation of the story, of which no recording has survived. A 1979 dramatization of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(1979_radio_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (1979 radio series)">The Lord of the Rings</a></i> was broadcast in the United States and subsequently issued on tape and CD. In 1981 the BBC broadcast, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(1981_radio_series)" title="The Lord of the Rings (1981 radio series)">The Lord of the Rings</a></i>, a new dramatization in 26 half-hour installments.
</p><p>Three film adaptations have been made. The first was <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_(1978_film)" title="The Lord of the Rings (1978 film)">J. R. R. Tolkien's The Lord of the Rings</a></i> (1978), by animator <a href="/wiki/Ralph_Bakshi" title="Ralph Bakshi">Ralph Bakshi</a>, the first part of what was originally intended to be a two-part adaptation of the story (hence its original title, <i>The Lord of the Rings Part 1</i>). It covers <i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> and part of <i>The Two Towers</i>. The second, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_King_(1980_movie)" title="The Return of the King (1980 movie)">The Return of the King</a></i> (1980), was an animated television special by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rankin-Bass" title="Rankin-Bass">Rankin-Bass</a>, who had produced a similar version of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit_(1977_animated_film)" title="The Hobbit (1977 animated film)">The Hobbit</a></i> (1977). The third was director <a href="/wiki/Peter_Jackson" title="Peter Jackson">Peter Jackson</a>'s live action <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings_film_trilogy" title="The Lord of the Rings film trilogy"><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> film trilogy</a>, produced by <a href="/wiki/New_Line_Cinema" title="New Line Cinema">New Line Cinema</a> and released in three installments as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring_(film)" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring (film)">The Lord of the Rings: The Fellowship of the Ring</a></i> (2001), <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Two_Towers_(film)" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers (film)">The Lord of the Rings: The Two Towers</a></i> (2002), and <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings:_The_Return_of_the_King_(film)" title="The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King (film)">The Lord of the Rings: The Return of the King</a></i> (2003). The live-action film trilogy has done much in particular to bring the book into the public consciousness.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-surge_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-surge-8">[9]</a></sup>
</p><p>There have been four stage productions based on the book. Three original full-length stage adaptations of <i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> (2001), <i>The Two Towers</i> (2002), and <i>The Return of the King</i> (2003) were staged in <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cincinnati,_Ohio" title="wikipedia:Cincinnati, Ohio">Cincinnati, Ohio</a>. A stage musical adaptation of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> (2006) was staged in Toronto, Canada.
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influences_on_the_fantasy_genre">Influences on the fantasy genre</span></h2>
<p>Following the massive success of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, Tolkien considered a sequel entitled <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Shadow" title="The New Shadow">The New Shadow</a></i>, in which the <a href="/wiki/Men_of_Gondor" title="Men of Gondor">Men of Gondor</a> turn to dark cults and consider an uprising against Aragorn's son, <a href="/wiki/Eldarion" title="Eldarion">Eldarion</a>. Tolkien decided not to do it, and the incomplete story can be found in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Peoples_of_Middle-earth" title="The Peoples of Middle-earth">The Peoples of Middle-earth</a></i>. Tolkien returned to finish his mythology, which was published in novel form posthumously by <a href="/wiki/Christopher_Tolkien" title="Christopher Tolkien">Christopher Tolkien</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1977" title="1977">1977</a>, and the remaining information of his legendarium was published through <i><a href="/wiki/Unfinished_Tales" title="Unfinished Tales">Unfinished Tales</a></i> (<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1980" title="1980">1980</a>) and <i><a href="/wiki/The_History_of_Middle-earth" title="The History of Middle-earth">The History of Middle-earth</a></i>, a 12 volume series published from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1983" title="1983">1983</a> to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/1996" title="1996">1996</a>, of which <i>The Peoples of Middle-earth</i> is part.
</p><p>Nonetheless, the enormous popularity of Tolkien's epic saga greatly expanded the demand for fantasy fiction. Largely thanks to <i>The Lord of the Rings,</i> the genre flowered throughout the 1960s. Many other books in a broadly similar vein were published (including the <i>Earthsea</i> books of Ursula K. Le Guin, the <i>Thomas Covenant</i> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Novel" title="Novel">novels</a> of Stephen R. Donaldson), and in the case of the <i>Gormenghast</i> books by Mervyn Peake, and <i>The Worm Ouroboros</i> by E. R. Eddison, rediscovered. <sup class="noprint">[<a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citing_sources" title="wikipedia:Citing sources"><span style="white-space: nowrap;" title="The material in the vicinity of this tag needs references to reliable sources."><font color="red">Source?</font></span></a>]</sup>
</p><p>It also strongly influenced the <a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Role-playing_game" title="wikipedia:Role-playing game">role playing game</a> industry which achieved popularity in the 1970s with <i><a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dungeons_%26_Dragons" title="wikipedia:Dungeons &amp; Dragons">Dungeons &amp; Dragons</a></i>. <i>Dungeons &amp; Dragons</i> features many races found in <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> most notably the presence of <a href="/wiki/Halfling" title="Halfling">halflings</a>, elves, dwarves, <a href="/wiki/Half-elven" title="Half-elven">half-elves</a>, <a href="/wiki/Orcs" title="Orcs">orcs</a>, and dragons. However, Gary Gygax, lead designer of the game, maintains that he was influenced very little by <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, stating that he included these elements as a marketing move to draw on the then-popularity of the work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31">[32]</a></sup> <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> also has influenced <i>Magic: The Gathering</i>. <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> has also influenced the creation of various video games, including <i>Baldur's Gate</i>, <i>Everquest</i>, <i>The Elder Scrolls</i>, <i>Neverwinter Nights</i>, and the <i>Warcraft</i> series,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32">[33]</a></sup> as well as <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth_in_video_games" title="Middle-earth in video games">video games set in Middle-earth</a> itself.
</p><p>As in all artistic fields, a great many lesser derivatives of the more prominent works appeared. The term "Tolkienesque" is used in the genre to refer to the oft-used and abused storyline of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>: a group of adventurers embarking on a quest to save a magical fantasy world from the armies of an evil "<a href="/wiki/Dark_Lord" title="Dark Lord">dark lord</a>," and is a testament to how much the popularity of these books has increased, since many critics initially decried it as being "Wagner for children" (a reference to the Ring Cycle) â an especially interesting commentary in light of a possible interpretation of the books as a Christian response to Wagner.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33">[34]</a></sup>
</p><p>The work has also had an influence upon such science fiction authors as Isaac Asimov and Arthur C. Clarke. In fact, Clarke (who compared it to Frank Herbert's <i>Dune</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34">[35]</a></sup>) makes a reference to <a href="/wiki/Mount_Doom" title="Mount Doom">Mount Doom</a> in his work <i>2061: Odyssey Three</i>.<sup title="Needs citation">[<a class="extiw exitstitial" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Citing_sources" title="wikipedia:Citing sources"><i>citationÂ needed</i></a>]</sup> Tolkien also influenced George Lucas' <i>Star Wars</i> films. .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35">[36]</a></sup>
</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Pop_culture_references">Pop culture references</span></h2>
<dl><dd><i>Main article: <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth_in_popular_culture" title="Middle-earth in popular culture">Middle-earth in popular culture</a></i>
</dd></dl>
<p><i>The Lord of the Rings</i> has had a profound and wide-ranging impact on popular culture, from its publication in the 1950s, but especially throughout the 1960s and 1970s, where young people embraced it as a countercultural saga. Its influence has been vastly extended in the present day, thanks to the Peter Jackson live-action films. Well known examples include "Frodo Lives!" and "Gandalf for President", two phrases popular among American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tolkien_fandom" title="Tolkien fandom">Tolkien fans</a> during the 1960s and 1970s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36">[37]</a></sup> "Ramble On", "<a href="/wiki/The_Battle_of_Evermore" title="The Battle of Evermore">The Battle of Evermore</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Misty_Mountain_Hop" title="Misty Mountain Hop">Misty Mountain Hop</a>", three compositions by the British rock band Led Zeppelin that contain explicit references to <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> (with others, such as "Stairway to Heaven", alleged by some to contain such), "Rivendell", a song about the joys of a stay at the Elven haven by the band Rush]] (found on their album <i>Fly by Night</i>, 1975), "Lord of the Rings" and "Gandalf the Wizard" by the German power metal band <a href="/wiki/Blind_Guardian" title="Blind Guardian">Blind Guardian</a> (who have also produced a <i>Silmarillion</i>-inspired album, <i><a href="/wiki/Nightfall_in_Middle-Earth" title="Nightfall in Middle-Earth">Nightfall in Middle-Earth</a></i>), nearly the entire discography of Austrian black metal band Summoning (who have also looked to other Tolkien works for inspiration)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37">[38]</a></sup> Rock band Marillion also take their name from Tolkien's Silmarillion. <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>-themed editions of popular board games (e.g., <a href="/wiki/Risk:_Lord_of_the_Rings_Trilogy_Edition" title="Risk: Lord of the Rings Trilogy Edition">Risk: Lord of the Rings Trilogy Edition</a>, chess and Monopoly),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38">[39]</a></sup> and parodies such as <i><a href="/wiki/Bored_of_the_Rings" title="Bored of the Rings">Bored of the Rings</a></i>, produced for the Harvard Lampoon, and the South Park episode "<a href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_Lord_of_the_Rings_to_the_Two_Towers" title="The Return of the Lord of the Rings to the Two Towers">The Return of the Lord of the Rings to the Two Towers</a>".
</p>
