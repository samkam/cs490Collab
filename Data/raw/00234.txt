<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Unobtainium</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><p>In <a href="/wiki/Engineering" title="Engineering">engineering</a>, <a href="/wiki/Fiction" title="Fiction">fiction</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Thought_experiment" title="Thought experiment">thought experiments</a>, <b>unobtainium</b> is any fictional, extremely rare, costly, or impossible material, or (less commonly) device needed to fulfill a given design for a given application. The properties of any particular unobtainium depend on the intended use. For example, a pulley made of unobtainium might be massless and frictionless; however, if used in a <a href="/wiki/Nuclear_propulsion" title="Nuclear propulsion">nuclear rocket</a>, unobtainium would be light, strong at high temperatures, and resistant to radiation damage. The concept of unobtainium is often applied flippantly or humorously.</p>
<p>The word <i><a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unobtainium" title="wikt:unobtainium">unobtainium</a></i> is derived from <i><a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/unobtainable" title="wikt:unobtainable">unobtainable</a></i> + <i><a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/-ium" title="wikt:-ium">-ium</a></i> (the suffix for a number of <a href="/wiki/Chemical_element" title="Chemical element">elements</a>). It pre-dates the similar-sounding IUPAC <a href="/wiki/Systematic_element_name" title="Systematic element name">systematic element names</a>, such as <a href="/wiki/Ununoctium" title="Ununoctium">Ununoctium</a>. An alternative spelling, <b>unobtanium</b>, is sometimes used, based on a parallel construction to metals such as <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering_origin">Engineering origin</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unobtainium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Engineering origin">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Since the late 1950s,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>a<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langley_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-Langley-2"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> aerospace engineers have used the term <i>"unobtainium"</i> when referring to unusual or costly materials, or when theoretically considering a material perfect for their needs in all respects, except that it does not exist. By the 1990s, the term was in wide use, even in formal engineering papers such as "Towards unobtainium [new composite materials for space applications]."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The word <i>unobtainium</i> may well have been coined in the aerospace industry to refer to materials capable of withstanding the extreme temperatures expected in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atmospheric_reentry" title="Atmospheric reentry">reentry</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Langley_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-Langley-2"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Aerospace_engineering" title="Aerospace engineering">Aerospace engineers</a> are frequently tempted to design aircraft which require parts with strength or resilience beyond that of currently available materials.</p>
<p>Later, <i>unobtainium</i> became an engineering term for practical materials that really exist, but are difficult to get.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> For example, during the development of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/SR-71_Blackbird" title="SR-71 Blackbird">SR-71 Blackbird</a> spy plane, <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_Corporation" title="Lockheed Corporation">Lockheed</a> engineers at the "<a href="/wiki/Skunk_Works" title="Skunk Works">Skunk Works</a>" under Clarence "Kelly" Johnson used <i>unobtainium</i> as a <a href="/wiki/Dysphemism" title="Dysphemism">dysphemism</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a>.</i> Titanium allowed a higher strength-to-weight ratio at the high temperatures the Blackbird would reach, but the <a href="/wiki/Soviet_Union" title="Soviet Union">Soviet Union</a> controlled its supply and was trying to deprive the US armed forces of this valuable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Resource_(economics)" title="Resource (economics)">resource</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>b<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the 1970s, bicycle magazines, such as <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Bike_World&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Bike World (page does not exist)">Bike World</a>,</i> sometimes referred to exotic lightweight bicycle parts as being made of unobtanium, although while expensive they were commercially obtainable. In the same period, driver &amp; engineer <a href="/wiki/Mark_Donohue" title="Mark Donohue">Mark Donohue</a> claimed unobtainium was used in the construction of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Penske" title="Penske">Penske</a> race cars.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Contemporary_popularization">Contemporary popularization</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unobtainium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Contemporary popularization">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>As of 2010, the term has diffused beyond engineering, and appears in the headlines of mainstream newspapers, especially to describe the commercially useful <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rare-earth_metals" title="Rare-earth metals">rare earth elements</a> (particularly <a href="/wiki/Terbium" title="Terbium">terbium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Erbium" title="Erbium">erbium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Dysprosium" title="Dysprosium">dysprosium</a>, <a href="/wiki/Yttrium" title="Yttrium">yttrium</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Neodymium" title="Neodymium">neodymium</a>). These are essential to the performance of <a href="/wiki/Consumer_electronics" title="Consumer electronics">consumer electronics</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Green_technology" title="Green technology">green technology</a>, but the projected <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Demand_(economics)" title="Demand (economics)">demand</a> for them so outstrips their current supply that they are called "unobtainiums" within the <a href="/wiki/Extractive_metallurgy" title="Extractive metallurgy">ore industry</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and by commentators on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/US_Congress" title="US Congress">US Congressional</a> hearings into <a href="/wiki/Plug-in_electric_vehicle#Rare_earth_metals_availability_and_supply_security" title="Plug-in electric vehicle">the "supply security" of rare-earths</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kosich2010_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kosich2010-9"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>"Unobtainium"</i> has come to be used as a synonym for "unobtainable" among people who are neither science fiction fans nor engineers to denote an object that actually exists, but which is very hard to obtain either because of high price (sometimes referred to as "unaffordium") or limited availability. It usually refers to a very <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/High-end" title="High-end">high-end</a> and desirable product; for instance, in the <a href="/wiki/Mountain_biking" title="Mountain biking">mountain biking</a> <a href="/wiki/Community" title="Community">community</a>, "These <a href="/wiki/Titanium" title="Titanium">titanium</a> hubs are unobtainium, man!" Old-car enthusiasts use "unobtainium" to describe parts that are vanishingly rare or no longer available.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In maintaining old equipment, <i>unobtainium</i> refers to replacement parts that are no longer made, such as parts for reel-to-reel audio-tape recorders, or rare vacuum tubes that cost more than the equipment they are fitted to (especially true of certain vacuum tubes, such as the <a href="/wiki/1L6" title="1L6">1L6</a>, used almost exclusively in <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">American</a> battery-powered <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shortwave" title="Shortwave">shortwave</a> radios or the <a href="/wiki/WD-11" title="WD-11">WD-11</a> used in certain early 1920s radios). Similarly, parts for classic &amp; vintage Ferraris are made by a company actually named Unobtainium Supply Co.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>There have been repeated attempts to attribute the name to a real material. Because of the long-standing usage of the term "unobtainium" within the <a href="/wiki/Space_elevator" title="Space elevator">space elevator</a> research community to describe a material with the necessary characteristics,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/LiftPort_Group" title="LiftPort Group">LiftPort Group</a> President Michael Laine has advocated assigning the term as the generic name for cables woven of <a href="/wiki/Carbon_nanotube" title="Carbon nanotube">carbon nanotube</a> fibers, which seem to satisfy the requirements for this application. Since he claimed that sufficiently long nanotube cables will be prohibitively expensive to develop without inexpensive access to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Microgravity_environment" title="Microgravity environment">microgravity</a>, these cables would still be close enough to unobtainable to meet the definition. However, this usage does not seem to have become widespread. The eyewear and fashion wear company <a href="/wiki/Oakley,_Inc." title="Oakley, Inc.">Oakley, Inc.</a> also frequently denotes the material used for many of their eyeglass nosepieces and earpieces, which has the unusual property of increasing tackiness and thus grip when wet, as unobtanium.</p>
<p>Frequent Sunday night/Monday morning host of <i><a href="/wiki/Coast_to_Coast_AM" title="Coast to Coast AM">Coast to Coast AM</a></i> <a href="/wiki/George_Knapp_(journalist)" title="George Knapp (journalist)">George Knapp</a> usually opens his show mentioning unobtainium. As a play on the word, <i><a href="/wiki/Obtainium" title="Obtainium">Obtainium</a></i> is an album by <a href="/wiki/Skeleton_Key_(band)" title="Skeleton Key (band)">Skeleton Key</a>, released in 2002 by <a href="/wiki/Ipecac_Recordings" title="Ipecac Recordings">Ipecac Recordings</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Science_fiction">Science fiction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unobtainium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Science fiction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Unobtainium</i> can refer to any substance needed to build some device critical to the plot of a <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction" title="Science fiction">science fiction</a> story, but which does not exist in the universe as we know it. A hull material that gets stronger with pressure in the film <i><a href="/wiki/The_Core" title="The Core">The Core</a></i> was nicknamed <i>unobtainium</i>, but the concept under different names can be seen in the anti-gravity material <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cavorite" title="Cavorite">cavorite</a> from H. G. Wells' 1901 novel <a href="/wiki/The_First_Men_in_the_Moon" title="The First Men in the Moon">The First Men in the Moon</a>, as well as the super-strong material <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Scrith" title="Scrith">scrith</a> from <a href="/wiki/Larry_Niven" title="Larry Niven">Larry Niven</a>'s novel <i><a href="/wiki/Ringworld" title="Ringworld">Ringworld</a></i>, which requires a tensile strength on the order of the forces binding an <a href="/wiki/Atomic_nucleus" title="Atomic nucleus">atomic nucleus</a>.</p>
<p>More recently, the term was used in <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron</a>'s 2009 movie <i><a href="/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)" title="Avatar (2009 film)">Avatar</a></i>, as a substance that was named (in the film's dialog) <i>unobtanium</i> (note the slightly different spelling). In the film, it was mined on the fictional moon <a href="/wiki/Fictional_universe_of_Avatar" title="Fictional universe of Avatar">Pandora</a>, and was described as key to human space exploration and survival.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kosich2010_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-Kosich2010-9"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i>Unobtainium</i> can also refer to any rare but desirable material used to motivate a conflict over its possession, making it a <a href="/wiki/MacGuffin" title="MacGuffin">MacGuffin</a> (it appears in the story as something to obtain, not something that is significantly used).</p>
<p><i>Unobtainium</i> can be used in a disparaging context (e.g., "That idea is silly; you'd need unobtainium wires to hold the planet up!") or a hypothetical one ("If one were to build an unobtainium shell around a <a href="/wiki/Black_hole" title="Black hole">black hole</a>'s event horizon, what would happen to the material piling up on it?").</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_real_life">In real life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unobtainium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: In real life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Element_66" title="Element 66">Element 66</a> is named dysprosium, from the Greek word dysprositos meaning hard to get.</p>
<p>One <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Altcoin" title="Altcoin">altcoin</a> (alternate cryptocurrency) is named Unobtainium, based on Bitcoin's code source.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Similar_terms">Similar terms</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Unobtainium&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Similar terms">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term <b>handwavium</b> (suggesting <a href="/wiki/Handwaving" title="Handwaving">handwaving</a>) is another term for this hypothetical material, as are <b>buzzwordium</b>, <b>impossibrium</b>, <b>hardtofindium</b>, <b>flangium</b>, and, less commonly, <b>phlebotinum</b>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (April 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The term <b>eludium</b> (also spelled with variants such as <b>illudium</b>) has been used to describe a material which has <i>eluded</i> attempts to develop it. This was mentioned in several <a href="/wiki/Looney_Tunes" title="Looney Tunes">Looney Tunes</a> cartoons, where <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marvin_Martian" title="Marvin Martian">Marvin Martian</a> tried (unsuccessfully) to use his "Eludium Q-36 Explosive Space Modulator" to blow up the Earth.</p>
<p>Another largely synonymous term is <b>wishalloy</b>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> although the sense is often subtly different in that a wishalloy usually does not exist at all, whereas unobtainium may merely be unavailable.</p>
<p>A similar conceptual material in alchemy is the <a href="/wiki/Philosopher%27s_stone" title="Philosopher's stone">philosopher's stone</a>, a mythical substance with the ability to turn <a href="/wiki/Lead" title="Lead">lead</a> into <a href="/wiki/Gold" title="Gold">gold</a>, or bestow <a href="/wiki/Immortality" title="Immortality">immortality</a> and <a href="/wiki/Youth" title="Youth">youth</a>. While the search to find such a substance was not successful, it did lead to discovery of a new substance: <a href="/wiki/Phosphorus" title="Phosphorus">phosphorus</a>.</p>

<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-1"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-1">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">"unobtainium, n. A substance having the exact high test properties required for a piece of hardware or other item of use, but not obtainable either because it theoretically cannot exist or because technology is insufficiently advanced to produce it. Humorous or ironical." Listed in "Interim Glossary, Aero-Space Terms," as compiled by Woodford Heflin and published in February 1958 by the Air University of the US Air Force.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-6">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Relatively large amounts of titanium are used in aircraft such as the F-15, F-18, and F-22 fighters and the B-1 bomber.</span></li>
</ol>
