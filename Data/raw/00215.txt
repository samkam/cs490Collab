<p><b>Richard George Adams</b> (born 9 May 1920) is an English novelist who is known as the author of <i><a href="/wiki/Watership_Down" title="Watership Down">Watership Down</a></i>. He studied modern history at university before serving in the British Army during World War II. Afterward he completed his studies and then joined the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_Civil_Service" title="British Civil Service">British Civil Service</a>. In 1974, two years after <i>Watership Down</i> was published, Adams became a full-time author. He is now semi-retired.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Structo_talks_to_Richard_Adams_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Structo_talks_to_Richard_Adams-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adams was born on 9 May 1920 in <a href="/wiki/Wash_Common" title="Wash Common">Wash Common</a> near <a href="/wiki/Newbury,_Berkshire" title="Newbury, Berkshire">Newbury</a>, Berkshire, England. He attended <a href="/wiki/Horris_Hill_School" title="Horris Hill School">Horris Hill School</a> from 1926 to 1933, and then <a href="/wiki/Bradfield_College" title="Bradfield College">Bradfield College</a> from 1933 to 1938. In 1938, he went to <a href="/wiki/Worcester_College,_Oxford" title="Worcester College, Oxford">Worcester College, Oxford</a> to read <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Modern_History#Oxford" title="Modern History">Modern History</a>. In July 1940, shortly after the declaration of war between Britain and Germany, Adams was called up to join the <a href="/wiki/British_Army" title="British Army">British Army</a>, He was posted to the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Army_Service_Corps" title="Royal Army Service Corps">Royal Army Service Corps</a> and was selected for the Airborne Company, where he worked as a brigade liaison. He served in <a href="/wiki/Palestine" title="Palestine">Palestine</a>, Europe and the Far East but saw no direct action against either the Germans or the Japanese.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After being released from the army in 1946, Adams returned to Worcester College to continue his studies for a further two years. He took the degrees of Bachelor of Arts in 1948 and <a href="/wiki/Master_of_Arts_(Oxbridge_and_Dublin)" title="Master of Arts (Oxbridge and Dublin)">Master of Arts</a> in 1953.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> After his BA graduation in 1948, Adams joined the British Civil Service and held the rank of <a href="/wiki/Assistant_Secretary" title="Assistant Secretary">Assistant Secretary</a> to the <a href="/wiki/Ministry_of_Housing_and_Local_Government" title="Ministry of Housing and Local Government">Ministry of Housing and Local Government</a>, later part of the <a href="/wiki/Secretary_of_State_for_the_Environment" title="Secretary of State for the Environment">Department of the Environment</a>. It was during this period that he began writing fiction in his spare time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Author">Author</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Author">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adams had originally begun telling the story of <i><a href="/wiki/Watership_Down" title="Watership Down">Watership Down</a></i> to his two daughters, and they insisted that he publish it as a book. It took two years to write. In 1972, after four publishers and three writers' agencies had turned down the manuscript for <i>Watership Down</i>, Rex Collings agreed to publish the work.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The book gained international acclaim almost immediately.</p>
<p>Over the next few years <i>Watership Down</i> sold over a million copies worldwide and became a modern <a href="/wiki/Classic_book" title="Classic book">classic</a>. Adams won both of the two most prestigious British children's book awards, one of six authors to do so: the <a href="/wiki/Carnegie_Medal_(literary_award)" title="Carnegie Medal (literary award)">Carnegie Medal</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Guardian_Children%27s_Fiction_Prize" title="Guardian Children's Fiction Prize">Guardian Children's Fiction Prize</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medal1972_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-medal1972-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-relaunch_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-relaunch-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GCFP_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-GCFP-8"><span>[</span>a<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1974, following publication of his second novel, <i><a href="/wiki/Shardik" title="Shardik">Shardik</a></i>, he left the Civil Service to become a full-time author. He was elected a Fellow of the <a href="/wiki/Royal_Society_of_Literature" title="Royal Society of Literature">Royal Society of Literature</a> in 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At one point, Adams served as Writer in Residence at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Florida" title="University of Florida">University of Florida</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> and at <a href="/wiki/Hollins_University" title="Hollins University">Hollins University</a> in Virginia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Structo_talks_to_Richard_Adams_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Structo_talks_to_Richard_Adams-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Adams was the recipient of the inaugural Whitchurch Arts Award for inspiration in January 2010, presented at the Watership Down pub in <a href="/wiki/Freefolk" title="Freefolk">Freefolk</a>, Hampshire.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Public_figure">Public figure</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Public figure">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1982, Adams served one year as President of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RSPCA" title="RSPCA">RSPCA</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Structo_talks_to_Richard_Adams_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Structo_talks_to_Richard_Adams-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> He also made a voyage through the <a href="/wiki/Antarctic" title="Antarctic">Antarctic</a> in the company of <a href="/wiki/Ronald_Lockley" title="Ronald Lockley">Ronald Lockley</a>, the well-known ornithologist.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Richard_Adams&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Adams celebrated his 90th birthday in 2010 with a party at the White Hart in his hometown of <a href="/wiki/Whitchurch,_Hampshire" title="Whitchurch, Hampshire">Whitchurch, Hampshire</a> where <a href="/wiki/Sir_George_Young,_6th_Baronet" title="Sir George Young, 6th Baronet">Sir George Young</a> presented him with a painting made by a local artist. Adams wrote a poetic piece celebrating his home of the past 28 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> Just before his 90th birthday, he wrote a new story for a charity book <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Gentle_Footprints&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Gentle Footprints (page does not exist)">Gentle Footprints</a> to raise funds for The <a href="/wiki/Born_Free_Foundation" title="Born Free Foundation">Born Free Foundation</a>.</p>
<p>Adams currently lives with his wife, Elizabeth, in <a href="/wiki/Whitchurch,_Hampshire" title="Whitchurch, Hampshire">Whitchurch, Hampshire</a>, within 10 miles (16 km) of his birthplace. Their daughters, to whom Adams originally related the tales that became <i>Watership Down</i>, are Juliet and Rosamond. He has six grandchildren: Lucy, Sarah, Miranda, Grace, Robert, and Maeve.</p>
