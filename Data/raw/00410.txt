<p><i><b>The Lost World: Jurassic Park</b></i> (also known as <i><b>Jurassic Park II: The Lost World</b></i>) is a 1997 American <a href="/wiki/Science_fiction_film" title="Science fiction film">science fiction</a> <a href="/wiki/Adventure_film" title="Adventure film">adventure</a> <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dinosaur_film&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dinosaur film (page does not exist)">dinosaur film</a> directed by <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a>. It is the second installment in the <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park" title="Jurassic Park"><i>Jurassic Park</i> franchise</a>. The film was produced by <a href="/wiki/Gerald_R._Molen" title="Gerald R. Molen">Gerald R. Molen</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colin_Wilson_(producer)" title="Colin Wilson (producer)">Colin Wilson</a>. The screenplay was written by <a href="/wiki/David_Koepp" title="David Koepp">David Koepp</a>, loosely based on <a href="/wiki/Michael_Crichton" title="Michael Crichton">Michael Crichton</a>'s 1995 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_(Crichton_novel)" title="The Lost World (Crichton novel)">The Lost World</a></i>. The film stars <a href="/wiki/Jeff_Goldblum" title="Jeff Goldblum">Jeff Goldblum</a>, <a href="/wiki/Julianne_Moore" title="Julianne Moore">Julianne Moore</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vince_Vaughn" title="Vince Vaughn">Vince Vaughn</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pete_Postlethwaite" title="Pete Postlethwaite">Pete Postlethwaite</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Schiff" title="Richard Schiff">Richard Schiff</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arliss_Howard" title="Arliss Howard">Arliss Howard</a>, <a href="/wiki/Vanessa_Lee_Chester" title="Vanessa Lee Chester">Vanessa Lee Chester</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Attenborough" title="Richard Attenborough">Richard Attenborough</a>.</p>
<p>Four years after the events of <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a> have secretly survived and been allowed to roam free on a deserted island off of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>'s Pacific Coast. In the time between the two films, John Hammond loses control of his company, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InGen" title="InGen">InGen</a>, to his nephew, Peter Ludlow. Ludlow assembles a team to bring the animals back to the mainland to bring in revenue and restore the company. Hammond sees a chance to redeem himself for his past mistakes and sends an expedition led by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ian_Malcolm_(Jurassic_Park_character)" title="Ian Malcolm (Jurassic Park character)">Dr. Ian Malcolm</a> to reach the island before InGen's team can get there. The two groups confront each other in the face of extreme danger and must team up for their own survival.</p>
<p>After the original book's release and the first film's success, Crichton was pressured by fans and Spielberg himself for a sequel novel. After the book was published in 1995, production began on a film sequel. <i>The Lost World</i> '​s plot and imagery is substantially darker than the previous film. Despite mixed reviews, it was a box office success, grossing $618 million worldwide.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Four years after the events of <a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a>, a wealthy family is on a boat cruise near Isla Sorna off of <a href="/wiki/Central_America" title="Central America">Central America</a>'s Pacific coast. The daughter (<a href="/wiki/Camilla_Belle" title="Camilla Belle">Camilla Belle</a>) wanders off and is attacked by a <i><a href="/wiki/Compsognathus" title="Compsognathus">Compsognathus</a></i>. The incident allows Peter Ludlow (<a href="/wiki/Arliss_Howard" title="Arliss Howard">Arliss Howard</a>), the unscrupulous nephew of John Hammond (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Attenborough" title="Richard Attenborough">Richard Attenborough</a>), to gain control of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InGen" title="InGen">InGen</a>. Hammond summons Ian Malcolm (<a href="/wiki/Jeff_Goldblum" title="Jeff Goldblum">Jeff Goldblum</a>), whose publication of the incident at Jurassic Park destroyed his academic reputation, to his home. He explains that Isla Sorna (also known as "Site B") is the island where the dinosaurs were engineered and nurtured before being moved to Isla Nublar, Jurassic Park's location. He also explains that after the park was shut down, a hurricane destroyed the containment facilities on Isla Sorna, and the dinosaurs have been living free in the wild ever since. Hammond requests Malcolm to join a team that will travel to Isla Sorna to document the dinosaurs in their natural habitat as a way of rallying public support to prevent Ludlow from exploiting the site. Ian initially declines but, after learning that his girlfriend—<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paleontologist" title="Paleontologist">paleontologist</a> Sarah Harding (<a href="/wiki/Julianne_Moore" title="Julianne Moore">Julianne Moore</a>)—is part of the team and is already on the island, agrees to go.</p>
<p>Ian meets the other two members of the team: equipment specialist and engineer Eddie Carr (<a href="/wiki/Richard_Schiff" title="Richard Schiff">Richard Schiff</a>), and video documentarian Nick Van Owen (<a href="/wiki/Vince_Vaughn" title="Vince Vaughn">Vince Vaughn</a>). Shortly after arriving on the island, they find Sarah after a brief encounter with a herd of <i><a href="/wiki/Stegosaurus" title="Stegosaurus">Stegosaurus</a></i> and discover that Ian's daughter Kelly has stowed away on the trailer that the group is using as a mobile base. Ian tries to take Kelly home, but they're interrupted by the arrival of an InGen team of mercenaries, hunters and paleontologists led by Ludlow, whom they spot chasing and capturing several dinosaurs. Meanwhile, tracker and team leader Roland Tembo (<a href="/wiki/Pete_Postlethwaite" title="Pete Postlethwaite">Pete Postlethwaite</a>) separates from the main group and goes after his big prize: a male <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tyrannosaurus_rex" title="Tyrannosaurus rex">Tyrannosaurus rex</a></i>. He decides to capture it by luring it to the cries of its injured offspring. That night, Ian's team sneak into the InGen camp and learn the captured dinosaurs will be brought to <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a> as the main attraction of a newly proposed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Theme_park" title="Theme park">theme park</a>. This prompts Nick and Sarah to free the caged dinosaurs, wreaking havoc upon the camp.</p>
<p>During the commotion, Nick frees the infant <i>T-Rex</i> and takes it to the trailer so Sarah can mend its broken leg. Ian, Kelly, and Eddie secure themselves in a "high hide", a lift Eddie built to keep them safe above the trees. Fearing that the infant's parents might be searching for it, Ian rushes to the trailer to warn them. As soon as he arrives, two adult <i>Tyrannosaurus rex</i> emerge from the woods and threaten Ian, Sarah, and Nick. They decide to release the infant, but after a few minutes the adult <i>Tyrannosaurus</i> return and attack the trailer, pushing it over the edge of a nearby cliff with Ian, Sarah and Nick trapped inside. Eddie tries to pull the trailer back over the edge with his SUV, but is devoured by the <i>T-Rex</i>, sending the trailer and Eddie's SUV plummeting down the cliff. Ian, Sarah and Nick survive and are found by Kelly and the InGen team. With both groups' communications equipment and vehicles destroyed in the attacks, they team up to reach the old InGen compound's radio station on foot. During the trip, Dieter Stark (<a href="/wiki/Peter_Stormare" title="Peter Stormare">Peter Stormare</a>), Roland's second-in-command, is killed by a flock of <i>Compsognathus</i>.</p>
<p>The next night, the two Tyrannosaurus rex come across the group's camp, having followed the scent of the infant's blood on Sarah's jacket. One of the hunters notices them and screams, causing everyone to flee in panic. The female T-Rex chases the group, while Roland stays behind and manages to tranquilize the male. The hunters disband into the thick trees and pass through a field of tall grass, where a troop of breeding <i><a href="/wiki/Velociraptor" title="Velociraptor">Velociraptor</a></i> attack the hapless survivors. Ian, separated earlier from the group, reunites with Nick, Sarah and Kelly, and they continue on toward the compound. Nick finds the communications room and calls for rescue. After barely escaping a trio of Velociraptor, a helicopter finally arrives and whisks them off the island. From the air, they spot the unconscious male T-Rex being prepared for transport.</p>
<p>A freighter carries the <i>T-Rex</i> back to the mainland, but crashes into the dock at breakneck speed. Ludlow and several guards investigate and find that the crew has disappeared. A guard opens the cargo hold, accidentally releasing the T-Rex, which escapes into the city and goes on a rampage. Realizing the T-Rex is likely searching for its infant, Ian and Sarah learn from Ludlow that the infant was captured and is in a secure InGen building. They rush to retrieve the infant and use it to lure the adult back to the ship. Ludlow tries to intervene but is trapped in the cargo hold and killed by the young T-Rex. Before the adult can escape from the cargo hold, Sarah tranquilizes it while Ian closes the hold.</p>
<p>Ian, Sarah and Kelly watch on live television as the ship carrying the adult and infant T-Rex is escorted back to Isla Sorna. John Hammond explains in an interview that the American and Costa Rican governments have agreed to declare the island a nature preserve. He ends the interview by saying "life will find a way", paraphrasing what Malcolm told him in the first film. The two adult T-Rex and their baby are shown to have been returned safely.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the release of the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(novel)" title="Jurassic Park (novel)">Jurassic Park</a></i>, Michael Crichton was pressured by fans for a sequel novel. Having never written a sequel, he initially refused. Discussions about a sequel film began after the successful release of <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i> in 1993. <a href="/wiki/Steven_Spielberg" title="Steven Spielberg">Steven Spielberg</a> held discussions with David Koepp and Crichton to discuss possible ideas for a sequel film, and requested Crichton to write a sequel novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Joe_Johnston" title="Joe Johnston">Joe Johnston</a> offered to direct the film, but the job ultimately went to Spielberg.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Mercedes-w163-the-lost-world.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Mercedes-w163-the-lost-world.jpg/220px-Mercedes-w163-the-lost-world.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Mercedes-w163-the-lost-world.jpg/330px-Mercedes-w163-the-lost-world.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/92/Mercedes-w163-the-lost-world.jpg/440px-Mercedes-w163-the-lost-world.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Mercedes-w163-the-lost-world.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mercedes-Benz_W163" title="Mercedes-Benz W163">Mercedes-Benz W163</a> used in the film</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A production team was assembled in spring 1995, as Crichton was finishing the novel while Spielberg and Koepp were developing ideas for the screenplay. Production designer Rick Carter traveled to the Caribbean, Central America and New Zealand to scout possible locations for filming, before settling on the redwood forests of <a href="/wiki/Eureka,_California" title="Eureka, California">Eureka, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pre-production_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-pre-production-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Filming began on September 5, 1996, at <a href="/wiki/Fern_Canyon" title="Fern Canyon">Fern Canyon</a>, a part of California's <a href="/wiki/Prairie_Creek_Redwoods_State_Park" title="Prairie Creek Redwoods State Park">Prairie Creek Redwoods State Park</a>. Filming continued for two weeks in other state parks and on private land in northern California. The film's opening scenes were shot in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kauai,_Hawaii" title="Kauai, Hawaii">Kauai, Hawaii</a>. Throughout the fall of 1996, filming continued on stages at Universal Studios Hollywood.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-production-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The Site B workers village was constructed there and left intact after filming to become a part of the theme park tour.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pre-production_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-pre-production-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Filming concluded ahead of schedule on December 11, 1996.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-production_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-production-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Filming at New Zealand's <a href="/wiki/Fiordland_National_Park" title="Fiordland National Park">Fiordland National Park</a> was originally planned to take place over five days in December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Plans to shoot there were scrapped for unknown reasons.</p>
<p>Although the ending takes place in <a href="/wiki/San_Diego" title="San Diego">San Diego</a>, only one sequence is actually shot there, where the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/InGen" title="InGen">InGen</a> helicopter flies over the wharf and banks towards the city. The other sequences were all shot in <a href="/wiki/Burbank,_California" title="Burbank, California">Burbank, California</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Spielberg suggested the <i>Tyrannosaurus rex</i> '​s attack through San Diego be added to the film story, inspired by a similar attack scene of a <i><a href="/wiki/Apatosaurus" title="Apatosaurus">Brontosaurus</a></i> in London in the <a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_(1925_film)" title="The Lost World (1925 film)">1925 film adaptation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Sir Arthur Conan Doyle</a>'s 1912 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World_(Conan_Doyle_novel)" title="The Lost World (Conan Doyle novel)">The Lost World</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many elements from the original novel that were not ultimately used in the first film were instead used in <i>The Lost World</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The opening sequence of a vacationing family's young daughter being attacked by a group of <i>Compsognathus</i> was very similar to the novel's opening scene, and Dieter Stark's death is also analogous to John Hammond's compy-related death in the novel. Also, Nick, Sarah, Kelly, and Burke being trapped behind a waterfall by one of the <i>T. Rexes</i> was ultimately taken from the novel, where Tim and Lex are trapped behind a man-made waterfall with the <i>T. Rex</i> attempting to eat them, and Roland Tembo shoots the <i>T. Rex</i> with tranquilizer in the same way that Robert Muldoon did in the novel.</p>
<p>According to <a href="/wiki/Jack_Horner_(paleontologist)" title="Jack Horner (paleontologist)">Jack Horner</a>, part of the waterfall scene was written in as a favor for him by Spielberg. Burke greatly resembles Horner's rival <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Robert_Bakker" title="Robert Bakker">Robert Bakker</a>. In real life, Bakker argues for a predatory <i>Tyrannosaurus</i> while Horner views it as primarily a scavenger. So Spielberg wrote Burke into this part to have him killed by the <i>T. rex</i> as a favor for Horner. After the film came out, Bakker, who recognized himself in Burke and loved it, actually sent Horner a message saying "See, I told you <i>T. rex</i> was a hunter!".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Distribution">Distribution</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Distribution">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><span id="Distribution"></span><span id="Release"></span><span id="DVD"></span><span id="Home_media"></span> <i>The Lost World</i> was released on May 23, 1997. The film made its <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> and <a href="/wiki/LaserDisc" title="LaserDisc">LaserDisc</a> debut on November 4, 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The DVD, first released on October 10, 2000, includes deleted scenes involving Hammond's <a href="/wiki/Dismissal_(employment)" title="Dismissal (employment)">ouster</a> from InGen that were incorporated into the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fox_Network" title="Fox Network">Fox</a> broadcast television premiere of the film. The film's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Blu-ray" title="Blu-ray">Blu-ray</a> version, released in 2011, also includes the deleted scenes.</p>
<p>The film was also released in a package with <i><a href="/wiki/Jurassic_Park_(film)" title="Jurassic Park (film)">Jurassic Park</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> The DVD has also been re-released with both sequels on December 11, 2001 as the <i>Jurassic Park Trilogy</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> and as the <i>Jurassic Park Adventure Pack</i> on November 29, 2005.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park_(film_score)" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park (film score)">soundtrack</a> was released on May 20, 1997. On the same day it was first released to DVD, a deluxe <a href="/wiki/Special_edition" title="Special edition">limited edition</a> <a href="/wiki/Box_set" title="Box set">box set</a> was released that included <i>Jurassic Park</i> and <i>The Lost World</i>, soundtracks for both films with packaging made exclusively for the set, two <a href="/wiki/Lenticular_printing" title="Lenticular printing">lenticulars</a>, eight 8x10 stills (4 from each film), and a certificate of authenticity signed by all three producers of the set, all inside a collector case.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Lostworldjurassicparkt-rex.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="400" height="150" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/82/Lostworldjurassicparkt-rex.jpg/200px-Lostworldjurassicparkt-rex.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/8/82/Lostworldjurassicparkt-rex.jpg/300px-Lostworldjurassicparkt-rex.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/8/82/Lostworldjurassicparkt-rex.jpg 2x" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Lostworldjurassicparkt-rex.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The <i>T. rex</i> wreaking havoc received an <a href="/wiki/MTV_Movie_Awards" title="MTV Movie Awards">MTV Movie Awards</a> nomination for "Best Action Sequence"</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Following four years of growing anticipation and hype, <i>The Lost World</i> broke many <a href="/wiki/Box_office" title="Box office">box office</a> records upon its release. It took in $72,132,785 on its opening weekend ($92.6 million for the four-day Memorial Day holiday) in the U.S.,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> which was the biggest opening weekend at the time,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> surpassing the previous record-holder <i><a href="/wiki/Batman_Forever" title="Batman Forever">Batman Forever</a></i> at $52.8 million. It held onto this record for four and a half years, until the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Philosopher%27s_Stone_(film)" title="Harry Potter and the Philosopher's Stone (film)">Harry Potter and the Sorcerer's Stone</a></i> in November 2001. <i>The Lost World</i> took the record for highest single-day box office take of $26,083,950 on May 25,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> a record held until the release of <i><a href="/wiki/Star_Wars_Episode_I:_The_Phantom_Menace" title="Star Wars Episode I: The Phantom Menace">Star Wars Episode I: The Phantom Menace</a></i>. It also became the fastest film to pass the $100 million mark, achieving the feat in just six days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Its total box office gross fell below the total of the original film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> With grossing $229,086,679 domestically and $389,552,320 internationally, the film ended up grossing $618,638,999 worldwide,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BOM_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-BOM-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> becoming the second highest grossing film of 1997 behind <i><a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">Titanic</a></i>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_response">Critical response</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Critical response">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Lost World</i> has received mixed reviews from critics. <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> gives the film a score of 52% with 33 out of 64 reviewers giving it a positive review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Another aggregator <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a> gives the film a weighted average rating of 59/100 based on reviews from 18 critics, indicating "mixed or average reviews."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a>, who gave the first film three stars, gave <i>The Lost World</i> only two, writing that "It can be said that the creatures in this film transcend any visible signs of special effects and seem to walk the earth, but the same realism isn't brought to the human characters, who are bound by plot conventions and action formulas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Gene_Siskel" title="Gene Siskel">Gene Siskel</a> of the <i><a href="/wiki/Chicago_Tribune" title="Chicago Tribune">Chicago Tribune</a></i> also gave the film two stars and said, "I was disappointed as much as I was thrilled because 'The Lost World' lacks a staple of Steven Spielberg's adventure films: exciting characters. [...] Even in the original 'Jurassic Park,' the dinosaurs -- not to mention the human beings -- had more distinct personalities than they have here. Save for superior special effects, 'The Lost World' comes off as recycled material."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Conversely, Kevin Thomas of the <i><a href="/wiki/Los_Angeles_Times" title="Los Angeles Times">Los Angeles Times</a></i> saw improved character development over the original, saying, "It seemed such a mistake in <i>Jurassic Park</i> to sideline early on its most interesting character, the brilliant, free-thinking and outspoken theorist Ian Malcolm (<a href="/wiki/Jeff_Goldblum" title="Jeff Goldblum">Jeff Goldblum</a>) with a broken leg, but in its most inspired stroke, <i>The Lost World</i> brings back Malcolm and places him front and center," calling it "a pleasure to watch such wily pros as Goldblum and Attenborough spar with each other with wit and assurance."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> The dinosaurs were even more developed as characters, with Stephen Holden of the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> saying, "<i>The Lost World</i>, unlike <i>Jurassic Park</i>, humanizes its monsters in a way that <i><a href="/wiki/E.T._the_Extra-Terrestrial" title="E.T. the Extra-Terrestrial">E.T.</a></i> would understand."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Owen_Gleiberman" title="Owen Gleiberman">Owen Gleiberman</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> gave the film a B grade; he remarked, "Mr. T-Rex was cool in the first Spielberg flick, sure, but it wasn't until [it was in] San Diego that things got crazy-cool. It's the old 'tree falling in the woods' conundrum: Unless your giant monster is causing massive property damage, can you really call it a giant monster?"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Spielberg confessed that during production he became increasingly disenchanted with the film, admitting, "I beat myself up... growing more and more impatient with myself... It made me wistful about doing a talking picture, because sometimes I got the feeling I was just making this big silent-roar movie... I found myself saying, 'Is that all there is? It's not enough for me.'"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Lost World: Jurassic Park</i> was adapted into a four-part comic series released by <a href="/wiki/Topps_Comics" title="Topps Comics">Topps Comics</a>. Each issue had one regular cover, and one photo-variant cover that used stills from the film. The first issue was released on May 1st 1997 with the final issue being released on August 1st 1997.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Games">Games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote">For the complete list of games based on The Lost World: Jurassic Park, see <a href="/wiki/The_Lost_World:_Jurassic_Park_(video_game)" title="The Lost World: Jurassic Park (video game)">The Lost World: Jurassic Park (video game)</a>.</div>
<p>There were also a number of games released based on the movie. These included a <a href="/wiki/Pinball" title="Pinball">pinball</a> game, an <a href="/wiki/Arcade_game" title="Arcade game">arcade game</a>, and a number of releases for home systems including the <a href="/wiki/Sega_Genesis" title="Sega Genesis">Sega Genesis</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sony_Playstation" title="Sony Playstation">Sony Playstation</a>, and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nintendo_Game_Boy" title="Nintendo Game Boy">Nintendo Game Boy</a>.</p>
