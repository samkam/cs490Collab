<div class="inner" id="msg_24567">Hello Mrs FedUp...I think you'll find that Neil Armstrong's Apollo moon mission's landing craft was called the Eagle.<br/><br/>Sorry Tec, here's what I wrote before Mrs P-K summoned me in to lunch. You got there first but here's what I wrote anyway as there is a little bit more info...<br/><br/>Vide: <a class="bbc_link" href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_11" target="_blank">http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Apollo_11</a><br/><br/><em>"Their Lunar Module, Eagle, spent 21 hours 31 minutes on the lunar surface, while Collins remained in orbit in the Command/Service Module, Columbia.[2] The three astronauts returned to Earth on July 24, landing in the Pacific Ocean."</em><br/><br/>Hence the in-joke about "The Eagle has landed".<br/><br/>Neil and his pal Buzz brought back 47.5 pounds (21.5 kg) of rock...none of  it lettered throughout 'Moon'.<br/><br/>In case there is the chance of thinking that "The Eagle has Landed" somehow relates to Jack Higgins book<br/><br/><img alt="" class="bbc_img resized" height="300" src="http://photo.goodreads.com/books/1173480227l/295961.jpg" width="188"/><br/><br/>This was only published in 1975 whereas Armstrong and Aldrin touched down on the Moon on July 20, 1969, at 17 minutes and 35 seconds past 8 pm UTC (Coordinated Universal Time).<br/><br/>So it's clear where Jack Higgins got the idea for a snappy title for a book.<br/><br/>The phrase has now become so embedded in the English language that even my brother uses the phrase to text me when he has arrived on a visit to me in Lewes. ..(and he does not look at all an eagle unless we are talking about Bald Eagles, <br/><br/></div>
