<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Burrow</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="hatnote">For other uses, see <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Burrow_(disambiguation)" title="Burrow (disambiguation)">Burrow (disambiguation)</a>.</div>
<p>A <b>burrow</b> is a hole or tunnel excavated into the ground by an <a href="/wiki/Animal" title="Animal">animal</a> to create a space suitable for habitation, temporary refuge, or as a byproduct of <a href="/wiki/Animal_locomotion" title="Animal locomotion">locomotion</a>. Burrows provide a form of shelter against <a href="/wiki/Predation" title="Predation">predation</a> and exposure to the elements, so the burrowing way of life is quite popular amongst various types of animal. Burrows can be constructed into a wide variety of substrates, and can range in complexity from a simple tube a few centimetres long to a complex network of interconnecting tunnels and chambers hundreds or thousands of metres in total length, such as a well-developed <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbit</a> warren.</p>
<p>Examples of vertebrate burrowing animals include a number of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">mammals</a>, <a href="/wiki/Amphibian" title="Amphibian">amphibians</a>, fish (<a href="/wiki/Dragonet" title="Dragonet">dragonet</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lungfish" title="Lungfish">lungfish</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>), <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Reptiles" title="Reptiles">reptiles</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Birds" title="Birds">birds</a> (including small <a href="/wiki/Dinosaur" title="Dinosaur">dinosaurs</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VMK07_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-VMK07-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup>). Examples of burrowing <a href="/wiki/Invertebrate" title="Invertebrate">invertebrates</a> are <a href="/wiki/Insect" title="Insect">insects</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spiders" title="Spiders">spiders</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sea_urchins" title="Sea urchins">sea urchins</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Crustaceans" title="Crustaceans">crustaceans</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Clams" title="Clams">clams</a> and <a href="/wiki/Worm" title="Worm">worms</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Vertebrate_burrows">Vertebrate burrows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Vertebrate burrows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Pr%C3%A4riehund_P1010308.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2736" data-file-width="3648" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Pr%C3%A4riehund_P1010308.JPG/220px-Pr%C3%A4riehund_P1010308.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Pr%C3%A4riehund_P1010308.JPG/330px-Pr%C3%A4riehund_P1010308.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/01/Pr%C3%A4riehund_P1010308.JPG/440px-Pr%C3%A4riehund_P1010308.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Pr%C3%A4riehund_P1010308.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A <a href="/wiki/Black-tailed_prairie_dog" title="Black-tailed prairie dog">black-tailed prairie dog</a>, with young, emerges from its burrow</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>A wide variety of vertebrates construct or use burrows in many different types of substrate. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mammals" title="Mammals">Mammals</a> are perhaps most well known for burrowing, especially <a href="/wiki/Insectivora" title="Insectivora">Insectivora</a> like the voracious <a href="/wiki/Mole_(animal)" title="Mole (animal)">mole</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Rodent" title="Rodent">rodents</a> like the prolific <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gopher_(animal)" title="Gopher (animal)">gopher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Groundhog" title="Groundhog">groundhog</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbit</a>, a member of the family <a href="/wiki/Lagomorpha" title="Lagomorpha">Lagomorpha</a>, is a well-known burrower. There are estimations that a single groundhog burrow occupies a full cubic metre, displacing 320 kilograms of dirt. Even <a href="/wiki/Carnivora" title="Carnivora">Carnivora</a> like the <a href="/wiki/Meerkat" title="Meerkat">meerkat</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marsupials" title="Marsupials">marsupials</a>, are burrowers. The largest burrowing animal is probably the <a href="/wiki/Polar_bear" title="Polar bear">polar bear</a> when it makes its <a href="/wiki/Maternity_den" title="Maternity den">maternity den</a> in snow or earth.</p>
<p>Burrows by birds are usually made in soft soils; some penguins and other <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Pelagic" title="Pelagic">pelagic</a> seabirds are noted for such burrows. The <a href="/wiki/Magellanic_penguin" title="Magellanic penguin">Magellanic penguin</a> is an example of such a burrow constructor, making burrows along coastal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Patagonian" title="Patagonian">Patagonian</a> regions of <a href="/wiki/Chile" title="Chile">Chile</a> and <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Other burrowing birds are <a href="/wiki/Puffin" title="Puffin">puffins</a>, <a href="/wiki/Kingfisher" title="Kingfisher">kingfishers</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bee-eaters" title="Bee-eaters">bee-eaters</a>.</p>

<p><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kangaroo_mice" title="Kangaroo mice">Kangaroo mice</a> construct burrows in fine sand.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Invertebrate_burrows">Invertebrate burrows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Invertebrate burrows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Scabies" title="Scabies">Scabies</a> mites construct their burrows in the skin of the infested animal or human. <a href="/wiki/Termite" title="Termite">Termites</a> construct burrows in wood. Some sea urchins and clams can burrow into rock.</p>
<p>The burrows produced by invertebrate animals can either be actively or passively. Dwelling burrows which remain open during occupation by an organism are filled with passively, by gravity rather than by the organism. Actively filled burrows, on the other hand, are filled with material by the burrowing organism itself.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donovan_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Donovan-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The establishment of an invertebrate burrow often involves the soaking of surrounding sediment in mucus in order to prevent collapse and to seal off water flow.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Donovan_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Donovan-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Fossil_burrows">Fossil burrows</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Burrow&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Fossil burrows">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
