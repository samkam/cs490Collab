<p><i><b>Watership Down</b></i> is a 1978 British <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animated</a> <a href="/wiki/Adventure_film" title="Adventure film">adventure</a> <a href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">drama film</a> written, produced and directed by <a href="/wiki/Martin_Rosen_(director)" title="Martin Rosen (director)">Martin Rosen</a> and based on the <a href="/wiki/Watership_Down" title="Watership Down">book of the same name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Richard_Adams" title="Richard Adams">Richard Adams</a>. It was financed by a consortium of British financial institutions. Originally released on 19 October 1978, the film was an immediate success and it became the sixth most popular film of 1979 at the British box office.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It was the first animated feature film to be presented in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dolby" title="Dolby">Dolby</a> surround sound.</p>
<p>It featured the voices of <a href="/wiki/John_Hurt" title="John Hurt">John Hurt</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Briers" title="Richard Briers">Richard Briers</a>, <a href="/wiki/Harry_Andrews" title="Harry Andrews">Harry Andrews</a>, <a href="/wiki/Simon_Cadell" title="Simon Cadell">Simon Cadell</a>, <a href="/wiki/Nigel_Hawthorne" title="Nigel Hawthorne">Nigel Hawthorne</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roy_Kinnear" title="Roy Kinnear">Roy Kinnear</a>, among others, and was the last film appearance of <a href="/wiki/Zero_Mostel" title="Zero Mostel">Zero Mostel</a>, as the voice of Kehaar the gull. The musical score was by <a href="/wiki/Angela_Morley" title="Angela Morley">Angela Morley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Williamson" title="Malcolm Williamson">Malcolm Williamson</a>. <a href="/wiki/Art_Garfunkel" title="Art Garfunkel">Art Garfunkel</a>'s hit single "<a href="/wiki/Bright_Eyes_(Art_Garfunkel_song)" title="Bright Eyes (Art Garfunkel song)">Bright Eyes</a>", which was written by songwriter <a href="/wiki/Mike_Batt" title="Mike Batt">Mike Batt</a>, briefly features.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watership_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>According to Adams' <a href="/wiki/Lapine_language" title="Lapine language">lapine</a> culture and mythology, the world was created by the god Frith, who represents the Sun. All animals lived harmoniously, but the rabbits eventually multiplied, and their appetite led to a food shortage. At the prayers of the desperate animals, Frith warned the rabbit prince El-ahrairah to control his people, but was scoffed at. In retaliation, Frith gave special gifts to every animal, but some animals he made predators to prey upon the rabbits. Satisfied that El-ahrairah ("Prince of a Thousand Enemies") had learned his lesson, Frith also gave the rabbits speed and cunning; while many would seek to kill them, the rabbits could survive by their wits and quickness.</p>
<p>In the present, in the English countryside of Sandleford, Fiver, a rabbit <a href="/wiki/Clairvoyance" title="Clairvoyance">seer</a> has an apocalyptic vision and goes with his older brother Hazel to beg the chief to have the warren evacuated, but they are dismissed and attempt to make an exodus themselves. The group meets resistance from the warren's police force called the Owsla, but eight manage to fight and escape: Fiver, Hazel, Bigwig, Blackberry, Pipkin, Dandelion, Silver and Violet. They travel through the dangerous woods and make it to a bean field to rest. In the morning, Violet is killed by a hawk, leaving the group without a female.</p>
<p>After several dangerous situations, they meet the enigmatic rabbit Cowslip, who invites them to his warren. They are grateful, but Fiver senses something bizarre in the atmosphere, and the resident rabbits' overly resigned attitudes, and leaves. An irked Bigwig follows, and chastises Fiver for supposedly causing senseless tension with his instincts. Moments later, however, he is caught in a <a href="/wiki/Animal_trapping" title="Animal trapping">snare trap</a>. Fiver attempts to get help from their hosts, but is ignored. Bigwig is freed after nearly dying. As Fiver reveals, the warren is fed by a farmer who snares rabbits in return for his food and protection from predators. After Bigwig's narrow escape, the other rabbits willingly follow Fiver's and Hazel's advice and set out once more.</p>
<p>The rabbits discover Nuthanger farm, which contains a <a href="/wiki/Hutch_(animal_cage)" title="Hutch (animal cage)">hutch</a> of female rabbits, necessary for a new warren. However, they do not manage to free them, on account of the territorial farm animals. They find the injured Owsla captain Holly, who recounts the destruction of Sandleford by humans, and a mysterious group called the "Efrafrans" before falling unconscious. Fiver finally leads the group to the hill he envisioned, <a href="/wiki/Watership_Down,_Hampshire" title="Watership Down, Hampshire">Watership Down</a>, where the rabbits settle.</p>
<p>They settle in, developing their own warren, with Hazel as chief. They befriend an <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/acerbic#English" title="wiktionary:acerbic">acerbic</a> injured <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Seagull" title="Seagull">seagull</a>, Kehaar, who offers to survey the local area for does. The rabbits return to Nuthanger to free the does; Hazel is shot by a farmhand and presumed dead, but Fiver has a vision and follows the apparition of the Black Rabbit of Inle (a rabbit version of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grim_Reaper" title="Grim Reaper">Grim Reaper</a>) to his injured brother. Kehaar returns and while removing <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Buckshot" title="Buckshot">buckshot</a> pellets from Hazel's leg, reports of Efrafa, a large warren with many females. Holly, who encountered Efrafa, begs them not to go there, describing it as a totalitarian state, run by vicious and heavily territorial rabbits. Hazel feels they have no choice but to go there. Bigwig infiltrates the colony and is made an Owsla officer by the cruel chief, General Woundwort. Bigwig recruits several potential escapees to his cause, including Hyzenthlay, an idealistic doe and Blackavar, a scarred attempted escapee. They flee, with Woundwort and his Owsla in pursuit. Using a boat to float down the river, they evade capture, helped by Kehaar. That night, Kehaar leaves for his homeland, with the gratitude of the warren.</p>
<p>Several days later, Efrafan trackers discover their trail and follow them to Watership Down. Hazel offers a treaty with Woundwort, who dismisses Hazel, telling him to turn over Bigwig and the other deserters or he will kill the entire warren. The Watership rabbits barricade their warren and are besieged by the Efrafans. Fiver slips into a <a href="/wiki/Trance" title="Trance">trance</a>, in which he envisions a dog loose in the woods. His moans inspire Hazel to free the dog from Nuthanger and lead him to the warren to intervene. He escapes with Blackberry, Dandelion and Hyzenthlay.</p>
<p>Hazel prays to Frith, offering his life for that of those in the warren, a bargain Frith acknowledges, but doesn't accept, as the outcome is ultimately up to Hazel. Hazel frees the dog while his companions bait it into following them to Watership Down; Hazel is attacked by the cat, but saved by Lucy (the owner of the hutch rabbits). When the Efrafans break through the warren's defences, Woundwort leads the attack. Blackavar confronts Woundwort, but is overpowered and killed. Bigwig <a href="/wiki/Ambush" title="Ambush">ambushes</a> Woundwort and they fight to exhaustion. The dog arrives and kills many of the Efrafan soldiers. Hearing the commotion, Woundwort abandons Bigwig and fearlessly confronts the dog. No trace of Woundwort is found, leaving his fate ambiguous.</p>
<p>Years later, the warren is thriving. An elderly Hazel is visited by the Black Rabbit Of Inle, who invites him to "join his Owsla", assuring him of Watership Down's perpetual safety. Reassured, Hazel accepts and dies peacefully. Hazel's spirit follows the Black Rabbit Of Inle through the woodland and trees towards the Sun, which metamorphoses into Frith, and the <a href="/wiki/Afterlife" title="Afterlife">afterlife</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watership_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film was originally to be directed by <a href="/wiki/John_Hubley" title="John Hubley">John Hubley</a>, who died in 1977. His work can still be found in the film, most notably in the "fable" scene. He was replaced by the film's producer Martin Rosen, his directorial debut.</p>
<p>After the genesis story rendered in a narrated simple cartoon fashion, the animation style changes to a detailed, naturalist one, with concessions to render the animals <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anthropomorphic" title="Anthropomorphic">anthropomorphic</a> only to suggest they have human voices and minds, some facial expressions for emotion and paw gestures. The animation backgrounds are watercolours. Only one of the predators, the farm cat, Tab, is given a few lines, the rest remaining mute.</p>
<p>The backgrounds and locations, especially Efrafa and the nearby railway, are based to the diagrams and maps in Richard Adams's book. Most of the locations in this movie either exist or were based on real spots in <a href="/wiki/Hampshire" title="Hampshire">Hampshire</a> and surrounding areas.</p>
<p>Although the film was fairly faithful to the novel, several changes were made to the storyline, mainly to decrease overdetailed complexity and improve pace and flow of the plot. In addition, the order in which some events occur is re-arranged. Unlike many animated features, the film faithfully emulated the dark and violent sophistication of the book. As a result, many reviewers took to warning parents that children might find the content disturbing. When the film was first submitted to the <a href="/wiki/British_Board_of_Film_Classification" title="British Board of Film Classification">British Board of Film Classification</a>, the BBFC passed the film with a 'U' certificate (suitable for all ages), deciding that "...Whilst the film may move children emotionally during the film's duration, it could not seriously trouble them once the spell of the story is broken and a 'U' certificate was therefore quite appropriate".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBFC_Examiners_Report_15th_February_1978_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBFC_Examiners_Report_15th_February_1978-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> However in 2012, the BBFC admitted that it "has received complaints about the suitability of Watership Down at U almost every year since its classification".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBFC_Centenary_Archive.2C_From_the_Archive.E2.80.A6_a_.E2.80.9Crepressive_rabbit_regime_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBFC_Centenary_Archive.2C_From_the_Archive.E2.80.A6_a_.E2.80.9Crepressive_rabbit_regime-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>This attitude extended to when the animated <a href="/wiki/Watership_Down_(TV_series)" title="Watership Down (TV series)"><i>Watership Down</i> TV series</a> was marketed with the producers making an effort to reassure parents that the violence was softened and that the main characters would not be permanently harmed in their adventures.</p>
<p>Some marketers in the U.S. were also worried that the main promotional poster (see above) appeared too dark and might scare some children. The poster is actually showing Bigwig in a snare (his distinctive fur is clearly visible), and the image on the poster does not appear in the film, which has a far bloodier depiction of the scene.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watership_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The musical score was by <a href="/wiki/Angela_Morley" title="Angela Morley">Angela Morley</a> and <a href="/wiki/Malcolm_Williamson" title="Malcolm Williamson">Malcolm Williamson</a>, Morley replacing Williamson after the composer had fallen behind and only composed the prelude and main title theme in sketch form.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> A list of the musical cues for the film can be found on the composer's website, which also gives information about the different composers working on the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The soundtrack included <a href="/wiki/Art_Garfunkel" title="Art Garfunkel">Art Garfunkel</a>'s British No. 1 hit, "<a href="/wiki/Bright_Eyes_(Art_Garfunkel_song)" title="Bright Eyes (Art Garfunkel song)">Bright Eyes</a>", which was written by the British singer and songwriter <a href="/wiki/Mike_Batt" title="Mike Batt">Mike Batt</a> (although in a different arrangement from the one released as a single). Batt also wrote other songs for the film which were not used. The composer recorded three songs with vocals by Garfunkel, but only "Bright Eyes" made it to the film. The song "When You're Losing Your Way in the Rain" has a very similar feeling and arrangement, and was recorded by the former Zombies vocalist <a href="/wiki/Colin_Blunstone" title="Colin Blunstone">Colin Blunstone</a> in 1979. Garfunkel's version was heard years later, on the TV series soundtrack released in 2000. The song, like many others which appeared on the TV soundtrack, was never used in the show.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Another song, "Watership Down", was written by <a href="/wiki/Gerry_Beckley" title="Gerry Beckley">Gerry Beckley</a> of <a href="/wiki/America_(band)" title="America (band)">America</a> for use as the theme song. It was never used, but the band recorded it for their 1976 <a href="/wiki/Album" title="Album">album</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Hideaway_(America_album)" title="Hideaway (America album)">Hideaway</a></i>. An alternative mix can be found on the <a href="/wiki/Box_set" title="Box set">boxed set</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Highway_(America_album)" title="Highway (America album)">Highway</a></i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watership_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<tr>

<p>The film was an immediate success at the UK box office and has received a generally positive critical reception, with an 81% 'Fresh' rating on Rotten Tomatoes, and a rating of 67% from select critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The film was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Award_for_Best_Dramatic_Presentation" title="Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation">Hugo Award for Best Dramatic Presentation</a> in 1979. In 2004, the magazine <i><a href="/wiki/Total_Film" title="Total Film">Total Film</a></i> named <i>Watership Down</i> the 47th greatest British film of all time and it was also ranked 15th in the "100 Greatest Tearjerkers".</p>
<p>Investors in the film reportedly received a return of 5,000% on their investment.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-icon_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-icon-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Picture_book">Picture book</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watership_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Picture book">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A picture book of the animated film was also produced, titled <i>The Watership Down Film Picture Book</i>. Two editions of the book were published, one a hard-cover, the other a reinforced cloth-bound edition. The contents include stills from the film linked with a combination of narration and extracts from the script, as well as a preface written by Richard Adams and a foreword written by Martin Rosen.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <h3><span class="mw-headline" id="TV_series">TV series</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Watership_Down_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: TV series">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Almost twenty years later a TV series with the same title <a href="/wiki/Watership_Down_(TV_series)" title="Watership Down (TV series)"><i>Watership Down</i></a> was created. It has thirty-nine episodes and was loosely based on the events of the film, albeit the storyline is more child-friendly. However, the third season does take a slightly darker turn than the first two seasons did.</p>
