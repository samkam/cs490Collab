<b>Vladimir Sirin</b>; 22 April [<a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">O.S.</a> 10 April] 1899<sup id="fn_c_back"><a href="#fn_c">c</a></sup> – 2 July 1977) was a Russian novelist. Nabokov's first nine novels were in Russian. He then rose to international prominence as a writer of English prose. He also made serious contributions as a <a href="/wiki/Lepidopterist" title="Lepidopterist">lepidopterist</a> and <a href="/wiki/Chess_composer" title="Chess composer">chess composer</a>.</p>
<p>Nabokov's <i><a href="/wiki/Lolita" title="Lolita">Lolita</a></i> (1955) is his most famous novel, and often considered his finest work in English. It exhibits the love of intricate word play and <a href="/wiki/Synesthesia" title="Synesthesia">synesthetic</a> detail that characterised all his works. The novel was ranked fourth in the list of the <a href="/wiki/Modern_Library_100_Best_Novels" title="Modern Library 100 Best Novels">Modern Library 100 Best Novels</a>;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/Pale_Fire" title="Pale Fire">Pale Fire</a></i> (1962) was ranked at 53rd on the same list, and his memoir, <i><a href="/wiki/Speak,_Memory" title="Speak, Memory">Speak, Memory</a></i>, was listed eighth on the Modern Library nonfiction list.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> He was a finalist for the <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award_for_Fiction" title="National Book Award for Fiction">National Book Award for Fiction</a> seven times, but never won it.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Life_and_career">Life and career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Life and career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Russia">Russia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Russia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Nabokov was born on 22 April 1899 (10 April 1899 <a href="/wiki/Old_Style_and_New_Style_dates" title="Old Style and New Style dates">Old Style</a>), in <a href="/wiki/Saint_Petersburg" title="Saint Petersburg">Saint Petersburg</a>,<sup id="fn_b_back"><a href="#fn_b">b</a></sup> to a wealthy and prominent family of minor nobility. He was the eldest of five children of liberal lawyer, statesman, and journalist <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Dmitrievich_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Dmitrievich Nabokov">Vladimir Dmitrievich Nabokov</a> and his wife, Elena Ivanovna née Rukavishnikova. His cousins included the composer <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Nabokov" title="Nicolas Nabokov">Nicolas Nabokov</a>. He spent his childhood and youth in St. Petersburg and at the country estate <i>Vyra</i> near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Siverskaya" title="Siverskaya">Siverskaya</a>, to the south of the city.</p>
<p>Nabokov's childhood, which he had called "perfect", was remarkable in several ways. The family spoke Russian, English, and French in their household, and Nabokov was trilingual from an early age. In fact, much to his patriotic father's chagrin, Nabokov could read and write in English before he could in Russian. In <i><a href="/wiki/Speak,_Memory" title="Speak, Memory">Speak, Memory</a></i> Nabokov recalls numerous details of his privileged childhood, and his ability to recall in vivid detail memories of his past was a boon to him during his permanent exile, and provided a theme that echoes from his first book <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_(novel)" title="Mary (novel)">Mary</a></i> to later works such as <i><a href="/wiki/Ada_or_Ardor:_A_Family_Chronicle" title="Ada or Ardor: A Family Chronicle">Ada or Ardor: A Family Chronicle</a></i>. While the family was nominally <a href="/wiki/Eastern_Orthodox_Church" title="Eastern Orthodox Church">Orthodox</a>, they felt no religious fervor, and Vladimir was not forced to attend church after he lost interest. In 1916, Nabokov inherited the estate Rozhdestveno, next to Vyra, from his uncle Vasiliy Ivanovich Rukavishnikov ("Uncle Ruka" in <i>Speak, Memory</i>), but lost it in the <a href="/wiki/October_Revolution" title="October Revolution">October Revolution</a> one year later; this was the only house he ever owned.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Emigration">Emigration</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Emigration">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the 1917 <a href="/wiki/February_Revolution" title="February Revolution">February Revolution</a>, Nabokov's father became a secretary of the <a href="/wiki/Russian_Provisional_Government" title="Russian Provisional Government">Russian Provisional Government</a> and, after the Bolshevik (October) Revolution, the family was forced to flee the city for <a href="/wiki/Crimea" title="Crimea">Crimea</a>, not expecting to be away for very long. They lived at a friend's estate and in September 1918 moved to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Livadiya,_Ukraine" title="Livadiya, Ukraine">Livadiya</a>, at the time part of the short-lived first <a href="/wiki/Ukrainian_People%27s_Republic" title="Ukrainian People's Republic">Ukrainian Republic</a>; Nabokov's father became a minister of justice in the <a href="/wiki/Crimean_Regional_Government" title="Crimean Regional Government">Crimean Regional Government</a>.</p>
<p>After the withdrawal of the German Army (November 1918) and the defeat of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/White_Army" title="White Army">White Army</a> (early 1919), the Nabokovs sought exile in western Europe. They settled briefly in England and Vladimir enrolled in <a href="/wiki/Trinity_College,_Cambridge" title="Trinity College, Cambridge">Trinity College, Cambridge</a>, first studying <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a>, then <a href="/wiki/Slavic_languages" title="Slavic languages">Slavic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Romance_languages" title="Romance languages">Romance languages</a>. Nabokov later drew on his Cambridge experiences to write several works, including the novels <i><a href="/wiki/Glory_(novel)" title="Glory (novel)">Glory</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Real_Life_of_Sebastian_Knight" title="The Real Life of Sebastian Knight">The Real Life of Sebastian Knight</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1920, Nabokov's family moved to Berlin, where his father set up the émigré newspaper <i>Rul'</i> ("Rudder"). Nabokov followed them to Berlin two years later, after completing his studies at Cambridge.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Berlin_years_.281922.E2.80.9337.29">Berlin years (1922–37)</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Berlin years (1922–37)">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In March 1922, Nabokov's father was assassinated in Berlin by the Russian monarchist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Piotr_Shabelsky-Bork" title="Piotr Shabelsky-Bork">Piotr Shabelsky-Bork</a> as he was trying to shield the real target, <a href="/wiki/Pavel_Milyukov" title="Pavel Milyukov">Pavel Milyukov</a>, a leader of the <a href="/wiki/Constitutional_Democratic_Party" title="Constitutional Democratic Party">Constitutional Democratic Party</a>-in-exile. This mistaken, violent death would echo again and again in Nabokov's fiction, where characters would meet their deaths under accidental terms. (In <i><a href="/wiki/Pale_Fire" title="Pale Fire">Pale Fire</a></i>, for example, one interpretation of the novel has an assassin mistakenly kill the poet John Shade, when his actual target is a fugitive European monarch.) Shortly after his father's death, Nabokov's mother and sister moved to <a href="/wiki/Prague" title="Prague">Prague</a>.</p>
<p>Nabokov stayed in Berlin, where he had become a recognised poet and writer within the émigré community and published under the nom de plume <i>V. Sirin</i> (a reference to the <a href="/wiki/Sirin" title="Sirin">fabulous bird</a> of Russian folklore). To supplement his scant writing income, he taught languages and gave tennis and boxing lessons.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MA_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-MA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Of his fifteen Berlin years, <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Dieter_E._Zimmer&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Dieter E. Zimmer (page does not exist)">Dieter E. Zimmer</a> wrote: "He never became fond of Berlin, and at the end intensely disliked it. He lived within the lively Russian community of Berlin that was more or less self-sufficient, staying on after it had disintegrated because he had nowhere else to go to. He knew little German. He knew few Germans except for landladies, shopkeepers, the petty immigration officials at the police headquarters."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1922 Nabokov became engaged to Svetlana Siewert; she broke off the engagement in early 1923, her parents worrying that he could not provide for her.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> In May 1923 he met a Jewish-Russian woman, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/V%C3%A9ra_Evseyevna_Slonim" title="Véra Evseyevna Slonim">Véra Evseyevna Slonim</a>, at a charity ball in Berlin<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MA_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-MA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> and married her in April 1925.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MA_4-2"><a href="#cite_note-MA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Their only child, <a href="/wiki/Dmitri_Nabokov" title="Dmitri Nabokov">Dmitri</a>, was born in 1934.</p>
<p>In 1936, Véra lost her job because of the increasingly anti-Semitic environment; also in that year the assassin of Nabokov's father was appointed second-in-command of the Russian émigré group. In the same year Nabokov began seeking a job in the English-speaking world. In 1937 he left Germany for France, where he had a short affair with Russian émigrée Irina Guadanini; his family followed, making their last visit to Prague en route. They settled in Paris, but also spent time in <a href="/wiki/Cannes" title="Cannes">Cannes</a>, <a href="/wiki/Menton" title="Menton">Menton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cap_d%27Antibes" title="Cap d'Antibes">Cap d'Antibes</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Fr%C3%A9jus" title="Fréjus">Fréjus</a>. In May 1940 the Nabokov family fled from the advancing German troops to the United States on board the <i><a href="/wiki/SS_Champlain" title="SS Champlain">SS Champlain</a></i>, with the exception of Nabokov's brother Sergei, who died at the <a href="/wiki/Neuengamme_concentration_camp" title="Neuengamme concentration camp">Neuengamme concentration camp</a> on 9 January 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="United_States">United States</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: United States">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The Nabokovs settled in Manhattan and Vladimir began volunteer work as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entomologist" title="Entomologist">entomologist</a> at the <a href="/wiki/American_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="American Museum of Natural History">American Museum of Natural History</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Nabokov joined the staff of <a href="/wiki/Wellesley_College" title="Wellesley College">Wellesley College</a> in 1941 as resident lecturer in comparative literature. The position, created specifically for him, provided an income and free time to write creatively and pursue his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lepidoptery" title="Lepidoptery">lepidoptery</a>. Nabokov is remembered as the founder of Wellesley's Russian Department. The Nabokovs resided in <a href="/wiki/Wellesley,_Massachusetts" title="Wellesley, Massachusetts">Wellesley, Massachusetts</a>, during the 1941–42 academic year. In September 1942 they moved to <a href="/wiki/Cambridge,_Massachusetts" title="Cambridge, Massachusetts">Cambridge</a> where they lived until June 1948. Following a lecture tour through the United States, Nabokov returned to Wellesley for the 1944–45 academic year as a lecturer in Russian. In 1945, he became a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Naturalised_citizen" title="Naturalised citizen">naturalised citizen</a> of the United States. He served through the 1947–48 term as Wellesley's one-man Russian Department, offering courses in Russian language and literature. His classes were popular, due as much to his unique teaching style as to the wartime interest in all things Russian.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (November 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> At the same time he was the <i>de facto</i> curator of lepidoptery at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Comparative_Zoology" title="Museum of Comparative Zoology">Museum of Comparative Zoology</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> After being encouraged by <a href="/wiki/Morris_Bishop" title="Morris Bishop">Morris Bishop</a>, Nabokov left Wellesley in 1948 to teach Russian and European literature at <a href="/wiki/Cornell_University" title="Cornell University">Cornell University</a>, where he taught until 1959. Among his students at Cornell was future <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Supreme_Court" title="U.S. Supreme Court">U.S. Supreme Court</a> <a href="/wiki/Associate_Justice_of_the_Supreme_Court_of_the_United_States" title="Associate Justice of the Supreme Court of the United States">Justice</a> <a href="/wiki/Ruth_Bader_Ginsburg" title="Ruth Bader Ginsburg">Ruth Bader Ginsburg</a>, who later identified Nabokov as a major influence on her development as a writer.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Nabokov wrote <i><a href="/wiki/Lolita" title="Lolita">Lolita</a></i> while travelling on butterfly-collection trips in the western United States that he undertook every summer. Véra acted as "secretary, typist, editor, proofreader, translator and bibliographer; his agent, business manager, legal counsel and chauffeur; his research assistant, teaching assistant and professorial understudy"; when Nabokov attempted to burn unfinished drafts of <i>Lolita</i>, it was Véra who stopped him. He called her the best-humoured woman he had ever known.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MA_4-3"><a href="#cite_note-MA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In June 1953 Nabokov and his family went to <a href="/wiki/Ashland,_Oregon" title="Ashland, Oregon">Ashland, Oregon</a>. There he finished <i>Lolita</i> and began writing the novel <i><a href="/wiki/Pnin" title="Pnin">Pnin</a></i>. He roamed the nearby mountains looking for butterflies, and wrote a poem called <i>Lines Written in Oregon</i>. On 1 October 1953, he and his family returned to <a href="/wiki/Ithaca,_New_York" title="Ithaca, New York">Ithaca, New York</a>, where he would later teach the young writer <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pynchon" title="Thomas Pynchon">Thomas Pynchon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-medfordmail_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-medfordmail-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Montreux_and_death">Montreux and death</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Montreux and death">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Nabokov%27s_grave.JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2304" data-file-width="3072" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Nabokov%27s_grave.JPG/220px-Nabokov%27s_grave.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Nabokov%27s_grave.JPG/330px-Nabokov%27s_grave.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7b/Nabokov%27s_grave.JPG/440px-Nabokov%27s_grave.JPG 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Nabokov%27s_grave.JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The grave of the Nabokovs at Cimetière de <a href="/wiki/Clarens,_Switzerland" title="Clarens, Switzerland">Clarens</a> near <a href="/wiki/Montreux" title="Montreux">Montreux</a>, Switzerland</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>After the great financial success of <i>Lolita</i>, Nabokov was able to return to Europe and devote himself exclusively to writing. His son had obtained a position as an operatic bass at <a href="/wiki/Reggio_Emilia" title="Reggio Emilia">Reggio Emilia</a>. On 1 October 1961, he and Véra moved to the <a href="/wiki/Montreux_Palace" title="Montreux Palace">Montreux Palace</a> Hotel in <a href="/wiki/Montreux" title="Montreux">Montreux</a>, Switzerland; he stayed there until the end of his life.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PR_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-PR-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> From his sixth-floor quarters he conducted his business and took tours to the Alps, Corsica, and Sicily to hunt butterflies. In 1976 he was hospitalised with an undiagnosed fever. He was rehospitalised in Lausanne in 1977 suffering from severe bronchial congestion. He died on 2 July in <a href="/wiki/Montreux" title="Montreux">Montreux</a> surrounded by his family and, according to his son, Dmitri, "with a triple moan of descending pitch".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> His remains were cremated and are buried at the <a href="/wiki/Clarens,_Switzerland" title="Clarens, Switzerland">Clarens</a> cemetery in <a href="/wiki/Montreux" title="Montreux">Montreux</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>At the time of his death, he was working on a novel titled <i><a href="/wiki/The_Original_of_Laura" title="The Original of Laura">The Original of Laura</a></i>. His wife Véra and son Dmitri were entrusted with Nabokov's <a href="/wiki/Literary_executor" title="Literary executor">literary executorship</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MA_4-4"><a href="#cite_note-MA-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> and though he asked them to burn the manuscript,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> they chose not to destroy his final work. The incomplete manuscript, around 125 handwritten index cards long,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> remained in a Swiss bank vault where only two people, Dmitri Nabokov and an unknown person, had access. Portions of the manuscript were shown to Nabokov scholars. In April, 2008, Dmitri announced that he would publish the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-New_York_Times_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-New_York_Times-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>The Original of Laura</i> was published on 17 November 2009.</p>
<p>Prior to the incomplete novel's publication, several short excerpts of <i>The Original of Laura</i> were made public, most recently by German weekly <i><a href="/wiki/Die_Zeit" title="Die Zeit">Die Zeit</a></i>, which in its 14 August 2008 issue for the first time reproduced some of Nabokov's original index cards obtained by its reporter Malte Herwig. In the accompanying article, Herwig concludes that <i>Laura</i>, although fragmentary, is "vintage Nabokov".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In July 2009, <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i> magazine acquired the rights to print a 5,000-word excerpt from <i>The Original of Laura</i>. It was printed in the December issue.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Work">Work</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Work">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nabokov's first writings were in Russian, but he came to his greatest distinction in the English language. For this achievement, he has been compared to <a href="/wiki/Joseph_Conrad" title="Joseph Conrad">Joseph Conrad</a>, yet Nabokov viewed this as a dubious comparison, as Conrad composed in French and English. Nabokov disdained the comparison for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aesthetic" title="Aesthetic">aesthetic</a> reasons, lamenting to the critic Edmund Wilson, "I am too old to change Conradically" – which John Updike later called, "itself a jest of genius".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> Nabokov translated many of his own early works into English, sometimes in cooperation with his son Dmitri. His trilingual upbringing had a profound influence on his artistry.</p>
<p>Nabokov himself translated into Russian two books that he had originally written in English, <i>Conclusive Evidence</i> and <i>Lolita</i>. The "translation" of <i>Conclusive Evidence</i> was made because of Nabokov's feeling of imperfection in the English version. Writing the book, he noted that he needed to translate his own memories into English, and to spend a lot of time explaining things that are well known in Russia; then he decided to re-write the book once again, in his first native language, and after that he made the final version, <i>Speak, Memory</i> (Nabokov first wanted to name it "Speak, <a href="/wiki/Mnemosyne" title="Mnemosyne">Mnemosyne</a>"). Nabokov was a proponent of <a href="/wiki/Individualism" title="Individualism">individualism</a>, and rejected concepts and ideologies that curtailed individual freedom and expression, such as <a href="/wiki/Totalitarianism" title="Totalitarianism">totalitarianism</a> in its various forms, as well as <a href="/wiki/Sigmund_Freud" title="Sigmund Freud">Sigmund Freud</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Psychoanalysis" title="Psychoanalysis">psychoanalysis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Poshlost" title="Poshlost">Poshlost</a>, or as he transcribed it, <i>poshlust</i>, is disdained and frequently mocked in his works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> On translating <i>Lolita</i>, Nabokov writes, "I imagined that in some distant future somebody might produce a Russian version of Lolita. I trained my inner telescope upon that particular point in the distant future and I saw that every paragraph, pock-marked as it is with pitfalls, could lend itself to hideous mistranslation. In the hands of a harmful drudge, the Russian version of Lolita would be entirely degraded and botched by vulgar paraphrases or blunders. So I decided to translate it myself."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Nabokov published under the pseudonym "Vladimir Sirin" in the 1920s to 1940s, occasionally to mask his identity from critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> He also makes cameo appearances in some of his novels, such as the character "<b>Vivian Darkbloom</b>" (an <a href="/wiki/Anagram" title="Anagram">anagram</a> of "Vladimir Nabokov"), who appears in both <i>Lolita</i> and <i>Ada, or Ardor,"</i> and the character Blavdak Vinimori (another anagram of Nabokov's name) in <i>"King, Queen, Knave."</i></p>
<p>Nabokov is noted for his complex plots, clever word play, and use of <a href="/wiki/Alliteration" title="Alliteration">alliteration</a>. He gained both fame and notoriety with his novel <i>Lolita</i> (1955), which tells of a grown man's devouring passion for a twelve-year-old girl. This and his other novels, particularly <i>Pale Fire</i> (1962), won him a place among the greatest novelists of the 20th century. His longest novel, which met with a mixed response, is <i><a href="/wiki/Ada_or_Ardor:_A_Family_Chronicle" title="Ada or Ardor: A Family Chronicle">Ada</a></i> (1969). He devoted more time to the composition of this novel than any of his others. Nabokov's fiction is characterised by linguistic playfulness. For example, his short story "<a href="/wiki/The_Vane_Sisters" title="The Vane Sisters">The Vane Sisters</a>" is famous in part for its <a href="/wiki/Acrostic" title="Acrostic">acrostic</a> final paragraph, in which the first letters of each word spell out a message from beyond the grave.</p>
<p>Nabokov's stature as a literary critic is founded largely on his four-volume <a href="/wiki/Translation" title="Translation">translation</a> and commentary for <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Pushkin" title="Alexander Pushkin">Alexander Pushkin</a>'s epic of the Russian soul, <i><a href="/wiki/Eugene_Onegin" title="Eugene Onegin">Eugene Onegin</a></i>, published in 1964. That commentary ended with an appendix titled <i><a href="/wiki/Notes_on_Prosody" title="Notes on Prosody">Notes on Prosody</a></i>, which has developed a reputation of its own. It stemmed from his observation that while Pushkin's <a href="/wiki/Iambic_tetrameter" title="Iambic tetrameter">iambic tetrameters</a> had been a part of <a href="/wiki/Russian_literature" title="Russian literature">Russian literature</a> for a fairly short two centuries, they were clearly understood by the Russian prosodists. On the other hand, he viewed the much older English iambic tetrameters as muddled and poorly documented. In his own words:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>I have been forced to invent a simple little terminology of my own, explain its application to English verse forms, and indulge in certain rather copious details of classification before even tackling the limited object of these notes to my translation of Pushkin's <i>Eugene Onegin</i>, an object that boils down to very little—in comparison to the forced preliminaries—namely, to a few things that the non-Russian student of Russian literature must know in regard to Russian prosody in general and to <i>Eugene Onegin</i> in particular.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>Nabokov's lectures at Cornell University, as collected in <i>Lectures on Literature</i>, reveal his controversial ideas concerning art.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> He firmly believed that novels should not aim to teach and that readers should not merely empathise with characters but that a 'higher' aesthetic enjoyment should be attained, partly by paying great attention to details of style and structure. He detested what he saw as 'general ideas' in novels, and so when teaching <i><a href="/wiki/Ulysses_(novel)" title="Ulysses (novel)">Ulysses</a></i>, for example, he would insist students keep an eye on where the characters were in Dublin (with the aid of a map) rather than teaching the complex Irish history that many critics see as being essential to an understanding of the novel. During his ten years at Cornell, Nabokov introduced undergraduates to the delights of great fiction, including <i><a href="/wiki/Bleak_House" title="Bleak House">Bleak House</a></i> by <a href="/wiki/Charles_Dickens" title="Charles Dickens">Charles Dickens</a>, in fifty-minute classroom lectures.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2010, <i><a href="/wiki/Kitsch_(magazine)" title="Kitsch (magazine)">Kitsch</a></i> magazine, a student publication at Cornell, published a piece that focused on student reflections on these lectures and also explored Nabokov's long relationship with <i><a href="/wiki/Playboy" title="Playboy">Playboy</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Nabokov's detractors fault him for being an aesthete and for his over-attention to language and detail rather than character development. In his essay "Nabokov, or Nostalgia", <a href="/wiki/Danilo_Ki%C5%A1" title="Danilo Kiš">Danilo Kiš</a> wrote that Nabokov's is "a magnificent, complex, and sterile art."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Russian poet <a href="/wiki/Yevgeny_Yevtushenko" title="Yevgeny Yevtushenko">Yevgeny Yevtushenko</a> said in a <i>Playboy</i> interview that he could hear the clatter of surgical tools in Nabokov's prose.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Nabokov.27s_synesthesia">Nabokov's synesthesia</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Nabokov's synesthesia">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nabokov was a self-described <a href="/wiki/Synesthesia" title="Synesthesia">synesthete</a>, who at a young age equated the number five with the colour red.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Aspects of synesthesia can be found in several of his works. His wife also exhibited synesthesia; like her husband, her mind's eye associated colours with particular letters. They discovered that Dmitri shared the trait, and moreover that the colours he associated with some letters were in some cases blends of his parents' hues—"which is as if <a href="/wiki/Gene" title="Gene">genes</a> were painting in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Aquarelle" title="Aquarelle">aquarelle</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>For some synesthetes, letters are not simply <i>associated with</i> certain colours, they <i>are themselves</i> coloured. Nabokov frequently endowed his protagonists with a similar gift. In <i><a href="/wiki/Bend_Sinister_(novel)" title="Bend Sinister (novel)">Bend Sinister</a></i> Krug comments on his perception of the word "loyalty" as being like a golden fork lying out in the sun. In <i>The Defense</i>, Nabokov mentioned briefly how the main character's father, a writer, found he was unable to complete a novel that he planned to write, becoming lost in the fabricated storyline by "starting with colours". Many other subtle references are made in Nabokov's writing that can be traced back to his synesthesia. Many of his characters have a distinct "sensory appetite" reminiscent of synesthesia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Entomology">Entomology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Entomology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>His career as an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Entomologist" title="Entomologist">entomologist</a> was equally distinguished. His interest in this field had been inspired by books of <a href="/wiki/Maria_Sibylla_Merian" title="Maria Sibylla Merian">Maria Sibylla Merian</a> he had found in the attic of his family's country home in Vyra.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Throughout an extensive career of collecting he never learned to drive a car, and he depended on his wife Véra to take him to collecting sites. During the 1940s, as a research fellow in <a href="/wiki/Zoology" title="Zoology">zoology</a>, he was responsible for organizing the butterfly collection of the <a href="/wiki/Museum_of_Comparative_Zoology" title="Museum of Comparative Zoology">Museum of Comparative Zoology</a> at <a href="/wiki/Harvard_University" title="Harvard University">Harvard University</a>. His writings in this area were highly technical. This, combined with his specialty in the relatively unspectacular tribe <a href="/wiki/Polyommatini" title="Polyommatini">Polyommatini</a> of the family <a href="/wiki/Lycaenidae" title="Lycaenidae">Lycaenidae</a>, has left this facet of his life little explored by most admirers of his literary works. He described the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Karner_blue" title="Karner blue">Karner blue</a>. The <a href="/wiki/Genus" title="Genus">genus</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Nabokovia" title="Nabokovia">Nabokovia</a></i> was named after him in honor of this work, as were a number of butterfly and moth species (e.g. many species in the genera <i><a href="/wiki/Madeleinea" title="Madeleinea">Madeleinea</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Pseudolucia" title="Pseudolucia">Pseudolucia</a></i> bear epithets alluding to Nabokov or names from his novels).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-35"><a href="#cite_note-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1967, Nabokov commented: "The pleasures and rewards of literary inspiration are nothing beside the rapture of discovering a new organ under the microscope or an undescribed species on a mountainside in Iran or Peru. It is not improbable that had there been no revolution in Russia, I would have devoted myself entirely to lepidopterology and never written any novels at all."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PR_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-PR-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The palaeontologist and essayist <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Jay_Gould" title="Stephen Jay Gould">Stephen Jay Gould</a> discussed Nabokov's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lepidoptery" title="Lepidoptery">lepidoptery</a> in his essay, "No Science Without Fancy, No Art Without Facts: The Lepidoptery of Vladimir Nabokov" (reprinted in <i><a href="/wiki/I_Have_Landed" title="I Have Landed">I Have Landed</a></i>). Gould notes that Nabokov was occasionally a scientific "stick-in-the-mud". For example, Nabokov never accepted that <a href="/wiki/Genetics" title="Genetics">genetics</a> or the counting of <a href="/wiki/Chromosome" title="Chromosome">chromosomes</a> could be a valid way to distinguish species of insects, and relied on the traditional (for lepidopterists) microscopic comparison of their <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Genitalia" title="Genitalia">genitalia</a>.</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Harvard_Museum_of_Natural_History" title="Harvard Museum of Natural History">Harvard Museum of Natural History</a>, which now contains the Museum of Comparative Zoology, still possesses Nabokov's "genitalia cabinet", where the author stored his collection of male blue butterfly genitalia.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> "Nabokov was a serious taxonomist," according to the museum staff writer <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Nancy_Pick&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Nancy Pick (page does not exist)">Nancy Pick</a>, author of <i>The Rarest of the Rare: Stories Behind the Treasures at the Harvard Museum of Natural History</i>. "He actually did quite a good job at distinguishing species that you would not think were different—by looking at their genitalia under a microscope six hours a day, seven days a week, until his eyesight was permanently impaired."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Though his work was not taken seriously by professional lepidopterists during his life, new genetic research supports Nabokov's hypothesis that a group of butterfly species, called the <i><a href="/wiki/Polyommatus" title="Polyommatus">Polyommatus</a></i> blues, came to the <a href="/wiki/New_World" title="New World">New World</a> over the <a href="/wiki/Bering_Strait" title="Bering Strait">Bering Strait</a> in five waves, eventually reaching Chile.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many of Nabokov's fans have tried to ascribe literary value to his scientific papers, Gould notes. Conversely, others have claimed that his scientific work enriched his literary output. Gould advocates a third view, holding that the other two positions are examples of the <i><a href="/wiki/Post_hoc_ergo_propter_hoc" title="Post hoc ergo propter hoc">post hoc ergo propter hoc</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Fallacy" title="Fallacy">fallacy</a>. Rather than assuming that either side of Nabokov's work caused or stimulated the other, Gould proposes that <i>both</i> stemmed from Nabokov's love of detail, contemplation, and symmetry.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Chess_problems">Chess problems</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Chess problems">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nabokov spent considerable time during his exile on the composition of <a href="/wiki/Chess_problem" title="Chess problem">chess problems</a>. Such compositions he published in the Russian émigré press, <i><a href="/wiki/Poems_and_Problems" title="Poems and Problems">Poems and Problems</a></i> (18 chess compositions) and <i><a href="/wiki/Speak,_Memory" title="Speak, Memory">Speak, Memory</a></i> (one problem). He describes the process of composing and constructing in his memoir: "The strain on the mind is formidable; the element of time drops out of one's consciousness". To him, the "originality, invention, conciseness, harmony, complexity, and splendid insincerity" of creating a chess problem was similar to that in any other art.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Politics_and_views">Politics and views</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Politics and views">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Nabokov described himself as a <a href="/wiki/Classical_liberalism" title="Classical liberalism">classical liberal</a>, in the tradition of his father.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> In a poem he wrote in 1917, he described Lenin's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bolshevik" title="Bolshevik">Bolsheviks</a> as "grey rag-tag people".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Later, during his American period, he expressed contempt for <a href="/wiki/Student_activism" title="Student activism">student activism</a>, and all collective movements. In both letters and interviews, he reveals a profound contempt for the <a href="/wiki/New_Left" title="New Left">New Left</a> movements of the 1960s, describing the protestors as "conformists" and "goofy hoodlums".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> Nabokov supported the <a href="/wiki/Vietnam_War" title="Vietnam War">Vietnam War</a> effort and voiced admiration for President <a href="/wiki/Richard_Nixon" title="Richard Nixon">Richard Nixon</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> On his religious views, Nabokov was an agnostic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Nabokov admitted to having a "prejudice" against women writers. He wrote to Edmund Wilson, who had been making suggestions for his lectures: "I dislike Jane [Austen], and am prejudiced, in fact against all women writers. They are in another class."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> However, on rereading <i><a href="/wiki/Mansfield_Park" title="Mansfield Park">Mansfield Park</a></i> he soon changed his mind and taught it in his literature course; he also praised the work of <a href="/wiki/Mary_McCarthy_(author)" title="Mary McCarthy (author)">Mary McCarthy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> Although his wife <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A9ra_Nabokov" title="Véra Nabokov">Véra Nabokov</a> worked as his personal translator and secretary, he made publicly known that his ideal translator was a male, and particularly not a "Russian-born female".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-51"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> In the first chapter of <i><a href="/wiki/Glory_(novel)" title="Glory (novel)">Glory</a></i> he attributes the protagonist's similar prejudice to the impressions made by children's writers like <a href="/wiki/Lidia_Charskaya" title="Lidia Charskaya">Lidiya Charski</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup> and in the short story "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=The_Admiralty_Spire&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="The Admiralty Spire (page does not exist)">The Admiralty Spire</a>" deplores the posturing, snobbery, antisemitism, and cutesiness he considered characteristic of Russian women authors.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Influence">Influence</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Influence">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>The Russian literary critic <a href="/wiki/Yuly_Aykhenvald" title="Yuly Aykhenvald">Yuly Aykhenvald</a> was an early admirer of Nabokov, citing in particular his ability to imbue objects with life: "he saturates trivial things with life, sense and psychology and gives a mind to objects; his refined senses notice colorations and nuances, smells and sounds, and everything acquires an unexpected meaning and truth under his gaze and through his words."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Chamberlain_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Chamberlain-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> The critic <a href="/wiki/James_Wood_(critic)" title="James Wood (critic)">James Wood</a> argued that Nabokov's use of descriptive detail proved an "overpowering, and not always very fruitful, influence on two or three generations after him", including authors such as <a href="/wiki/Martin_Amis" title="Martin Amis">Martin Amis</a> and <a href="/wiki/John_Updike" title="John Updike">John Updike</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> While a student at Cornell in the 1950s, <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Pynchon" title="Thomas Pynchon">Thomas Pynchon</a> attended several of Nabokov's lectures<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> and went on to make a direct allusion to <i><a href="/wiki/Lolita" title="Lolita">Lolita</a></i> in chapter six of his novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Crying_of_Lot_49" title="The Crying of Lot 49">The Crying of Lot 49</a></i> (1966) in which Serge, counter-tenor in the band the Paranoids, sings:</p>
<dl>
<dd>What chance has a lonely surfer boy</dd>
<dd>For the love of a surfer chick,</dd>
<dd>With all these Humbert Humbert cats</dd>
<dd>Coming on so big and sick?</dd>
<dd>For me, my baby was a woman,</dd>
<dd>For him she's just another nymphet.</dd>
</dl>
<p>It has also been argued that Pynchon's prose style is influenced by Nabokov's preference for actualism over realism.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-57"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> Of the authors who came to prominence during Nabokov's lifetime, <a href="/wiki/John_Banville" title="John Banville">John Banville</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-58"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Don_DeLillo" title="Don DeLillo">Don DeLillo</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Edmund_White" title="Edmund White">Edmund White</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> were all influenced by him.</p>
<p>Several authors who came to prominence in the 1990s and 2000s have also cited Nabokov's work as a literary influence. <a href="/wiki/Aleksandar_Hemon" title="Aleksandar Hemon">Aleksandar Hemon</a>, whose high-wire wordplay and sense of the absurd are often compared to Nabokov's, has acknowledged the latter's impact on his writing. <a href="/wiki/Pulitzer_Prize" title="Pulitzer Prize">Pulitzer Prize</a>-winning novelist <a href="/wiki/Michael_Chabon" title="Michael Chabon">Michael Chabon</a> listed <i>Lolita</i> and <i>Pale Fire</i> among the "books that, I thought, changed my life when I read them,"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> and stated that "Nabokov's English combines aching lyricism with dispassionate precision in a way that seems to render every human emotion in all its intensity but never with an ounce of schmaltz or soggy language".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> Pulitzer Prize winner <a href="/wiki/Jeffrey_Eugenides" title="Jeffrey Eugenides">Jeffrey Eugenides</a> said that "Nabokov has always been and remains one of my favorite writers. He's able to juggle ten balls where most people can juggle three or four."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/T._Coraghessan_Boyle" title="T. Coraghessan Boyle">T. Coraghessan Boyle</a> said that "Nabokov's playfulness and the ravishing beauty of his prose are ongoing influences" on his writing,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-65"><a href="#cite_note-65"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Jhumpa_Lahiri" title="Jhumpa Lahiri">Jhumpa Lahiri</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-66"><a href="#cite_note-66"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Marisha_Pessl" title="Marisha Pessl">Marisha Pessl</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-67"><a href="#cite_note-67"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Maxim_D._Shrayer" title="Maxim D. Shrayer">Maxim D. Shrayer</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-68"><a href="#cite_note-68"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Zadie_Smith" title="Zadie Smith">Zadie Smith</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-69"><a href="#cite_note-69"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Ki_Longfellow" title="Ki Longfellow">Ki Longfellow</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> have also acknowledged Nabokov's influence. Nabokov is featured both as an individual character and implicitly in <a href="/wiki/W._G._Sebald" title="W. G. Sebald">W. G. Sebald</a>'s 1993 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Emigrants_(Sebald_novel)" title="The Emigrants (Sebald novel)">The Emigrants</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Cohen_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Cohen-71"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> The song cycle "Sing, Poetry" on the 2011 contemporary classical album <i><a href="/wiki/Troika_(album)" title="Troika (album)">Troika</a></i> comprises settings of Russian and English versions of three of Nabokov's poems by such composers as <a href="/wiki/Jay_Greenberg" title="Jay Greenberg">Jay Greenberg</a>, <a href="/wiki/Michael_Schelle" title="Michael Schelle">Michael Schelle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lev_Zhurbin" title="Lev Zhurbin">Lev Zhurbin</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-72"><a href="#cite_note-72"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Vladimir_Nabokov&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="refbegin" style="">
<ul>
<li><cite id="fn_a"><a href="#fn_a_back">Note a:</a></cite> <cite id="fn_b"><a href="#fn_b_back">Note b:</a></cite> <cite id="fn_c"><a href="#fn_c_back">Note c:</a></cite></li>
</ul>
<p>Confusion over his birth date was generated by some people misunderstanding the relationship between the <a href="/wiki/Julian_calendar" title="Julian calendar">Julian</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gregorian_calendar" title="Gregorian calendar">Gregorian</a> calendars. At the time of Nabokov's birth, the offset between the calendars was 12 days. His date of birth in the Julian calendar was 10 April 1899;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B1993p37_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-B1993p37-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> in the Gregorian, 22 April 1899.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-B1993p37_73-1"><a href="#cite_note-B1993p37-73"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup> The fact that the offset increased from 12 to 13 days for dates occurring after February 1900 was always irrelevant to earlier dates, and hence a 13-day offset should never have been applied to Nabokov's date of birth. Nevertheless, it was so misapplied by some writers, and 23 April came to be erroneously shown in many places as his birthday. In his memoirs <i><a href="/wiki/Speak,_Memory" title="Speak, Memory">Speak, Memory</a></i> Nabokov indicates that 22 April was the correct date but that he nevertheless preferred to celebrate his birthday "with diminishing pomp" on 23 April (p. 6). As he happily pointed out on several occasions during interviews, this meant he also shared a birthday with <a href="/wiki/William_Shakespeare" title="William Shakespeare">William Shakespeare</a> and <a href="/wiki/Shirley_Temple" title="Shirley Temple">Shirley Temple</a> (see, for example, his <i>New York Times</i> interview with Alden Whitman on 23 April 1969, p. 20; see also <a href="#Boyd1993">Brian Boyd's biography</a>).</p>
</div>
