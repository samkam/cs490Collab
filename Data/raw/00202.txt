<h2 class="date-header"><span>Wednesday, 18 July 2012</span></h2>
<div class="date-posts">
<div class="post-outer">
<div class="post hentry" itemprop="blogPost" itemscope="itemscope" itemtype="http://schema.org/BlogPosting">
<meta content="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-l1wiNKB5a_I/UAZumdAvykI/AAAAAAAAG78/oqKTVL3x8m8/s640/Higgins_Eagle_Has_Landed.jpg" itemprop="image_url"/>

<h3 class="post-title entry-title" itemprop="name">
The Eagle Has Landed by Jack Higgins; the Original Novel (Collins, 1975): Book Review, Basis for the 1976 Movie
</h3>
<div class="post-header">
<div class="post-header-line-1"></div>
</div>
<div class="post-body entry-content" id="post-body-4679086476536270993" itemprop="articleBody">
After an unscheduled but necessary <a href="http://existentialennui.blogspot.co.uk/2012/07/book-review-kinds-of-love-kinds-of.html">Donald E. Westlake interlude</a> (necessary because it allowed me to finish reading the novel I'm about to discuss), let's have the final <a href="http://existentialennui.blogspot.co.uk/2012/07/book-review-boys-from-brazil-by-ira.html">book which begat a perhaps more famous film</a>. And having begun this series of posts (over three weeks ago now) with a <a href="http://existentialennui.blogspot.co.uk/2012/06/book-review-where-eagles-dare-by.html">World War II-set a tale </a><a href="http://existentialennui.blogspot.co.uk/2012/06/book-review-where-eagles-dare-by.html">with an "Eagle" in its title </a><a href="http://existentialennui.blogspot.co.uk/2012/06/book-review-where-eagles-dare-by.html">of a daring raid by a squad of British (and one American) troops</a>, I'm ending it with a World War II-set tale with an "Eagle" in <i>its</i> title of a daring raid by a squad of <i>German</i> (and one British) troops. Moreover, seeing as this is the grand finale of this series (for which I'm sure, by this point, we're all immensely grateful), I've managed to secure a copy of the first edition of the novel that's really quite remarkable...<br/>
<br/>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-l1wiNKB5a_I/UAZumdAvykI/AAAAAAAAG78/oqKTVL3x8m8/s1600/Higgins_Eagle_Has_Landed.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="640" src="http://2.bp.blogspot.com/-l1wiNKB5a_I/UAZumdAvykI/AAAAAAAAG78/oqKTVL3x8m8/s640/Higgins_Eagle_Has_Landed.jpg" width="400"/></a></div>
<br/>
First published in the UK by Collins in 1975 under a dust jacket illustrated by Barry Glynn, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jack_Higgins">Jack Higgins</a>'s <i>The Eagle Has Landed</i> didn't have long to wait to be filmed: the eponymous movie adaptation, directed by <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Sturges">John Sturges</a> – the final film of his stellar directing career (highlights including <i>The Great Escape</i>, <i>The Magnificent Seven</i>, <i>Gunfight at the OK Corral</i>, and 
one of my favourite movies of all time, the sort-of-sequel to <i>Gunfight</i>, the excellent <a href="http://buddiesinthesaddle.blogspot.co.uk/2012/02/hour-of-gun-1967.html"><i>Hour of the Gun</i></a>) – arrived in cinemas the following year, starring Michael Caine as Lieutenant-Colonel Kurt Steiner, Robert Duvall as Colonel Radl and Donald Sutherland as Liam Devlin. <br/>
<br/>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-njDEH4GeDi0/UAZu3zg2EXI/AAAAAAAAG8E/Pebe17k8NCg/s1600/Eagle_Has_Landed1.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="640" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-njDEH4GeDi0/UAZu3zg2EXI/AAAAAAAAG8E/Pebe17k8NCg/s640/Eagle_Has_Landed1.jpg" width="416"/></a></div>
<br/>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
</div>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
</div>
Like <a href="http://existentialennui.blogspot.co.uk/2012/06/book-review-where-eagles-dare-by.html"><i>Where Eagles Dare</i></a>, <a href="http://www.imdb.com/title/tt0074452/"><i>The Eagle Has Landed</i></a> is one of those terrific war films I'm always happy to watch when it pops up on telly on a Saturday afternoon. It's perfectly cast (Caine, Duvall and Sutherland all brilliantly personifying their respective characters – plus it's got <a href="http://www.jennyagutter.net/">Jenny Agutter</a> in it, a woman who holds a special place in the hearts – and loins – of men of a certain age), nicely scripted (by <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tom_Mankiewicz">Tom Mankiewicz</a>), and of course solidly directed by Sturges. But the best thing about it is the story, and that's down to Higgins. With only minor differences – the removal of a character and subplot or two in the film – both book and movie follow the same trajectory: a plan is hatched by Himmler to either kidnap or kill British Prime Minister Winston Churchill, who, the Germans learn from Joanna Grey, a spy living in the (fictional) Norfolk village of Studley Constable, will be staying with the local lord of the manor. Tasked with planning and carrying out this mission are Radl, Steiner – along with what remains of Steiner's paratroop assault group after having fought on the Eastern Front and then being assigned in disgrace (following a bust-up with the SS) to the Channel Islands – and Devlin, an IRA man resident in Berlin.<br/>
<br/>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-BWFQ2Ts3Qnc/UAZvFVeJ4NI/AAAAAAAAG8M/qbbiWSK9_tg/s1600/Higgins_Eagle_case.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="292" src="http://4.bp.blogspot.com/-BWFQ2Ts3Qnc/UAZvFVeJ4NI/AAAAAAAAG8M/qbbiWSK9_tg/s400/Higgins_Eagle_case.jpg" width="400"/></a></div>
<br/>
The planning actually takes up two-thirds of the novel, Higgins rattling through the final incursion into Studley Constable, its subsequent collapse after one of the German paratroopers rescues two kids who've fallen under the watermill, and consequent firefight with an American Ranger unit at a breathless pace. But both parts of the book are splendidly written – not especially stylishly, but populated with memorable, well-drawn characters and penned with the gusto of an author who knows he has a cracking yarn on his hands. That said, a yarn isn't how Higgins presents the tale: his Author's Note at the start claims that "at least fifty per cent of it is documented historical fact", while a framing device sees Higgins himself visiting Studley Constable in the then-present day, researching a magazine article and inadvertently uncovering Steiner's story.<br/>
<br/>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-E9cQBBIB3lo/UAZvQii_ESI/AAAAAAAAG8c/VNxRT2S7_gU/s1600/Higgins_Eagle_ends1.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="330" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-E9cQBBIB3lo/UAZvQii_ESI/AAAAAAAAG8c/VNxRT2S7_gU/s400/Higgins_Eagle_ends1.jpg" width="400"/></a></div>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-YclTYXqzmkc/UAZvR2e41YI/AAAAAAAAG8k/U6DqQBpZnMY/s1600/Higgins_Eagle_ends2.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="325" src="http://3.bp.blogspot.com/-YclTYXqzmkc/UAZvR2e41YI/AAAAAAAAG8k/U6DqQBpZnMY/s400/Higgins_Eagle_ends2.jpg" width="400"/></a></div>
<br/>
Higgins wrote a belated sequel to <i>The Eagle Has Landed</i>, <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Eagle_Has_Flown"><i>The Eagle Has Flown</i></a> (1991), which apparently reveals that a protagonist who dies at the end of the original novel in fact didn't (I've got a copy on my shelves still waiting to be read); he also featured <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liam_Devlin">Liam Devlin</a> in a handful of other novels. As for first editions of <i>The Eagle Has Landed</i>, those aren't exactly scarce – AbeBooks currently has <a href="http://www.abebooks.co.uk/servlet/SearchResults?an=Jack+Higgins&amp;bt.x=50&amp;bt.y=18&amp;kn=Collins+1975&amp;sts=t&amp;tn=The+Eagle+Has+Landed">around fifty listed</a> – but my copy is unusual, in that it bears a signed Jack Higgins bookplate:<br/>
<br/>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-1NgilzRYzC4/UAZvqbShIlI/AAAAAAAAG8s/-NNxCM5xtC0/s1600/Higgins_Eagle_sig.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="321" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-1NgilzRYzC4/UAZvqbShIlI/AAAAAAAAG8s/-NNxCM5xtC0/s400/Higgins_Eagle_sig.jpg" width="400"/></a></div>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
</div>
<br/>
I know of only one other signed copy of <i>The Eagle Has Landed </i>in any edition – another Collins first, again bearing a bookplate, and offered by an American dealer for over £350, which is a hell of a lot more than I paid for mine.<br/>
<br/>
<div class="separator" style="clear: both; text-align: center;">
<a href="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-Zy9p6pCLqRQ/UAZxIXYTp9I/AAAAAAAAG88/_wWksHBN5eQ/s1600/Higgins_Eagle_back.jpg" imageanchor="1" style="margin-left: 1em; margin-right: 1em;"><img border="0" height="640" src="http://1.bp.blogspot.com/-Zy9p6pCLqRQ/UAZxIXYTp9I/AAAAAAAAG88/_wWksHBN5eQ/s640/Higgins_Eagle_back.jpg" width="392"/></a></div>
<br/>
And there'll be <a href="http://existentialennui.blogspot.co.uk/2012/07/the-harvey-award-nominated-alan-moore.html">more signed editions</a> on <a href="http://existentialennui.blogspot.co.uk/">Existential Ennui</a> shortly, after some <a href="http://existentialennui.blogspot.co.uk/2012/07/further-beautiful-british-book-covers.html">further additions to Beautiful British Book Jacket Design of the 1950s and 1960s</a>...
