<p><b>John Richard Deacon</b> (born 19 August 1951) is a retired English musician, best known as the bassist for the rock band <a href="/wiki/Queen_(band)" title="Queen (band)">Queen</a>. Of the four members of the band, he was the last to join and also the youngest, being only 19 years old when he was recruited by the other members of the band in February 1971. Deacon wrote a number of Queen's hit singles, including "<a href="/wiki/You%27re_My_Best_Friend_(Queen_song)" title="You're My Best Friend (Queen song)">You're My Best Friend</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Spread_Your_Wings" title="Spread Your Wings">Spread Your Wings</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Back_Chat" title="Back Chat">Back Chat</a>", "<a href="/wiki/I_Want_to_Break_Free" title="I Want to Break Free">I Want to Break Free</a>", and the band's biggest selling single in the United States, "<a href="/wiki/Another_One_Bites_the_Dust" title="Another One Bites the Dust">Another One Bites the Dust</a>", as well as a number of album tracks. He also played electric and <a href="/wiki/Acoustic_guitar" title="Acoustic guitar">acoustic guitars</a> on several albums and, to a lesser extent, keyboards, synthesizers, programming and drums. He very occasionally provided backing vocals during live shows.</p>
<p>Following <a href="/wiki/The_Freddie_Mercury_Tribute_Concert" title="The Freddie Mercury Tribute Concert">The Freddie Mercury Tribute Concert</a> in 1992, Deacon performed only 2 more times with the remaining members of Queen, in a charity concert in 1993<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and a one-off rendition of "<a href="/wiki/The_Show_Must_Go_On_(Queen_song)" title="The Show Must Go On (Queen song)">The Show Must Go On</a>" in 1997 with Mercury's friend <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> (who had sung the track with the band at Mercury's tribute) at the opening of the Bejart ballet in Paris. He played bass guitar on the final Queen song, "<a href="/wiki/No-One_but_You_(Only_the_Good_Die_Young)" title="No-One but You (Only the Good Die Young)">No-One but You (Only the Good Die Young)</a>" – released that year on the <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_Rocks" title="Queen Rocks">Queen Rocks</a></i> compilation – after which he retired from the music industry. He did not participate in the <a href="/wiki/Queen_%2B_Paul_Rodgers" title="Queen + Paul Rodgers">Queen + Paul Rodgers</a> collaboration, in Queen's induction into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> in 2001, in Queen's performance during the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics_closing_ceremony" title="2012 Summer Olympics closing ceremony">closing ceremony</a> of the <a href="/wiki/2012_Summer_Olympics" title="2012 Summer Olympics">2012 Summer Olympics</a>, or the <a href="/wiki/Queen_%2B_Adam_Lambert" title="Queen + Adam Lambert">Queen + Adam Lambert</a> collaboration.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deacon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>John Richard Deacon grew up in <a href="/wiki/Oadby" title="Oadby">Oadby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Leicestershire" title="Leicestershire">Leicestershire</a> with father Arthur, mother Lilian and younger sister Julie. He had an older brother, Robert Bryan, who died in July 1953 at the age of 6. His father worked for the <a href="/wiki/Norwich_Union" title="Norwich Union">Norwich Union</a> insurance company and died on 27 May 1963 at the age of 44. Deacon was known to friends and his bandmates as 'Deaks' or 'Deaky' and attended Linden Junior School in Leicester, <a href="/wiki/Gartree_High_School" title="Gartree High School">Gartree High School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Beauchamp_College" title="Beauchamp College">Beauchamp Grammar School</a> in Oadby and achieved 8 GCE <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/O_level" title="O level">O level</a> and 3 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/A_level" title="A level">A level</a> passes, all at grade A.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deacon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>It was in Leicester that Deacon played in his first band, <i>The Opposition</i>, in 1965 at the age of 14. He played rhythm guitar in the band, which he had bought with money borrowed from Richard Young the founding member. He became the bassist after the original bassist was fired for not being of the same quality as the rest of the band. As well as a dedicated musician, Deacon also was the band's archivist, taking clippings from newspapers of even the advertisements featuring The Opposition. After being in the band for four years, Deacon played his final concert with the band (then called The Art) in August 1969. He left as he had been accepted to study at Chelsea College.</p>
<p>Although he left his bass and amplifier at home in <a href="/wiki/Oadby" title="Oadby">Oadby</a>, after less than a year in London, where he went on to achieve a First Class Honours Degree in electronics at Chelsea College, now part of <a href="/wiki/King%27s_College_London" title="King's College London">King's College London</a>, he decided he wanted to join a band. By this time Queen had already been formed by <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mercury" title="Freddie Mercury">Freddie Mercury</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">Brian May</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Taylor_(Queen_drummer)" title="Roger Taylor (Queen drummer)">Roger Taylor</a>, and Deacon even saw them in October 1970. In early 1971 he was introduced to Taylor and May by a friend at a disco who told him that they were in a band that had just lost its bassist. A couple of days later he auditioned in a lecture room at <a href="/wiki/Imperial_College_London" title="Imperial College London">Imperial College London</a> and became the last member of Queen to join the band. Since the band's last bassist drew attention away from Mercury, Deacon was selected for his musical talent, his quiet demeanour and his electrical skills. A persistent legend claims Deacon was the seventh bassist auditioned. According to various sources, prior bassists with the band included <a href="/wiki/Mike_Grose" title="Mike Grose">Mike Grose</a>, Barry Mitchell and Doug Ewood.</p>
<p>On Queen's <a href="/wiki/Queen_(Queen_album)" title="Queen (Queen album)">first album</a> he was credited as "Deacon John".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-queen1_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-queen1-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Not long after its release, he requested that he be referred to by his proper name.</p>
<p>Deacon's first writing credit did not come until Queen's third album, <i><a href="/wiki/Sheer_Heart_Attack" title="Sheer Heart Attack">Sheer Heart Attack</a></i>. It was on the song "<a href="/wiki/Stone_Cold_Crazy" title="Stone Cold Crazy">Stone Cold Crazy</a>", but it was also credited to the other band members. The first song Deacon wrote on his own was "Misfire" from the same album, a Caribbean-themed song that garnered little attention. He would achieve much greater success with his second song, "<a href="/wiki/You%27re_My_Best_Friend_(Queen_song)" title="You're My Best Friend (Queen song)">You're My Best Friend</a>", which was featured on the group's fourth album, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Night_at_the_Opera_(Queen_album)" title="A Night at the Opera (Queen album)">A Night at the Opera</a></i>, and went on to be an international hit.</p>
<p>By the mid-1980s, Deacon had started to play with other bands as well. He performed on the single "Picking Up Sound" by Man Friday &amp; Jive Junior and played with The Immortals, which released the track "No Turning Back." Deacon also worked with <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> and was part of the soundtrack to the film Biggles: Adventures in Time (1988).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deacon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Deacon has often been described as the "quiet" member of the band,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deacon_personal_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-deacon_personal-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and the others said that he was in charge of most of the finances. His last public appearance with the band was at an AIDS charity event in 1997, and his last direct involvement with Queen was with the recording of "<a href="/wiki/No-One_but_You_(Only_the_Good_Die_Young)" title="No-One but You (Only the Good Die Young)">No-One But You (Only The Good Die Young)</a>". According to his friend Robert Ahwai, "John had lost his father when he was quite young. He suffered from depression after Freddie Mercury died and I am not sure he has ever come out of it."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deacon_personal_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-deacon_personal-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>He lives in <a href="/wiki/Putney" title="Putney">Putney</a> in South West London with his wife Veronica Tetzlaff. Tetzlaff was already two months pregnant with their first child when they were married on 18 January 1975.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The two have six children: Robert (1975), Michael (1978), Laura (1979), Joshua (1983), Luke (1992), and Cameron (1993).</p>
<p>According to <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Sunday_Times_(UK)" title="The Sunday Times (UK)">The Sunday Times</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Sunday_Times_Rich_List" title="Sunday Times Rich List">Rich List</a>, Deacon is worth £65 million or around $103 million as of 2011<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=John_Deacon&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>As a trained electronics engineer, Deacon would often build equipment for the band. His most famous creation is the "<a href="/wiki/Deacy_Amp" title="Deacy Amp">Deacy Amp</a>", used by himself and May.</p>

<p>Deacon played guitar in addition to bass, taking over rhythm parts in many albums, as well as several acoustic performances. Some of the guitar work on <i><a href="/wiki/Hot_Space" title="Hot Space">Hot Space</a></i> (the clean <a href="/wiki/Fender_Telecaster" title="Fender Telecaster">Fender Telecaster</a> single-coil sound) is his work. He would occasionally play synthesizers on his own compositions and often composed at the piano, playing a Wurlitzer on his top ten hit "You're My Best Friend". He can also be seen playing the grand piano in the music video to "<a href="/wiki/Spread_Your_Wings" title="Spread Your Wings">Spread Your Wings</a>", although on the actual recording the piano was played by Mercury.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Highlights">Highlights</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deacon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Highlights">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Mostly, Deacon's compositions varied from pop rock to funk. He has been responsible for some of Queen's biggest hits such as "You're My Best Friend" (from <i><a href="/wiki/A_Night_at_the_Opera_(Queen_album)" title="A Night at the Opera (Queen album)">A Night at the Opera</a></i>), "Another One Bites the Dust" (from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Game_(Queen_album)" title="The Game (Queen album)">The Game</a></i>) and "I Want to Break Free" (from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Works_(Queen_album)" title="The Works (Queen album)">The Works</a></i>). He also co-wrote "<a href="/wiki/Friends_Will_Be_Friends" title="Friends Will Be Friends">Friends Will Be Friends</a>" with Mercury and developed the chord arrangement for "<a href="/wiki/The_Miracle_(song)" title="The Miracle (song)">The Miracle</a>" with Mercury. Both went on the <i>Greatest Hits II</i> album. He also wrote two other popular songs, ("<a href="/wiki/Spread_Your_Wings" title="Spread Your Wings">Spread Your Wings</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Back_Chat" title="Back Chat">Back Chat</a>"), and created the riff for "<a href="/wiki/Under_Pressure" title="Under Pressure">Under Pressure</a>".</p>
<p>As a bass guitarist, Deacon's notable works include "Another One Bites the Dust", "Father to Son", "The Fairy Feller's Master-Stroke", "Liar", "Dragon Attack", "Brighton Rock", "The March of the Black Queen", "You're My Best Friend", "The Millionaire Waltz", "<a href="/wiki/We_Are_the_Champions" title="We Are the Champions">We Are the Champions</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Crazy_Little_Thing_Called_Love" title="Crazy Little Thing Called Love">Crazy Little Thing Called Love</a>", and "<a href="/wiki/Under_Pressure" title="Under Pressure">Under Pressure</a>". As a guitarist, he did some rhythm playing in songs like "Staying Power" (both live and in the studio) and "Back Chat", as well as lead parts in "Another One Bites the Dust" and "Misfire" and Spanish acoustic fills in "Who Needs You". He also played <a href="/wiki/Double_bass" title="Double bass">double bass</a>, notably on May's 1975 song "<a href="/wiki/A_Night_at_the_Opera_(Queen_album)" title="A Night at the Opera (Queen album)">'39</a>". May had asked him to play upright bass as a joke but a couple of days later he found Deacon in the studio with the instrument, and he had already learned to play it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Queen_Legends_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Queen_Legends-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Deacon's contributions in keyboards were mostly just background chords; his most notable work is in his composition "You're My Best Friend", which was the first song he wrote on the electric piano. Deacon also played <a href="/wiki/Triangle_(instrument)" title="Triangle (instrument)">triangle</a> in live versions of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Killer_Queen_(song)" title="Killer Queen (song)">Killer Queen</a>" (it hung off his microphone) and some piano (notably on "Another One Bites the Dust"). He can also be seen playing the drums on the video for "One Vision". This is perhaps a visual trick, although he did play drums on certain tracks on "Hot Space". He also helped producer David Richards compose on synthesizer a slap-bass style lead for the verses of "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Invisible_Man_(song)" title="The Invisible Man (song)">The Invisible Man</a>" from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Miracle_(Queen)" title="The Miracle (Queen)">The Miracle</a></i> album, while the chorus retains Roger Taylor's melody from an earlier demo.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Style">Style</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deacon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Style">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deacon used the bass as a lead and rhythm instrument. Some of his bass lines, such as "The Millionaire Waltz", "I'm Going Slightly Mad" or "You're My Best Friend", granted his technique an affinity with <a href="/wiki/Motown" title="Motown">Motown</a>'s lead style basslines. Some of Queen's songs, such as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dragon_Attack" title="Dragon Attack">Dragon Attack</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Another_One_Bites_the_Dust" title="Another One Bites the Dust">Another One Bites the Dust</a>", "Don't Try Suicide" or "<a href="/wiki/A_Kind_of_Magic_(song)" title="A Kind of Magic (song)">A Kind of Magic</a>", feature the bass as the main instrument. When performing with Queen, Deacon delivered a highly technical style, with numerous runs, walking bass lines and tight quick note changes. Deacon mainly used his fingers, though sometimes he did use a pick. He generally did not play with a floating thumb, and occasionally played with his thumb itself.</p>
<p>A trademark of Deacon's playing are his bass runs. In a 1975 review of <i><a href="/wiki/Sheer_Heart_Attack" title="Sheer Heart Attack">Sheer Heart Attack</a></i>, the reviewer wrote: "Only at the end would a new initiate to Queen recognise John Deacon's unmistakable trademark: the bass runs under the fade are as fast and facile as any to be heard. The least well known musician in Queen is one of his rock generation's most able."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Singer">Singer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deacon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Singer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Deacon is the only member of Queen never to sing any lead vocals on any of their tracks. He has stated in interviews that he was incapable of competing with the three strong vocalists in the group.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> Deacon is not credited as a vocalist on any Queen album.</p>
<p>In live shows, Deacon did not receive his own microphone until the band's first headlining tour in support of their <i><a href="/wiki/Sheer_Heart_Attack" title="Sheer Heart Attack">Sheer Heart Attack</a></i> album. Despite this promising development, the main purpose of this was to play one note on the triangle in "Killer Queen". Before this, he had sung backing vocals during "<a href="/wiki/Liar_(Queen_song)" title="Liar (Queen song)">Liar</a>" into Mercury's microphone. There have been occasions where his microphone was turned up to a point where his voice can be heard, such as a 1977 <a href="/wiki/Earls_Court" title="Earls Court">Earls Court</a> bootleg, where his voice was clearly heard on "<a href="/wiki/Somebody_to_Love_(Queen_song)" title="Somebody to Love (Queen song)">Somebody to Love</a>" and "In The Lap of the Gods Revisited". He can also be heard in a 1984 Milan bootleg singing part of a chorus of "<a href="/wiki/Radio_Ga_Ga" title="Radio Ga Ga">Radio Ga Ga</a>". He is also credited as backing vocalist on the closing credits of the band's first live video, "Queen at the Rainbow" (shot live in November 1974 and released on <i><a href="/wiki/Box_of_Tricks" title="Box of Tricks">Box of Tricks</a></i>).</p>
<p>Despite this, Deacon is almost always shown to be singing in Queen's music videos, such as "Bohemian Rhapsody", "You're My Best Friend", "Somebody To Love", "Tie Your Mother Down", and "Don't Stop Me Now", among others.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Retirement">Retirement</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deacon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Retirement">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After playing live with Queen three more times – at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Freddie_Mercury_Tribute_Concert" title="Freddie Mercury Tribute Concert">Freddie Mercury Tribute Concert</a> for AIDS Awareness on 20 April 1992, in a charity concert with Roger Taylor in Midhurst on 18 October 1993, and at the opening of the Bejart Ballet in Paris on 17 January 1997, performing only "<a href="/wiki/The_Show_Must_Go_On_(Queen_song)" title="The Show Must Go On (Queen song)">The Show Must Go On</a>" with Elton John on lead vocals – he made the decision to retire from music, re-appearing only briefly by joining his ex-band mates in October 1997 for the recording of the final Queen song "<a href="/wiki/No-One_but_You_(Only_the_Good_Die_Young)" title="No-One but You (Only the Good Die Young)">No-One but You (Only the Good Die Young)</a>", included in the <i><a href="/wiki/Queen_Rocks" title="Queen Rocks">Queen Rocks</a></i> album released a month later.</p>
<p>Deacon has reportedly spoken out about the May/Taylor/<a href="/wiki/Robbie_Williams" title="Robbie Williams">Robbie Williams</a> cover of "<a href="/wiki/We_Are_the_Champions" title="We Are the Champions">We Are the Champions</a>", recorded for <i><a href="/wiki/A_Knight%27s_Tale" title="A Knight's Tale">A Knight's Tale</a></i>. In an interview with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Sun_(United_Kingdom)" title="The Sun (United Kingdom)">The Sun</a></i> about the collaboration he said, "It is one of the greatest songs ever written but I think they've ruined it ... I don't want to be nasty but let's just say Robbie Williams is no Freddie Mercury. Freddie can never be replaced – and certainly not by him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>He chose not to be present at Queen's induction into the <a href="/wiki/Rock_and_Roll_Hall_of_Fame" title="Rock and Roll Hall of Fame">Rock and Roll Hall of Fame</a> in 2001, or join in the <a href="/wiki/Queen_%2B_Paul_Rodgers" title="Queen + Paul Rodgers">collaboration with Paul Rodgers</a>. On the Queen + Paul Rodgers collaboration album <i><a href="/wiki/The_Cosmos_Rocks" title="The Cosmos Rocks">The Cosmos Rocks</a></i> which featured new material from the trio, he was listed in the Thanks Notes on the CD. May insists that Deacon is still involved on the business side of Queen but chooses to hide from public sight. May also confirmed that Deacon approves of the proposed Freddie Mercury biopic.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In a 2014 interview with <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> regarding the upcoming <a href="/wiki/Queen_%2B_Adam_Lambert" title="Queen + Adam Lambert">Queen + Adam Lambert</a> North American tour with <a href="/wiki/Adam_Lambert" title="Adam Lambert">Adam Lambert</a>, <a href="/wiki/Brian_May" title="Brian May">May</a> and <a href="/wiki/Roger_Taylor_(Queen_drummer)" title="Roger Taylor (Queen drummer)">Taylor</a> admitted that they no longer have much contact with Deacon except regarding finances, with Taylor stating that "[Deacon]'s completely retired from any kind of social contact", and describing him as "fragile." May added however, "He still keeps an eye on the finances, though. John Deacon is still John Deacon. We don't undertake anything financial without talking to him."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Collaborations">Collaborations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=John_Deacon&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Collaborations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>1975: All four members of Queen helped produce a session with the soul band Trax. Nothing was ever released.</li>
<li>1983: "Picking Up Sounds" by Man Friday and Jive Junior – co-wrote, produced and played bass guitar on this single.</li>
<li>1984: "It's An Illusion" by Roger Taylor – bass guitar on this track from the album <i><a href="/wiki/Strange_Frontier" title="Strange Frontier">Strange Frontier</a></i>.</li>
<li>1984: "I Cry For You" by Roger Taylor – bass guitar on the remixed version of this song, on the single <i>Strange Frontier</i>.</li>
<li>1985: "Too Young" by <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> – bass guitar on this song from the LP <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ice_On_Fire" title="Ice On Fire">Ice On Fire</a></i>.</li>
<li>1985: <a href="/wiki/Strawberry_Switchblade" title="Strawberry Switchblade">Strawberry Switchblade</a> – produced self-titled first album.</li>
<li>1986: "Angeline" by Elton John – bass guitar on this song from the LP <i><a href="/wiki/Leather_Jackets_(album)" title="Leather Jackets (album)">Leather Jackets</a></i>.</li>
<li>1986: "This Is Your Time" by <a href="/wiki/Errol_Brown" title="Errol Brown">Errol Brown</a> – co-wrote and bass guitar on this track, which was never released.</li>
<li>1987: "I Dream Of Christmas" by <a href="/wiki/Anita_Dobson" title="Anita Dobson">Anita Dobson</a> – bass guitar on this track from the album <i>Talkin' Of Love</i>.</li>
<li>1988: "Roulette" by <a href="/wiki/Minako_Honda" title="Minako Honda">Minako Honda</a> – co-wrote this song (in fact "No Turning Back" remake with other lyrics) from the album <i>Cancel</i>. John did not participate in the Minako Honda recording, although his bandmate May wrote and produced two songs for this album.</li>
<li>1988: "How Can I Go On" by <a href="/wiki/Freddie_Mercury" title="Freddie Mercury">Freddie Mercury</a> &amp; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montserrat_Caballe" title="Montserrat Caballe">Montserrat Caballe</a> – bass guitar on this single from the album <i><a href="/wiki/Barcelona_(album)" title="Barcelona (album)">Barcelona</a></i>.</li>
<li>1988: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stutter_Rap_(No_Sleep_Til_Bedtime)" title="Stutter Rap (No Sleep Til Bedtime)">Stutter Rap (No Sleep Til Bedtime)</a> by `<a href="/wiki/Morris_Minor_and_the_Majors" title="Morris Minor and the Majors">Morris Minor and the Majors</a> John makes an appearance, wearing a blue wig and playing guitar.</li>
<li>1989: "Who Wants to Live Forever" by Ian &amp; Belinda – bass on this charity record, produced by Brian May, also featuring May and Roger Taylor.</li>
<li>1992: "Nothin' But Blue" by Brian May – plays bass on this track from <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Back_To_The_Light" title="Back To The Light">Back To The Light</a></i>.</li>
<li>1992: "Somewhere In Time" by <a href="/wiki/Cozy_Powell" title="Cozy Powell">Cozy Powell</a> – plays bass on this instrumental version of "Nothin' But Blue" from Cozy Powell's album <i>The Drums Are Back</i>.</li>
<li>1994: "Bushfire" by <a href="/wiki/Steve_Gregory" title="Steve Gregory">Steve Gregory</a> – plays bass on this track from the eponymous album.</li>
<li>1997: "That's The Way God Planned It" by SAS Band – plays bass on this track from their début (and only studio) album. Roger Taylor sings a verse as well on this cover track, originally recorded by <a href="/wiki/Billy_Preston" title="Billy Preston">Billy Preston</a>.</li>
</ul>
