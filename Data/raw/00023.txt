<p><b>Antoine de Saint-Exupéry</b> (<small>French: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_French" title="Help:IPA for French">[ɑ̃twan də sɛ̃tɛɡzypeʁi]</a></span>), officially <b>Antoine Marie Jean-Baptiste Roger, comte de Saint Exupéry</b><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006xi_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006xi-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeverson2004158_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeverson2004158-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>Note 1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>Note 2<span>]</span></a></sup> (29 June 1900 – 31 July 1944) was a French aristocrat, writer, poet, and pioneering aviator. He became a laureate of several of France's highest <a href="/wiki/Literary_award" title="Literary award">literary awards</a> and also won the U.S. <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_National_Book_Award_winners#1935_to_1941" title="List of National Book Award winners">National Book Award</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-1940.02.14_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-1940.02.14-9"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> He is best remembered for his novella <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a></i> (<i>Le Petit Prince</i>) and for his lyrical aviation writings, including <i><a href="/wiki/Wind,_Sand_and_Stars" title="Wind, Sand and Stars">Wind, Sand and Stars</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Night_Flight_(novel)" title="Night Flight (novel)">Night Flight</a></i>.</p>
<p>Saint-Exupéry was a successful commercial pilot before World War II, working <a href="/wiki/Airmail" title="Airmail">airmail</a> routes in Europe, Africa and South America. At the outbreak of war, he joined the <a href="/wiki/French_Air_Force" title="French Air Force">French Air Force</a> (<i>Armée de l'Air</i>), flying reconnaissance missions until <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armistice_with_France_(Second_Compi%C3%A8gne)" title="Armistice with France (Second Compiègne)">France's armistice with Germany</a> in 1940. After being demobilised from the French Air Force, he travelled to the United States to persuade its government to enter the war against <a href="/wiki/Nazi_Germany" title="Nazi Germany">Nazi Germany</a>. Following a 27-month hiatus in North America, during which he wrote three of his most important works, he joined the <a href="/wiki/Free_French_Air_Force" title="Free French Air Force">Free French Air Force</a> in North Africa, although he was far past the maximum age for such pilots and in declining health. He disappeared over the Mediterranean on his last assigned reconnaissance mission in July 1944, and is believed to have died at that time.</p>
<p>Prior to the war, Saint-Exupéry had achieved fame in France as an aviator. His literary works – among them <i>The Little Prince</i>, translated into over 250 languages and dialects – posthumously boosted his stature to national hero status in France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2005.04.03_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2005.04.03-10"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peak-2011.03_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peak-2011.03-11"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> He earned further widespread recognition with international translations of his other works. His 1939 philosophical memoir <i>Terre des hommes</i> became the name of a <a href="/wiki/Terre_des_hommes" title="Terre des hommes">major international humanitarian group</a>, and was also used <a href="/wiki/Expo_67#Montebello_conference_produces_theme" title="Expo 67">to create the central theme</a> (<i>Terre des hommes—Man and His World</i>) of the most successful world's fair of the 20th century, <a href="/wiki/Expo_67" title="Expo 67">Expo 67</a> in Montreal, Canada.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Berton_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-Berton-12"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Youth_and_aviation">Youth and aviation</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Youth and aviation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Birthplace_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry_1.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1957" data-file-width="3163" height="136" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Birthplace_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry_1.jpg/220px-Birthplace_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry_1.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Birthplace_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry_1.jpg/330px-Birthplace_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry_1.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Birthplace_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry_1.jpg/440px-Birthplace_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry_1.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Birthplace_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry_1.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Birthplace of Saint-Exupéry in the <a href="/wiki/Presqu%27%C3%AEle" title="Presqu'île">Presqu'île</a> section of <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a>, on the street now named after him, in blue at lower left.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Saint-Exupéry was born in <a href="/wiki/Lyon" title="Lyon">Lyon</a> to an aristocratic family that could trace its lineage back several centuries. He was the third of five children of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Countess" title="Countess">Countess</a> Marie de Fonscolombe and <a href="/wiki/Count" title="Count">Count</a> Jean de Saint Exupéry (1863–1904).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWebster199412_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWebster199412-13"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saint-Exup.C3.A9ry.com-b_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saint-Exup.C3.A9ry.com-b-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>Note 3<span>]</span></a></sup> His father, an executive of the <i>Le Soleil</i> (The Sun) insurance brokerage, died of a stroke in Lyon's <i>La Foux</i> train station before his son's fourth birthday. His father's death would greatly affect the entire family, transforming their status to that of 'impoverished aristocrats'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006ix_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006ix-17"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Saint-Exupéry was the third of five children, with three sisters and a younger blond-haired brother, François, who at age 15 would tragically die of rheumatic fever contracted while both were attending the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Marianist" title="Marianist">Marianist</a> <a href="/wiki/Villa_St._Jean_International_School" title="Villa St. Jean International School">College Villa St. Jean</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fribourg,_Switzerland" title="Fribourg, Switzerland">Fribourg, Switzerland</a>, during World War I. Saint-Exupéry attended to his brother, his closest confidant, beside François' death bed, and later wrote that François "...remained motionless for an instant. He did not cry out. He fell as gently as a [young] tree falls", imagery which would much later be recrafted into the climactic ending of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a></i>. At age 17, now the only "man" in the family following the death of his brother, the young author was left as distraught as his mother and sisters, but he soon assumed the mantle of a protector and took to consoling them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff199661.E2.80.9362_18-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff199661.E2.80.9362-18"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After twice failing his final exams at a preparatory <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Navale" title="École Navale">Naval Academy</a> (intentionally, some believe), Saint-Exupéry entered the <a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_des_Beaux-Arts" title="École des Beaux-Arts">École des Beaux-Arts</a> as an <a href="/wiki/Academic_audit" title="Academic audit">auditor</a> to study architecture for 15 months, again without graduating, and then fell into the habit of accepting odd jobs. In 1921, Saint-Exupéry began his military service with the <i>2e Régiment de <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chasseurs" title="Chasseurs">chasseurs</a> à cheval</i> (2nd Regiment of <a href="/wiki/Light_cavalry" title="Light cavalry">light cavalry</a>) and was sent to Neuhof, near <a href="/wiki/Strasbourg" title="Strasbourg">Strasbourg</a>. While there he took private flying lessons and the following year was offered a transfer from the French Army to the French Air Force. He received his <a href="/wiki/Aircrew_brevet" title="Aircrew brevet">pilot's wings</a> after being posted to the 37th Fighter Regiment in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Casablanca,_Morocco" title="Casablanca, Morocco">Casablanca, Morocco</a>. Later, being reposted to the 34th Aviation Regiment at <a href="/wiki/Le_Bourget" title="Le Bourget">Le Bourget</a> on the outskirts of Paris, and then experiencing the first of his many <a href="/wiki/Aviation_accidents_and_incidents" title="Aviation accidents and incidents">aircraft crashes</a>, Saint-Exupéry bowed to the objections of the family of his fiancée, future novelist <a href="/wiki/Louise_L%C3%A9v%C3%AAque_de_Vilmorin" title="Louise Lévêque de Vilmorin">Louise Lévêque de Vilmorin</a>, and left the air force to take an office job. The couple ultimately broke off their engagement and he worked at several more odd jobs without success over the next few years.</p>
<p>By 1926, Saint-Exupéry was flying again. He became one of the pioneers of international <a href="/wiki/Airmail" title="Airmail">postal flight</a>, in the days when aircraft had few instruments. Later he complained that those who flew the more advanced aircraft had become more like accountants than pilots. He worked for <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9ropostale_(aviation)" title="Aéropostale (aviation)">Aéropostale</a> between <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">Toulouse</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a>, and then also became the airline stopover manager for the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Juby" title="Cape Juby">Cape Juby</a> airfield in the Spanish zone of <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">South Morocco</a>, in the <a href="/wiki/Sahara" title="Sahara">Sahara</a> desert. His duties included negotiating the safe release of downed fliers taken hostage by hostile <a href="/wiki/Moors" title="Moors">Moors</a>, a perilous task which earned him his first <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%C3%A9gion_d%27honneur" title="Légion d'honneur">Légion d'honneur</a> from the French Government.</p>
<p>In 1929, Saint-Exupéry was transferred to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>, where he was appointed director of the <a href="/wiki/Aeroposta_Argentina" title="Aeroposta Argentina">Aeroposta Argentina</a> airline. He surveyed new air routes across South America, negotiated agreements, and even occasionally flew the airmail as well as search missions looking for downed fliers. This period of his life is briefly explored in <i><a href="/wiki/Wings_of_Courage" title="Wings of Courage">Wings of Courage</a></i>, an <a href="/wiki/IMAX" title="IMAX">IMAX</a> film by French director <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Annaud" title="Jean-Jacques Annaud">Jean-Jacques Annaud</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-1995.04.21_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-1995.04.21-19"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_career">Writing career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="326" data-file-width="450" height="159" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry.jpg/220px-Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry.jpg/330px-Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/68/Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry.jpg/440px-Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Saint-Exupéry photographed near <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Montreal,_Canada" title="Montreal, Canada">Montreal, Canada</a>, in May 1942, on a speaking tour in support of France after its armistice with Germany. Despite his appearance, he had been beset with stress and bedridden with <a href="/wiki/Cholecystitis" title="Cholecystitis">cholecystitis</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saint-Exup.C3.A9ry.com-a_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-Saint-Exup.C3.A9ry.com-a-20"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian-2010.04.09_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2010.04.09-21"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Saint-Exupéry's first novella, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/L%27Aviateur" title="L'Aviateur">L'Aviateur</a></i> (<i>The Aviator</i>), was published in a short-lived literary magazine <i>le Navire d'argent</i> (<i>The Silver Ship</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ibert_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ibert-22"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In 1929, his first book, <i><a href="/wiki/Courrier_sud_(novel)" title="Courrier sud (novel)">Courrier Sud</a></i> (<i>Southern Mail</i>) was published; his career as an aviator and journalist was about to begin. That same year, Saint-Exupéry flew the <a href="/wiki/Casablanca" title="Casablanca">Casablanca</a>—<a href="/wiki/Dakar" title="Dakar">Dakar</a> route.</p>
<p>The 1931 publication of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Vol_de_nuit" title="Vol de nuit">Vol de nuit</a></i> (<i>Night Flight</i>) established Saint-Exupéry as a rising star in the literary world. It was the first of his major works to gain widespread acclaim and won the <a href="/wiki/Prix_Femina" title="Prix Femina">prix Femina</a>. The novel mirrored his experiences as a mail pilot and director of the <a href="/wiki/Aeroposta_Argentina" title="Aeroposta Argentina">Aeroposta Argentina</a> airline, based in <a href="/wiki/Buenos_Aires" title="Buenos Aires">Buenos Aires</a>, <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentina</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff1994210_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff1994210-23"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> That same year, at <a href="/wiki/Grasse" title="Grasse">Grasse</a>, Saint-Exupéry married <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Consuelo_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry" title="Consuelo de Saint-Exupéry">Consuelo Suncin</a> (née Suncín Sandoval), a twice-widowed <a href="/wiki/El_Salvador" title="El Salvador">Salvadoran</a> writer and artist, who possessed a bohemian spirit and a "viper's tongue". Saint-Exupéry, thoroughly enchanted by the diminutive woman, would leave and then return to her many times – she was both his muse and, over the long term, the source of much of his angst.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Guardian-2000.06.24_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Guardian-2000.06.24-24"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> It was a stormy union, with Saint-Exupéry travelling frequently and indulging in numerous affairs, most notably with the Frenchwoman Hélène de Vogüé (1908–2003), known as "Nelly" and referred to as "Madame de B." in Saint-Exupéry biographies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Nelly_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Nelly-25"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>Note 4<span>]</span></a></sup> De Vogüé became Saint-Exupéry's literary <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Executrix" title="Executrix">executrix</a> after his death, and also wrote her own Saint-Exupéry biography under a pseudonym, Pierre Chevrier.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2000.06.25_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2000.06.25-28"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Saint-Exupéry continued to write until the spring of 1943, when he left the United States with American troops bound for North Africa in World War II.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Desert_crash">Desert crash</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Desert crash">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Tarfaya_Saint-Exupery.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1846" data-file-width="3030" height="134" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Tarfaya_Saint-Exupery.jpg/220px-Tarfaya_Saint-Exupery.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Tarfaya_Saint-Exupery.jpg/330px-Tarfaya_Saint-Exupery.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fb/Tarfaya_Saint-Exupery.jpg/440px-Tarfaya_Saint-Exupery.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Tarfaya_Saint-Exupery.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A monument in <a href="/wiki/Tarfaya" title="Tarfaya">Tarfaya</a>, <a href="/wiki/Cape_Juby" title="Cape Juby">Cape Juby</a>, <a href="/wiki/Morocco" title="Morocco">Morocco</a>, commemorating <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9ropostale_(aviation)" title="Aéropostale (aviation)">Aéropostale's</a> mail stopover station and Saint-Exupéry, its manager</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 30 December 1935, at 2:45 a.m., after 19 hours and 44 minutes in the air, Saint-Exupéry, along with his mechanic-navigator André Prévot, crashed in the Sahara desert.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff1996258_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff1996258-29"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> They were attempting to break the speed record in a Paris-to-Saigon air race (called a <i>raid</i>) and win a prize of 150,000 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Francs" title="Francs">francs</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schiff-1996a_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Schiff-1996a-30"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>Note 5<span>]</span></a></sup> The crash site is thought to have been near the <a href="/wiki/Wadi_El_Natrun" title="Wadi El Natrun">Wadi Natrun</a> valley, close to the <a href="/wiki/Nile_Delta" title="Nile Delta">Nile Delta</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff1996263_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff1996263-32"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Both Saint-Exupéry and Prévot miraculously survived the crash, only to face rapid dehydration in the intense desert heat. Their maps were primitive and ambiguous, leaving them with no idea of their location. Lost among the sand dunes, their sole supplies were strawberries, ten oranges, a thermos of sweet juice, chocolate, a handful of crackers, and a small ration of wine. The pair had only one day's worth of liquid.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006258_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006258-33"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> They both began to see mirages and experience auditory hallucinations, which were quickly followed by more vivid hallucinations. By the second and third day, they were so dehydrated that they stopped sweating altogether. Finally, on the fourth day, a <a href="/wiki/Bedouin" title="Bedouin">Bedouin</a> on a camel discovered them and administered a native rehydration treatment that saved their lives.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Schiff-1996a_30-1"><a href="#cite_note-Schiff-1996a-30"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> The near brush with death would figure prominently in his 1939 memoir, <i><a href="/wiki/Wind,_Sand_and_Stars" title="Wind, Sand and Stars">Wind, Sand and Stars</a></i>, winner of several awards. Saint-Exupéry's classic novella <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a></i>, which begins with a pilot being marooned in the desert, is, in part, a reference to this experience.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="American_and_Canadian_sojourn_and_The_Little_Prince">American and Canadian sojourn and <i>The Little Prince</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: American and Canadian sojourn and The Little Prince">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a></div>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Consuelo_en_1942_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="554" data-file-width="420" height="290" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Consuelo_en_1942_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al.jpg/220px-Consuelo_en_1942_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/c/ce/Consuelo_en_1942_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al.jpg/330px-Consuelo_en_1942_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/c/ce/Consuelo_en_1942_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Consuelo_en_1942_%C3%A0_Montr%C3%A9al.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The vain and petulant Rose in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a></i> was likely inspired by Saint-Exupéry's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salvadoran" title="Salvadoran">Salvadoran</a> wife, <a href="/wiki/Consuelo_de_Saint_Exup%C3%A9ry" title="Consuelo de Saint Exupéry">Consuelo de Saint Exupéry</a>.</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Following the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">German invasion of France in 1940</a>, Saint-Exupery flew a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloch_MB.174" title="Bloch MB.174">Bloch MB.174</a> with the GR II/33 reconnaissance squadron of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Arm%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air" title="Armée de l'Air">Armée de l'Air</a>.</p>
<p>After France's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armistice_with_France_(Second_Compi%C3%A8gne)" title="Armistice with France (Second Compiègne)">armistice with Germany</a>, Saint-Exupery went into exile in North America, escaping through Portugal. He arrived in New York on the last day of 1940, with the intention of convincing the U.S. to enter the conflict against Nazi Germany quickly.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff1996331_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff1996331-34"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> On 14 January 1941, at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hotel_Astor" title="Hotel Astor">Hotel Astor</a> author luncheon attended by approximately 1,500, he belatedly received his <a href="/wiki/National_Book_Award" title="National Book Award">National Book Award</a> for <i><a href="/wiki/Wind,_Sand_and_Stars" title="Wind, Sand and Stars">Wind, Sand and Stars</a></i>, won a year earlier while he was occupied witnessing the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">destruction of the French Army</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-1941.01.15_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-1941.01.15-35"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Consuelo followed him to New York several months later after a chaotic migration to the southern French town of <a href="/wiki/Opp%C3%A8de" title="Oppède">Oppède</a>, where she lived in an artist's commune, the basis of her autobiography, <i>Kingdom of the Rocks: Memories of Oppède</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SaintExup.C3.A9ryConsuelo_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-SaintExup.C3.A9ryConsuelo-36"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff1996338_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff1996338-37"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Between January 1941 and April 1943, the Saint-Exupérys lived in New York City's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Central_Park_South" title="Central Park South">Central Park South</a> in twin penthouse apartments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEDunning1989_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEDunning1989-38"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> as well as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Bevin_House" title="The Bevin House">The Bevin House</a> mansion in <a href="/wiki/Asharoken,_New_York" title="Asharoken, New York">Asharoken</a> on <a href="/wiki/Long_Island" title="Long Island">Long Island, New York</a> and a townhouse on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Beekman_Place_(Manhattan)" title="Beekman Place (Manhattan)">Beekman Place</a> in <a href="/wiki/Manhattan" title="Manhattan">Manhattan</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECotsalas2000_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECotsalas2000-39"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Some researchers have implied that during his stay in the United States, Saint-Exupéry became intimate with <a href="/wiki/Anne_Morrow_Lindbergh" title="Anne Morrow Lindbergh">Anne Morrow Lindbergh</a>, wife of the famous American aviator <a href="/wiki/Charles_Lindbergh" title="Charles Lindbergh">Charles Lindbergh</a>. Ironically, while his intention was to convince the U.S. Government of the need to fight fascism, both Anne and her husband Charles were strongly advocating against American intervention in the European war. Anne wrote a 41-page booklet, <i>The Wave of the Future</i>, in support of her husband, who was lobbying for a U.S.–German peace treaty similar to Stalin's treaty with Hitler. The Roosevelt administration subsequently attacked <i>The Wave of the Future</i> as "the bible of every American Nazi, Fascist, <a href="/wiki/German_American_Bund" title="German American Bund">Bundist</a> and Appeaser", and the booklet became one of the most despised writings of the period.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent-2001.02.15_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-2001.02.15-40"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum,_Le_Bourget,_Paris,_France,_absent_artwork_and_not_subject_to_copyright.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1704" data-file-width="2272" height="165" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France%2C_absent_artwork_and_not_subject_to_copyright.jpg/220px-Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France%2C_absent_artwork_and_not_subject_to_copyright.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France%2C_absent_artwork_and_not_subject_to_copyright.jpg/330px-Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France%2C_absent_artwork_and_not_subject_to_copyright.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/17/Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France%2C_absent_artwork_and_not_subject_to_copyright.jpg/440px-Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France%2C_absent_artwork_and_not_subject_to_copyright.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

</div>
<p>With further irony, Saint-Exupéry and Charles Lindbergh both became <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_P-38_Lightning" title="Lockheed P-38 Lightning">P-38</a> pilots during World War II, with a disgraced Lindbergh fighting surreptitiously in the <a href="/wiki/Pacific_War" title="Pacific War">Pacific War</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PBS_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-PBS-41"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> and with Saint-Exupéry fighting and dying very publicly over the Mediterranean.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time-1944.08.14_42-0"><a href="#cite_note-Time-1944.08.14-42"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>It was after Saint-Exupéry's arrival in the United States that the author adopted the hyphen within his surname, as he was annoyed with Americans addressing him as "Mr. Exupéry".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006xi_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006xi-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> It was also during this period that he authored <i>Pilote de guerre (<a href="/wiki/Flight_to_Arras" title="Flight to Arras">Flight to Arras</a>)</i>, which earned widespread acclaim, and <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lettre_%C3%A0_un_otage" title="fr:Lettre à un otage"><i>Lettre à un otage</i></a> (<i>Letter to a Hostage</i>), dedicated to the 40 million French <a href="/wiki/German_military_administration_in_occupied_France_during_World_War_II" title="German military administration in occupied France during World War II">living under Nazi oppression</a>, plus numerous shorter pieces in support of France. The Saint-Exupérys also resided in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Quebec_City,_Canada" title="Quebec City, Canada">Quebec City, Canada</a> for several weeks during the late spring of 1942, during which time they met a precocious eight-year-old boy with blond curly hair, Thomas, the son of philosopher <a href="/wiki/Charles_De_Koninck" title="Charles De Koninck">Charles De Koninck</a>, with whom the Saint-Exupérys resided.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006379_43-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006379-43"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEBrown2004_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEBrown2004-44"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>Note 6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After he returned from his stay in Quebec, which had been fraught with illness and stress, the French wife of one of his publishers helped persuade Saint-Exupéry to produce a children's book,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff1996278_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff1996278-46"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> hoping to calm his nerves and also compete with the new series of <i><a href="/wiki/Mary_Poppins" title="Mary Poppins">Mary Poppins</a></i> stories by <a href="/wiki/P._L._Travers" title="P. L. Travers">P.L. Travers</a>. Saint-Exupéry wrote and illustrated <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a></i> in New York City and the village of <a href="/wiki/Asharoken,_New_York" title="Asharoken, New York">Asharoken</a> in mid-to-late 1942, with the manuscript being completed in October.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006379_43-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006379-43"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> It would be first published months later in early 1943 in both English and French in the United States, and would only later appear in his native homeland posthumously after the <a href="/wiki/Western_Front_(World_War_II)#Liberation_of_France" title="Western Front (World War II)">liberation of France</a>, as his works had been banned by the collaborationist <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy Regime</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeverson2004166.2C_171_47-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeverson2004166.2C_171-47"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff1996366_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff1996366-48"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>Note 7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Return_to_war">Return to war</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Return to war">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In April 1943, following his 27 months in North America, Saint-Exupéry departed with an American military convoy for <a href="/wiki/Algiers" title="Algiers">Algiers</a>, to fly with the <a href="/wiki/Free_French_Air_Force" title="Free French Air Force">Free French Air Force</a> and fight with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_Allies" title="Western Allies">Allies</a> in a Mediterranean-based squadron. Then 43, soon to be promoted to the rank of commandant (major), he was far older than most men in operational units. Although eight years over the age limit for such pilots, he had petitioned endlessly for an exemption which had finally been approved by General <a href="/wiki/Dwight_D._Eisenhower" title="Dwight D. Eisenhower">Dwight Eisenhower</a>. However, Saint-Exupéry had been suffering pain and immobility due to his many previous crash injuries, to the extent that he could not dress himself in his own <a href="/wiki/Flight_suit" title="Flight suit">flight suit</a> or even turn his head leftwards to check for enemy aircraft.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006180_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006180-51"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Saint-Exupéry was assigned with a number of other pilots to <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_P-38_Lightning" title="Lockheed P-38 Lightning">F-5 Lightnings</a>, which an officer described as "war-weary, non-airworthy craft".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECate1970_52-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECate1970-52"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> The Lightnings were also more sophisticated than models he previously flew, requiring him to undertake seven weeks of stringent training before his first mission. After wrecking a P-38 through engine failure on his second mission, he was grounded for eight months, but was then later reinstated to flight duty on the personal intervention of <a href="/wiki/Ira_C._Eaker" title="Ira C. Eaker">General Ira Eaker</a>, Deputy Commander of the U.S. Army Air Forces.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006423_53-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006423-53"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time-1944.08.14_42-1"><a href="#cite_note-Time-1944.08.14-42"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-55"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>Note 8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After Saint-Exupéry resumed flying, he also returned to his longtime habit of reading and writing while flying his single seat F-5B (a <a href="/wiki/Reconnaissance_aircraft" title="Reconnaissance aircraft">specially configured</a> P-38 reconnaissance variant). His prodigious studies of literature gripped him, and on occasion he continued his readings of literary works until moments before takeoff, with mechanics having <a href="/wiki/Run-up_(aviation)" title="Run-up (aviation)">warmed up and tested</a> his aircraft for him in preparation for his flight. On one flight, to the chagrin of his colleagues awaiting his arrival, he circled the airport for an hour after returning, so that he could finish reading a novel. Saint-Exupéry frequently flew with a lined notebook (<i>carnet</i>) during his long solitary flights, and some of his philosophical writings were created during such periods when he could reflect on the world below him.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006_56-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006-56"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Disappearance">Disappearance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Disappearance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Prior to his return to flight duties with his squadron in North Africa, the collaborationist <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy Regime</a> unilaterally promoted Saint-Exupéry as one of its members – quite a shock to the author. Subsequently, French General (later, French President) <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a>, whom Saint-Exupéry and others held in low regard, publicly implied that the author-pilot was supporting Germany. Depressed at this, he began to drink heavily.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC-2004.08.07_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-2004.08.07-57"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, his health, both physically and mentally, had been deteriorating. Saint-Exupéry was said to be intermittently subject to depression and there was discussion of taking him off flying status.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006430.E2.80.93433.2C_436.E2.80.93437_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006430.E2.80.93433.2C_436.E2.80.93437-58"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>Note 9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Saint-Exupéry's last assigned reconnaissance mission was to collect intelligence on German troop movements in and around the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rhone" title="Rhone">Rhone</a> Valley preceding the <a href="/wiki/Operation_Dragoon" title="Operation Dragoon">Allied invasion of southern France</a> ("Operation Dragoon"). Although he had been reinstated to his old squadron with the provision that he was to fly only five missions,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006430_60-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006430-60"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> on 31 July 1944, he took off in an unarmed P-38 on his ninth reconnaissance mission from an airbase on <a href="/wiki/Corsica" title="Corsica">Corsica</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>Note 10<span>]</span></a></sup> To the great alarm of the squadron compatriots who revered him, he did not return, dramatically vanishing without a trace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006402.E2.80.93451_63-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006402.E2.80.93451-63"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-64"><a href="#cite_note-64"><span>[</span>Note 11<span>]</span></a></sup> Word of his disappearance shortly spread across the literary world and then into international headlines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006434.E2.80.93438_65-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006434.E2.80.93438-65"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Time-1944.08.14_42-2"><a href="#cite_note-Time-1944.08.14-42"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> A French woman reported much later having watched an aircraft crash around noon near the Bay of <a href="/wiki/Carqueiranne" title="Carqueiranne">Carqueiranne</a> off <a href="/wiki/Toulon" title="Toulon">Toulon</a>. An unidentifiable body wearing French uniform was found several days after his disappearance, east of the <a href="/wiki/Frioul_archipelago" title="Frioul archipelago">Frioul archipelago</a> south of <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>, and buried in Carqueiranne in September.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Discovery_at_sea">Discovery at sea</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Discovery at sea">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Gourmette_de_Saint_Exupery.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="668" data-file-width="884" height="166" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Gourmette_de_Saint_Exupery.jpg/220px-Gourmette_de_Saint_Exupery.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Gourmette_de_Saint_Exupery.jpg/330px-Gourmette_de_Saint_Exupery.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/97/Gourmette_de_Saint_Exupery.jpg/440px-Gourmette_de_Saint_Exupery.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Gourmette_de_Saint_Exupery.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Bracelet of Saint-Exupéry found in 1998</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In September 1998, Jean-Claude Bianco, a fisherman, found east of Riou Island, south of Marseille, a silver identity bracelet (<i>gourmette</i>) bearing the names of Saint-Exupéry and of his wife <a href="/wiki/Consuelo_de_Saint_Exup%C3%A9ry" title="Consuelo de Saint Exupéry">Consuelo</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDNN_66-0"><a href="#cite_note-CDNN-66"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup> and his American publisher, <a href="/wiki/Reynal_%26_Hitchcock" title="Reynal &amp; Hitchcock">Reynal &amp; Hitchcock</a>, hooked to a piece of fabric, presumably from his flight suit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2000.06.25_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2000.06.25-28"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The recovery of his bracelet was an emotionally-laden event in France, where Saint-Exupéry had by then assumed the mantle of a national icon, and some disputed its authenticity as it was found far from his intended flight path, implying that the aircraft may not have been shot down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Independent-2000.05.28_67-0"><a href="#cite_note-Independent-2000.05.28-67"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In May 2000, Luc Vanrell, a diver, found the partial remains of a Lockheed P-38 Lightning spread over thousands of square metres of the seabed off the coast of Marseille, near to where the bracelet was previously found. The discovery galvanized the country, which for decades had conducted searches for his aircraft and speculated on Saint-Exupéry's fate.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AgenceFrancePress-2004.04.07_68-0"><a href="#cite_note-AgenceFrancePress-2004.04.07-68"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> After a two-year delay imposed by the French government, the remnants of the aircraft were recovered in October 2003.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CDNN_66-1"><a href="#cite_note-CDNN-66"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-70"><a href="#cite_note-70"><span>[</span>Note 12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum,_Le_Bourget,_Paris,_France_(12).JPG"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2232" data-file-width="1205" height="315" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France_%2812%29.JPG/170px-Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France_%2812%29.JPG" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France_%2812%29.JPG/255px-Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France_%2812%29.JPG 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/8f/Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France_%2812%29.JPG/340px-Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum%2C_Le_Bourget%2C_Paris%2C_France_%2812%29.JPG 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Saint_Exupery_exhibit_-_Air_%26_Space_Museum,_Le_Bourget,_Paris,_France_(12).JPG" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
A landing gear of Saint-Exupéry's P-38 Lightning, recovered from the Mediterranean off the coast of France in 2003, displayed at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="French Air and Space Museum">French Air and Space Museum</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>On 7 April 2004, Patrick Granjean, head of the <a href="/wiki/Minister_of_Culture_(France)" title="Minister of Culture (France)">French Ministry of Culture</a>, Captain Frederic Solano of the <a href="/wiki/French_Air_Force" title="French Air Force">French Air Force</a>, plus investigators from the French <a href="/wiki/Maritime_archaeology" title="Maritime archaeology">Underwater Archaeological Department</a> confirmed that the remnants of the crash wreckage were, indeed, from Saint-Exupéry's P-38 <a href="/wiki/Lockheed_P-38_Lightning#Variants" title="Lockheed P-38 Lightning">F-5B reconnaissance variant</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AgenceFrancePress-2004.04.07_68-1"><a href="#cite_note-AgenceFrancePress-2004.04.07-68"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Globe.26Mail-2004.04.08_71-0"><a href="#cite_note-Globe.26Mail-2004.04.08-71"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>No marks or holes attributable to gunfire were found; however, that was not considered significant as only a small portion of the aircraft was recovered.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aero-relic_69-1"><a href="#cite_note-Aero-relic-69"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> In June 2004, the fragments were given to the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mus%C3%A9e_de_l%27Air_et_de_l%27Espace" title="Musée de l'Air et de l'Espace">Air and Space Museum</a> in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_%E2%80%93_Le_Bourget_Airport" title="Paris – Le Bourget Airport">Le Bourget, Paris</a>, where Saint-Exupéry's life is commemorated in a special exhibit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeMonde-2008-03-15_72-0"><a href="#cite_note-LeMonde-2008-03-15-72"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MuseeAireSpace.fr-a_73-0"><a href="#cite_note-MuseeAireSpace.fr-a-73"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The location of the crash site and the bracelet are less than 80 km by sea from where the unidentified French serviceman was found in <a href="/wiki/Carqueiranne" title="Carqueiranne">Carqueiranne</a>, and it remains plausible, but has not been confirmed, that the body was carried there by sea currents after the crash over the course of several days.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Speculations_in_1948_and_2008">Speculations in 1948 and 2008</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Speculations in 1948 and 2008">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1948, former <a href="/wiki/Luftwaffe" title="Luftwaffe">Luftwaffe</a> telegrapher Rev. Hermann Korth made public his war logs, noting an instance on July 31 around midday where a <a href="/wiki/Focke-Wulf_Fw_190" title="Focke-Wulf Fw 190">Focke-Wulf Fw 190</a> downed a P-38 Lightning, which ostensibly supported a shoot-down hypothesis for Saint-Exupery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheWingedLife_74-0"><a href="#cite_note-TheWingedLife-74"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Triebel_75-0"><a href="#cite_note-Triebel-75"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup> Though popular in the 1960s and 70s, Korth’s log was met with skepticism, as it described aerial combat, which was dissonant with the fact that Saint-Exupery’s plane was unarmed.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TheWingedLife_74-1"><a href="#cite_note-TheWingedLife-74"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> Evidence exists, however, of a P-38 flown by Second Lieutenant Gene Meredith downed in combat on July 30 around the same location.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AeroJournal-2008.No4_76-0"><a href="#cite_note-AeroJournal-2008.No4-76"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-78"><a href="#cite_note-78"><span>[</span>Note 13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Luftwaffe reconnaissance pilot Robert Heichele was shot down on 16 August 1944 and died five days later; he is buried in the German military cemetery at Dagneux, France.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Volksbund.de_79-0"><a href="#cite_note-Volksbund.de-79"><span>[</span>66<span>]</span></a></sup> Issue No. 725 of the German magazine "Der Landser" quoted a purported letter from Heichele in which he claimed to have shot down a P-38 on 31 July 1944. The credibility of this letter is suspect because it claims that Heichele and his wingman were flying the Focke-Wulf Fw 190 D-9, a variant which did not equip his unit, 2./Nahaufklärungsgruppe 13, and had not yet entered Luftwaffe service.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Urbanke_80-0"><a href="#cite_note-Urbanke-80"><span>[</span>67<span>]</span></a></sup> The letter also claims that the hostile aircraft initiated an attack on the Germans whereas Saint-Exupéry was flying an unarmed F-5 reconnaissance variant. In the lists held by the Bundesarchiv-Militärarchiv no victory is accredited to Heichele or his unit in July or August 1944; the decrypted report of the day's reconnaissance missions by the parent formation, Fliegerdivision 2 does not include any flights by 2./NAG 13's Fw 190s.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-UK_National_Archives_81-0"><a href="#cite_note-UK_National_Archives-81"><span>[</span>68<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, a French journalist from <i>La Provence</i>, investigating Saint-Exupery's death, contacted hundreds of former Luftwaffe pilots, eventually getting an account from Horst Rippert, who believed he shot down Saint-Exupery.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LeMonde-2008-03-15_72-1"><a href="#cite_note-LeMonde-2008-03-15-72"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTETagliabue2006_82-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTETagliabue2006-82"><span>[</span>69<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBCWorld_83-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBCWorld-83"><span>[</span>70<span>]</span></a></sup> After the war, Rippert's memoirs expressed both fears and doubts that he was responsible, but in 2003 he stated he became certain when he learned the location of Saint-Exupery’s wreckage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2008.4.10_84-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2008.4.10-84"><span>[</span>71<span>]</span></a></sup> Rippert stated he reported the kill over his radio, but no documents exist to verify his account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Triebel_75-1"><a href="#cite_note-Triebel-75"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AeroJournal-2008.No4_76-1"><a href="#cite_note-AeroJournal-2008.No4-76"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-85"><a href="#cite_note-85"><span>[</span>Note 14<span>]</span></a></sup> Former Luftwaffe comrades reacted with skepticism to Rippert's claim, as he held it private for 64 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEAltweg2008_86-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEAltweg2008-86"><span>[</span>72<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Spiegel_2008.03.22_87-0"><a href="#cite_note-Spiegel_2008.03.22-87"><span>[</span>73<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-88"><a href="#cite_note-88"><span>[</span>Note 15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Two French and German books discuss the alleged Saint-Exupery shoot-down.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AgenceFrancePresse-2008.03.15_89-0"><a href="#cite_note-AgenceFrancePresse-2008.03.15-89"><span>[</span>74<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Reuters-2008.03.16_90-0"><a href="#cite_note-Reuters-2008.03.16-90"><span>[</span>75<span>]</span></a></sup> Very little German documentation survived the war, and contemporary archival sources, consisting of allied intercepts of Luftwaffe signals, offer no evidence to verify Rippert’s account.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEB.C3.B6nischLeick2008_91-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEB.C3.B6nischLeick2008-91"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-JanBobek_92-0"><a href="#cite_note-JanBobek-92"><span>[</span>77<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEB.C3.B6nischLeick2008_91-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEB.C3.B6nischLeick2008-91"><span>[</span>76<span>]</span></a></sup> Thus, Rippert’s claim remains a hypothesis until proven otherwise, and the cause of Saint-Exupery’s death remains unsolved.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AeroJournal-2008.No4_76-3"><a href="#cite_note-AeroJournal-2008.No4-76"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VonGartzen_93-0"><a href="#cite_note-VonGartzen-93"><span>[</span>78<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_works">Literary works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Literary works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>While not precisely autobiographical, much of Saint-Exupéry's work is inspired by his experiences as a pilot. One notable example is his novella, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a>,</i> a poetic tale self-illustrated in watercolours in which a pilot stranded in the desert meets a young prince fallen to Earth from a tiny <a href="/wiki/Asteroid" title="Asteroid">asteroid</a>. <i>The Little Prince</i> is a philosophical story, including societal criticism, remarking on the strangeness of the adult world. One biographer wrote of his most famous work: "Rarely have an author and a character been so intimately bound together as Antoine de Saint-Exupéry and his Little Prince," and remarking of their dual fates, "...the two remain tangled together, twin innocents who fell from the sky." <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2000.06.25_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2000.06.25-28"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Saint-Exupéry's notable literary works (published English translations in brackets) are constituted by:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PetitPrince.net-b_94-0"><a href="#cite_note-PetitPrince.net-b-94"><span>[</span>79<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_works">Other works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Other works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>During the 1930s, Saint-Exupéry led a mixed life as an aviator, journalist, author and publicist for <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a>, <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9ropostale_(aviation)" title="Aéropostale (aviation)">Aéropostale's</a> successor. His journalistic writings for <i><a href="/wiki/Paris-Soir" title="Paris-Soir">Paris-Soir</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Marianne_(magazine)" title="Marianne (magazine)">Marianne</a></i> and other newspapers covered events in <a href="/wiki/Indochina" title="Indochina">Indochina</a> and the Far East (1934), the Mediterranean, Soviet Union and Moscow (1935), and the <a href="/wiki/Spanish_Civil_War" title="Spanish Civil War">Spanish Civil War</a> (1936–1937). Saint-Exupéry additionally wrote a number of shorter pieces, essays and commentaries for various other newspapers and magazines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaint-Exup.C3.A9ry1965_104-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaint-Exup.C3.A9ry1965-104"><span>[</span>87<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Censorship_and_publication_bans">Censorship and publication bans</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Censorship and publication bans">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Pilote de guerre</i> (<i>Flight To Arras</i>), describing the German invasion of France, was slightly censored when it was released in its original French in his homeland, by removing a derogatory remark made of Hitler (which French publisher <a href="/wiki/%C3%89ditions_Gallimard" title="Éditions Gallimard">Gallimard</a> failed to reinsert in subsequent editions after World War II). However, shortly after the book's release in France, Nazi appeasers and Vichy supporters objected to its praise of one of Saint-Exupéry's squadron colleagues, Captain <a href="/wiki/Jean_Isra%C3%ABl" title="Jean Israël">Jean Israël</a>, who was portrayed as being amongst the squadron's bravest defenders during the <a href="/wiki/Battle_of_France" title="Battle of France">Battle of France</a>. In support of their German occupiers and masters, Vichy authorities attacked the author as a defender of Jews (in racist terms) leading to the praised book being banned in France, along with prohibitions against further printings of Saint-Exupéry's other works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff1996366_48-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff1996366-48"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Prior to <a href="/wiki/Military_history_of_France_during_World_War_II#Campaign_of_France_.281944.E2.80.931945.29" title="Military history of France during World War II">France's liberation</a> new printings of Saint-Exupéry's works were made available there only by means of covert print runs,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff1996366_48-2"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff1996366-48"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESeverson2004166.2C_171_47-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESeverson2004166.2C_171-47"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> such as that of February 1943 when 1,000 copies of an <a href="/wiki/Resistance_during_World_War_II" title="Resistance during World War II">underground</a> version of <i>Pilote de guerre</i> were printed in Lyon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PetitPrince.net-c_107-0"><a href="#cite_note-PetitPrince.net-c-107"><span>[</span>89<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A further complication occurred due to Saint-Exupéry's and others' view of General <a href="/wiki/Charles_de_Gaulle" title="Charles de Gaulle">Charles de Gaulle</a>, who was held in low regard. Early in the war, de Gaulle became the leader of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Free_French_Forces" title="Free French Forces">Free French Forces</a> in exile, with his headquarters in London. Even though both men were working to free France from Nazi occupation, Saint-Exupéry viewed de Gaulle with apprehension as a possible post-war dictator, and consequently provided no public support to the General. In response, de Gaulle struck back at the author by implying that the author was a German supporter, and then had his literary works banned in <a href="/wiki/Resistance_during_World_War_II" title="Resistance during World War II">France's North African colonies</a>. Saint-Exupéry's writings were, with irony, banned simultaneously in both <a href="/wiki/German_military_administration_in_occupied_France_during_World_War_II" title="German military administration in occupied France during World War II">occupied France</a> and <a href="/wiki/Free_France" title="Free France">Free France</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2000.06.25_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2000.06.25-28"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff1996414_108-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff1996414-108"><span>[</span>90<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Extension_of_copyrights_in_France">Extension of copyrights in France</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Extension of copyrights in France">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to Saint-Exupéry's wartime death, his estate received the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Napoleonic_code" title="Napoleonic code">civil code</a> designation <i><a href="/wiki/Mort_pour_la_France" title="Mort pour la France">Mort pour la France</a></i> (English: <i>Died for France</i>), which was applied by the French Government in 1948. Amongst the law's provisions is an increase of 30 years to the duration of the original copyright's duration of 70 years;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Celog.fr_109-0"><a href="#cite_note-Celog.fr-109"><span>[</span>91<span>]</span></a></sup> thus most of Saint-Exupéry's creative works will not fall out of copyright status in France for an extra 30 years.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006438_110-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006438-110"><span>[</span>92<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<li>Saint-Exupéry is commemorated with an inscription in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panth%C3%A9on_(Paris)" title="Panthéon (Paris)">Panthéon</a> in Paris, France's repository of historical greats. Although his body was never identified, his name was added to the Panthéon in November 1967 by a French legislative act. The inscription reads: "<i>A LA MÉMOIRE DE • ANTOINE DE SAINT EXUPERY • POÈTE ROMANCIER AVIATEUR • DISPARU AU COURS D'UNE MISSION • DE RECONNAISSANCE AÉRIENNE • LE 31 JUILLET 1944</i>" (To the memory of Antoine de Saint Exupery, poet, novelist, aviator, missing during an aerial reconnaissance mission, 31 July 1944). Amongst other honours from France, he was named a <a href="/wiki/Legion_of_Honour" title="Legion of Honour">Chevalier de la Légion d'honneur</a> in April 1931, promoted to an Officier de la Légion d'honneur in January 1939 and also posthumously awarded the <a href="/wiki/Croix_de_guerre" title="Croix de guerre">Croix de guerre avec Palme</a> in 1944.</li>
<li>From 1993 until the introduction of the <a href="/wiki/Euro" title="Euro">euro</a>, Saint-Exupéry's portrait and several of his drawings from <i>The Little Prince</i> appeared on France's <a href="/wiki/French_franc#Banknotes" title="French franc">50-franc banknote</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2000.06.25_28-4"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2000.06.25-28"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> The French Government also later minted a 100-franc commemorative coin, with Saint-Exupéry on its <a href="/wiki/Obverse_and_reverse" title="Obverse and reverse">obverse side</a>, and the Little Prince on its reverse. Brass-plated souvenir <i>Monnaie de Paris</i> commemorative medallions were also created in his honour, depicting the pilot's portrait over the P-38 Lightning aircraft he last flew.</li>
<li>In 1999, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Government_of_Qu%C3%A9bec" title="Government of Québec">Government of Quebec</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Qu%C3%A9bec_City" title="Québec City">Quebec City</a> added a historical marker to the family home of <a href="/wiki/Charles_De_Koninck" title="Charles De Koninck">Charles De Koninck</a>, head of the Department of Philosophy at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Universit%C3%A9_Laval" title="Université Laval">Université Laval</a>, where the Saint-Exupérys stayed while lecturing in Canada for several weeks during May and June 1942.</li>
<li>In 2000, on the centenary of his birth, in the city where he was born, the Lyon Satolas Airport was renamed the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lyon-Saint_Exup%C3%A9ry_Airport" title="Lyon-Saint Exupéry Airport">Lyon-Saint Exupéry Airport</a> in his honour. Lyon's <a href="/wiki/TGV" title="TGV">TGV</a> bullet train station was also renamed as <a href="/wiki/Gare_de_Lyon_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry" title="Gare de Lyon Saint-Exupéry">Gare de Lyon Saint-Exupéry</a>. The author is additionally commemorated by a statue in Lyon, depicting a seated Saint-Exupéry with the little prince standing behind him.</li>
<li>A street in <a href="/wiki/Montesson" title="Montesson">Montesson</a>, a suburb of Paris, is named for him as Rue Antoine de Saint-Exupéry.</li>
</ul>

<p>Museum exhibits, exhibitions and theme villages dedicated to both him and his diminutive Little Prince have been created in <a href="/wiki/Le_Bourget" title="Le Bourget">Le Bourget, Paris</a> and other locations in France, as well as in the Republic of South Korea, Japan, Morocco, Brazil, the United States and Canada.</p>
<ul>
<li>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_Air_and_Space_Museum" title="French Air and Space Museum">Air and Space Museum</a> at Paris's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paris_%E2%80%93_Le_Bourget_Airport" title="Paris – Le Bourget Airport">Le Bourget Airport</a>, in cooperation with The Estate of Saint-Exupery-d'Agay, have created a permanent exhibit of 300 m² dedicated to the author, pilot, person and humanist. The <i>Espace Saint Exupéry</i> exhibit, officially inaugurated on the aviator's birthday in 2006,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Basse_111-0"><a href="#cite_note-Basse-111"><span>[</span>93<span>]</span></a></sup> traces each stage of his life as an airmail pioneer, eclectic intellectual-artist and military pilot. It includes artifacts from his life: photographs, his drawings, letters, some of his original notebooks (<i>carnets</i>) he scribbled in voluminously and which were later published posthumously, plus <a href="/wiki/Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry#Discovery_at_sea" title="Antoine de Saint-Exupéry">remnants of the unarmed P-38</a> he flew on his last reconnaissance mission and which were recovered from the Mediterranean Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-MAE-SaintEx_112-0"><a href="#cite_note-MAE-SaintEx-112"><span>[</span>94<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/Tarfaya" title="Tarfaya">Tarfaya, Morocco</a>, next to the <a href="/wiki/Cape_Juby" title="Cape Juby">Cape Juby</a> airfield where Saint-Exupéry was based as an <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9ropostale_(aviation)" title="Aéropostale (aviation)">Aéropostale</a> airmail pilot/station manager, an exhibit was created honouring both him and the company. A small monument at the airfield is also dedicated to them.</li>
<li>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Gyeonggi-do" title="Gyeonggi-do">Gyeonggi-do, South Korea</a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hakone" title="Hakone">Hakone, Japan</a>, theme village museums have been created honouring Saint-Exupéry's <i>Little Prince</i>.</li>
<li>In January 1995, the <a href="/wiki/Alberta_Aviation_Museum" title="Alberta Aviation Museum">Alberta Aviation Museum</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Edmonton,_Alberta" title="Edmonton, Alberta">Edmonton, Alberta</a>, Canada, in conjunction with the cultural organization <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alliance_fran%C3%A7aise" title="Alliance française">Alliance française</a>, presented a showing of Saint-Exupéry letters, watercolours, sketches and photographs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-EdmontonJournal-1995.01.17_113-0"><a href="#cite_note-EdmontonJournal-1995.01.17-113"><span>[</span>95<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In <a href="/wiki/S%C3%A3o_Paulo" title="São Paulo">São Paulo</a>, Brazil, through 2009, the Oca Art Exhibition Centre presented Saint-Exupéry and The Little Prince as part of The Year of France and The Little Prince. The displays covered over 10,000 m² on four floors, and chronicled Saint-Exupéry, <i>The Little Prince</i> and their philosophies, as visitors passed through theme areas of the desert, asteroids, stars and the cosmos. The ground floor of the giant exhibition was laid out as a huge map of the routes flown by the author with <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9ropostale_(aviation)" title="Aéropostale (aviation)">Aeropostale</a> in South America and around the world. Also included was a full scale replica of the author's crashed <a href="/wiki/Caudron_Simoun" title="Caudron Simoun">Caudron Simoun</a>, lying wrecked on the ground of a simulated Libyan desert following his disastrous Paris-Saigon race attempt. The miraculous survival of Saint-Exupéry and his mechanic/navigator was subsequently chronicled in the award-winning memoir <i><a href="/wiki/Wind,_Sand_and_Stars" title="Wind, Sand and Stars">Wind, Sand and Stars</a></i> (<i>Terre des hommes</i>), and also formed the introduction of his most famous work <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a></i> (<i>Le Petit Prince</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-LittlePrince.com-a_114-0"><a href="#cite_note-LittlePrince.com-a-114"><span>[</span>96<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In 2011, the <a href="/wiki/Toulouse" title="Toulouse">City of Toulouse, France</a>, home of <a href="/wiki/Airbus" title="Airbus">Airbus</a> and the pioneering airmail carrier <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9ropostale_(aviation)" title="Aéropostale (aviation)">Aéropostale</a>, in conjunction with the Estate of Saint-Exupery-d'Agay and the Youth Foundation of Antoine de Saint-Exupéry, hosted a major exposition on Saint-Exupéry and his experience with Aéropostale. The exposition, titled <i>L'année Antoine de Saint-Exupéry à Toulouse</i>, exhibited selected personal artifacts of the author-aviator, including gloves, photos, posters, maps, manuscripts, drawings, models of the aircraft he flew, some of the wreckage from his Sahara Desert plane crash, and the personal silver identification bracelet engraved with his and Consuelo's name, presented by his U.S. publisher, which was recovered from his last, ultimate crash site in the Mediterranean Sea.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toulouse-a_115-0"><a href="#cite_note-Toulouse-a-115"><span>[</span>97<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>On 27 February 2012, Russia's <a href="/wiki/Ulyanovsk_State_University" title="Ulyanovsk State University">Ulyanovsk State University</a> inaugurated its new International Saint-Exupéry Centre, led by its Director, Elena Mironova, an associate professor of French. The center will serve as a permanent museum dedicated to the author-aviator, as well as a cultural and linguistics center for the university. The museum was established with the assistance of Civil Aviation College teacher Nikolai Yatsenko, an author of 12 publications on Saint-Exupéry who personally donated some 6,000 related items. Located in <a href="/wiki/Ulyanovsk" title="Ulyanovsk">Ulyanovsk</a>, the university's new center will also help support the study of international languages in a city which promotes itself as a major aerospace and cultural centre. The centre's opening was attended by Ulyanovsk Governor <a href="/wiki/Sergey_Ivanovich_Morozov" title="Sergey Ivanovich Morozov">Sergey Morozov</a>, Sergey Krasnov of the Ulyanovsk Civil Aviation College and other aerospace and academic dignitaries, as well as Veronique Jober, <a href="/wiki/Sorbonne" title="Sorbonne">Sorbonne</a> professor of Slavic languages, who addressed the audience by video link from Paris.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-USU_116-0"><a href="#cite_note-USU-116"><span>[</span>98<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-VoiceOfRussia_117-0"><a href="#cite_note-VoiceOfRussia-117"><span>[</span>99<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>A number of other prominent exhibitions were created in France and the United States, many of them in 2000, honouring the centenary of the author-aviator's birth.</li>
<li>In January 2014, New York City's <a href="/wiki/Morgan_Library_%26_Museum" title="Morgan Library &amp; Museum">Morgan Library &amp; Museum</a> featured a <a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince#Museums_and_exhibits" title="The Little Prince">major three-month-long exhibition, <i>The Little Prince: A New York Story</i></a>. Celebrating the 70th anniversary year of the novella's publication, its exhibits included many of Saint-Exupéry's original manuscript pages, his story's preliminary drawings and watercolor paintings, and also examined Saint-Exupéry's creative writing processes.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2014.01.23.a_118-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2014.01.23.a-118"><span>[</span>100<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2014.01.23.b_119-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2014.01.23.b-119"><span>[</span>101<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Wall_Street_Journal-2014.01.23_120-0"><a href="#cite_note-Wall_Street_Journal-2014.01.23-120"><span>[</span>102<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Morgan_Museum-2014.01.main_121-0"><a href="#cite_note-Morgan_Museum-2014.01.main-121"><span>[</span>103<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph-2014.01.24_122-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-2014.01.24-122"><span>[</span>104<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="International">International</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: International">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Saint-Exupéry's 1939 memoir <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terre_des_hommes" title="fr:Terre des hommes"><i>Terre des hommes</i></a> (titled as <i><a href="/wiki/Wind,_Sand_and_Stars" title="Wind, Sand and Stars">Wind, Sand and Stars</a></i> in English) was chosen <a href="/wiki/Expo_67#Montebello_conference_produces_theme" title="Expo 67">to create the central theme</a> (<i>Terre des Hommes–Man and His World</i>) of the <a href="/wiki/Expo_67" title="Expo 67">1967 International and Universal Exposition</a> in Montreal, Canada (Expo '67), the most successful world's fair of the 20th century. The central theme, which also generated the 17 subsidiary elements used for the world's fair, was elucidated at a 1963 <a href="/wiki/Montebello,_Quebec" title="Montebello, Quebec">Montebello, Quebec</a>, conference held with some of Canada's leading thinkers. At Montebello, French-Canadian author <a href="/wiki/Gabrielle_Roy" title="Gabrielle Roy">Gabrielle Roy</a> helped choose the central theme by quoting Saint-Exupéry on mankind's place in the universe:</li>

<tr>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: top; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: left; padding: 10px 10px;">“</td>
<td style="vertical-align: top; border: none; padding: 4px 10px;">«<i>Être homme, c'est précisément être responsable. C'est sentir, en posant sa pierre, que l'on contribue à bâtir le monde</i>» (to be a man is to be responsible, to feel that by laying one's own stone, one contributes to building the world)</td>
<td style="width: 20px; vertical-align: bottom; border: none; color: #B2B7F2; font-size: 40px; font-family: 'Times New Roman', Times, serif; font-weight: bold; line-height: .6em; text-align: right; padding: 10px 10px;">”</td>
</tr>
</table>
<dl>
<dd>Additionally, <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mich%C3%A8le_Lalonde" title="fr:Michèle Lalonde">Michèle Lalonde</a> and <a href="/wiki/Andr%C3%A9_Pr%C3%A9vost_(composer)" title="André Prévost (composer)">André Prévost</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Oratorio" title="Oratorio">oratorio</a> <i>Terre des hommes</i>, performed at the <i>Place des Nations</i> opening ceremonies and attended by the international delegates of the participating countries, strongly projected the French writer's 'idealist rhetoric'. The <a href="/wiki/Consuelo_de_Saint_Exup%C3%A9ry" title="Consuelo de Saint Exupéry">Countess de Saint Exupéry</a> (1901–1979), Saint-Exupéry's widow, was also a guest of honour at the opening ceremonies of the world's fair.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kr.C3.B4ller_123-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kr.C3.B4ller-123"><span>[</span>105<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<ul>
<li>Asteroid <a href="/wiki/2578_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry" title="2578 Saint-Exupéry">2578 Saint-Exupéry</a>, discovered in November 1975 by Russian astronomer <a href="/wiki/Tamara_Mikhaylovna_Smirnova" title="Tamara Mikhaylovna Smirnova">Tamara Smirnova</a> and provisionally cataloged as Asteroid 1975 VW3, was renamed in the author-aviator's honour. Another asteroid was named as <a href="/wiki/46610_Besixdouze" title="46610 Besixdouze">46610 Bésixdouze</a> (translated to and from both <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hexidecimal" title="Hexidecimal">hexidecimal</a> and French as 'B612'). Additionally the terrestrial-asteroid protection organization <a href="/wiki/B612_Foundation" title="B612 Foundation">B612 Foundation</a> was named in tribute to the author's <i>Little Prince</i>, who fell to Earth from Asteroid B-612.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Philately" title="Philately">Philatelic</a> tributes have been printed in at least 25 other countries as of 2011.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trussel-all_124-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trussel-all-124"><span>[</span>106<span>]</span></a></sup> Only three years after his death, the pilot-aviator was first featured on an 8 franc <a href="/wiki/French_West_Africa" title="French West Africa">French West Africa</a> airmail stamp (Scott Catalog # C11). France followed several months later in 1948 with an 80 franc airmail stamp honouring him (CB1), and later with another stamp honouring both him and airmail pioneer Jean Mermoz, plus the supersonic <a href="/wiki/Concorde" title="Concorde">Concorde</a> passenger airliner, in 1970 (C43).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trussel-all_124-1"><a href="#cite_note-Trussel-all-124"><span>[</span>106<span>]</span></a></sup> In commemoration of the 50th anniversary of the writer's death, <a href="/wiki/Israel" title="Israel">Israel</a> issued a stamp honoring "Saint-Ex" and <i>The Little Prince</i> in 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Trussel-is_125-0"><a href="#cite_note-Trussel-is-125"><span>[</span>107<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In Argentina and Brazil, where Saint-Exupéry became the founding director of the pioneering South American airmail airline <a href="/wiki/Aeroposta_Argentina" title="Aeroposta Argentina">Aeroposta Argentina</a>:
<ul>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/Aguja_Saint_Exupery" title="Aguja Saint Exupery">Aguja Saint Exupery</a> is a mountain peak located near the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerro_Chalt%C3%A9n" title="Cerro Chaltén">Cerro Chaltén</a> (also known as Monte Fitz Roy) in the <a href="/wiki/Los_Glaciares_National_Park" title="Los Glaciares National Park">Los Glaciares National Park</a> in Patagonia, Argentina, The mountain peak is named in Saint-Exupéry's honour;</li>
<li>the <a href="/wiki/San_Antonio_Oeste" title="San Antonio Oeste">San Antonio Oeste</a> municipal airport was named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Antoine_de_Saint_Exup%C3%A9ry_Airport_(Argentina)" title="Antoine de Saint Exupéry Airport (Argentina)">Aerodromo Saint Exupery</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Airport-Info_126-0"><a href="#cite_note-Airport-Info-126"><span>[</span>108<span>]</span></a></sup> A small museum exhibit resides in the airport building;</li>
<li>the small Brazilian airport serving <a href="/wiki/Ocau%C3%A7u" title="Ocauçu">Ocauçu, São Paulo</a> is named after the pilot, and</li>
<li>several Argentinian schools are also named after the author-aviator.</li>
</ul>
</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Institutions_and_schools">Institutions and schools</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Institutions and schools">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>In 1960 the humanitarian organization <i><a href="/wiki/Terre_des_hommes" title="Terre des hommes">Terre des hommes</a></i>, named after Saint-Exupéry's 1939 philosophical memoir <a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Terre_des_hommes" title="fr:Terre des hommes"><i>Terre des hommes</i></a> (titled as <i><a href="/wiki/Wind,_Sand_and_Stars" title="Wind, Sand and Stars">Wind, Sand and Stars</a></i> in English),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TDH_127-0"><a href="#cite_note-TDH-127"><span>[</span>109<span>]</span></a></sup> was founded in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lausanne,_Switzerland" title="Lausanne, Switzerland">Lausanne, Switzerland</a> by Edmond Kaiser. Other <i>Terre des Hommes</i> societies were later organized in more countries with similar <a href="/wiki/Social_work" title="Social work">social aid</a> and humanitarian goals. The several independent groups joined together to form a new umbrella organization, <i>Terre des Hommes-Fédération Internationale</i> (TDHFI, in English: <a href="/wiki/Terre_des_hommes" title="Terre des hommes">International Federation of Terre des Hommes</a>, or IFTDH). The national constituents first met in 1966 to formalize their new parent organization, headquartered in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geneva,_Switzerland" title="Geneva, Switzerland">Geneva, Switzerland</a>. As of 2009 eleven organizations in Canada, Denmark, Germany, France, Italy, Luxembourg, the Netherlands, Spain, Switzerland, and Syria belonged to the Federation. An important part of their works is their consulting role to the <a href="/wiki/United_Nations_Economic_and_Social_Council" title="United Nations Economic and Social Council">United Nations Economic and Social Council</a> (ECOSOC).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TerreDesHommes.org-a_128-0"><a href="#cite_note-TerreDesHommes.org-a-128"><span>[</span>110<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>In June 2009, the Antoine de Saint-Exupéry Youth Foundation (FASEJ) was founded in Paris by the Saint-Exupéry–d'Agay Estate, to promote education, art, culture, health and sports for youth worldwide, especially those from disadvantaged backgrounds. This organization, which follows Saint-Exupéry's philosophies and his memory, was financed in part by the sale of one of his original 1936 handwritten manuscripts at a <a href="/wiki/Sotheby%27s" title="Sotheby's">Sotheby's</a> auction for €312,750.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peak-2011.03_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-Peak-2011.03-11"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Peak-2011.03.03_129-0"><a href="#cite_note-Peak-2011.03.03-129"><span>[</span>111<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Numerous public schools, lycées, high schools, colleges and technical schools have been named in honour of Saint-Exupéry across France, Europe, Québec and South America, as well as at least one in Africa. The <i><a href="/wiki/%C3%89cole_Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry_de_Kigali" title="École Antoine de Saint-Exupéry de Kigali">École Antoine de Saint-Exupéry de Kigali</a></i>, a French international school in <a href="/wiki/Rwanda" title="Rwanda">Rwanda</a>, is named after him.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other">Other</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Other">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Numerous <a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince#Honours_and_legacy" title="The Little Prince">other tributes have been awarded</a> to honour Saint-Exupéry and his most famous literary creation, his <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">Little Prince</a></i>:</p>
<ul>
<li>The <i><a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/Escadron_de_reconnaissance_1/33_Belfort" title="fr:Escadron de reconnaissance 1/33 Belfort">GR I/33</a></i> (later renamed as the 1/33 Belfort Squadron), one of the French Air Force squadrons Saint-Exupéry flew with, adopted the image of the <i>Little Prince</i> as part of the squadron and <a href="/wiki/Military_aircraft_insignia" title="Military aircraft insignia">tail insignia</a> on its <a href="/wiki/Dassault_Mirage_III" title="Dassault Mirage III">Dassault Mirage fighter jets</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006445_130-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006445-130"><span>[</span>112<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Google celebrated Saint-Exupery's 110th birthday with a special logotype depicting the little prince being hoisted through the heavens by a flock of birds.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Google_131-0"><a href="#cite_note-Google-131"><span>[</span>113<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Numerous streets and place names are named after the author-aviator throughout France and other countries.</li>
<li>Cafe Saint-Ex, a popular bar and nightclub in Washington, D.C. near the U-Street corridor, holds Saint-Exupéry as its name source.</li>
<li>Uruguayan airline <a href="/wiki/BQB_L%C3%ADneas_A%C3%A9reas" title="BQB Líneas Aéreas">BQB Líneas Aéreas</a> named one of its aircraft, an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ATR-72" title="ATR-72">ATR-72</a> (CX-JPL), in honor of the aviator.</li>
<li>International Watch Company (IWC) has created many St Exupery tribute versions of several of their wristwatch lines, with the distinctive 'A' from his signature featured on the dial.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="In_popular_culture">In popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: In popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/Wings_of_Courage" title="Wings of Courage">Wings of Courage</a></i> is a 1995 docudrama by French director <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Annaud" title="Jean-Jacques Annaud">Jean-Jacques Annaud</a>. The movie was the world's first dramatic picture shot in the <a href="/wiki/IMAX" title="IMAX">IMAX</a>-format, and is an account of the true story of early airmail pilots <a href="/wiki/Henri_Guillaumet" title="Henri Guillaumet">Henri Guillaumet</a> (played by <a href="/wiki/Craig_Sheffer" title="Craig Sheffer">Craig Sheffer</a>), Saint-Exupéry played by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hulce" title="Tom Hulce">Tom Hulce</a>, and several others.</li>
<li>Saint-Exupéry and his wife <a href="/wiki/Consuelo_de_Saint_Exup%C3%A9ry" title="Consuelo de Saint Exupéry">Consuelo</a> were portrayed by <a href="/wiki/Bruno_Ganz" title="Bruno Ganz">Bruno Ganz</a> and <a href="/wiki/Miranda_Richardson" title="Miranda Richardson">Miranda Richardson</a> in the 1997 biopic <i><a href="/wiki/Saint-Ex" title="Saint-Ex">Saint-Ex</a></i>, a British film biography of the French author-pilot. It also featured <a href="/wiki/Eleanor_Bron" title="Eleanor Bron">Eleanor Bron</a> and was filmed and distributed in the United Kingdom, with scripting by Frank Cottrell Boyce. The film combines elements of biography, documentary, and dramatic recreation.</li>
<li>A 2004 German short dramatic film, "<i>Der letzte Flug</i>" <i>(The Last Flight)</i>, portrays a fictional Luftwaffe pilot, Lieutenant Henrici Müller, who returns to his airbase on 31 July 1944 after having shot down a reconnaissance version Lightning during a mission to Corsica. He learns in the film's final moments that the missing pilot is Saint-Exupéry. The same Lufwaffe pilot is shown as an elderly man reminiscing that fateful day. He concludes the drama by reciting the final paragraph from <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a></i>. The 11-minute film was directed and written by Roger Moench, and starred André Hennicke.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IMDB-a_132-0"><a href="#cite_note-IMDB-a-132"><span>[</span>114<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literature">Literature</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Literature">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>After his disappearance, <a href="/wiki/Consuelo_de_Saint_Exup%C3%A9ry" title="Consuelo de Saint Exupéry">Consuelo de Saint Exupéry</a> wrote <i>The Tale of the Rose</i>, which was published in 2000 and subsequently translated into 16 languages.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESaint-Exup.C3.A9ry2000_133-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESaint-Exup.C3.A9ry2000-133"><span>[</span>115<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Saint-Exupéry is mentioned in <a href="/wiki/Tom_Wolfe" title="Tom Wolfe">Tom Wolfe</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Right_Stuff_(book)" title="The Right Stuff (book)">The Right Stuff</a></i>: "A saint in short, true to his name, flying up here at the right hand of God. The good Saint-Ex! And he was not the only one. He was merely the one who put it into words most beautifully and anointed himself before the altar of the right stuff."</li>
<li>In 2000, Jean-Pierre de Villers wrote a novella telling the imagined story of Saint-Exupéry's last flight, <i>The Last Flight of the Little Prince</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-deVillers_134-0"><a href="#cite_note-deVillers-134"><span>[</span>116<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
<li>Comic-book author <a href="/wiki/Hugo_Pratt" title="Hugo Pratt">Hugo Pratt</a> imagined the fantastic story of Saint-Exupéry's last flight in <i>Saint-Exupéry: le dernier vol</i> (1994).</li>
<li>Saint-Exupéry is the subject of the 2013 historical novel <i>Studio Saint-Ex</i> (Knopf, New York / Penguin, Canada) by <a href="/wiki/Ania_Szado" title="Ania Szado">Ania Szado</a>. In the novel Saint-Exupéry awaits the Americans' entry into World War II, while writing <i>The Little Prince</i> in New York.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li>Saint-Exupéry's death and speculation that Horst Rippert shot him down are the subject of "Saint Ex", a song on <a href="/wiki/Widespread_Panic" title="Widespread Panic">Widespread Panic</a>'s eleventh studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Dirty_Side_Down" title="Dirty Side Down">Dirty Side Down</a></i>.</li>
<li>The Norwegian progressive rock band <a href="/wiki/Gazpacho_(band)" title="Gazpacho (band)">Gazpacho</a>'s concept album <i>Tick Tock</i> is based on Saint-Exupéry's <a href="#Desert_crash">desert crash</a>.</li>
<li>"P 38", a song about Saint-Exupéry's last mission, was written in 1984 by the Swedish popgroup Webstrarna.</li>
<li>"On the Planet of the Living", a song sung by <a href="/wiki/Eduard_Khil" title="Eduard Khil">Eduard Khil</a>, was dedicated to Saint-Exupéry.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Theatre">Theatre</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=23" title="Edit section: Theatre">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In August 2011, the world premiere of <i>Saint-Ex</i>, a theatrical production of Saint-Exupéry's life, was launched at the Weston Playhouse in <a href="/wiki/Weston,_Vermont" title="Weston, Vermont">Weston, Vermont</a>. The live theatre musical explores in drama and song the aviator-author's early life, the aerial band-of-brothers he flew with at <a href="/wiki/A%C3%A9ropostale_(aviation)" title="Aéropostale (aviation)">Aéropostale</a>, and the raucous relations between him and his fiery Latin writer-artist wife, born <a href="/wiki/Consuelo_de_Saint_Exup%C3%A9ry" title="Consuelo de Saint Exupéry">Consuelo Suncín Sandoval Zeceña</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2011.08.17_135-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2011.08.17-135"><span>[</span>117<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Playbill_136-0"><a href="#cite_note-Playbill-136"><span>[</span>118<span>]</span></a></sup> The production was written by the husband and wife team of lyricist Sean Barry and composer Jenny Giering, and staged with the assistance of director Matt Castle and set designer Tim Mackabee, plus choreography by Jennifer Turey. The leading cast members include <a href="/wiki/Alexander_Gemignani" title="Alexander Gemignani">Alexander Gemignani</a> (playing Saint-Exupéry), <a href="/wiki/Krysta_Rodriguez" title="Krysta Rodriguez">Krysta Rodriguez</a> (playing his tempestuous wife Consuelo), Cass Morgan (Saint-Exupéry's mother, author Countess Marie de Fonscolombe), plus Charlie Brady (Aéropostale pilot and <a href="/wiki/Air_France" title="Air France">Air France</a> director <a href="/wiki/Henri_Guillaumet" title="Henri Guillaumet">Henri Guillaumet</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2011.08.17_135-1"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2011.08.17-135"><span>[</span>117<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Playbill_136-1"><a href="#cite_note-Playbill-136"><span>[</span>118<span>]</span></a></sup> Although the musical production successfully debuted on 25 August 2011, the theatre was soon deluged with two-to-four metres of water generated by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hurricane_Irene_(2011)" title="Hurricane Irene (2011)">Hurricane Irene</a>, which struck the east coast of the United States three days later. After pumping the floodwaters from the building and partially restoring the costumes and stage sets, the musical production "resurfaced" on 2 September.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes-2011.08.29_137-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-2011.08.29-137"><span>[</span>119<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Antoine_de_Saint-Exup%C3%A9ry&amp;action=edit&amp;section=29" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="reflist references-column-width" style="-moz-column-width: 30em; -webkit-column-width: 30em; column-width: 30em; list-style-type: decimal;">
<ol class="references">
<li id="cite_note-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-6">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Although Saint-Exupéry inherited his peerage title through his father and could employ it at will, he rarely did so. While stationed out of country, he asked his mother to no longer address him as 'Count' on her mail envelopes to him. He would later write, "I have worked eight years of my life, day and night, with working men. I have found myself sharing their table... I know very well what I am talking about when I speak of working-class people, and I love them."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECommire1980157_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECommire1980157-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-8"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-8">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Standard modern usage for the spelling of Saint-Exupéry's surname includes the hyphen. But according to French legal documents and his birth certificate, no hyphen is used in his name; thus, it is formally <i>de Saint Exupéry</i>, not <i>Saint-Exupéry</i>. The Armorial de l'ANF,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Armorial-ANF_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Armorial-ANF-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> which is a listing of French nobility, mentions the <i>Saint Exupéry (de)</i> family without a hyphen. However, both spellings (with and without a hyphen) are found for the author's relatives in the advanced research facility <i><a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/G%C3%A9opatronyme" title="fr:Géopatronyme">Géopatronyme</a></i>, for records in the Rhône department for the period 1891–1915, showing two birth certificates with a hyphen and one without. Most of his books were published under his name with a hyphen; memorial plaques, the 50-franc banknote, and the bracelet he was wearing at the time of his death also use the hyphen (Saint-Exupéry). The author adopted the hyphen after his arrival in the United States in 1941, as he was annoyed with Americans' addressing him as "Mr. Exupéry".</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-16"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-16">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Saint-Exupéry was born at No. 8 rue Peyrat (later rue Alphonse Fochier, and still later renamed as Rue de Saint Exupéry), in <a href="/wiki/Arrondissements_of_Lyon" title="Arrondissements of Lyon">Lyon's 2nd arrondissement</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEWebster199412_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEWebster199412-13"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> He was the third of five children (and nicknamed 'Tonio'), after older sisters Marie-Madeleine ('Biche', b. 26/01/1897–1927), Simone ('Monot', 26/01/1898–1978), and his younger siblings François (1902–1917) and Gabrielle ('Didi', 1903–1986). His father and mother were Count Jean de Saint Exupéry (1863–1904; different sources name his father as Jean-Marc or Caesar de Saint Exupéry<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTECommire1980154_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTECommire1980154-15"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup>) and Countess Marie, née Boyer de Fonscolombe (1875–1972). He was baptized in his great-aunt's chapel on 15 August 1900 in <a href="/wiki/Saint-Maurice-de-R%C3%A9mens" title="Saint-Maurice-de-Rémens">Saint-Maurice-de-Rémens</a>; his godfather was his uncle, Roger de Saint Exupéry, Earl of Miremont (1865 – August 1914, killed leading his battalion in Maissin, Belgium, during the First World War), and his godmother was his aunt Madeleine Fonscolombe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Saint-Exup.C3.A9ry.com-b_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-Saint-Exup.C3.A9ry.com-b-14"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-27"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-27">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Hélène (Nelly) de Vogüé (1908–2003), born Hélène Jaunez to a French businessman, became a well known French business executive and also an intellectual fluent in several languages. She married the equally well known French noble, Jean de Vogüé, in 1927 and had one child with him, a son named Patrice. Hélène is referred to only as "Madame de B." in multiple Saint-Exupéry biographies. This occurred due to agreements she made with writers before granting them access to her troves of the author-aviator's writings, which she deposited in the French national archives – from which they will not be released until 2053. It is believed she sought her anonymity in order to protect Saint-Exupéry's reputation, as during the Second World War, the U.S. <a href="/wiki/Office_of_Strategic_Services" title="Office of Strategic Services">O.S.S.</a> suspected she was a secret Vichy agent and Nazi collaborator.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Telegraph-2007.04.30_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-Telegraph-2007.04.30-26"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-31"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-31">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The aircraft Saint-Exupéry was flying when he crashed in the Sahara was a <a href="/wiki/Caudron" title="Caudron">Caudron</a> C-630 <a href="/wiki/Caudron_Simoun" title="Caudron Simoun">Simoun</a>, Serial Number 7042, with the French registration F-ANRY, with 'F' being the international designator for France, and the remainder being derived from 'ANtoine de saint-exupéRY'.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-45"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-45">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The large home of <a href="/wiki/Charles_De_Koninck" title="Charles De Koninck">Charles De Koninck</a> has since been classified as a historical building and has been visited frequently by numerous worldwide personalities from academic, scientific, intellectual, and political circles. Thomas kept a few memories from Saint-Exupéry's visit: "[He was] a great man. He was the aviator. Someone we would get attached to quite easily, who would show interest in us, the kids. He would make us paper planes, drawings. [...] He loved mathematical enigmas." The following year, he published <i><a href="/wiki/The_Little_Prince" title="The Little Prince">The Little Prince</a></i>. According to the local legend, Saint-Exupéry received his inspiration from the junior De Koninck, who asked many questions. However, Thomas De Koninck denied this interpretation: "<i>The Little Prince</i> is Saint-Exupéry himself."</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-50"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-50">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Although Saint-Exupéry's regular publisher in France, Gallimard, lists <i>Le Petit Prince</i> as being published in 1946, that apparently is a legalistic interpretation possibly designed to allow for an extra year of the novella's copyright protection period, and is based on Gallimard's explanation that sales of the book started only in 1946. Other sources, such as the one referenced, depict the first Librairie Gallimard printing of 12,250 copies as occurring on 30 November 1945.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-PetitPrince.net-a_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-PetitPrince.net-a-49"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-55"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-55">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">After being grounded following his crash, Saint-Exupéry spared no efforts in his campaign to return to active combat flying duty. He utilized all his contacts and powers of persuasion to overcome his age and physical handicap barriers, which would have completely barred an ordinary patriot from serving as a war pilot. Instrumental in his reinstatement was an agreement he proposed to John Phillips, a fluently bilingual <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_Magazine" title="Life Magazine">Life Magazine</a></i> correspondent in February 1944, where Saint-Exupéry committed to "...write, and I'll donate what I do to you, for your publication, if you get me reinstated into my squadron."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTESchiff2006421_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTESchiff2006421-54"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> Phillips later met with a high level U.S. Army Air Forces press officer in Italy, Colonel John Reagan McCrary, who conveyed the <i>Life Magazine</i> request to General Eaker. The approval for return to flying status would be made "...not through favoritism, but through exception". The brutalized French, it was noted, would cut a German's throat "...probably with more relish than anybody".</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-59"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-59">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Saint-Exupéry suffered recurring pain and immobility from multiple previous injuries due to his five serious aircraft crashes. After his death, there were also vague suggestions that his disappearance was the result of suicide rather than an aircraft failure or combat loss.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-62"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-62">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Various sources state that his final flight was either his seventh, eight, ninth, and even his 10th mission. He volunteered for almost every proposed mission submitted to his squadron, and protested fiercely, after being grounded following his second sortie which ended with a demolished P-38. His connections in high places, plus a publishing agreement with <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Life_Magazine" title="Life Magazine">Life Magazine</a></i>, were instrumental in having the grounding order against him lifted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Eyheramonno_61-0"><a href="#cite_note-Eyheramonno-61"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup> Saint-Exupéry's friends, colleagues and compatriots were actively working to keep him grounded and out of harm's way.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-64"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-64">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">One ruse contemplated by the GR II/33's commanders was to expose Saint-Exupéry "accidentally" to the plans of the pending invasion of France so he could be subsequently grounded. No air force general would countermand such a grounding order and risk Saint-Exupéry's being captured by the Germans if he were forced down. Saint-Exupéry's commanding officer – a close friend of his – was ill and absent when the author took off on his final flight. The commander "bawled out" his staff when he learned that a grounding scheme hadn't been implemented.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-70"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-70">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Saint-Exupéry's P-38, as identified in Aéro-Re.L.I.C.'s detailed crash wreckage recovery report, was an F-5B-1-LO, LAC 2734 variant, serial number 42-68223, which departed Borgo-Porreta, Bastia, Corsica, France on 31 July 1944, at 8h 45 (8:45 a.m.). The report is of eight pages length, broken into six technical sections, with five photographs, including an image of an identifying component serial number which verified the wreckage belonged to Saint-Exupéry's aircraft. Although the debris field, one kilometre long and 400 metres wide, was located in May 2000, it took over two years before the French government agreed to the organization's request to permit recovery of the crash debris from the seabed. It is believed that the wide distribution of crash wreckage which left hundreds of parts deposited on the sea floor was created by the Lightning striking the sea's surface at high velocity.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aero-relic_69-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aero-relic-69"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-78"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-78">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Lt. Meredith's remains were not recovered. He is listed on the Tablets of the Missing at the Florence, Italy ABMC Cemetery {ABMC Records}. Lt. Meredith was shot down by Feldwebel Guth of 3./Jagdgruppe 200, the same unit in which Hort Rippert (explained below) was serving. Guth's victory claim is recorded in the lists held by the German Bundesarchiv-Militärarchiv. The progress of the interception was followed by Allied radar and radio monitoring stations and documented in Missing Air Crew Report 7339 on the loss of Second Lieutenant Gene C. Meredith of the 23rd Photographic Squadron/5th Reconnaissance Group. The intercepted Mediterranean Allied Air Forces Signals Intelligence Report for 30 July records that "an Allied reconnaissance aircraft was claimed shot down at 1115 [GMT]".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-GhostBombers_77-0"><a href="#cite_note-GhostBombers-77"><span>[</span>65<span>]</span></a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-85"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-85">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The RAF's No. 276 Wing (Signals Intelligence, intercepted Luftwaffe communications) Operations Record Book for 31 July 1944 notes only: "... three enemy fighter sections between 0758/0929 hours operating in reaction to Allied fighters over Cannes, Toulon and the area to the North. No contacts. Patrol activity north of Toulon reported between 1410/1425 hours". Archive sources for Luftwaffe activity over Southern France on 30 and 31 July 1944 are cited in an extensive article on the Ghost Bombers aviation history webpage for the Saint-Exupéry episode.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-88"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-88">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The proposed "suppression" of Rippert's claim due to Saint-Exupery's stature was also met with skepticism as Luftwaffe pilots tended to immediately report their kills, and the allies did not broadcast Saint-Exupery's status as missing for at least two days.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-AeroJournal-2008.No4_76-2"><a href="#cite_note-AeroJournal-2008.No4-76"><span>[</span>64<span>]</span></a></sup> After the war, Horst Rippert became a television journalist and led the <a href="/wiki/ZDF" title="ZDF">ZDF</a> sports department. He was the brother of German singer <a href="/wiki/Ivan_Rebroff" title="Ivan Rebroff">Ivan Rebroff</a>. Rippert died in 2013.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-96"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-96">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The French and English versions of this book (<i>Terre des hommes</i>/<i><a href="/wiki/Wind,_Sand_and_Stars" title="Wind, Sand and Stars">Wind, Sand and Stars</a></i>) differed significantly, with Saint-Exupéry removing sections from the original French version he did not consider appropriate for its targeted U.S. audience, and adding new material specifically written for that group and translated into English, which he could not speak. Although it did not appear in its earliest editions of the English translation, <i>An Appreciation</i> was added to later printings, contributed by <a href="/wiki/Anne_Morrow_Lindbergh" title="Anne Morrow Lindbergh">Anne Morrow Lindbergh</a>, and earlier published in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Saturday_Review_of_Literature" title="The Saturday Review of Literature">The Saturday Review of Literature</a></i> on 14 October 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller-Fay-1946a_95-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-Fay-1946a-95"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup></span></li>
<li id="cite_note-103"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-103">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">The last paragraph of <i>Flying</i> '​s book review of <i>A Sense of Life</i> incorrectly states that Saint-Exupéry's last mission was a bombing run, when in fact it was a photo-reconnaissance assignment for the pending invasion of Southern France.</span></li>
<li id="cite_note-106"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-106">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">In exchange, Lindbergh contributed "An Appreciation" to the later printings of Saint-Exupéry's <i>Wind, Sand and Stars</i>, earlier published in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Saturday_Review_of_Literature" title="The Saturday Review of Literature">The Saturday Review of Literature</a></i> on 14 October 1939.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller-Fay-1946a_95-3"><a href="#cite_note-Miller-Fay-1946a-95"><span>[</span>80<span>]</span></a></sup></span></li>
