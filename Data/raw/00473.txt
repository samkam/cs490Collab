<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Penrose stairs</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
</div>
<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Impossible_staircase.svg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="283" data-file-width="372" height="167" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Impossible_staircase.svg/220px-Impossible_staircase.svg.png" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Impossible_staircase.svg/330px-Impossible_staircase.svg.png 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/34/Impossible_staircase.svg/440px-Impossible_staircase.svg.png 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Impossible_staircase.svg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Penrose stairs</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <b>Penrose stairs</b> or <b>Penrose steps</b>, also dubbed the <b>impossible staircase</b>, is an <a href="/wiki/Impossible_object" title="Impossible object">impossible object</a> created by <a href="/wiki/Lionel_Penrose" title="Lionel Penrose">Lionel Penrose</a> and his son <a href="/wiki/Roger_Penrose" title="Roger Penrose">Roger Penrose</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penrose_1958_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Penrose_1958-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> A variation on the <a href="/wiki/Penrose_triangle" title="Penrose triangle">Penrose triangle</a>, it is a two-dimensional depiction of a staircase in which the stairs make four 90-degree turns as they ascend or descend yet form a continuous loop, so that a person could climb them forever and never get any higher. This is clearly impossible in three dimensions.</p>
<p>The "continuous staircase" was first presented in an article that the Penroses wrote in 1959, based on the so-called "triangle of Penrose" published by Roger Penrose in the <i>British Journal of Psychology</i> in 1958.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Penrose_1958_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Penrose_1958-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/M.C._Escher" title="M.C. Escher">M.C. Escher</a> then discovered the Penrose stairs in the following year and made his now famous lithography <i>Klimmen en dalen</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Ascending_and_Descending" title="Ascending and Descending">Ascending and Descending</a></i>) in March 1960. Penrose and Escher were informed of each other's work that same year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Escher developed the theme further in his print <i>Waterval</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Waterfall_(M._C._Escher)" title="Waterfall (M. C. Escher)">Waterfall</a></i>), which appeared in 1961.</p>
<p>In their original article the Penroses noted that "each part of the structure is acceptable as representing a flight of steps but the connexions are such that the picture, as a whole, is inconsistent: the steps continually descend in a clockwise direction."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_discovery">History of discovery</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penrose_stairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History of discovery">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Penroses">The Penroses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penrose_stairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: The Penroses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>At an Escher conference in Rome in 1985, Roger Penrose said that he had been greatly inspired by Escher's work when he and his father discovered both the tri-bar structure (i.e., the Penrose triangle) and the continuous steps, although Escher, in the 1950s, had not yet drawn any impossible figures and was not aware of their existence. Roger Penrose had been introduced to Escher's work at the <a href="/wiki/International_Congress_of_Mathematicians" title="International Congress of Mathematicians">International Congress of Mathematicians</a> in Amsterdam in 1954. He was "absolutely spellbound" by Escher's work, and on his journey back to England he decided to produce something "impossible" on his own. After experimenting with various designs of bars overlying each other he finally arrived at the impossible triangle. Roger showed his drawings to his father, who immediately produced several variants, including the impossible flight of stairs. They wanted to publish their findings but didn't know in what field the subject belonged. Because Lionel Penrose knew the editor of <i>British Journal of Psychology</i> and convinced him to publish their short manuscript, the finding was finally presented as a psychological subject. After the publication in 1958 the Penroses sent a copy of the article to Escher as a token of their esteem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ernst-71_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ernst-71-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While the Penroses credited Escher in their article, Escher himself noted in a letter to his son in January 1960 that he was:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>working on the design of a new picture, which featured a flight of stairs which only ever ascended or descended, depending on how you saw it. [The stairs] form a closed, circular construction, rather like a snake biting its own tail. And yet they can be drawn in correct perspective: each step higher (or lower) than the previous one. [...] I discovered the principle in an article which was sent to me, and in which I myself was named as the maker of various 'impossible objects'. But I was not familiar with the continuous steps of which the author had included a clear, if perfunctory, sketch, although I was employing some of his other examples.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ernst-75-78_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ernst-75-78-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Escher was captivated by the endless stairs and subsequently wrote a letter to the Penroses in April 1960:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>A few months ago, a friend of mine sent me a photocopy of your article... Your figures 3 and 4, the 'continuous flight of steps', were entirely new to me, and I was so taken by the idea that they recently inspired me to produce a new picture, which I would like to send to you as a token of my esteem. Should you have published other articles on impossible objects or related topics, or should you know of any such articles, I would be most grateful if you could send me further details.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ernst-75-78_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ernst-75-78-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Oscar_Reutersv.C3.A4rd">Oscar Reutersv√§rd</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Penrose_stairs&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Oscar Reutersv√§rd">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The staircase design had been discovered previously by the Swedish artist <a href="/wiki/Oscar_Reutersv%C3%A4rd" title="Oscar Reutersv√§rd">Oscar Reutersv√§rd</a>, but neither Penrose nor Escher was aware of his designs.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-IllusionWorks_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-IllusionWorks-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Inspired by a radio programme on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mozart" title="Mozart">Mozart</a>'s method of composition‚Äîdescribed as "creative automatism", i.e. each creative idea written down inspired a new idea‚ÄîReutersv√§rd started to draw a series of impossible objects on a journey from Stockholm to Paris in 1950 in the same "unconscious, automatic" way. He did not realize that his figure was a continuous flight of stairs while drawing, but the process enabled him to trace his increasingly complex designs step by step. When M.C. Escher's <i>Ascending and Descending</i> was sent to Reutersv√§rd in 1961, he was impressed but didn't like the irregularities of the stairs (<span class="nowrap">2 √ó 15 + 2 √ó 9</span>). Throughout the 1960s, Reutersv√§rd sent several letters to Escher to express his admiration for his work, but the Dutch artist failed to respond.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Roger Penrose only discovered Reutersv√§rd's work in 1984.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ernst-71_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ernst-71-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
