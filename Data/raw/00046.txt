<p><i><b>The Fellowship of the Ring</b></i> is the first of three volumes of the epic<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings</a></i> by the English author <a href="/wiki/J._R._R._Tolkien" title="J. R. R. Tolkien">J. R. R. Tolkien</a>. It takes place in the <a href="/wiki/Fictional_universe" title="Fictional universe">fictional universe</a> of <a href="/wiki/Middle-earth" title="Middle-earth">Middle-earth</a>. It was originally published on July 29, 1954 in the United Kingdom. The volume consists of a Prologue titled "Concerning Hobbits, and other matters" followed by Book I and Book II.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Title_and_publication">Title and publication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Title and publication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Tolkien conceived of <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> as a multiple volume with six sections he called "books" along with extensive appendices. The original publisher made the decision to split the work into three parts. It was also the publisher's decision to place the fifth and sixth books and the appendices into one volume under the title <i><a href="/wiki/The_Return_of_the_King" title="The Return of the King">The Return of the King</a></i>, in reference to <a href="/wiki/Aragorn" title="Aragorn">Aragorn</a>'s assumption of the throne of <a href="/wiki/Gondor" title="Gondor">Gondor</a>. Tolkien indicated he would have preferred <i>The <a href="/wiki/War_of_the_Ring" title="War of the Ring">War of the Ring</a></i> as a title, as it gave away less of the story.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-let140_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-let140-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Before the decision to publish <i>The Lord of the Rings</i> in three volumes was made, Tolkien had hoped to publish the novel in one volume, possibly also combined with <i><a href="/wiki/The_Silmarillion" title="The Silmarillion">The Silmarillion</a></i>. However, he had proposed titles for the individual six sections. Of the two books that comprise what became <i>The Fellowship of the Ring</i> the first was to be called <i>The First Journey</i> or <i>The Ring Sets Out</i>. The name of the second was <i>The Journey of the Nine Companions</i> or <i>The Ring Goes South</i>. The titles <i>The Ring Sets Out</i> and <i>The Ring Goes South</i> were used in the <i>Millennium edition</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote boilerplate seealso">See also: <a href="/wiki/The_Lord_of_the_Rings#Plot_summary" title="The Lord of the Rings">The Lord of the Rings § Plot summary</a></div>
<p>The Prologue is meant partly to help people who have not read <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hobbit" title="The Hobbit">The Hobbit</a></i> to understand the events of that book. It also contains other background information to set the stage for the novel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_I:_The_Ring_Sets_Out">Book I: The Ring Sets Out</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Book I: The Ring Sets Out">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first chapter in the book begins in a light vein, following the tone of <i>The Hobbit</i>. <a href="/wiki/Bilbo_Baggins" title="Bilbo Baggins">Bilbo Baggins</a> celebrates his 111th (or eleventy-first, as it is called in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hobbiton" title="Hobbiton">Hobbiton</a>) birthday on the same day, 22 'September', that his younger cousin and adopted heir <a href="/wiki/Frodo_Baggins" title="Frodo Baggins">Frodo Baggins</a> celebrates his coming of age at 33. At the birthday party, Bilbo departs from the <a href="/wiki/Shire_(Middle-earth)" title="Shire (Middle-earth)">Shire</a>, the land of the Hobbits, for what he calls a permanent holiday. Bilbo does so by using the magic ring (that he had found on his journey) to disappear and is aided by <a href="/wiki/Gandalf" title="Gandalf">Gandalf</a> with a flash and puff of smoke, leading many in the Shire to believe he has gone mad. He leaves Frodo his remaining belongings, including his home, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bag_End" title="Bag End">Bag End</a>, and (after some persuasion by the wizard Gandalf) the Ring. Gandalf leaves on his own business, warning Frodo to keep the Ring secret.</p>
<p>Over the next 17 years Gandalf periodically pays short visits to Bag End. One spring night, he arrives to enlighten Frodo about Bilbo's ring; it is the <a href="/wiki/One_Ring" title="One Ring">One Ring</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sauron" title="Sauron">Sauron</a> the <i>Dark Lord</i>. Sauron had forged it to subdue and rule Middle-earth, but in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/War_of_the_Last_Alliance" title="War of the Last Alliance">War of the Last Alliance</a>, he had been defeated by <a href="/wiki/Gil-galad" title="Gil-galad">Gil-galad</a> the <a href="/wiki/Elf_(Middle-earth)" title="Elf (Middle-earth)">Elven</a> King and <a href="/wiki/Elendil" title="Elendil">Elendil</a>, High King of <a href="/wiki/Arnor" title="Arnor">Arnor</a> and <a href="/wiki/Gondor" title="Gondor">Gondor</a>, though they themselves perished in the deed. <a href="/wiki/Isildur" title="Isildur">Isildur</a>, Elendil's son, cut the Ring from Sauron's finger. Sauron had thus been overthrown, but the Ring itself was not destroyed as it should have been; Isildur kept it for himself. Isildur was slain soon afterwards in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Battle_of_the_Gladden_Fields" title="Battle of the Gladden Fields">Battle of the Gladden Fields</a>, and the Ring was lost in <a href="/wiki/Anduin" title="Anduin">Great River Anduin</a>. Thousands of years later, it had been found by the hobbit <a href="/wiki/D%C3%A9agol" title="Déagol">Déagol</a>; but Déagol was thereupon murdered by his friend <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sm%C3%A9agol" title="Sméagol">Sméagol</a>, who coveted the Ring for himself. Sméagol subsequently possessed the Ring for centuries, and under its influence he became the creature named <a href="/wiki/Gollum" title="Gollum">Gollum</a>. The Ring was found by <a href="/wiki/Bilbo_Baggins" title="Bilbo Baggins">Bilbo Baggins</a>, as told in <i>The Hobbit</i>, and Bilbo left it behind for Frodo.</p>
<p>Gandalf reveals that Sauron has risen again and returned to his stronghold in <a href="/wiki/Mordor" title="Mordor">Mordor</a>, and is exerting all his power toward the hunting of the Ring. Gandalf details the evil powers of the Ring and its ability to influence the bearer and those near him if it is worn for too long. Gandalf warns Frodo that the Ring is no longer safe in the Shire; he has learned through his investigations that Gollum had gone to Mordor, where he was captured and tortured until he revealed to Sauron that a hobbit named Baggins from the Shire possesses the Ring. Gandalf hopes Frodo can reach the elf-haven <a href="/wiki/Rivendell" title="Rivendell">Rivendell</a>, where he believes Frodo and the Ring will be safe from Sauron, and where its fate can be decided. <a href="/wiki/Samwise_Gamgee" title="Samwise Gamgee">Samwise Gamgee</a>, Frodo's gardener and friend, is discovered listening in on the conversation. Out of loyalty to his master, Sam agrees to accompany Frodo on his journey.</p>
<p>Over the summer Frodo makes plans to leave his home at Bag End, under the pretence that he is moving to a remote region near the Shire to retire. Helping with the plans are Frodo's friends Sam, <a href="/wiki/Peregrin_Took" title="Peregrin Took">Peregrin Took</a> (Pippin for short), <a href="/wiki/Meriadoc_Brandybuck" title="Meriadoc Brandybuck">Meriadoc Brandybuck</a> (Merry), and <a href="/wiki/Fredegar_Bolger" title="Fredegar Bolger">Fredegar Bolger</a> (Fatty), though Frodo does not tell them of the Ring or of his intention to leave the Shire. At midsummer, Gandalf leaves on pressing business, but promises to return before Frodo leaves. Frodo's birthday and departure date approach, but Gandalf does not appear, so Frodo decides to leave without him. Black Riders pursue Frodo's party; these turn out to be <a href="/wiki/Nazg%C3%BBl" title="Nazgûl">Nazgûl</a> or Ringwraiths, "the most terrible servants of the Dark Lord" (men who have been corrupted by Sauron through the Nine Rings), who are searching for "Baggins" and the Ring. In fact, one of the Riders comes to the door of Sam's father, the Gaffer, that very evening before they depart. With help of some elves and <a href="/wiki/Farmer_Maggot" title="Farmer Maggot">Farmer Maggot</a>, they reach Crickhollow beyond the eastern border of the Shire. There Merry, Pippin, Sam, and Fatty reveal that they know of the Ring and of Frodo's plan to leave the Shire. Sam, Merry, and Pippin decide to accompany Frodo, while Fatty stays behind as a decoy. In hopes of eluding the Nazgûl, the hobbits travel through the <a href="/wiki/Old_Forest" title="Old Forest">Old Forest</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Barrow-downs" title="Barrow-downs">Barrow-downs</a>. There in the forest, the group fall asleep by a tree and wake up to realize that its roots are trying to suffocate them, and <a href="/wiki/Tom_Bombadil" title="Tom Bombadil">Tom Bombadil</a> comes to their aid. Within the Barrow-downs, they are captured by a barrow wight but escape and are able to reach the village of <a href="/wiki/Bree_(Middle-earth)" title="Bree (Middle-earth)">Bree</a>, where they meet the ranger <a href="/wiki/Aragorn" title="Aragorn">Aragorn</a>, a friend of Gandalf who becomes their guide to Rivendell.</p>
<p>At the hill of <a href="/wiki/Weathertop" title="Weathertop">Weathertop</a>, five of the Nazgûl attack the travellers, and the <a href="/wiki/Witch-king_of_Angmar" title="Witch-king of Angmar">chief of the Nazgûl</a> stabs Frodo in the shoulder with a cursed knife before Aragorn drives off the Nazgûl with torches. Part of the blade remains within the wound, causing Frodo to fall increasingly ill as they travel to Rivendell; Aragorn warns them that, unless treated soon, Frodo will become a wraith himself. As the travellers near their destination, they meet <a href="/wiki/Glorfindel" title="Glorfindel">Glorfindel</a>, an elf-lord from Rivendell, who helps them reach the River <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bruinen" title="Bruinen">Bruinen</a> near Rivendell. But the Nazgûl, all nine now gathered together, ambush the party at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ford_of_Bruinen" title="Ford of Bruinen">Ford of Bruinen</a>. Glorfindel's horse outruns the pursuers and carries Frodo across the Ford. As the Nazgûl attempt to follow, a giant wave commanded by <a href="/wiki/Elrond" title="Elrond">Elrond</a>, the lord of Rivendell, sweeps them away.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Book_II:_The_Ring_Goes_South">Book II: The Ring Goes South</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Fellowship_of_the_Ring&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Book II: The Ring Goes South">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Book II opens in Rivendell at the house of Elrond. Frodo is healed by Elrond and discovers that Bilbo has been residing there. Bilbo asks to see the Ring again, but Frodo resists because of the ring's power, which leads Bilbo to understand at last. Frodo also meets many interesting figures, including Glóin—one of the dwarves who accompanied Bilbo on his journey to the <a href="/wiki/Lonely_Mountain" title="Lonely Mountain">Lonely Mountain</a>—and <a href="/wiki/Legolas" title="Legolas">Legolas</a>, Prince of the <a href="/wiki/Silvan_Elves" title="Silvan Elves">Silvan Elves</a> of <a href="/wiki/Mirkwood" title="Mirkwood">Mirkwood</a>. Frodo learns about the remaining dwarves, including Balin, Ori, and Óin who had not been heard from in some time. Elrond convenes the Council of Elrond, attended by Gandalf, Bilbo, Frodo and many others, including <a href="/wiki/Boromir" title="Boromir">Boromir</a>, son of <a href="/wiki/Denethor" title="Denethor">Denethor</a>, the Steward of Gondor. Glóin explains that Balin had led an expedition to reclaim the old dwarf kingdom of Moria, but they had not heard from him in years. Furthermore, Glóin tells the group that the Nazgûl had come to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dale_(Middle-earth)" title="Dale (Middle-earth)">Dale</a> and the Lonely Mountain looking for Bilbo and the Ring. Legolas then tells the council that Gollum had escaped from his captivity with the Elves and was also abroad and hunting for the Ring. Boromir then stands and relates the details of a dream he and his brother <a href="/wiki/Faramir" title="Faramir">Faramir</a> both received, telling them to seek "the Sword That Was Broken" and "Isildur's Bane" in Rivendell. Elrond then has Frodo bring out the Ring, which is revealed as "Isildur's Bane."</p>
<p>Gandalf explains that he had gone to <a href="/wiki/Isengard" title="Isengard">Isengard</a>, where the wizard <a href="/wiki/Saruman" title="Saruman">Saruman</a>, the chief of all <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Istari" title="Istari">wizards</a> in Middle-earth, dwells, to seek help and counsel. However, Saruman had turned against them, desiring the Ring for himself. Saruman imprisoned Gandalf in his tower, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Orthanc" title="Orthanc">Orthanc</a>, rightly suspecting that Gandalf knew where the Ring was. Gandalf, however, did not yield and managed to escape from Orthanc. He learns that Saruman is not yet in Sauron's service, and is mustering his own force of <a href="/wiki/Orc_(Middle-earth)" title="Orc (Middle-earth)">Orcs</a>. In the <i>Council of Elrond</i>, a plan is hatched to cast the <a href="/wiki/One_Ring" title="One Ring">One Ring</a> into the fires of <a href="/wiki/Mount_Doom" title="Mount Doom">Mount Doom</a> in Mordor, which will destroy the Ring and end Sauron's power for good. Boromir objects and argues for using the Ring to overcome Sauron and relieve Gondor, but Elrond says that the Ring cannot be used for good because of its intrinsic evil and its corrupting power even on those with the best intentions. Frodo offers to undertake this dangerous <a href="/wiki/Quest" title="Quest">quest</a>, and is thus chosen to be the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ring-bearer" title="Ring-bearer">Ring-bearer</a>. He sets forth from Rivendell with eight companions: two <a href="/wiki/Man_(Middle-earth)" title="Man (Middle-earth)">Men</a>, Aragorn and Boromir; Legolas; Gandalf; <a href="/wiki/Gimli_(Middle-earth)" title="Gimli (Middle-earth)">Gimli</a> the Dwarf, the son of Glóin; and Frodo's three Hobbit companions. These Nine Walkers (called the Fellowship of the Ring) are chosen to represent all the free races of Middle-earth and as a balance to the Nazgûl. They are also accompanied by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bill_the_Pony" title="Bill the Pony">Bill the Pony</a>, whom Aragorn and the Hobbits acquired in Bree as a pack horse.</p>
<p>The Fellowship attempt to cross the <a href="/wiki/Misty_Mountains" title="Misty Mountains">Misty Mountains</a> is foiled by heavy snow, and then they are attacked by a host of Wargs that have moved west of the Mountains to hunt for the Ring. Thus, they are forced to take a path under the mountains, through the mines of <a href="/wiki/Moria_(Middle-earth)" title="Moria (Middle-earth)">Moria</a>, the ancient <a href="/wiki/Dwarf_(Middle-earth)" title="Dwarf (Middle-earth)">dwarf</a> kingdom. There, they discover that Balin, Ori, and Óin were all killed by Orcs and other evil creatures that thwarted their attempt to retake Moria. Those same orcs then attack the travellers, and during the battle that ensues, Gandalf encounters a <a href="/wiki/Balrog" title="Balrog">Balrog</a> of <a href="/wiki/Morgoth" title="Morgoth">Morgoth</a>, an ancient demon of fire and shadow. Gandalf challenges the Balrog, but both fall into an abyss. The remaining eight members of the Fellowship escape from Moria and head toward the elf-haven of <a href="/wiki/Lothl%C3%B3rien" title="Lothlórien">Lothlórien</a>, where they are given gifts from the rulers <a href="/wiki/Celeborn" title="Celeborn">Celeborn</a> and <a href="/wiki/Galadriel" title="Galadriel">Galadriel</a> that in many cases prove useful later during the Quest. As Frodo tries to decide the future course of the Fellowship, Boromir succumbs to the Ring's corruptive influence and tries to take it for himself, and Frodo ends up putting on the Ring to escape from Boromir. While the rest of the Fellowship scatters to hunt for Frodo, Frodo decides the Fellowship has to be broken, and he must depart secretly for Mordor alone to prevent the rest of his companions from becoming enslaved by the influence of the Ring. Sam insists on coming along, however, and they set off together to Mordor. The Fellowship is thus broken.</p>

<p>In <i>The Lord of the Rings</i>, Tolkien speaks more often of the "Company" of the Ring rather than the "Fellowship", as reflected in the page references in <a href="/wiki/Robert_Foster_(author)" title="Robert Foster (author)">Robert Foster</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/The_Complete_Guide_to_Middle-earth" title="The Complete Guide to Middle-earth">The Complete Guide to Middle-earth</a></i>.
