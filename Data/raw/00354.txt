<p><i><b>Alice in Wonderland</b></i> is a 1951 American <a href="/wiki/Animation" title="Animation">animated</a> <a href="/wiki/Musical_film" title="Musical film">musical</a> <a href="/wiki/Fantasy_film" title="Fantasy film">fantasy comedy</a>-<a href="/wiki/Adventure_film" title="Adventure film">adventure film</a> produced by <a href="/wiki/The_Walt_Disney_Company" title="The Walt Disney Company">Walt Disney Productions</a> and based primarily on <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Carroll" title="Lewis Carroll">Lewis Carroll</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a></i> with several additional elements from <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking-Glass" title="Through the Looking-Glass">Through the Looking-Glass</a></i>. The 13th in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Animated_Classics" title="Walt Disney Animated Classics">Walt Disney Animated Classics series</a>, the film was released in New York City and London on July 26, 1951. The film features the voices of <a href="/wiki/Kathryn_Beaumont" title="Kathryn Beaumont">Kathryn Beaumont</a> (who later voiced <a href="/wiki/Wendy_Darling" title="Wendy Darling">Wendy Darling</a> in the 1953 Disney film <i><a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan_(1953_film)" title="Peter Pan (1953 film)">Peter Pan</a></i>) as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Alice_(Disney)" title="Alice (Disney)">Alice</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Ed_Wynn" title="Ed Wynn">Ed Wynn</a> as the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mad_Hatter" title="Mad Hatter">Mad Hatter</a>. The theme song, "<a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(song)" title="Alice in Wonderland (song)">Alice in Wonderland</a>", has since become a jazz standard. While the film was critically panned on its initial release, the movie proved to be ahead of its time and has since been regarded as one of Disney's greatest animated classics, as well as one of the best film adaptations of Lewis Carroll's novel.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>On a golden summer day at the bank of a tranquil river in the year 1862, a young girl named Alice grows bored listening to her older sister read aloud from a history book of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_I_of_England" title="William I of England">William I of England</a>. When her sister chastises Alice's daydreaming, Alice tells her kitten Dinah that she can live in a nonsensical magical land called Wonderland. While daydreaming, Alice spots a waistcoat-wearing <a href="/wiki/White_Rabbit" title="White Rabbit">White Rabbit</a> passing by, exclaiming that he is "late for an important date". Alice gives chase and follows him into a rabbit hole, and falls into a large furnished chasm. Her dress catches her fall like a parachute and she floats gently down. She sees the White Rabbit disappear into a tiny door and tries to follow, but the door's talking knob advises her to <a href="/wiki/Resizing_(fiction)" title="Resizing (fiction)">alter her size</a> using a mysterious bottle marked "Drink Me." The contents shrink her to a fraction of her normal size, but the door is locked and the key is out of reach. She then takes a bite of a cookie that says “Eat Me” and grows large enough to fill the entire room. She begins to weep large tears that flood the room. The doorknob then tells Alice to drink from the bottle again, which causes her to shrink once more. Alice falls into the bottle and passes through the door's keyhole and into Wonderland. She meets several strange characters including the <a href="/wiki/Dodo_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Dodo (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">Dodo</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tweedledee_and_Tweedledum" title="Tweedledee and Tweedledum">Tweedledee and Tweedledum</a> who recount the tale of "<a href="/wiki/The_Walrus_and_the_Carpenter" title="The Walrus and the Carpenter">The Walrus and the Carpenter</a>".</p>
<p>Alice eventually finds the White Rabbit in his house; she is sent to fetch some gloves after being mistaken for his housemaid. She eats a cookie and grows into a giant again, getting stuck in the Rabbit's house. She tries to pull herself out, but is too big. The White Rabbit, the Dodo, and chimney sweep <a href="/wiki/Bill_the_Lizard" title="Bill the Lizard">Bill the Lizard</a> believe Alice to be a monster and plot to burn the house down, but Alice escapes by eating a carrot and shrinking down to the size of an insect. She meets a garden of talking flowers who initially welcome her with a song, but then chase her away when an iris accuses her of being a weed. Alice is then instructed by a hookah-smoking <a href="/wiki/Caterpillar_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Caterpillar (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">Caterpillar</a> to eat a part of his mushroom grow back to her original size. Alice decides to keep the remaining pieces of the mushroom on hand.</p>
<p>Alice meets the <a href="/wiki/Cheshire_Cat" title="Cheshire Cat">Cheshire Cat</a> who advises her to visit the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mad_Hatter" title="Mad Hatter">Mad Hatter</a>, <a href="/wiki/March_Hare" title="March Hare">March Hare</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dormouse_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Dormouse (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">Dormouse</a>. The three are hosting a mad tea party and celebrate Alice's "<a href="/wiki/Unbirthday" title="Unbirthday">unbirthday</a>". The White Rabbit appears, but the Mad Hatter and the March Hare destroy his pocketwatch and throw him out of the party. Fed up with all the nonsense, Alice abandons her pursuit of the White Rabbit and decides to go home, but gets lost in the Tulgey Wood. Fearing she is lost forever, Alice breaks down into tears. The Cheshire Cat appears again and leads Alice into a giant <a href="/wiki/Hedge_maze" title="Hedge maze">hedge maze</a> ruled by the tyrannical <a href="/wiki/Queen_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Queen of Hearts (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">Queen of Hearts</a> and her meek husband, the <a href="/wiki/King_of_Hearts_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="King of Hearts (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">King of Hearts</a>. The Queen orders the beheading of anyone who enrages her, and invites Alice to a bizarre croquet match using flamingoes and hedgehogs as the equipment.</p>
<p>The Cheshire Cat appears again and pulls a trick on the Queen, which she accuses Alice of doing. Alice is put on trial and unfairly judged. She then remembers she still has the remains of the Caterpillar's mushroom and consumes both. Quickly growing to a gigantic size, Alice feels free to speak her mind and openly insults the Queen. However, she shrinks back to her normal size just as rapidly. Enraged, the Queen orders her execution. Alice flees and becomes pursued by most of Wonderland's characters until she finally reunites with the Doorknob.</p>
<p>Alice begs to be let through the door. The Doorknob then tells her she is having a dream, forcing Alice to wake herself up just in time. Now realizing that logic and reason exist for a purpose, Alice walks home with her sister and Dinah for tea.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Development">Development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The history of <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney" title="Walt Disney">Walt Disney</a>'s association with Lewis Carroll's <i>Alice</i> books (<i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Alice's Adventures in Wonderland</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Through_the_Looking-Glass" title="Through the Looking-Glass">Through the Looking-Glass</a></i>) stretches all the way back to his childhood. Like many children of the time he was familiar with the <i>Alice</i> books and had read them as a school boy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1923, when Disney was still a 21-year-old filmmaker trying to make a name for himself by working at the <a href="/wiki/Laugh-O-Gram_Studio" title="Laugh-O-Gram Studio">Laugh-O-Gram Studio</a> in Kansas City, making the unsuccessful <a href="/wiki/Short_film" title="Short film">short</a> cartoon series by the name of <i>Newman Laugh-O-Grams.</i> The last of <i>Newman Laugh-O-Grams</i> was called <i><a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Wonderland" title="Alice's Wonderland">Alice's Wonderland</a>,</i> which was loosely inspired by the <i>Alice</i> books. The short featured a live-action girl (<a href="/wiki/Virginia_Davis" title="Virginia Davis">Virginia Davis</a>) interacting in an animated world. Faced with business problems, however, the Laugh-O-Gram Studio went bankrupt in July 1923, and the film was never released to the general public.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrier.2C_Michael_1999_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Barrier.2C_Michael_1999-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> However, Disney left for Hollywood and used the film as a sort of <a href="/wiki/Television_pilot" title="Television pilot">pilot</a> to show to potential distributors. <a href="/wiki/Margaret_J._Winkler" title="Margaret J. Winkler">Margaret J. Winkler</a> of <i>Winkler Pictures</i> agreed to distribute the <i>Alice Comedies,</i> and Disney partnered with his older brother <a href="/wiki/Roy_O._Disney" title="Roy O. Disney">Roy O. Disney</a> and re-hired Kansas City co-workers including <a href="/wiki/Ub_Iwerks" title="Ub Iwerks">Ub Iwerks</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Rudolph_Ising" title="Rudolph Ising">Rudolph Ising</a>, <a href="/wiki/Friz_Freleng" title="Friz Freleng">Friz Freleng</a>, <a href="/wiki/Carman_Maxwell" title="Carman Maxwell">Carman Maxwell</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hugh_Harman" title="Hugh Harman">Hugh Harman</a> to form <i>Disney Bros. Studios</i> (later <i>Walt Disney Productions</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Barrier.2C_Michael_1999_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-Barrier.2C_Michael_1999-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The series began in 1924 before being retired in 1927.</p>
<p>In 1932, Walt began toying with the idea of making an animated feature film and repeatedly turned to the idea of making a feature-length animated/live-action version of <i>Alice</i> starring <a href="/wiki/Mary_Pickford" title="Mary Pickford">Mary Pickford</a>, and even purchased the rights to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sir_John_Tenniel" title="Sir John Tenniel">Sir John Tenniel</a>'s illustrations (still under copyright at the time).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> However, these plans were eventually scrapped in favor of <i><a href="/wiki/Snow_White_and_the_Seven_Dwarfs_(1937_film)" title="Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs (1937 film)">Snow White and the Seven Dwarfs</a></i>, mainly because Disney was put off by <a href="/wiki/Paramount_Pictures" title="Paramount Pictures">Paramount</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(1933_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (1933 film)">1933 live-action adaptation</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> However, Disney did not completely abandon the idea of adapting <i>Alice</i>, and in 1936 made the <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mouse" title="Mickey Mouse">Mickey Mouse</a> cartoon <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Thru_the_Mirror" title="Thru the Mirror">Thru the Mirror</a></i>.</p>
<p>In 1938, after the enormous success of <i>Snow White</i> Walt Disney revived the idea of making an <i>Alice</i> feature and officially registered the title <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> with the <a href="/wiki/Motion_Picture_Association_of_America" title="Motion Picture Association of America">Motion Picture Association of America</a> and hired storyboard artist Al Perkins and art director <a href="/wiki/David_S._Hall_(art_director)" title="David S. Hall (art director)">David S. Hall</a> to develop the story and concept art for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> A storyreel was complete in 1939, but Walt was not pleased as he felt that Hall's drawings resembled Tenniel's drawings too closely making them too difficult to animate and that the overall tone of Perkins' script was too grotesque and dark.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Realising the amount of work needed for <i>Alice in Wonderland</i>, as well as the economic devastation of the <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a> and the production demands of <i><a href="/wiki/Pinocchio_(1940_film)" title="Pinocchio (1940 film)">Pinocchio</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fantasia_(1940_film)" title="Fantasia (1940 film)">Fantasia</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Bambi" title="Bambi">Bambi</a></i>, Walt shelved production on <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> shortly after the screening.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1945, shortly after the war ended, Disney once again revived <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> and assigned British author <a href="/wiki/Aldous_Huxley" title="Aldous Huxley">Aldous Huxley</a> to re-write the script. However Walt felt that Huxley's version was too much of a literal adaptation of Carroll's book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Background_artist" title="Background artist">Background artist</a> <a href="/wiki/Mary_Blair" title="Mary Blair">Mary Blair</a> submitted some concept drawings for <i>Alice in Wonderland</i>. Blair's paintings moved away from Tenneil's sketchy illustrations by taking a <a href="/wiki/Modernism" title="Modernism">modernist</a> stance, using bold and unreal colors. Walt liked Blair's designs, and the script was re-written to focus on comedy, music, and the whimsical side to Carroll's book.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-3"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Disney toyed with the idea of having a live-action/animated version of <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> (in a similar fashion to his <i>Alice Comedies</i>) that would star <a href="/wiki/Ginger_Rogers" title="Ginger Rogers">Ginger Rogers</a> and would utilize the recently developed <a href="/wiki/Sodium_vapor_process" title="Sodium vapor process">sodium vapor process</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lisa_Davis_Waltz" title="Lisa Davis Waltz">Lisa Davis Waltz</a> (who would later voice Anita Radcliffe in <i><a href="/wiki/One_Hundred_and_One_Dalmatians" title="One Hundred and One Dalmatians">One Hundred and One Dalmatians</a></i>) and <a href="/wiki/Luana_Patten" title="Luana Patten">Luana Patten</a> were also considered for the role of Alice.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceB_5-4"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceB-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> However Walt soon realised that he could only do justice to the book by making an all animated-feature, and in 1946, work began on an all-animated version of <i>Alice in Wonderland</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-5"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing">Writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Through various drafts of the script, many sequences that were present in Caroll's book drifted in and out of the story. However, Walt insisted that the scenes themselves keep close to those in the novel since most of its humor is in the writing.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-6"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>One omitted scene from the 1939 treatment of the film occurred outside the <a href="/wiki/Duchess_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Duchess (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">Duchess</a>' manor, where <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor_characters_in_the_Alice_series#Fish_Footman" title="List of minor characters in the Alice series">The Fish Footman</a> is giving a message to the <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor_characters_in_the_Alice_series#Frog_Footman" title="List of minor characters in the Alice series">The Frog Footman</a> to take to the Duchess saying that she is invited to play croquet with The Queen of Hearts. Alice overhears this and sneaks into the kitchen of the manor, where she finds <a href="/wiki/List_of_minor_characters_in_the_Alice_series#The_Duchess.27_Cook" title="List of minor characters in the Alice series">The Duchess' Cook</a> maniacally cooking and the Duchess nursing her baby. The cook is spraying pepper all over the room causing the Duchess and Alice to sneeze and the Baby to cry. After a quick conversation between Alice and the Duchess, the quick-tempered Cook starts throwing pots and pans at the noisy baby. Alice rescues the baby, but as she leaves the house the baby turns into a pig and runs away.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The scene was scrapped for pacing reasons.</p>
<p>Another scene that was deleted from a later draft occurred in Tulgey Wood, where Alice encountered what appeared to be a sinister-looking <a href="/wiki/Jabberwocky" title="Jabberwocky">Jabberwocky</a> hiding in the dark, before revealing himself as a comical looking dragon-like beast with bells and factory whistles on his head. A song "Beware the Jabberwock" was also written. However, the scene was scrapped in favor of <i>The Walrus and the Carpenter</i> poem.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-7"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In desire to keep the Jabberwocky poem in the film, it was made to replace an original song for the Chesire Cat "I'm Mad"</p>
<p>Another deleted scene in Tulgey Wood shows Alice consulting with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_White_Knight_(Through_the_Looking_Glass)" title="The White Knight (Through the Looking Glass)">The White Knight</a>, which was meant to be somewhat of a caracature of Walt Disney. Although Walt liked the scene, he felt it was better if Alice learned her lesson by herself, hence the song "Very Good Advice"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ReferenceA_3-8"><a href="#cite_note-ReferenceA-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Other characters, such as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Mock_Turtle" title="The Mock Turtle">The Mock Turtle</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Gryphon_(Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland)" title="Gryphon (Alice's Adventures in Wonderland)">Gryphon</a> were discarded for pacing reasons.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Music">Music</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Music">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In an effort to retain some of Carroll's imaginative verses and poems, Disney commissioned top songwriters to compose songs built around them for use in the film. A record number of potential songs were written for the film, based on Carroll's verses—over 30—and many of them found a way into the film, if only for a few brief moments. <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> would boast the greatest number of songs included in any Disney film, but because some of them last for mere seconds (like "How Do You Do and Shake Hands", "We'll Smoke the Monster Out", "<a href="/wiki/Jabberwocky" title="Jabberwocky">'Twas Brillig</a>", "The Caucus Race", and others), this fact is frequently overlooked. The original song that Alice was to sing in the beginning was titled "Beyond the Laughing Sky". The song, like so many other dropped songs, was not used by the producers. However, the composition was kept and the lyrics were changed. It later became the title song for <i><a href="/wiki/Peter_Pan_(1953_film)" title="Peter Pan (1953 film)">Peter Pan</a></i> (which was in production at the same time), "The Second Star to the Right".</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(song)" title="Alice in Wonderland (song)">title song</a>, composed by <a href="/wiki/Sammy_Fain" title="Sammy Fain">Sammy Fain</a>, has become a jazz standard, first adopted by <a href="/wiki/Jazz" title="Jazz">jazz</a> pianist <a href="/wiki/Dave_Brubeck" title="Dave Brubeck">Dave Brubeck</a>; featured first in 1952, then on his 1957 Columbia album <i><a href="/wiki/Dave_Digs_Disney" title="Dave Digs Disney">Dave Digs Disney</a></i>.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Izumi_Yukimura" title="Izumi Yukimura">Izumi Yukimura</a> sang her own theme song for the Japanese release of the film.</p>
<p>The song, "In a World of My Own," is included on the <a href="/wiki/Classic_Disney:_60_Years_of_Musical_Magic#Disc_5_.28orange.29" title="Classic Disney: 60 Years of Musical Magic">orange disc</a> of <a href="/wiki/Classic_Disney:_60_Years_of_Musical_Magic" title="Classic Disney: 60 Years of Musical Magic">Classic Disney: 60 Years of Musical Magic</a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release_and_reception">Release and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Release and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>At the time, these creative decisions were met with great criticism from fans of Lewis Carroll, as well as from <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_Kingdom" title="Cinema of the United Kingdom">British film</a> and <a href="/wiki/Literary_criticism" title="Literary criticism">literary critics</a> who accused Disney of "Americanizing" a great work of <a href="/wiki/English_literature" title="English literature">English literature</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Disney was not surprised by the critical reception to <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> – his version of <i>Alice</i> was intended for large family audiences, not literary critics – but despite all the long years of thought and effort, the film met with a lukewarm response at the box office and was a sharp disappointment in its initial release,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> earning an estimated $2.4 million at the US box office in 1951.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Though not an outright disaster, the film was never re-released theatrically in Walt Disney's lifetime, airing instead every so often on network television. In fact, <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> aired as the second episode of Walt Disney's <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Disney_anthology_television_series" title="Disney anthology television series"><i>Disneyland</i></a> TV series on <a href="/wiki/American_Broadcasting_Company" title="American Broadcasting Company">ABC</a> in 1954, in a severely edited version cut down to less than an hour. In <i>The Disney Films</i>, <a href="/wiki/Leonard_Maltin" title="Leonard Maltin">Leonard Maltin</a> relates animator <a href="/wiki/Ward_Kimball" title="Ward Kimball">Ward Kimball</a> felt the film failed because "it suffered from too many cooks – directors. Here was a case of five directors each trying to top the other guy and make his sequence the biggest and craziest in the show. This had a self-canceling effect on the final product."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Walt Disney himself felt that the film failed because Alice the character had no "heart."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Almost two decades after its original release, after the North American success of <a href="/wiki/George_Dunning" title="George Dunning">George Dunning</a>'s animated film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yellow_Submarine_(1968_film)" title="Yellow Submarine (1968 film)">Yellow Submarine</a></i> (1968), Disney's version of <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> suddenly found itself in vogue with the times. In fact, because of Mary Blair's <a href="/wiki/Art_director" title="Art director">art direction</a> and the long-standing association of Carroll's <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> with the <a href="/wiki/Drug_culture" title="Drug culture">drug culture</a>, the feature was re-discovered as something of a "<a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Head_film&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Head film (page does not exist)">head film</a>" (along with <i><a href="/wiki/Fantasia_(1940_film)" title="Fantasia (1940 film)">Fantasia</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Three_Caballeros" title="The Three Caballeros">The Three Caballeros</a></i>) among the college-aged and was shown in various <a href="/wiki/College_town" title="College town">college towns</a> across the country. The Disney company resisted this association, and even withdrew prints of the film from universities, but then, in 1974, the Disney company gave <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> its first theatrical re-release ever, and the company even promoted it as a film in tune with the "<a href="/wiki/Psychedelic_era" title="Psychedelic era">psychedelic</a>" times (mostly from the hit song "<a href="/wiki/White_Rabbit_(song)" title="White Rabbit (song)">White Rabbit</a>" performed by <a href="/wiki/Jefferson_Airplane" title="Jefferson Airplane">Jefferson Airplane</a>). This re-release was so successful it warranted a subsequent re-release in 1981. Its first UK re-release was on July 26, 1979. By the 1980s, the initial consensus of the movie proved to be outdated. The film gained critical acclaim and became one of the most popular Disney movies of all time. Today, not only is the film considered the best movie adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Adventures_in_Wonderland" title="Alice's Adventures in Wonderland">Lewis Carroll's novel</a>, but also one of Disney's great classics.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (August 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>Later, with the advent of the <a href="/wiki/Home_video" title="Home video">home video</a> market, the Disney company chose to make <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> one of the first titles available for the <a href="/wiki/Video_rental_shop" title="Video rental shop">rental market</a> on VHS and <a href="/wiki/Betamax" title="Betamax">Beta</a> and for retail sale on RCA's short-lived CED Videodisc format. The film was released on October 15, 1981 on VHS, <a href="/wiki/Capacitance_Electronic_Disc" title="Capacitance Electronic Disc">CED Videodisc</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Betamax" title="Betamax">Betamax</a> and May 28, 1986 on VHS, Betamax, and Laserdisc in the <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Classics" title="Walt Disney Classics">Walt Disney Classics</a>, (though it was mastered for tape in 1985), staying in general release ever since, with a 40th Anniversary video release in 1991 (this and the 1986 video release were in Disney's <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Classics" title="Walt Disney Classics">Classics Collection</a>), followed by an October 28, 1994 October 14, 1997 VHS and Laserdisc release as a part of the Walt Disney Masterpiece Collection.VHS and Laserdisc release as a part of the Walt Disney Masterpiece Collection. It was released on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Region_1" title="Region 1">Region 1</a> DVD on July 4, 2000 (under the Gold Classic Collection DVD series). A fully restored two-disc "Masterpiece Edition" was released on January 27, 2004, including the full hour-long episode of the Disney television show with Kathryn Beaumont, <a href="/wiki/Edgar_Bergen" title="Edgar Bergen">Edgar Bergen</a>, Charlie McCarthy and Mortimer Snerd, <a href="/wiki/Bobby_Driscoll" title="Bobby Driscoll">Bobby Driscoll</a> and others that promoted the film, computer games, deleted scenes, songs and related materials, which went back on moratorium in January 2009. Disney released a 2-disc special "Un-Anniversary" edition DVD on March 30, 2010 in order to promote <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(2010_film)" title="Alice in Wonderland (2010 film)">the then-new Tim Burton film</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The movie was released in a Blu-ray/DVD combo on February 1, 2011 to celebrate its 60th anniversary<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> featuring a new HD restoration of the movie and many released bonus features.</p>
<p>On the film aggregator website, <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a>, the overall rating of the film is a "fresh" 77% from 26 critical reviews with an average rating of 6.4/10. Its consensus states that "A good introduction to Lewis Carroll's classic, <i>Alice in Wonderland</i> boasts some of the Disney canon's most surreal and twisted images."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The film was nominated for an Academy Award for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Academy_Award_for_Original_Music_Score" title="Academy Award for Original Music Score">Best Scoring of a Musical Picture</a>, but lost to <i><a href="/wiki/An_American_in_Paris_(film)" title="An American in Paris (film)">An American in Paris</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Media_and_merchandise">Media and merchandise</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Media and merchandise">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage_version">Stage version</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Stage version">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>Alice in Wonderland</i> has been condensed into a one act stage version entitled, <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland,_Jr.&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alice in Wonderland, Jr. (page does not exist)">Alice in Wonderland, Jr.</a></i>. The stage version is solely meant for middle and high school productions and includes the majority of the film's songs and others including <i><a href="/wiki/Song_of_the_South" title="Song of the South">Song of the South</a>'</i>s "<a href="/wiki/Zip-a-Dee-Doo-Dah" title="Zip-a-Dee-Doo-Dah">Zip-a-Dee-Doo-Dah</a>", two new reprises of "I'm Late!", and three new numbers entitled "Ocean of Tears", "Simon Says", and "Who Are You?" respectively. This 60–80 minute version is owned by <a href="/wiki/Music_Theatre_International" title="Music Theatre International">Music Theatre International</a> in the Broadway, Jr. Collection along with other Disney Theatrical shows such as <i><a href="/wiki/Aladdin,_Jr." title="Aladdin, Jr.">Disney's Aladdin, Jr.</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Mulan_Jr." title="Mulan Jr.">Disney's Mulan, Jr.</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Beauty_and_the_Beast_(musical)" title="Beauty and the Beast (musical)">Beauty and the Beast</a></i>, Disney's <i><a href="/wiki/High_School_Musical" title="High School Musical">High School Musical</a>: On Stage!</i>, <a href="/wiki/Elton_John" title="Elton John">Elton John</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tim_Rice" title="Tim Rice">Tim Rice</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Aida" title="Aida">Aida</a></i>, and many more.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="References_in_other_Disney_films">References in other Disney films</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: References in other Disney films">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Donald_in_Mathmagic_Land" title="Donald in Mathmagic Land">Donald in Mathmagic Land</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Donald_Duck" title="Donald Duck">Donald Duck</a> wears Alice's dress and has her hairstyle but brown not blond. A larger pencil bird is in the film as well. Bill the Lizard appears as one of Professor Ratigan's henchmen in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Great_Mouse_Detective" title="The Great Mouse Detective">The Great Mouse Detective</a></i>. Alice and several other characters from the film were featured as guests in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/House_of_Mouse" title="House of Mouse">House of Mouse</a></i>, and the Cheshire Cat and the Queen of Hearts were two of the villains featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Mickey%27s_House_of_Villains" title="Mickey's House of Villains">Mickey's House of Villains</a></i>. The Mad Hatter was also featured in <i><a href="/wiki/Mickey%27s_Magical_Christmas:_Snowed_in_at_the_House_of_Mouse" title="Mickey's Magical Christmas: Snowed in at the House of Mouse">Mickey's Magical Christmas: Snowed in at the House of Mouse</a></i>. The Mad Hatter and the March Hare were also featured in several episodes of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bonkers_(cartoon)" title="Bonkers (cartoon)">Bonkers</a></i>. Bill the Lizard, Tweedledum, Cheshire Cat, the doorknob, and an orange-colored version of one of the bulb-horn birds also appear in the 1988 <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_Pictures" title="Walt Disney Pictures">Disney</a> film <i><a href="/wiki/Who_Framed_Roger_Rabbit" title="Who Framed Roger Rabbit">Who Framed Roger Rabbit</a></i>. In the opening of <i><a href="/wiki/Aladdin" title="Aladdin">Aladdin</a></i> the peddler tries to sell a hookah much like the one the Caterpillar used. In <i><a href="/wiki/Aladdin_and_the_King_of_Thieves" title="Aladdin and the King of Thieves">Aladdin and the King of Thieves</a></i>, the Genie turns into the White Rabbit. In <a href="/wiki/Darkwing_Duck" title="Darkwing Duck">Darkwing Duck</a>, there is a villain called Tuskernini, a character that resembles the walrus in some ways. Weebo shows clips of the movie on her screen in <i><a href="/wiki/Flubber_(film)" title="Flubber (film)">Flubber</a></i>. An episode <i><a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mouse_Clubhouse" title="Mickey Mouse Clubhouse">Mickey Mouse Clubhouse</a></i>, entitled "Mickey's Adventures in Wonderland", is based on the film.</p>

<p>Costumed versions of Alice, The Mad Hatter, The White Rabbit, The Queen of Hearts, Tweedledum and Tweedledee make regular appearances at the Disney theme parks and resorts, and other characters from the film (including the Walrus and the March Hare) have featured in the theme parks. More famously, all five Disneyland-style theme parks feature <a href="/wiki/Mad_Tea_Party" title="Mad Tea Party">Mad Tea Party</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Teacups" title="Teacups">teacups</a> ride based on Disney's adaptation of <i>Alice in Wonderland</i>.</p>
<p><i>Alice in Wonderland</i> is also frequently featured in many parades and shows in the Disney Theme Parks, including <a href="/wiki/Main_Street_Electrical_Parade" title="Main Street Electrical Parade">The Main Street Electrical Parade</a>, <a href="/wiki/SpectroMagic" title="SpectroMagic">SpectroMagic</a>, <i><a href="/wiki/Fantasmic!" title="Fantasmic!">Fantasmic!</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Main_Street_Electrical_Parade" title="Main Street Electrical Parade">Dreamlights</a>, The Move It! Shake It! Celebrate It! Street Party and <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney%27s_Parade_of_Dreams" title="Walt Disney's Parade of Dreams">Walt Disney's Parade of Dreams</a>. <a href="/wiki/Disneyland" title="Disneyland">Disneyland</a> contains a <a href="/wiki/Alice_in_Wonderland_(Disneyland_attraction)" title="Alice in Wonderland (Disneyland attraction)">dark ride based on the film</a> in addition to the teacups,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Disneyland_Paris" title="Disneyland Paris">Disneyland Paris</a> also contains a hedge maze called <a href="/wiki/Alice%27s_Curious_Labyrinth" title="Alice's Curious Labyrinth">Alice's Curious Labyrinth</a>, which takes its inspiration from the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> The now-defunct <a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mouse_Revue" title="Mickey Mouse Revue">Mickey Mouse Revue</a>, shown at <a href="/wiki/Walt_Disney_World" title="Walt Disney World">Walt Disney World</a> and later at <a href="/wiki/Tokyo_Disneyland" title="Tokyo Disneyland">Tokyo Disneyland</a>, contained characters and scenes from the film.</p>
<p>The later Tim Burton Disney movie had much less of an effect on the park, only inspiring <a href="/wiki/Mad_T_Party" title="Mad T Party">Mad T Party</a>, a nighttime event at <a href="/wiki/Disney_California_Adventure" title="Disney California Adventure">Disney California Adventure</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Video_games">Video games</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Alice_in_Wonderland_(1951_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Video games">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Disney%27s_Villains%27_Revenge" title="Disney's Villains' Revenge">Disney's Villains' Revenge</a></i>, the Queen of Hearts is one of the villains who tries to turn the ending to her story to where she finally cuts off Alice's head. <i><a href="/wiki/Mickey_Mousecapade" title="Mickey Mousecapade">Mickey Mousecapade</a></i> features various characters from the film. The Japanese version, in fact, is based very heavily on the film, with almost every reference in the game coming from the film.</p>
<p>A video game version of the film was released on <a href="/wiki/Game_Boy_Color" title="Game Boy Color">Game Boy Color</a> by <a href="/wiki/Nintendo" title="Nintendo">Nintendo of America</a> on October 4, 2000 in North America. Additionally, in the video games <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Hearts" title="Kingdom Hearts">Kingdom Hearts</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Hearts:_Chain_of_Memories" title="Kingdom Hearts: Chain of Memories">Kingdom Hearts: Chain of Memories</a></i>, Wonderland is a playable world. Alice is also a major character in the overall plot of the first game due to her role as one of seven "Princesses of Heart". Other characters from the movie that appear include the Queen of Hearts, the Cheshire Cat, the White Rabbit, the Doorknob, the Caterpillar (<a href="/wiki/V_Cast" title="V Cast">V Cast</a> only), and the Deck of Cards. The Mad Hatter and the March Hare appear in portrait form as well. All except the Doorknob also appear in <i>Chain of Memories</i>, albeit in the form of illusions made from the main character's memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> While the world is absent in <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Hearts_II" title="Kingdom Hearts II">Kingdom Hearts II</a></i>, it returns in <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Hearts_358/2_Days" title="Kingdom Hearts 358/2 Days">Kingdom Hearts 358/2 Days</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Kingdom_Hearts_coded" title="Kingdom Hearts coded">Kingdom Hearts coded</a></i>, the latter featuring a digitized version of the world originating from data in <a href="/wiki/Jiminy_Cricket" title="Jiminy Cricket">Jiminy Cricket</a>'s royal journal.</p>
<p>In <i><a href="/wiki/Toy_Story_3:_The_Video_Game" title="Toy Story 3: The Video Game">Toy Story 3: The Video Game</a></i> the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mad_Hatter" title="Mad Hatter">Mad Hatter</a>'s hat is one of the hats you can have the townsfolk wear. <i>In <a href="/wiki/Kinect_Disneyland_Adventures" title="Kinect Disneyland Adventures">Kinect Disneyland Adventures</a></i>, Alice, Mad Hatter, White Rabbit, and the Queen of Hearts make appearances.</p>
<p>A company, "<a href="/wiki/Spicy_Horse" title="Spicy Horse">Spicy Horse</a>" created two video games based off the book version of Alice's Adventures In Wonderland, with a fun twist. Alice is the main character of the video game, and she fights off her bad memories as she traverses through her "new" Wonderland.</p>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Disney_Infinity" title="Disney Infinity">Disney Infinity</a> there are power discs based on Alice in Wonderland.</p>
