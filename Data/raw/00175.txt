<p><i><b>The Name of the Rose</b></i> is the <a href="/wiki/Debut_novel" title="Debut novel">first novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Italy" title="Italy">Italian</a> author <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Eco" title="Umberto Eco">Umberto Eco</a>. It is a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Historical_novel" title="Historical novel">historical</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murder_mystery" title="Murder mystery">murder mystery</a> set in an Italian <a href="/wiki/Monastery" title="Monastery">monastery</a> in the year 1327, an intellectual mystery combining <a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">semiotics</a> in fiction, biblical analysis, medieval studies and literary theory. First published in <a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a> in 1980 under the title <i><b>Il nome della rosa</b></i> <span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Italian" title="Help:IPA for Italian">[il ˈnome ˈdella ˈrɔza]</a></span>, it appeared in English in 1983, translated by <a href="/wiki/William_Weaver" title="William Weaver">William Weaver</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan friar</a> <a href="/wiki/William_of_Baskerville" title="William of Baskerville">William of Baskerville</a> and his <a href="/wiki/Novice#Christianity" title="Novice">novice</a> Adson of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Stift_Melk" title="Stift Melk">Melk</a> travel to a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> monastery in Northern Italy to attend a <a href="/wiki/Disputation" title="Disputation">theological disputation</a>. As they arrive, the monastery is disturbed by a suicide. As the story unfolds, several other monks die under mysterious circumstances. William is tasked by the <a href="/wiki/Abbot" title="Abbot">abbot</a> of the monastery to investigate the deaths, and fresh clues with each murder victim lead William to dead ends and new clues. The protagonists explore a labyrinthine medieval library, discuss the subversive power of laughter, and come face to face with the <a href="/wiki/Medieval_Inquisition" title="Medieval Inquisition">Inquisition</a>, a reaction to the <a href="/wiki/Waldensians" title="Waldensians">Waldensians</a>, a movement which was started in the 12th century and advocated an adherence to the Gospel as taught by Jesus and his disciples. William's innate curiosity and highly developed powers of logic and deduction provide the keys to unraveling the mysteries of the abbey.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Major_themes">Major themes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Major themes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>

<p>Eco, being a <a href="/wiki/Semiotics" title="Semiotics">semiotician</a>, is hailed by semiotics students who like to use his novel to explain their discipline. The techniques of telling stories within stories, partial fictionalization, and purposeful linguistic ambiguity are all apparent. The solution to the central murder mystery hinges on the contents of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Poetics_(Aristotle)" title="Poetics (Aristotle)">book on Comedy</a>, of which <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lost_book" title="Lost book">no copy survives</a>; Eco nevertheless plausibly describes it and has his characters react to it appropriately in their medieval setting – which, though realistically described, is partly based on Eco's scholarly guesses and imagination. It is virtually impossible to untangle fact / history from fiction / conjecture in the novel. Through the motive of this lost and possibly suppressed book which might have aestheticized the farcical, the unheroic and the skeptical, Eco also makes an ironically slanted plea for tolerance and against dogmatic or self-sufficient metaphysical truths – an angle which reaches the surface in the final chapters.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Umberto Eco is a significant <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Postmodern" title="Postmodern">postmodernist</a> theorist and <i>The Name of the Rose</i> is a postmodern novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butler_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-butler-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> The quote in the novel, "books always speak of other books, and every story tells a story that has already been told," refers to a postmodern idea that all texts perpetually refer to other texts, rather than external reality.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butler_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-butler-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> In true postmodern style, the novel ends with uncertainty: "very little is discovered and the detective is defeated" (postscript). William of Baskerville solves the mystery in part by mistake; he thought there was a pattern but it in fact, numerous "patterns" were involved and combined with haphazard mistakes by the killers. William concludes in fatigue that there "was no pattern". Thus Eco turns the modernist quest for finality, certainty and meaning on its head, leaving the overall plot partly the result of accident and arguably without meaning.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-butler_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-butler-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Even the novel's title alludes to the possibility of many meanings or of nebulous meaning; Eco saying in the Postscript he chose the title "because the rose is a symbolic figure so rich in meanings that by now it hardly has any meaning left".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-postscript_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-postscript-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Aedificium.27s_Labyrinth">The Aedificium's Labyrinth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Aedificium's Labyrinth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The mystery revolves around the abbey library, situated in a fortified tower called the Aedificium. This structure has three floors - the ground floor contains the kitchen and <a href="/wiki/Refectory" title="Refectory">refectory</a>, the first floor the <a href="/wiki/Scriptorium" title="Scriptorium">scriptorium</a> and the top floor is occupied by the library.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The two lower floors are open to all, while only the librarian may enter the last. A catalogue of books is kept in the scriptorium, where <a href="/wiki/Manuscript" title="Manuscript">manuscripts</a> are read and copied. A monk who wishes to read a book would send a request to the librarian, who, if he thought the request justified, would bring it to the scriptorium. Finally, the library is in the form of a labyrinth, whose secret only the librarian and the assistant librarian know. <sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Aedificium has four towers at the four cardinal points, and the top floor of each has seven rooms on the outside, surrounding a central room. There are another eight rooms on the outer walls, and sixteen rooms in the centre of the maze. Thus, the library has a total of fifty-six rooms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Each room has a scroll containing a verse from the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a>. The first letter of the verse is the letter corresponding to that room.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> The letters of adjacent rooms, read together, give the name of a region (e.g. <a href="/wiki/Hibernia" title="Hibernia">Hibernia</a> in the West tower), and those rooms contain books from that region. The geographical regions are:</p>

<p>Two rooms have no lettering - the easternmost room, which has an alter, and the central room on the South Tower, the 'finis Africae' (tip of Africa) which contains the most heavily guarded books, and can only be entered through a secret door. The entrance to the library is in the central room of the East Tower, which is connected to the scriptorium by a staircase.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Title">Title</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Title">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Much attention has been paid to the mystery of what the title of the novel refers to. In fact, Eco has stated that his intention was to find a "totally neutral title".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-postscript_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-postscript-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> In one version of the story, when he had finished writing the novel, Eco hurriedly suggested some ten names for it and asked a few of his friends to choose one. They chose <i>The Name of the Rose</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-onLiterature_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-onLiterature-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> In another version of the story, Eco had wanted the neutral title <i>Adso of Melk</i>, but that was vetoed by his publisher, and then the title <i>The Name of the Rose</i> "came to me virtually by chance."</p>
<p>The book's last line, <span lang="la" xml:lang="la"><i>"Stat rosa pristina nomine, nomina nuda tenemus"</i></span> translates as: "The primordial rose abides only in its name; we hold names stripped." The general sense, as Eco pointed out,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> was that from the beauty of the past, now disappeared, we hold only the name. In this novel, the lost "rose" could be seen as <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Poetics_(Aristotle)" title="Poetics (Aristotle)">book on comedy</a> (now forever lost), the exquisite library now destroyed, or the beautiful peasant girl now dead. We only know them by the description Adso provides us — we only have the name of the book on comedy, not its contents. As Adso points out at the end of the fifth day, he does not even know the name of the peasant girl to lament her. Does this mean she does not endure at all?</p>
<p>Perhaps this is a deliberate mis-translation. This quote has also been translated as "Yesterday's Rome stands only in name, we hold only empty names". This line is a verse by twelfth century monk <a href="/wiki/Bernard_of_Cluny" title="Bernard of Cluny">Bernard of Cluny</a> (also known as Bernard of Morlaix). Medieval manuscripts of this line are not in agreement (but the best are secure for Roma). Roma here introduces a "false" quantity, with a short-o for the Classical long-o; thus, a foolish scribe, or a mere fool, wrote the "Classically impeccable" rosa, which betrays the overall context and flexible prosody of Bernard. Eco quotes one Medieval variant verbatim,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> but Eco was not aware at the time of the text more commonly printed in modern editions, in which the reference is to <a href="/wiki/Rome" title="Rome">Rome</a> (<i>Roma</i>), not to a rose (<i>rosa</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> The alternative text, with its context, runs: <i>Nunc ubi Regulus aut ubi Romulus aut ubi Remus? [Remus here also has a "false" long-e]/ Stat Roma pristina nomine, nomina nuda tenemus</i>. This translates as "Where now is Regulus, or Romulus, or Remus? / Primordial Rome abides only in its name; we hold names stripped (of presence)". See the new excellent source edition of 2009: Bernard of Cluny, De contemptu mundi: Une vision du monde vers 1144, ed. and trans. A. Cresson, Témoins de notre histoire (Turnhout, 2009), p. 126 (bk. 1, 952), and note thereto p. 257.</p>
<p>Also the title of the book has been inspired by a poem written by the Mexican poet and mystic <a href="/wiki/Nun" title="Nun">Sor</a> <a href="/wiki/Juana_In%C3%A9s_de_la_Cruz" title="Juana Inés de la Cruz">Juana Inés de la Cruz</a> (1651–1695):</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Rosa que al prado, encarnada,<br/>
te ostentas presuntuosa<br/>
de grana y carmín bañada:<br/>
campa lozana y gustosa;<br/>
pero no, que siendo hermosa<br/>
también serás desdichada.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>which appears in Eco's <i>Postscript to the Name of the Rose</i>, and is translated into English in "Note 1" of that book as:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Red rose growing in the meadow,<br/>
you vaunt yourself bravely<br/>
bathed in crimson and carmine:<br/>
a rich and fragrant show.<br/>
But no: Being fair,<br/>
You will be unhappy soon.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-postscript_3-2"><a href="#cite_note-postscript-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="To_other_works">To other works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: To other works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The historical novel with medieval time setting was re-discovered in Italy a short time before by <a href="/wiki/Italo_Alighiero_Chiusano" title="Italo Alighiero Chiusano">Italo Alighiero Chiusano</a>, with his <i><a href="/wiki/L%27ordalia" title="L'ordalia">L'ordalia</a></i>. The similarities between the two novels (time setting, the fact that both are <i><a href="/wiki/Bildungsroman" title="Bildungsroman">bildungsroman</a></i> [coming-of-age novels], the novice main character, and the older monk mentor), and the notoriety that <i>L′ordalia</i> had in 1979,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> of which an expert on literature such as Umberto Eco was definitely aware, making <i>L'ordalia</i> likely one of the first sources of inspiration of <i>The Name of the Rose</i>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The name of the central character, William of Baskerville, alludes both to the fictional detective <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> (compare <i><a href="/wiki/The_Hound_of_the_Baskervilles" title="The Hound of the Baskervilles">The Hound of the Baskervilles</a></i> – also, Adso's description of William in the beginning of the book resembles, almost word for word, Dr. Watson's description of Sherlock Holmes when he first makes his acquaintance in <i><a href="/wiki/A_Study_in_Scarlet" title="A Study in Scarlet">A Study in Scarlet</a></i>) and to <a href="/wiki/William_of_Ockham" title="William of Ockham">William of Ockham</a> (see the next section).</p>
<p>The name of the narrator, his apprentice Adso of Melk is among other things a pun on Simplicio from <a href="/wiki/Galileo_Galilei" title="Galileo Galilei">Galileo Galilei</a>'s <i>Dialogue</i>; <b>Adso</b> = <b>ad</b> <b>S</b>implici<b>o</b> ("to Simplicio"). Adso's putative place of origin, Melk, is the site of a famous medieval library, at <a href="/wiki/Melk_Abbey" title="Melk Abbey">Melk Abbey</a>.</p>
<p>The blind librarian Jorge from <a href="/wiki/Burgos" title="Burgos">Burgos</a> is a nod to <a href="/wiki/Argentina" title="Argentina">Argentinian</a> writer <a href="/wiki/Jorge_Luis_Borges" title="Jorge Luis Borges">Jorge Luis Borges</a>, a major influence on Eco. Borges was blind during his later years and was also director of <a href="/wiki/National_Library_of_the_Argentine_Republic" title="National Library of the Argentine Republic">Argentina's national library</a>; his <a href="/wiki/Short_story" title="Short story">short story</a> "<a href="/wiki/The_Library_of_Babel" title="The Library of Babel">The Library of Babel</a>" is a clear inspiration for the secret library in Eco's book: "The Library is composed of an indefinite, perhaps infinite, number of <a href="/wiki/Hexagon" title="Hexagon">hexagonal</a> galleries, with enormous ventilation shafts in the middle, encircled by very low railings." Another of Borges's stories, "<a href="/wiki/The_Secret_Miracle" title="The Secret Miracle">The Secret Miracle</a>", features a blind librarian. In addition, a number of other themes drawn from various of Borges's works are used throughout <i>The Name of the Rose</i>: <a href="/wiki/Labyrinth" title="Labyrinth">labyrinths</a>, mirrors, sects and obscure manuscripts and books.</p>
<p>The ending also owes a debt to Borges's "<a href="/wiki/Death_and_the_Compass" title="Death and the Compass">Death and the Compass</a>", in which a detective proposes a theory for the behavior of a murderer. The murderer learns of the theory and uses it to trap the detective. In <i>The Name of the Rose</i>, the librarian Jorge uses William's belief that the murders are based on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Revelation_of_John" title="Revelation of John">Revelation of John</a> to misdirect William, though in Eco's tale, the detective succeeds in solving the crime.</p>
<p>The "poisoned page" gimmick is taken from the film <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Il_gioved%C3%AC" title="Il giovedì">Il giovedì</a></i> (1963) by Italian director <a href="/wiki/Dino_Risi" title="Dino Risi">Dino Risi</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Lolito_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Lolito-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Eco seems also to have been aware of <a href="/wiki/Rudyard_Kipling" title="Rudyard Kipling">Rudyard Kipling</a>'s short story <i><a href="/wiki/Debits_and_Credits_(Kipling)" title="Debits and Credits (Kipling)">The Eye of Allah</a></i>, which touches on many of the same themes – optics, manuscript-illumination, music, medicine, priestly authority and the Church's attitude to scientific discovery and independent thought – and which includes a character named John of <a href="/wiki/Burgos" title="Burgos">Burgos</a>.</p>
<p>Eco spent some time at the <a href="/wiki/University_of_Toronto" title="University of Toronto">University of Toronto</a> while writing the book. Throughout the book, there are <a href="/wiki/Latin" title="Latin">Latin</a> quotes, authentic and apocryphal. There are also discussions of the philosophy of <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a> and of a variety of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Millenarism" title="Millenarism">millenarist</a> heresies, especially those associated with the <a href="/wiki/Fraticelli" title="Fraticelli">fraticelli</a>. Numerous other philosophers are referenced throughout the book, often anachronistically, including <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Wittgenstein" title="Wittgenstein">Wittgenstein</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="To_actual_history_and_geography">To actual history and geography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: To actual history and geography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tright">

<p>William of Ockham, who lived during the time at which the novel is set, first put forward the principle known as "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ockham%27s_Razor" title="Ockham's Razor">Ockham's Razor</a>": often summarised as the <i>dictum</i> that one should always accept as most-likely the simplest explanation that accounts for all the facts (a method used by William of Baskerville in the novel).</p>
<p>The book describes monastic life in the 14th century. The action takes place at a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> abbey during the controversy surrounding the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_poverty" title="Apostolic poverty">Apostolic poverty</a> between branches of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Franciscans" title="Franciscans">Franciscans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dominican_Order" title="Dominican Order">Dominicans</a>; <i>see <a href="/wiki/Franciscan#Renewed_controversy_on_the_question_of_poverty" title="Franciscan">Renewed controversy on the question of poverty</a></i>. The setting was inspired by monumental <a href="/wiki/Sacra_di_San_Michele" title="Sacra di San Michele">Saint Michael's Abbey</a> in <a href="/wiki/Susa_Valley" title="Susa Valley">Susa Valley</a>, <a href="/wiki/Piedmont" title="Piedmont">Piedmont</a> and visited by Umberto Eco.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Verifiability" title="Wikipedia:Verifiability"><span title="The material near this tag failed verification of its source citation(s). (May 2014)">not in citation given</span></a></i>]</sup> The Spirituals abhor wealth, bordering on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Apostolics" title="Apostolics">Apostolics</a> or <a href="/wiki/Dulcinian" title="Dulcinian">Dulcinian</a> <a href="/wiki/Heresy" title="Heresy">heresy</a>. The book highlights this tension that existed within <a href="/wiki/Christianity" title="Christianity">Christianity</a> during the medieval era: the Spirituals, one faction within the Franciscan order, demanded that the Church should abandon all wealth, and some heretical sects began killing the well-to-do, while the majority of the Franciscans and the clergy took to a broader interpretation of the gospel.</p>
<p>A number of the characters, such as the <a href="/wiki/Inquisitor" title="Inquisitor">Inquisitor</a> <a href="/wiki/Bernard_Gui" title="Bernard Gui">Bernard Gui</a>, <a href="/wiki/Ubertino_of_Casale" title="Ubertino of Casale">Ubertino of Casale</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Minorite" title="Minorite">Minorite</a> <a href="/wiki/Michael_of_Cesena" title="Michael of Cesena">Michael of Cesena</a>, are historical figures, though the novel's characterization of them is not always historically accurate. <a href="/wiki/Dante_Alighieri" title="Dante Alighieri">Dante Alighieri</a> and his <i>Comedy</i> are mentioned once in passing. However, Eco notes in a companion book that he had to site the monastery in mountains so it would experience early frosts, in order for that action to take place at a time when Bernard Gui could have been in the area. For the purposes of the plot, he needed a quantity of pig blood, but at that time pigs were not usually slaughtered until a frost had arrived. Later in the year Gui was known to have been away from Italy and could not have participated in the events at the monastery.</p>
<p>Part of the dialogue in the inquisition scene of the novel is lifted bodily from the historical Bernard's own Manual for Inquisitors, the <i>Practica Inquisitionis Heretice Pravitatis</i>, for example the dialogue: "What do you believe?" "What do you believe, my Lord?" "I believe in all that the Creed teaches." "So I believe, My Lord"; whereupon Bernard points out that what Remigius the cellarer is saying is not that <i>he</i> (Remigius himself) believes in the Creed, but that he believes that <i>Bernard</i> believes in the Creed. This is itself an example given by Bernard in his book to warn inquisitors against the slipperiness and manipulation of words by heretics. This usage of Bernard's own book by Eco is self-consciously of a piece with his perspective that "books always speak of other books"; in this case, the author makes his character Bernard speak the historical Bernard's own words literally as the latter's text becomes part of the drama played out in the novel.</p>
<p>Adso's description of the portal of the monastery is recognisably that of the portal of the church at <a href="/wiki/Moissac" title="Moissac">Moissac, France</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2011)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> There is also a quick reference to a famous "Umberto of Bologna" – Umberto Eco himself.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li><i><a href="/wiki/The_Name_of_the_Rose_(film)" title="The Name of the Rose (film)">The Name of the Rose</a></i> was made into a <a href="/wiki/The_Name_of_the_Rose_(film)" title="The Name of the Rose (film)">film</a> in 1986, directed by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Annaud" title="Jean-Jacques Annaud">Jean-Jacques Annaud</a> and starring <a href="/wiki/Sean_Connery" title="Sean Connery">Sean Connery</a> as <a href="/wiki/William_of_Baskerville" title="William of Baskerville">William of Baskerville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Slater" title="Christian Slater">Christian Slater</a> as Adso.</li>
<li>A play adaptation by Grigore Gonţa had its premiere at <a href="/wiki/National_Theatre_Bucharest" title="National Theatre Bucharest">National Theatre Bucharest</a> in 1998, starring <a href="/wiki/Radu_Beligan" title="Radu Beligan">Radu Beligan</a>, <a href="/wiki/Gheorghe_Dinic%C4%83" title="Gheorghe Dinică">Gheorghe Dinică</a> and <a href="/wiki/Ion_Cojar" title="Ion Cojar">Ion Cojar</a>.</li>
<li>A 2 part radio drama based on the novel and adapted by <a href="/wiki/Chris_Dolan" title="Chris Dolan">Chris Dolan</a> was broadcast on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> on the 16th and 23 June 2006.</li>
<li>A radio <a href="/wiki/Parody" title="Parody">parody</a> inspired by the film adaptation was made as part of the <i>Crème de la Crime</i> series by <a href="/wiki/Punt_and_Dennis" title="Punt and Dennis">Punt and Dennis</a>, also on <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a>.</li>
<li>A <a href="/wiki/Spain" title="Spain">Spanish</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> adaptation was released in 1987 under the title <i><a href="/wiki/La_Abad%C3%ADa_del_Crimen" title="La Abadía del Crimen">La Abadía del Crimen</a></i> (<i>The Abbey of Crime</i>).</li>
<li>A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Boardgame" title="Boardgame">boardgame</a> with the same name has been published in 2008 by <a href="/wiki/Ravensburger" title="Ravensburger">Ravensburger</a>. The game is written by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Stephan_Feld&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Stephan Feld (page does not exist)">Stephan Feld</a> and is based on the events of the book.</li>
<li>An <a href="/wiki/Graphic_adventure_game" title="Graphic adventure game">adventure</a> <a href="/wiki/Video_game" title="Video game">video game</a> adaptation titled "Murder in the Abbey" developed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Alcachofa_Soft&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Alcachofa Soft (page does not exist)">Alcachofa Soft</a> and published in 2008 by <a href="/wiki/DreamCatcher_Interactive" title="DreamCatcher Interactive">DreamCatcher Interactive</a>.</li>
<li>The British Melodic Hard Rock band <a href="/wiki/Ten_(band)" title="Ten (band)">Ten</a> released an album entitled <a href="/wiki/The_Name_of_the_Rose_(album)" title="The Name of the Rose (album)">The Name of the Rose</a> in 1996, the eponymus track of which is loosely based around some of the philosophical concepts of the novel.</li>
</ul>
