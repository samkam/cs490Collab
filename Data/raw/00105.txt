<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en">Priory of Sion</h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>
<div class="mw-jump" id="jump-to-nav">

<div class="mw-content-ltr" dir="ltr" id="mw-content-text" lang="en"><div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:221px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Priory_of_Sion_Logo.png"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="300" data-file-width="219" height="300" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/8/8c/Priory_of_Sion_Logo.png" width="219"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Priory_of_Sion_Logo.png" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
The official <a href="/wiki/Emblem" title="Emblem">emblem</a> of the Priory of Sion is partly based on the <a href="/wiki/Fleur-de-lis" title="Fleur-de-lis">fleur-de-lis</a>, which was a symbol particularly associated with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_monarchy" title="French monarchy">French monarchy</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1982_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1982-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The <i><b>Prieuré de Sion</b></i>, translated from <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a> as <b>Priory of Sion</b>, is a name given to numerous groups, both real and fictitious. The most controversial is a fringe <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraternal_organisation" title="Fraternal organisation">fraternal organisation</a>, founded and dissolved in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> in 1956 (abiding by the 1901 French Law of Associations) by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Plantard" title="Pierre Plantard">Pierre Plantard</a>. In the 1960s, Plantard created a fictitious history for that organization, describing it as a <a href="/wiki/Secret_society" title="Secret society">secret society</a> founded by <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_of_Bouillon" title="Godfrey of Bouillon">Godfrey of Bouillon</a> on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Zion" title="Mount Zion">Mount Zion</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Jerusalem" title="Kingdom of Jerusalem">Kingdom of Jerusalem</a> in 1099, conflating it with a genuine historical monastic order, the <a href="/wiki/Abbey_of_Our_Lady_of_Mount_Zion" title="Abbey of Our Lady of Mount Zion">Abbey of Our Lady of Mount Zion</a>. In Plantard's version, the priory was devoted to installing a secret <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloodline" title="Bloodline">bloodline</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Merovingian_dynasty" title="Merovingian dynasty">Merovingian dynasty</a> on the thrones of France and the rest of <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> This <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">myth</a> was expanded upon and popularised by the 1982 <a href="/wiki/Pseudohistory" title="Pseudohistory">pseudohistorical</a> book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Blood_and_the_Holy_Grail" title="The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail">The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1982_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1982-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> and later claimed as factual in the preface of the 2003 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="The Da Vinci Code">The Da Vinci Code</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVC_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-DVC-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After becoming a <i><a href="/wiki/Cause_c%C3%A9l%C3%A8bre" title="Cause célèbre">cause célèbre</a></i> from the late 1960s to the 1980s, the mythical Priory of Sion was exposed as a <a href="/wiki/Ludibrium" title="Ludibrium">ludibrium</a> created by Plantard as a framework for his claim of being the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Catholic_Monarch" title="Great Catholic Monarch">Great Monarch</a> prophesied by <a href="/wiki/Nostradamus" title="Nostradamus">Nostradamus</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> Evidence presented in support of its historical existence and activities before 1956 was discovered to have been forged and then planted in various locations around France by Plantard and his accomplices. Nevertheless, many <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conspiracy_theorist" title="Conspiracy theorist">conspiracy theorists</a> still <a href="/wiki/True-believer_syndrome" title="True-believer syndrome">persist in believing</a> that the Priory of Sion is an age-old <a href="/wiki/Cabal" title="Cabal">cabal</a> that conceals a <a href="/wiki/Subversion" title="Subversion">subversive</a> secret.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Putnam_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Putnam-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The Priory of Sion myth has been exhaustively <a href="/wiki/Debunker" title="Debunker">debunked</a> by journalists and scholars as one of the great <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">hoaxes</a> of the 20th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CBS_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-CBS-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Some <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Skeptic" title="Skeptic">skeptics</a> have expressed concern that the proliferation and popularity of books, websites and films inspired by this hoax have contributed to the problem of <a href="/wiki/List_of_conspiracy_theories" title="List of conspiracy theories">conspiracy theories</a>, <a href="/wiki/Pseudohistory" title="Pseudohistory">pseudohistory</a> and other confusions becoming more <a href="/wiki/Mainstream" title="Mainstream">mainstream</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_13.2F01.2F2008_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson_13.2F01.2F2008-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Others are troubled by the <a href="/wiki/Romanticism" title="Romanticism">romantic</a> <a href="/wiki/Reactionary" title="Reactionary">reactionary</a> ideology unwittingly promoted in these works.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Klinghoffer_2006_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-Klinghoffer_2006-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fraternal_organisation" title="Fraternal organisation">fraternal organisation</a> was founded in the town of <a href="/wiki/Annemasse" title="Annemasse">Annemasse</a>, <a href="/wiki/Haute-Savoie" title="Haute-Savoie">Haute-Savoie</a> in eastern France in 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> The 1901 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/French_law" title="French law">French law</a> of Associations required that the Priory of Sion be registered with the government; although the statutes and the registration Documents are dated 7 May 1956, the registration took place at the <a href="/wiki/Subprefectures_in_France" title="Subprefectures in France">subprefecture</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint-Julien-en-Genevois" title="Saint-Julien-en-Genevois">Saint-Julien-en-Genevois</a> on 25 June 1956 and this was announced in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Journal_Officiel_de_la_R%C3%A9publique_Fran%C3%A7aise" title="Journal Officiel de la République Française">Journal Officiel de la République Française</a></i> on 20 July 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The Headquarters of the Priory of Sion and its journal <i>Circuit</i> were based in the apartment of Plantard, in a social housing block known as Sous-Cassan newly constructed in 1956.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> The founders and signatories inscribed with their real names and aliases were Pierre Plantard, also known as "Chyren", and André Bonhomme, also known as "Stanis Bellas". Bonhomme was the President while Plantard was the Secretary General. The registration documents also included the names of Jean Deleaval as the Vice-President and Armand Defago as the Treasurer. The offices of the Priory of Sion and its journal <i>Circuit</i> were located at Plantard's apartment. The choice of the name "Sion" was based on a popular local feature, a hill south of Annemasse in France, known as Mont Sion, where the founders intended to establish a <a href="/wiki/Retreat_(spiritual)" title="Retreat (spiritual)">retreat</a> center.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Bradley_2006_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Bradley_2006-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> The accompanying title to the name was "<i>Chevalerie d'Institutions et Règles Catholiques d'Union Indépendante et Traditionaliste</i>": this subtitle forms the acronym CIRCUIT and translates in English as Chivalry of Catholic Rules and Institutions of Independent and Traditionalist Union".</p>
<p>The statutes of the Priory of Sion indicate its purpose was to allow and encourage members to engage in studies and <a href="/wiki/Social_work_with_groups#Mutual_aid" title="Social work with groups">mutual aid</a>. The articles of the association expressed the goal of creating a <a href="/wiki/Traditionalist_Catholic" title="Traditionalist Catholic">Traditionalist Catholic</a> <a href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" title="Chivalric order">chivalric order</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jean-Luc_Chaumeil_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jean-Luc_Chaumeil-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Article 7 of the statutes of the Priory of Sion stated that its members were expected "to carry out good deeds, to help the Roman Catholic Church, teach the truth, defend the weak and the oppressed". Towards the end of 1956 the association had planned to forge partnerships with the local Catholic Church of the area which would have involved a school bus service run by both the Priory of Sion and the church of Saint-Joseph in Annemasse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Plantard is described as the President of the Tenants' Association of Annemasse in the issues of <i>Circuit</i>.</p>
<p>The bulk of the activities of the Priory of Sion, however, bore no resemblance to the objectives as outlined in its statutes: <i>Circuit</i>, the official journal of the Priory of Sion, was indicated as a news bulletin of an "organisation for the defence of the rights and the freedom of affordable housing" rather than for the promotion of chivalry-inspired charitable work. The first issue of the journal is dated 27 May 1956, and, in total, twelve issues appeared. Some of the articles took a political position in the local council elections. Others criticised and even attacked real-estate developers of Annemasse.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jean-Luc_Chaumeil_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-Jean-Luc_Chaumeil-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to a letter written by Léon Guersillon the Mayor of Annemasse in 1956, contained in the folder holding the 1956 Statutes of the Priory of Sion in the subprefecture of Saint-Julien-en-Genevois, Plantard was given a six-month sentence in 1953 for fraud.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The formally registered association was dissolved some time after October 1956 but intermittently revived for different reasons by Plantard between 1961 and 1993, though in name and on paper only. The Priory of Sion is considered dormant by the subprefecture because it has indicated no activities since 1956. According to French law, subsequent references to the Priory bear no legal relation to that of 1956 and no one, other than the original signatories, is entitled to use its name in an official capacity. André Bonhomme played no part in the association after 1956. He officially resigned in 1973 when he heard that Plantard was linking his name with the association. In light of Plantard's death in 2000, there is no one who is currently alive who has official permission to use the name.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2002, Gino Sandri (former secretary to Pierre Plantard) announced the revival of Priory of Sion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Myth">Myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Plantard.27s_plot">Plantard's plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Plantard's plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Plantard set out to have the Priory of Sion perceived as a prestigious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esoteric_Christian" title="Esoteric Christian">esoteric Christian</a> chivalric order, whose members would be <a href="/wiki/%C3%89minence_grise" title="Éminence grise">people of influence</a> in the fields of finance, politics and philosophy, devoted to installing the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Catholic_Monarch" title="Great Catholic Monarch">Great Monarch</a>", prophesied by <a href="/wiki/Nostradamus" title="Nostradamus">Nostradamus</a>, on the throne of France. Plantard's choice of the pseudonym "Chyren" was a reference to "Chyren Selin", Nostradamus's <a href="/wiki/Anagram" title="Anagram">anagram</a> for the name for this <a href="/wiki/Christian_eschatology" title="Christian eschatology">eschatological</a> figure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Between 1961 and 1984, Plantard contrived a mythical pedigree for the Priory of Sion claiming that it was the offshoot of a real <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Roman_Catholic_religious_order" title="Roman Catholic religious order">Roman Catholic religious order</a> housed in the Abbey of Our Lady of Mount Zion, which had been founded in the <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Jerusalem" title="Kingdom of Jerusalem">Kingdom of Jerusalem</a> during the <a href="/wiki/First_Crusade" title="First Crusade">First Crusade</a> in 1099 and later absorbed by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesuits" title="Jesuits">Jesuits</a> in 1617. The mistake is often made that this Abbey of Sion was a Priory of Sion, but there is a difference between an <a href="/wiki/Abbey" title="Abbey">abbey</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Priory" title="Priory">priory</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Introvigne_2005_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-Introvigne_2005-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> Calling his original 1956 group "Priory of Sion" presumably gave Plantard the later idea to claim that his organisation had been historically founded by crusading knight <a href="/wiki/Godfrey_of_Bouillon" title="Godfrey of Bouillon">Godfrey of Bouillon</a> on <a href="/wiki/Mount_Zion" title="Mount Zion">Mount Zion</a> near <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">Middle Ages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Putnam_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-Putnam-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<div class="thumb tleft">

<p>Furthermore, Plantard was inspired by a 1960 magazine <i>Les Cahiers de l'Histoire</i> to center his personal genealogical claims, as found in the "Priory of Sion documents", on the <a href="/wiki/Merovingian_dynasty" title="Merovingian dynasty">Merovingian</a> king <a href="/wiki/Dagobert_II" title="Dagobert II">Dagobert II</a>, who had been assassinated in the 7th century.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> He also adopted <i>"<a href="/wiki/Et_in_Arcadia_ego" title="Et in Arcadia ego">Et in Arcadia ego</a> ..."</i>, a slightly altered version of a <a href="/wiki/List_of_Latin_phrases" title="List of Latin phrases">Latin phrase</a> that most famously appears as the title of two paintings by <a href="/wiki/Nicolas_Poussin" title="Nicolas Poussin">Nicolas Poussin</a>, as the <a href="/wiki/Motto" title="Motto">motto</a> of both his family and the Priory of Sion,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> because the tomb which appears in these paintings resembled one in the Les Pontils area near <a href="/wiki/Rennes-le-Ch%C3%A2teau" title="Rennes-le-Château">Rennes-le-Château</a>. This tomb would become a symbol for his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynastic" title="Dynastic">dynastic</a> claims as the last legacy of the Merovingians on the territory of <a href="/wiki/Raz%C3%A8s" title="Razès">Razès</a>, left to remind the select few who have been initiated into these <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mystery_cult" title="Mystery cult">mysteries</a> that the "lost king", Dagobert II, would figuratively come back in the form of a hereditary <a href="/wiki/Pretender" title="Pretender">pretender</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Jean_Delaude_1994_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-Jean_Delaude_1994-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>To give credibility to the fabricated lineage and pedigree, Plantard and his friend, <a href="/wiki/Philippe_de_Ch%C3%A9risey" title="Philippe de Chérisey">Philippe de Chérisey</a>, needed to create "independent evidence". So during the 1960s, they created and deposited a series of <a href="/wiki/False_document" title="False document">false documents</a>, the most famous of which was entitled <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dossiers_Secrets" title="Dossiers Secrets">Dossiers Secrets d'Henri Lobineau</a></i> ("The Secret Files of Henri Lobineau"), at the <a href="/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France" title="Bibliothèque nationale de France">Bibliothèque nationale de France</a> in Paris. During the same decade, Plantard commissioned de Chérisey to forge two medieval <a href="/wiki/Parchment" title="Parchment">parchments</a>. These parchments contained encrypted messages that referred to the Priory of Sion. They adapted, and used to their advantage, the earlier false claims put forward by <a href="/wiki/No%C3%ABl_Corbu" title="Noël Corbu">Noël Corbu</a> that a Catholic priest named <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9renger_Sauni%C3%A8re" title="Bérenger Saunière">Bérenger Saunière</a> had supposedly discovered ancient parchments inside a pillar while renovating his church in <a href="/wiki/Rennes-le-Ch%C3%A2teau" title="Rennes-le-Château">Rennes-le-Château</a> in 1891. Inspired by the popularity of media reports and books in France about the discovery of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dead_Sea_scrolls" title="Dead Sea scrolls">Dead Sea scrolls</a> in the <a href="/wiki/West_Bank" title="West Bank">West Bank</a>, they hoped this same theme would attract attention to their parchments.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChaumTest_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-ChaumTest-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Their version of the parchments was intended to prove Plantard's claims about the Priory of Sion being a medieval society that was the source of the "<a href="/wiki/Alfeios" title="Alfeios">underground stream</a>" of <a href="/wiki/Esotericism" title="Esotericism">esotericism</a> in Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Putnam_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-Putnam-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Plantard then enlisted the aid of author <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_S%C3%A8de" title="Gérard de Sède">Gérard de Sède</a> to write a book based on his unpublished manuscript and forged parchments,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ChaumTest_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-ChaumTest-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> alleging that Saunière had discovered a link to a hidden treasure. The 1967 book <i>L'or de Rennes, ou La vie insolite de Bérenger Saunière, curé de Rennes-le-Château</i> ("The Gold of Rennes, or The Strange Life of Bérenger Saunière, Priest of Rennes-le-Château"), which was later published in paperback under the title <i>Le Trésor Maudit de Rennes-le-Château</i> ("The Accursed Treasure of Rennes-le-Château") in 1968, became a popular read in France. It included copies of the found parchments (the originals were of course never produced), though it did not provide the decoded hidden texts contained within them. One of the Latin texts in the parchments was copied from the <i><a href="/wiki/New_Testament" title="New Testament">Novum Testamentum</a></i>, an attempted restoration of the <a href="/wiki/Vulgate" title="Vulgate">Vulgate</a> by <a href="/wiki/John_Wordsworth" title="John Wordsworth">John Wordsworth</a> and Henry White.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The other text was copied from the <a href="/wiki/Codex_Bezae" title="Codex Bezae">Codex Bezae</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Based on the wording used, the versions of the Latin texts found in the parchments can be shown to have been copied from books first published in 1889 and 1895, which is problematic considering that de Sède's book was trying to make a case that these documents were centuries old.</p>
<p>In 1969, English actor and science-fiction scriptwriter <a href="/wiki/Henry_Lincoln" title="Henry Lincoln">Henry Lincoln</a> became intrigued after reading <i>Le Trésor Maudit</i>. He discovered one of the encrypted messages, which read "<i>À Dagobert II Roi et à Sion est ce trésor, et il est là mort</i>" ("To Dagobert II, King, and to Sion belongs this treasure and he is there dead"). This was possibly an allusion to the tomb and <a href="/wiki/Shrine" title="Shrine">shrine</a> of <a href="/wiki/Sigebert_IV" title="Sigebert IV">Sigebert IV</a>, a real or mythical son of Dagobert II which would not only prove that the Merovingian dynasty did not end with the death of the king but that the Priory of Sion has been entrusted with the duty to protect his <a href="/wiki/Relic" title="Relic">relics</a> like a treasure.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1982_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1982-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Lincoln expanded on the conspiracy theories, writing his own books on the subject, and inspiring and presenting three <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">BBC Two</a> <a href="/wiki/Chronicle_(UK_TV_series)" title="Chronicle (UK TV series)">Chronicle</a> documentaries between 1972-1979 about the alleged mysteries of the Rennes-le-Château area. In response to a tip from Gérard de Sède, Lincoln claims he was also the one who discovered the <i>Dossiers Secrets</i>, a series of planted genealogies which appeared to further confirm the link with the extinct Merovingian bloodline. The documents claimed that the Priory of Sion and the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Knights Templar</a> were two fronts of one unified organisation with the same leadership until 1188.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1982_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1982-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Letters in existence dating from the 1960s written by Plantard, de Chérisey and de Sède to each other confirm that the three were engaging in an out-and-out <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">hoax</a>. The letters describe schemes to combat criticisms of their various allegations and ways they would make up new allegations to try to keep the hoax alive. These letters (totalling over 100) are in the possession of French researcher Jean-Luc Chaumeil, who has also retained the original envelopes. A letter later discovered at the subprefecture of Saint-Julien-en-Genevois also indicated that Plantard had a criminal conviction as a <a href="/wiki/Confidence_trick" title="Confidence trick">confidence trickster</a> .<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Holy_Blood_and_the_Holy_Grail"><i>The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote boilerplate seealso">For more details on this topic, see <a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Blood_and_the_Holy_Grail" title="The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail">The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail</a>.</div>
<p>After reading <i>Le Trésor Maudit</i>, Lincoln persuaded <a href="/wiki/BBC_Two" title="BBC Two">BBC Two</a> to devote three episodes in their <i>Chronicle</i> documentary series to the topic. These became quite popular and generated thousands of responses. Lincoln then joined forces with <a href="/wiki/Michael_Baigent" title="Michael Baigent">Michael Baigent</a> and <a href="/wiki/Richard_Leigh_(author)" title="Richard Leigh (author)">Richard Leigh</a> for further research. This led them to the pseudohistorical <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dossiers_Secrets" title="Dossiers Secrets">Dossiers Secrets</a></i> at the <a href="/wiki/Biblioth%C3%A8que_nationale_de_France" title="Bibliothèque nationale de France">Bibliothèque nationale de France</a>, which though alleging to portray hundreds of years of medieval history, were actually all written by Plantard and de Chérisey under the pseudonym of "Philippe Toscan du Plantier". Unaware that the documents had been forged, Lincoln, Baigent and Leigh used them as a major source for their 1982 controversial non-fiction book <i>The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail,</i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1982_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1982-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> in which they presented the following <a href="/wiki/Factoid" title="Factoid">myths as facts</a> to support their hypotheses:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Thompson_13.2F01.2F2008_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-Thompson_13.2F01.2F2008-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>there is a <a href="/wiki/Secret_society" title="Secret society">secret society</a> known as the Priory of Sion, which has a long history starting in 1099, and had illustrious <a href="/wiki/Priory_of_Sion#Alleged_Grand_Masters" title="Priory of Sion">Grand Masters</a> including <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a>;</li>
<li>it created the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Knights Templar</a> as its military arm and financial branch; and</li>
<li>it is devoted to installing the <a href="/wiki/Merovingian_dynasty" title="Merovingian dynasty">Merovingian dynasty</a>, that ruled the <a href="/wiki/Franks" title="Franks">Franks</a> from 457 to 751, on the thrones of France and the rest of Europe.</li>
</ul>
<p>However, the authors re-interpreted the <i>Dossiers Secrets</i> in the light of their own interest in undermining the Roman Catholic Church's institutional reading of <a href="/wiki/Judeo-Christian" title="Judeo-Christian">Judeo-Christian</a> history.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> Contrary to Plantard's initial <a href="/wiki/French_Israelism" title="French Israelism">Franco-Israelist</a> claim that the Merovingians were only descended from the <a href="/wiki/Tribe_of_Benjamin" title="Tribe of Benjamin">Tribe of Benjamin</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> they asserted that:</p>
<ul>
<li>the Priory of Sion protects Merovingian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynast" title="Dynast">dynasts</a> because they may be the lineal <a href="/wiki/Jesus_bloodline" title="Jesus bloodline">descendants of the historical Jesus</a> and his alleged wife, <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>, traced further back to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_David" title="King David">King David</a>;</li>
<li>the legendary <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a> is simultaneously the womb of <a href="/wiki/Saint" title="Saint">saint</a> Mary Magdalene and the sacred <a href="/wiki/Royal_descent" title="Royal descent">royal bloodline</a> she gave birth to; and</li>
<li>the Church tried to kill off all remnants of this bloodline and their supposed guardians, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cathars" title="Cathars">Cathars</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Templars</a>, so <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Popes" title="Popes">popes</a> could hold the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Episcopal_throne" title="Episcopal throne">episcopal throne</a> through the <a href="/wiki/Apostolic_succession" title="Apostolic succession">apostolic succession</a> of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Peter</a> without fear of it ever being <a href="/wiki/Usurper" title="Usurper">usurped</a> by an <a href="/wiki/Antipope" title="Antipope">antipope</a> from the <a href="/wiki/Primogeniture" title="Primogeniture">hereditary succession</a> of Mary Magdalene.</li>
</ul>
<p>The authors therefore concluded that the modern goals of the Priory of Sion are:</p>
<ul>
<li>the public revelation of the tomb and shrine of <a href="/wiki/Sigebert_IV" title="Sigebert IV">Sigebert IV</a> as well as the <a href="/wiki/Arch_of_Titus#Significance_in_Jewish_history" title="Arch of Titus">lost treasure</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Temple_in_Jerusalem" title="Temple in Jerusalem">Temple in Jerusalem</a>, which supposedly contains genealogical records that prove the Merovingian dynasty was of the <a href="/wiki/Davidic_line" title="Davidic line">Davidic line</a>, to facilitate Merovingian restoration in France;</li>
<li>the re-institutionalization of <a href="/wiki/Chivalry" title="Chivalry">chivalry</a> and the promotion of <a href="/wiki/Pan-European_nationalism" title="Pan-European nationalism">pan-European nationalism</a>;</li>
<li>the establishment of a <a href="/wiki/Theocracy" title="Theocracy">theocratic</a> "<a href="/wiki/United_States_of_Europe" title="United States of Europe">United States of Europe</a>": a Holy European Empire politically and religiously unified through the <a href="/wiki/Imperial_cult" title="Imperial cult">imperial cult</a> of a Merovingian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Great_Catholic_Monarch" title="Great Catholic Monarch">Great Monarch</a> who occupies both the throne of Europe and the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Holy See</a>; and</li>
<li>the actual governance of Europe residing with the Priory of Sion through a <a href="/wiki/Single-party_state" title="Single-party state">single-party</a> <a href="/wiki/European_Parliament" title="European Parliament">European Parliament</a>.</li>
</ul>
<p>The authors also incorporated the <a href="/wiki/Antisemitism" title="Antisemitism">antisemitic</a> and <a href="/wiki/Anti-Masonry" title="Anti-Masonry">anti-Masonic</a> tract known as <i><a href="/wiki/The_Protocols_of_the_Elders_of_Zion" title="The Protocols of the Elders of Zion">The Protocols of the Elders of Zion</a></i> into their story, concluding that it was actually based on the master plan of the Priory of Sion. They presented it as the most persuasive piece of evidence for the existence and activities of the Priory of Sion by arguing that:</p>
<ul>
<li>the original text on which the published version of <i>The Protocols of the Elders of Zion</i> was based had nothing to do with <a href="/wiki/Judaism" title="Judaism">Judaism</a> or an "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jewish_world_domination" title="Jewish world domination">international Jewish conspiracy</a>". It issued from a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masonic_body" title="Masonic body">Masonic body</a> practicing the <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Rite" title="Scottish Rite">Scottish Rite</a> which incorporated the word "<a href="/wiki/Zion" title="Zion">Zion</a>" in its name;</li>
<li>the original text was not intended to be released publicly, but was a program for gaining control of <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Freemasonry</a> as part of a strategy to infiltrate and reorganise church and state according to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Esoteric_Christian" title="Esoteric Christian">esoteric Christian</a> principles;</li>
<li>after a failed attempt to gain influence in the court of Tsar <a href="/wiki/Nicholas_II_of_Russia" title="Nicholas II of Russia">Nicholas II of Russia</a>, <a href="/wiki/Sergei_Nilus" title="Sergei Nilus">Sergei Nilus</a> changed the original text to forge an inflammatory tract in 1903 to discredit the esoteric clique around <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Papus" title="Papus">Papus</a> by implying they were <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Judaeo-Masonic_conspiracy_theory" title="Judaeo-Masonic conspiracy theory">Judaeo-Masonic conspirators</a>; and</li>
<li>some esoteric Christian elements in the original text were ignored by Nilus and hence remained unchanged in the <a href="/wiki/Antisemitic_canard" title="Antisemitic canard">antisemitic canard</a> he published.</li>
</ul>
<p>In reaction to this <a href="/wiki/Memetic_engineering" title="Memetic engineering">memetic synthesis</a> of investigative journalism with religious <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Conspiracism" title="Conspiracism">conspiracism</a>, many secular conspiracy theorists added the Priory of Sion to their list of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Secret_societies" title="Secret societies">secret societies</a> collaborating or competing to manipulate political happenings from behind the scenes in their bid for <a href="/wiki/Hegemony" title="Hegemony">world domination</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Moench_1995_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Moench_1995-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> Some <a href="/wiki/List_of_occultists" title="List of occultists">occultists</a> speculated that the emergence of the Priory of Sion and Plantard closely follows <i>The Prophecies by M. Michel Nostradamus</i> (unaware that Plantard was <a href="/wiki/Self-fulfilling_prophecy" title="Self-fulfilling prophecy">intentionally trying to fulfill</a> them).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-34"><a href="#cite_note-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> Fringe <a href="/wiki/Christian_eschatology" title="Christian eschatology">Christian eschatologists</a> countered that it was a fulfilment of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prophecies" title="Prophecies">prophecies</a> found in the <a href="/wiki/Book_of_Revelation" title="Book of Revelation">Book of Revelation</a> and further proof of an <a href="/wiki/Antichrist" title="Antichrist">anti-Christian</a> conspiracy of epic proportions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Aho_1997_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Aho_1997-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>However, professional historians and scholars from related fields do not accept <i>The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail</i> as a serious dissertation.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-36"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>French authors like Franck Marie (1978),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> Pierre Jarnac (1985),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-38"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> (1988),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-39"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup> Jean-Luc Chaumeil (1994),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-40"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> and more recently Marie-France Etchegoin and Frédéric Lenoir (2004),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-41"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Massimo_Introvigne" title="Massimo Introvigne">Massimo Introvigne</a> (2005),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> Jean-Jacques Bedu (2005),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup> and Bernardo Sanchez Da Motta (2005),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-44"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> have never taken Plantard and the Priory of Sion as seriously as Lincoln, Baigent and Leigh. They eventually concluded that it was all a <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">hoax</a>, outlining in detail the reasons for their verdict, and giving detailed evidence that the <i>Holy Blood</i> authors had not reported comprehensively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller_2004_45-0"><a href="#cite_note-Miller_2004-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> They imply that this evidence had been ignored by Lincoln, Baigent, and Leigh to bolster the mythical version of the Priory's history that was developed by Plantard during the early 1960s after meeting author <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_S%C3%A8de" title="Gérard de Sède">Gérard de Sède</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Miller_2004_45-1"><a href="#cite_note-Miller_2004-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Messianic_Legacy"><i>The Messianic Legacy</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: The Messianic Legacy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1986, Lincoln, Baigent and Leigh published <i>The Messianic Legacy</i>, a sequel to <i>The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail</i>. The authors assert that the Priory of Sion is not only <i>the</i> archetypal <a href="/wiki/Cabal" title="Cabal">cabal</a> but an ideal repository of the cultural legacy of <a href="/wiki/Jewish_messianism" title="Jewish messianism">Jewish messianism</a> that could end the “crisis of <a href="/wiki/Meaning_of_life" title="Meaning of life">meaning</a>” within the <a href="/wiki/Western_world" title="Western world">Western world</a> by providing a Merovingian <a href="/wiki/Sacred_king" title="Sacred king">sacred king</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Messiah" title="Messiah">messianic figure</a> in which the West and, by extension, humanity can place its trust. However, the authors are led to believe by Plantard that he has resigned as <a href="/wiki/Grand_Master_(order)" title="Grand Master (order)">Grand Master</a> of the Priory of Sion in 1984 and that the organisation has since gone underground in reaction to both an internal power struggle between Plantard and an “Anglo-American contingent” as well as a campaign of <a href="/wiki/Character_assassination" title="Character assassination">character assassination</a> against Plantard in the press and books written by skeptics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1987_46-0"><a href="#cite_note-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1987-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Although Lincoln, Baigent and Leigh remain convinced that the pre-1956 history of the Priory of Sion is true, they confess to the possibility that all of Plantard's claims about a post-1956 Priory of Sion were part of an elaborate <a href="/wiki/Hoax" title="Hoax">hoax</a> to build a <a href="/wiki/Cult_of_personality" title="Cult of personality">cult of personality</a> and <a href="/wiki/The_CIA_and_the_Cult_of_Intelligence#In_popular_culture" title="The CIA and the Cult of Intelligence">cult of intelligence</a> around himself in French <a href="/wiki/Esotericism" title="Esotericism">esoteric</a> circles.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1987_46-1"><a href="#cite_note-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1987-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Revised_myth">Revised myth</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Revised myth">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1989, Plantard tried but failed to salvage his reputation and agenda as a <a href="/wiki/Mystagogue" title="Mystagogue">mystagogue</a> in esoteric circles by claiming that the Priory of Sion had actually been founded in 1681 at <a href="/wiki/Rennes-le-Ch%C3%A2teau" title="Rennes-le-Château">Rennes-le-Château</a>, and was focused more on harnessing the <a href="/wiki/Paranormal" title="Paranormal">paranormal</a> power of <a href="/wiki/Ley_line" title="Ley line">ley lines</a> and sunrise lines,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> and a promontory called "Roc Noir" (Black Rock) in the area,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-48"><a href="#cite_note-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup> than installing a Merovingian <a href="/wiki/Pretender" title="Pretender">pretender</a> on the restored throne of France. In 1990, Plantard revised himself by claiming he was only descended from a <a href="/wiki/Cadet_branch" title="Cadet branch">cadet branch</a> of the line of Dagobert II, while arguing that the direct descendant was really <a href="/wiki/Otto_von_Habsburg" title="Otto von Habsburg">Otto von Habsburg</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-49"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-50"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Pelat_Affair">Pelat Affair</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Pelat Affair">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In September 1993, while investigative judge Thierry Jean-Pierre was investigating the activities of multi-millionaire Roger-Patrice Pelat in the context of the Pechiney-Triangle Affair, he was informed that Pelat may have once been <a href="/wiki/Grand_Master_(order)" title="Grand Master (order)">Grand Master</a> of an esoteric society known as the Priory of Sion. Pelat's name had been on Plantard's list of Grand Masters since 1989. In fact, Pelat had died in 1989, while he was being indicted for <a href="/wiki/Insider_trading" title="Insider trading">insider trading</a> - "<i><a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/d%C3%A9lit_d%27initi%C3%A9" title="fr:délit d'initié">délit d'initié</a></i>" in French. Plantard may have been naive about financial terms and interpreted the word "<i><a class="extiw" href="//fr.wikipedia.org/wiki/initi%C3%A9" title="fr:initié">initié</a></i>" esoterically, to mean "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Initiate" title="Initiate">initiate</a>". Following a long established pattern, Plantard "recruited" the "initiate" Pelat soon after his death and included him as the most recent Priory of Sion Grand Master.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lepoint_51-0"><a href="#cite_note-lepoint-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup> Plantard had first claimed that Pelat had been a Grand Master in a Priory of Sion pamphlet dated 8 March 1989, then claimed it again later in a 1990 issue of <i>Vaincre</i>, the revived publication of Alpha Galates, a pseudo-<a href="/wiki/Chivalric_order" title="Chivalric order">chivalric order</a> created by Plantard in <a href="/wiki/Vichy_France" title="Vichy France">Vichy France</a> to support the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/National_Revolution" title="National Revolution">National Revolution</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-52"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-53"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Pelat had been a friend of <a href="/wiki/Fran%C3%A7ois_Mitterrand" title="François Mitterrand">François Mitterrand</a>, then <a href="/wiki/President_of_France" title="President of France">President of France</a>, and at the centre of a scandal involving French Prime Minister <a href="/wiki/Pierre_B%C3%A9r%C3%A9govoy" title="Pierre Bérégovoy">Pierre Bérégovoy</a>. As an investigative judge, Jean-Pierre could not dismiss any information brought to his attention pertaining to the case, especially if it might have led to a scandal similar to the one implicating an illegal <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Masonic_lodge" title="Masonic lodge">Masonic lodge</a> named <a href="/wiki/Propaganda_Due" title="Propaganda Due">Propaganda Due</a> in the 1982 <a href="/wiki/Banco_Ambrosiano" title="Banco Ambrosiano">Banco Ambrosiano</a> bank failure in Italy, Jean-Pierre ordered a search of Plantard's home. The search turned up a hoard of false documents, including some proclaiming Plantard the true <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/King_of_France" title="King of France">king of France</a>. Plantard admitted under oath that he had fabricated everything, including Pelat's involvement with the Priory of Sion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-lepoint_51-1"><a href="#cite_note-lepoint-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Philippe_Lapr.C3.A9v.C3.B4te_1996_p._140_54-0"><a href="#cite_note-Philippe_Lapr.C3.A9v.C3.B4te_1996_p._140-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> Plantard was threatened with legal action by the Pelat family and therefore disappeared to his house in <a href="/wiki/Southern_France" title="Southern France">southern France</a>. He was 74 years old at the time. Nothing more was heard of him until he died in Paris on 3 February 2000.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Octonovo_55-0"><a href="#cite_note-Octonovo-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sandri_revival">Sandri revival</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Sandri revival">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 27 December 2002, an open letter announced the revival of the Priory of Sion as an <a href="/wiki/Traditionalist_School" title="Traditionalist School">integral traditionalist</a> esoteric society, which stated that: "The Commanderies of Saint-Denis, Millau, Geneva and Barcelona are fully operative. According to the Tradition, the first Commanderie is under the direction of a woman", claiming there were 9,841 members.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-56"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> It was signed by Gino Sandri (who claims to be Plantard's former private secretary) under the title of General Secretary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buccholtzer.2C_2008_57-0"><a href="#cite_note-Buccholtzer.2C_2008-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> and by "P. Plantard" (Le Nautonnier, G. Chyren). Sandri is a well-versed <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Occultist" title="Occultist">occultist</a> who has spent his life infiltrating esoteric societies only to get expelled from them.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Buccholtzer.2C_2008_57-1"><a href="#cite_note-Buccholtzer.2C_2008-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup> After interviewing Sandri, independent researcher Laurent Octonovo Buchholtzer wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>I’ve personally met this Gino Sandri on one occasion, and I had the opportunity to have a really good talk with him, but I think that he's simply seeking attention. He seemed to me to be something of a mythomaniac, which would certainly be an excellent qualification for being Secretary of the Priory of Sion. During our conversation he said something in passing that I found quite extraordinary. He said, “Ultimately, what is the Priory of Sion? It's nothing more than a well-known brand name, but with goodness knows what behind it?” He gave a good brief account of the phenomenon of the Priory of Sion. Thanks to Dan Brown, hundreds of millions of people now have “brand awareness”, and several million of them seem to take it seriously.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Octonovo_55-1"><a href="#cite_note-Octonovo-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Da_Vinci_Code"><i>The Da Vinci Code</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: The Da Vinci Code">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote boilerplate seealso">For more details on this topic, see <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Priory_of_Sion_in_the_Da_Vinci_Code" title="The Priory of Sion in the Da Vinci Code">The Priory of Sion in the Da Vinci Code</a>.</div>
<p>As a result of <a href="/wiki/Dan_Brown" title="Dan Brown">Dan Brown</a>'s best-selling 2003 <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_fiction" title="Conspiracy fiction">conspiracy fiction</a> novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="The Da Vinci Code">The Da Vinci Code</a></i> and the subsequent 2006 <a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code_(film)" title="The Da Vinci Code (film)">film</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-DVC_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-DVC-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> there was a new level of public interest in the Priory of Sion. Brown's novel promotes the mythical version of the Priory but departs from the ultimate conclusions presented in <i>The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail</i>. Rather than plotting to create a Federal Europe ruled by a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merovingian" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> <a href="/wiki/Sacred_king" title="Sacred king">sacred king</a> descended from the <a href="/wiki/Historical_Jesus" title="Historical Jesus">historical Jesus</a>, the Priory of Sion initiates its members into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mystery_cult" title="Mystery cult">mystery cult</a> seeking to restore the <a href="/wiki/Feminist_theology" title="Feminist theology">feminist theology</a> necessary for a complete understanding of <a href="/wiki/Early_Christianity" title="Early Christianity">early Christianity</a>, which was supposedly suppressed by the Roman Catholic Church. The author has presented this speculation as fact in his non-fiction preface, as well as in his public appearances and interviews.</p>
<p>Furthermore, in their 1987 sequel <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Messianic_Legacy" title="The Messianic Legacy">The Messianic Legacy</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1987_46-2"><a href="#cite_note-Baigent.2C_Leigh_.26_Lincoln_1987-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Lincoln, Baigent and Leigh suggested that there was a current conflict between the Priory of Sion and the <a href="/wiki/Sovereign_Military_Order_of_Malta" title="Sovereign Military Order of Malta">Sovereign Military Order of Malta</a>, which they speculated might have originated from an earlier rivalry between the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Knights Templar</a> and <a href="/wiki/Knights_Hospitaller" title="Knights Hospitaller">Knights Hospitaller</a> during the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusades</a>. However, for the dramatic structure of <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>, Brown chose the <a href="/wiki/Controversies_about_Opus_Dei" title="Controversies about Opus Dei">controversial</a> Roman Catholic <a href="/wiki/Personal_prelature" title="Personal prelature">prelature</a> <a href="/wiki/Opus_Dei" title="Opus Dei">Opus Dei</a> as the <a href="/wiki/The_Assassini#Similarities" title="The Assassini">Assassini</a>-like nemesis of the Priory of Sion, despite the fact that no author had ever argued that there is a conflict between these two groups.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="The_Sion_Revelation"><i>The Sion Revelation</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: The Sion Revelation">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Further conspiracy theories were reported in the 2006 non-fiction book <i>The Sion Revelation: The Truth About the Guardians of Christ's Sacred Bloodline</i> by <a href="/wiki/Lynn_Picknett" title="Lynn Picknett">Lynn Picknett</a> and Clive Prince (authors of the 1997 non-fiction book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Templar_Revelation" title="The Templar Revelation">The Templar Revelation</a></i>, the principal source for Dan Brown's claims about hidden messages in the work of <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Picknett_.26_Prince_2006_58-0"><a href="#cite_note-Picknett_.26_Prince_2006-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup> They accepted that the pre-1956 history of the Priory of Sion was a hoax created by Plantard, and that his claim that he was a Merovingian <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Dynast" title="Dynast">dynast</a> was a lie. However, they insist that this was part of a complex <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_herring_(narrative)" title="Red herring (narrative)">red herring</a> intended to distract the public from the hidden agenda of Plantard and his <a href="/wiki/Agent_handling" title="Agent handling">"controllers"</a>. They argue that the Priory of Sion was a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Front_organisation" title="Front organisation">front organisation</a> for one of the many crypto-political societies which have been plotting to create a "<a href="/wiki/United_States_of_Europe" title="United States of Europe">United States of Europe</a>" in line with French <a href="/wiki/List_of_occultists" title="List of occultists">occultist</a> <a href="/wiki/Alexandre_Saint-Yves_d%27Alveydre" title="Alexandre Saint-Yves d'Alveydre">Alexandre Saint-Yves d'Alveydre</a>'s <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Synarchist" title="Synarchist">synarchist</a> vision of an ideal form of government.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Bloodline_movie"><i>Bloodline</i> movie</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Priory_of_Sion&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Bloodline movie">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The 2008 documentary <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bloodline_(documentary)" title="Bloodline (documentary)">Bloodline</a></i><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-59"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> by <a href="/wiki/Bruce_Burgess" title="Bruce Burgess">Bruce Burgess</a>, a filmmaker with an interest in <a href="/wiki/Paranormal" title="Paranormal">paranormal</a> claims, expands on the "<a href="/wiki/Jesus_bloodline" title="Jesus bloodline">Jesus bloodline</a>" hypothesis and other elements of <i>The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-60"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup> Accepting as valid the testimony of an amateur archaeologist codenamed "Ben Hammott" relating to his discoveries made in the vicinity of <a href="/wiki/Rennes-le-Ch%C3%A2teau" title="Rennes-le-Château">Rennes-le-Château</a> since 1999; Burgess claims Ben has found the treasure of <a href="/wiki/B%C3%A9renger_Sauni%C3%A8re" title="Bérenger Saunière">Bérenger Saunière</a>: a mummified corpse, which they believe is <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>, in an underground tomb they claim is connected to both the Knights Templar and the Priory of Sion. In the film, Burgess interviews several people with alleged connections to the Priory of Sion, including a Gino Sandri and Nicolas Haywood. A book by one of the documentary's researchers, Rob Howells, entitled <i>Inside the Priory of Sion: Revelations from the World's Most Secret Society - Guardians of the Bloodline of Jesus</i> presented the version of the Priory of Sion as given in the 2008 documentary,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-61"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> which contained several erroneous assertions, such as the claim that Plantard believed in the Jesus bloodline hypothesis.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-62"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup> On 21 March 2012, Ben Hammott confessed and apologised on <a href="/wiki/Podcast" title="Podcast">Podcast</a> interview (using his real name Bill Wilkinson) that everything to do with the tomb and related artifacts was a hoax; revealing that the actual tomb was now destroyed, being part of a full sized set located in a warehouse in England.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-63"><a href="#cite_note-63"><span>[</span>63<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
