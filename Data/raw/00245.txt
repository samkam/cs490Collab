<h2 class="date-header">January 07, 2010</h2>
<div class="entry-category-film entry-category-taoism entry-author-sam_crane entry-type-post entry" id="entry-6a00d83451cdc869e20120a7b17f3e970b">
<div class="entry-inner">
<h3 class="entry-header">The Tao of Avatar - and why this sort of movie cannot be made in the PRC at this point in time...</h3>
<div class="entry-content">
<div class="entry-body">
<p><span style="font-size: 15px;">We saw <em><a href="http://movies.nytimes.com/2009/12/18/movies/18avatar.html">Avatar</a></em> last night (I know, I've been on a movie kick of late...).  It was visually stunning.  The story was unremarkable: a melodramatic morality tale (the good guy wins in the end!).  But it did have a couple of Taoist elements, which were no doubt quite consciously incorporated into the story.</span></p><p><span style="font-size: 15px;">First is the indirect invocation of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Qi"><em>Qi (Ch'i)</em>.</a>  In the movie, the planet of Pandora is, as the Sigorney Weaver character says, a complex, organic network of energy.  Everything is interconnected and a life's-force energy flows through and around all.  That is pretty much the notion of <em>Qi</em>.  Now, strictly speaking, this is not just a Taoist concept but something more general in Chinese culture.  Confucius and Mencius both mention <em>Qi</em>.  But the notion of <em>Qi</em> as a broader natural force, which humans must learn to live with and through, is very much a part of Taoism, in both its philosophical and religious forms.  In the film, the indigenous people are able to flow with <em>Qi</em> to such an extent that they can use it as a restorative and healing force. This is the essence of Chinese medicine (which owes much to Taoism): working with the flow of <em>Qi</em> to gain maximum physical health.  </span></p><p><span style="font-size: 15px;">The movie also embraces a notion of <a href="http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tao"><em>Tao</em> (Way)</a> that comes rather close to a Taoist understanding.  The grand maternal spirit, said to encompass all things, is called upon at a critical juncture: the hero character asks it to intervene in the consummating battle.  But he is told by his indigenous lover that the grand maternal spirit does not take sides.  All inclusive but indifferent - sounds like the <em>Tao Te Ching</em> to me:</span></p><blockquote><p><em><span style="font-size: 15px;">Way is the mystery of these ten thousand things.</span></em></p><p><em><span style="font-size: 15px;">It's a good person's treasure and an evil person's refuge.  Its beautiful words are bought and sole and its noble deeds are gifts enriching people.</span></em></p><p><em><span style="font-size: 15px;">It never abandons even the evil among us.</span></em></p><p><em><span style="font-size: 15px;">When the Son of Heaven is enthroned and the three dukes installed, parades with jade discs ans stately horses can't compare to sitting still in Way's company.</span></em></p><p><em><span style="font-size: 15px;">Isn't it said that the ancients exalted Way because in it whatever we seek we find, and whatever seeks us we escape?</span></em></p><p><span style="font-size: 15px;"><em>No wonder it's exalted throughout all beneath heaven. </em> (62)</span></p></blockquote><p><span style="font-size: 15px;">It turns out, in the film, that the grand maternal spirit does intervene at the crucial moment, which suggests that Way is not perfectly neutral but may tend in certain directions.  This, to my mind, comes close to Chuang Tzu's notion of Way, which provides a kind of liberating joy.  Way "acts," it "governs," in a manner beyond human comprehension, but with a tendency to balance "evil" with "good."  <br/></span></p><p><span style="font-size: 15px;">In any event, there are other more obvious Taoist themes: a pro-environmental message and an anti-militarist stance.  All told, Pandora, the planet-utopia, is something of a Taoist ideal, which the greedy earthling humans are destroying...</span></p><p><span style="font-size: 15px;">I should also mention the name of the mineral that fuels human greed: "unobtainium."  Nice!  The thing we desire the most is unobtainable.  Again: an idea right out of the <em>Tao Te Ching</em>...</span></p><p><span style="font-size: 15px;">Finally, let me comment on the Chinese reactions to the movie that <a href="http://www.zonaeuropa.com/weblog.htm">Roland, over at ESWN</a>, posts.   Take <a href="http://www.zonaeuropa.com/201001a.brief.htm#015">this response by film director Lu Chuan</a>:</span></p><blockquote><p><em><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;"><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;">I felt as if I had gone back to my childhood and became a 
 small boy full of dreams ... but the simple story was flawless and the plot 
 was very engrossing.  &lt;Avatar&gt; let us know that we lack not only just 
 in technology.  Technology can be acquired.  &lt;Avatar&gt; let me 
 realize how far our movies are from simple perfection; how far our movies 
 are from crystal-clear purity; how far our movies from passionate dreams; 
 how far from genuine sincerity are we who are embroiled in grim 
 entanglements and dim vulgarity!  We ought to ashamed in the face of 
 the purity of &lt;Avatar&gt;.  This is a complete defeat that we Chinese 
 filmmakers must collectively witness and concede.</span></span></em></p></blockquote><p><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;"><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;">This strikes me as an overreaction, but one with a kernel of truth.  I suspect that Chinese filmmakers will embrace and expand upon the technology of <em>Avatar</em> with little trouble.  And certain kinds of storytelling, especially melodrama, are very much a part of the Chinese cinematic repertoire.  But something that distinguishes <em>Avatar</em>, and makes it unlikely to be reproduced in Chinese film making any time soon, is its critical nature.</span></span></p><p><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;"><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;">The film is a direct assault on American modernity in general and the Bush administration more particularly.  Yes, the critiques lapse into caricatures - the evil military man crying out that we must "fight terrorism with terrorism" and launch a preemptive attack (Dick Cheney is clearly the model here) - but they are critiques nonetheless.  And such obvious criticisms of specific political authorities and broader historical trends are just not possible in the PRC, given the political sensitivities of the CCP and the power of state censors.  Even though ideological policing has relaxed significantly since the Maoist period, it is still constricting enough to prevent movie stories like<em> Avatar</em>.</span></span></p><p><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;"><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;">If China wants to make movies like <em>Avatar</em>, the powers that be will have to press further with a famous Deng Xiaoping line: liberate thought - </span></span><span style="font-size: 17px; font-family: Arial;">解放思想.  Or, as <a href="http://news.xinhuanet.com/english/2008-03/18/content_7813548.htm">Wen Jiabao said in 2008</a>: <span style="font-size: 18px; font-family: Arial;"> </span></span><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;">"We have to free the minds of everyone, particularly 
of leaders, so that everyone can have independent thought, critical thinking and 
innovation capabilities"... </span></p><p><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;">UPDATE: <a href="http://www.chinasmack.com/stories/avatar-movie-chinese-reactions-long-lines-shanghai/">more Chinese reaction to the film over at Chinasmack.</a><br/></span></p><p><span style="font-size: 15px; font-family: Arial;"><span style="text-decoration: underline;"><a href="http://uselesstree.typepad.com/.a/6a00d83451cdc869e2012876b407c4970c-pi" style="display: inline;"><img alt="Avatar2" class="asset asset-image at-xid-6a00d83451cdc869e2012876b407c4970c " src="http://uselesstree.typepad.com/.a/6a00d83451cdc869e2012876b407c4970c-500wi"/></a> <br/></span><br/></span></p>

<span id="comment-6a00d83451cdc869e20120a830d064970b-content"><p>Hi Sam,</p>
<p>I wrote and posted a long reply and it disappeared. So, here's a few points I made.</p>
<p>I can see a parallel with the Chinese notions of Qi, but I think this notion occurs in many aboriginal cultures, including the Native American cultures. Same with the ancestral cult we have in the movie. In fact, the movie really reminded me of Native American culture, and Cameron has conceded influences from movies like Dances With Wolves. I can also see some resemblance of Dao and Eywa, but not too much. Eywa <i>does</i> take sides, which, perhaps many religious Daoists may accept. But not my idea of the Dao and its workings. The floating mountains were largely based on mountains in China.</p>
<p>The Wikipedia article on the movie has a lot of information and links to interviews with james cameron, where I learned a whole lot:<br/>
http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Avatar_(2009_film)</p>
<p>Quotes of Cameron:</p>
<p>"[Avatar] certainly it is about imperialism in the sense that the way human history has always worked is that people with more military or technological might tend to supplant or destroy people who are weaker, usually for their resources ... So there's a conscience within the film, but it's not boldly stated. It's kind of there if you want it to be there; it's not there if you don't want it to be there. It can be as classic a story of fighting back against cruel might as 'Star Wars.' You can take it back to the origins of America in a fight of rebels against an imperial dominating force. You can interpret it many, many different ways. The bad guys could be America in this movie, or the good guys could be America in this movie," he says, laughing. "Depending on your perspective."</p>
<p>"War and the consequences to the soldier is a theme in my movies. Terminator has a scarred soldier character. We have to face the consequences of setting our people off to do our bidding in a foreign land. But it's not a major undercurrent of the story. I'm not trying to make some anti-Bush statement."</p>
<p>Although Ben Hoyle of The Australian tries to argue the movie "contains heavy implicit criticism of America's conduct in the War on Terror," Cameron repeatedly says it's not.<br/>
"That's not what the movie's about - that's only a minor part of it. For me it feels consistent only in a very generalised theme of us looking at ourselves as human beings in a technical society with all its skills, part of which is the ability to do mechanised warfare, part of which is the ability to do warfare at a distance, at a remove, which seems to make it morally easier to deal with, but its not."<br/>
http://www.theaustralian.com.au/news/arts/war-on-terror-backdrop-to-james-camerons-avatar/story-e6frg8pf-1225809286903</p>
<p>"The film is definitely not anti-American. It’s not anti-human either. My perception of the film is that the N’avi represent that sort of aspirational part of ourselves that wants to be better, that wants to respect nature. And the humans in the movie represent the more venal versions of ourselves, the banality of evil that comes with corporate decisions that are made out of remove of the consequences."</p></span>
