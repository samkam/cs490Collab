<p><b>Lapine</b> is a <a href="/wiki/Fictional_language" title="Fictional language">fictional language</a> created by author <a href="/wiki/Richard_Adams" title="Richard Adams">Richard Adams</a> for his 1972 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Watership_Down" title="Watership Down">Watership Down</a></i>, where it is spoken by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_fictional_rabbits" title="List of fictional rabbits">fictional rabbit</a> characters. The language was again used in Adams' 1996 sequel, <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_from_Watership_Down" title="Tales from Watership Down">Tales from Watership Down</a></i>, and has appeared in both the <a href="/wiki/Watership_Down_(film)" title="Watership Down (film)">film</a> and <a href="/wiki/Watership_Down_(TV_series)" title="Watership Down (TV series)">television adaptations</a>. The fragments of language presented by Adams consist of a few dozen distinct words, and are chiefly used for the naming of <a href="/wiki/Rabbit" title="Rabbit">rabbits</a>, their mythological characters, and objects in their world. The name "Lapine" comes from the French word for rabbit, <i>lapin</i>, and can also be used to describe rabbit society.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-henning_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-henning-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogers_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-rogers-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lapine_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The words of the Lapine language were developed by Adams piecemeal and organically as required by the circumstances of the plot. In a <a href="/wiki/Reddit" title="Reddit">Reddit</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/AMA_(Reddit)" title="AMA (Reddit)">"Ask Me Anything" interview</a>, Adams noted that "I just constructed Lapine as I went - when the rabbits needed a word for something so did I." Reflecting on his inspirations for the words, Adams stated that "some of them are <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Onomatopoeic" title="Onomatopoeic">onomatopoeic</a> like <i>hrududu</i> (which is a car), but overall they simply came from my <a href="/wiki/Subconscious" title="Subconscious">subconscious</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams2014_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams2014-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Adams commented that the motivation for the sound of Lapine was that it should sound "wuffy, fluffy" as in the word "Efrafa".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams2005_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams2005-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogers_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-rogers-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i>, Keren Levy described the Lapine language as "somehow easy to accept as [a language] we have always known. It is the language of the countryside, of its copses and beeches and of the weather."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-levy_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-levy-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The sound of Lapine has been ascribed to influence from <a href="/wiki/Welsh_language" title="Welsh language">Welsh</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_language" title="Irish language">Irish</a>, <a href="/wiki/Scottish_Gaelic" title="Scottish Gaelic">Scottish Gaelic</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rogers_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-rogers-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Arabic_language" title="Arabic language">Arabic languages</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cain_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-cain-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> Author <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Cain" title="Stephen Cain">Stephen Cain</a> bolsters the Arabic connection by noting in particular that "Adams had occasion to study [Arabic] during his military service in the <a href="/wiki/Middle_East" title="Middle East">Middle East</a>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-cain_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-cain-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The Lapine language has also been frequently compared to <a href="/wiki/Sindarin" title="Sindarin">Sindarin</a> (the language of the Elves in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/J.R.R._Tolkien" title="J.R.R. Tolkien">J.R.R. Tolkien</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Middle-earth" title="Middle-earth">Middle-earth</a></i> series) in terms of its effect on setting in the novels.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-henning_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-henning-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murray_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-murray-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Following the success of <i>Watership Down</i>, Richard Adams would go on to invent another constructed language for his <a href="/wiki/Beklan_Empire" title="Beklan Empire">Beklan</a> novels, <i><a href="/wiki/Shardik" title="Shardik">Shardik</a></i> (1974) and <i><a href="/wiki/Maia_(novel)" title="Maia (novel)">Maia</a></i> (1984).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Linguistic_analyses">Linguistic analyses</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lapine_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Linguistic analyses">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Fans of the book, including authors and academics, have written about the words and phrases extant in the corpus of <i>Watership Down</i> and have analyzed the language and its variations on a linguistic level. Within the books, the rabbit characters use of Lapine is presented to readers as <a href="/wiki/Standard_English" title="Standard English">Standard English</a> with the inclusion of a number of specialized Lapine lexical terms.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corder_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-corder-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Writing for the <i><a href="/wiki/International_Journal_of_the_Sociology_of_Language" title="International Journal of the Sociology of Language">International Journal of the Sociology of Language</a></i>, Albert Valdman notes that inter-rabbit Lapine is alternately <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formality_level" title="Formality level">formal</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Colloquial_language" title="Colloquial language">colloquial</a> "marked by hesitations, interruptions, interjections, incomplete sentences, and false starts".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valdman_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-valdman-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Linguist, <a href="/wiki/Pit_Corder" title="Pit Corder">Pit Corder</a> breaks this down further, finding that the Lapine spoken by the rabbits consists of 64% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Simple_sentence" title="Simple sentence">simple sentences</a>, 14% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Compound_sentence" title="Compound sentence">compound sentences</a> (with 30% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Paratactic" title="Paratactic">paratactic</a> and 70% marked <a href="/wiki/Conjunction_(grammar)#Coordinating_conjunctions" title="Conjunction (grammar)">coordination</a>), and 22% <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Complex_sentence" title="Complex sentence">complex sentences</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corder_8-1"><a href="#cite_note-corder-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The mean Lapine sentence length is 6.3 words.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corder_8-2"><a href="#cite_note-corder-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When speaking to other animals, the rabbits adopt a <a href="/wiki/Lingua_franca" title="Lingua franca">lingua franca</a> known as "hedgerow," however in both examples given in the book (i.e. the mouse<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valdman_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-valdman-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kehaar" title="Kehaar">Kehaar</a> the gull<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corder_8-3"><a href="#cite_note-corder-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup>) the conversation reverts to Lapine once initial contact has been established. More specifically, the rabbits adopt <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Formality_level" title="Formality level">formal</a> Lapine<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valdman_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-valdman-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> and the other animals employ a Lapine <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foreigner_talk_theory" title="Foreigner talk theory">Foreigner Talk</a> that Pit Corder describes as "a reduced code or incipient <a href="/wiki/Pidgin" title="Pidgin">pidgin</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corder_8-4"><a href="#cite_note-corder-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valdman_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-valdman-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/RELC_Journal" title="RELC Journal">RELC Journal</a></i>, Corder notes that the general rules of "Foreigner Talk" are well-established in societies even among natives who have never communicated with a foreigner. Corder attributes the learning of the rules of "Foreigner Talk" to its use within native-speaker-oriented literature and other media as a proxy for <a href="/wiki/Interlanguage" title="Interlanguage">interlanguage</a>. Because Lapine is presented in the novels as Standard English, Lapine Foreigner Talk is essentially English Foreigner Talk with a Lapine gloss and thus provides an example of linguistic <a href="/wiki/Enculturation" title="Enculturation">enculturation</a> for children who read the books. Breaking down the <a href="/wiki/Syntax" title="Syntax">syntax</a> of Lapine Foreigner Talk to compare with that of standard Lapine, Corder finds that they are roughly the same with the only notable difference being an inversion of the proportion of paratactic to marked coordination in compound sentences. Specifically, Corder reports Lapine Foreigner Talk to consist of 73% simple sentences, 15% compound sentences (70% paratactic and 30% marked coordination), and 12% complex sentences (with 60% <a href="/wiki/Complement_(linguistics)" title="Complement (linguistics)">complemented</a> by the four verbs "think", "know", "say", and "tell").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-corder_8-5"><a href="#cite_note-corder-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Albert Valdman further notes differences between the Lapine Foreigner Talk used to facilitate discussion as with Kehaar the gull, and that used to signal the depreciated status of the unnamed mouse (a less powerful animal in the rabbit world).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-valdman_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-valdman-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The use of Lapine outside of the fictional world of the novels has also been written about by Thomas E. Murray in an article for <i><a href="/wiki/American_Speech" title="American Speech">American Speech</a></i>. Murray notes that the Lapine word "silflay" (meaning "To go above ground to feed. Literally, to feed outside."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams2005-2_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-adams2005-2-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup>) has entered the English lexicon as more than a mere <a href="/wiki/Nonce_word" title="Nonce word">nonce word</a>. In a survey conducted by Murray, he found that the term was in use (meaning "the act of rabbits eating above-ground") primarily in the <a href="/wiki/Midwestern_United_States" title="Midwestern United States">Midwest and North Central United States</a>. Murray also noted differing levels of use according to <a href="/wiki/Socioeconomic_status" title="Socioeconomic status">socioeconomic status</a> with usage highest among <a href="/wiki/Middle_class" title="Middle class">middle</a> and <a href="/wiki/Lower_middle_class" title="Lower middle class">lower middle class</a> speakers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murray_7-1"><a href="#cite_note-murray-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Murray suggests that the geographical spread of the term may in part be limited by interactions with rabbits, highlighting the comment of a <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a>-based survey participant who knew the word but never used it due to the lack of rabbits in the city. Another Lapine word that Murray claims to have gained usage outside of the novels is "Crixa" (meaning "The center of Efrafa, at the crossing point of two bridle paths."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams2005-2_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-adams2005-2-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup>) which is used by students to refer to the residential dormitories within <a href="/wiki/Ohio_State_University" title="Ohio State University">Ohio State University</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-murray_7-2"><a href="#cite_note-murray-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Selected_vocabulary">Selected vocabulary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lapine_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Selected vocabulary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><small>(<b>Note</b>: All terms and definitions drawn from the "Lapine Glossary" of <i><a href="/wiki/Watership_Down" title="Watership Down">Watership Down</a></i> and <i><a href="/wiki/Tales_from_Watership_Down" title="Tales from Watership Down">Tales from Watership Down</a></i>.)</small><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-adams2005-2_10-2"><a href="#cite_note-adams2005-2-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li><i>Crixa</i>: The center of Efrafa, at the crossing of two paths.</li>
<li><i>elil</i>: Enemies of rabbits, including fox, stoat, weasel, cat, owl, man, etc.</li>
<li><i>embleer</i>: Stinking, the word for the smell of a fox.</li>
<li><i>flay</i>: Food, specifically grass or other greens.</li>
<li><i>flayrah</i>: Unusually good food, such as lettuce, carrots, etc.</li>
<li><i>Frith</i>: The Sun, viewed by the rabbits as God.</li>
<li><i>Frithrah</i>: "Lord Sun", used as an exclamation. Analogous to "My God!"</li>
<li><i>fu-InlÃ©</i>: After moonrise.</li>
<li><i>hain</i>: A song.</li>
<li><i>hlao</i>: A depression in the ground formed by a daisy or a thistle, specifically one that can hold moisture. Also used as a rabbit's name.</li>
<li><i>hlessi</i>: A rabbit who lives aboveground or otherwise out of a warren; a wandering rabbit. Plural <i>hlessil</i>.</li>
<li><i>homba</i>: A fox. Plural <i>hombil</i>.</li>
<li><i>hrair</i>: Many, uncountable, any number above four. It also means thousand, a big, uncountable number.</li>
<li><i>hraka</i>: Droppings, excreta. Used as a curse.</li>
<li><i>hrududu</i>: Any type of motor vehicle, such as a tractor, car or train. Plural <i>hrududil</i></li>
<li><i>InlÃ©</i>: The moon, moonrise. Also means fear, darkness or death (as in the Black Rabbit of InlÃ©)</li>
<li><i>lendri</i>: A badger.</li>
<li><i>li</i>: Head.</li>
<li><i>marli</i>: A doe,a mother.</li>
<li><i>m'saion</i>: "We meet them"</li>
<li><i>narn</i>: Nice, tasty.</li>
<li><i>ni-Frith</i>: Noon.</li>
<li><i>nildro</i>: A blackbird.</li>
<li><i>Owsla</i>: An elite group of rabbits, second year or older, surrounding the chief rabbit of a warren. (Not all warrens have an Owsla.)</li>
<li><i>Owslafa</i>: A strong, older group of the Owsla that serves as the Council's police at the Efrafa warren.</li>
<li><i>pfeffa</i>: A cat.</li>
<li><i>-rah</i>: A suffix denoting meaning prince, lord or Chief Rabbit (as in Threarah, Hazel-rah)</li>
<li><i>-roo</i>: A diminutive suffix meaning "little" (as in Hlao-roo or Hrairoo).</li>
<li><i>silf</i>: Outside.</li>
<li><i>silflay</i>: To eat above ground; to graze.</li>
<li><i>tharn</i>: A state of paralyzed fear or confusion. Can also be used to mean "looking foolish", "forlorn", "heartbroken".</li>
<li><i>thlay</i>: Fur.</li>
<li><i>threar</i>: A Rowan or Mountain Ash tree.</li>
<li><i>u</i>: The.</li>
<li><i>U hrair</i>: "The Thousand". The term used by rabbits which refers to all their collective enemies.</li>
<li><i>vair</i>: To excrete, to pass droppings.</li>
<li><i>yona</i>: A hedgehog. Plural <i>yonil</i>.</li>
<li><i>zorn</i>: Destroyed, murdered. A catastrophe.</li>
</ul>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Proper_names">Proper names</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lapine_language&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Proper names">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<ul>
<li><i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/El-ahrairah" title="El-ahrairah">El-ahrairah</a></i>: The rabbits' folk hero. The literal translation of the full name(Elil-hrair-rah) is "Enemies-thousand-prince"; "Prince with a thousand enemies".</li>
<li><i>Hlao-roo</i>: "Little Hlao", Pipkin's name in Lapine.</li>
<li><i>Hrairoo</i>: "Little thousand". <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fiver_(Watership_Down)" title="Fiver (Watership Down)">Fiver</a>'s name in Lapine.</li>
<li><i>Hyzenthlay</i>: The literal translation is "shine-dew-fur"; "Fur shining like the dew". The name of a doe.</li>
<li><i>Nildro-hain</i>: "The Blackbird's song". The name of a doe.</li>
<li><i>Sayn</i>: Groundsel.</li>
<li><i>Thlayli</i>: "Fur-head"; <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bigwig_(Watership_Down)" title="Bigwig (Watership Down)">Bigwig</a>'s name in Lapine.</li>
<li><i>Thethuthinnang</i>: "Movement of leaves". The name of a doe.</li>
<li><i>Threarah</i>: "Lord Threar", "Lord of the Rowan tree". The <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chief_Rabbit" title="Chief Rabbit">Chief Rabbit</a> of the Sandleford warren.</li>
</ul>
