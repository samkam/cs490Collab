<p><i><b>And Then There Were None</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/Mystery_fiction" title="Mystery fiction">mystery novel</a> by <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie" title="Agatha Christie">Agatha Christie</a>, widely considered her <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">masterpiece</a> and described by her as the most difficult of her books to write.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harpercollins2009_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-harpercollins2009-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It was first published in the United Kingdom by the <a href="/wiki/Collins_Crime_Club" title="Collins Crime Club">Collins Crime Club</a> on 6 November 1939<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vuyqxh_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-vuyqxh-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> as <i>Ten Little Niggers</i>, after the British <a href="/wiki/Ten_Little_Indians#Derivative_songs" title="Ten Little Indians">blackface song</a> which serves as a major plot point.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CC_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-CC-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pendergast_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-pendergast-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The title was changed to the last line of the rhyme – <i>And Then There Were None</i> – for the first American edition, which used the <a href="/wiki/Ten_Little_Indians" title="Ten Little Indians">original American version</a> of the song.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-US-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> That song title, <i>Ten Little Indians</i>, was used for some editions, until the Christie estate formally approved the US title of the work.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (September 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In the novel, ten people are enticed into coming to an island under different pretexts, e.g. offers of employment or to enjoy a late summer holiday, or to meet with old friends. All have been complicit in the death(s) of other human beings but either escaped justice or committed an act that was not subject to legal sanction. The guests are charged with their respective "crimes" by a gramophone recording after dinner the first night and informed that they have been brought to the island to pay for their actions. They are the only people on the island, and cannot escape due to the distance from the mainland and the inclement weather, yet gradually all ten are killed in turn, in a manner that seems to parallel the ten deaths in the nursery rhyme. Nobody else seems to be left alive on the island by the apparently last death. A confession in the form of a postscript to the novel, unveils how the killings took place and who was responsible.</p>
<p>It is Christie's best-selling novel with over 100 million copies sold, also making it the world's best-selling mystery, and one of the <a href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_books" title="List of best-selling books">best-selling books</a> of all time. Publications International lists the novel as the seventh best-selling title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Eight people arrive on an isolated island off the <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devonshire</a> coast of <a href="/wiki/England" title="England">England</a>. Each appears to have an invitation tailored to his or her personal circumstances, such as an offer of employment or an unexpected late summer holiday. They are met by the island owners' <a href="/wiki/Butler" title="Butler">butler</a> and <a href="/wiki/Cook_(profession)" title="Cook (profession)">cook</a> (who have never met their employer), making a total of ten people known to be on the island. While awaiting their hosts, they find a framed copy of the nursery rhyme "Ten Little Soldiers" ("Niggers" or "Indians" in respective earlier editions) hanging on the wall, and notice ten figurines on the dining room table, as well as discussing other oddities about the house and their visit. The butler plays a <a href="/wiki/Gramophone_record" title="Gramophone record">gramophone (or "phonograph") record</a> while they are talking, as he had been instructed to do; unexpectedly the recording contains a voice that describes each visitor in turn and accuses each of having committed murder but evading justice, and asks if any of "the accused" wishes to give a defense. All are shocked and in the aftermath one of the guests (Anthony Marston) has a drink to help with the shock; however, his drink was poisoned with <a href="/wiki/Potassium_cyanide" title="Potassium cyanide">potassium cyanide</a> and he chokes and dies. Subsequently the guests notice one of the ten figurines is now broken, and the nursery rhyme appears to reflect the manner of death ("One choked his little self and then there were nine").</p>

</dl>
<div class="poem">
<p>Ten little Indian Boys went out to dine;<br/>
One choked his little self and then there were nine.<br/>

<br/>
Eight little Indian Boys travelling in Devon;<br/>

<br/>
Seven little Indian Boys chopping up sticks;<br/>
One chopped himself in halves and then there were six.<br/>
<br/>
Six little Indian Boys playing with a hive;<br/>
A bumblebee stung one and then there were five.<br/>
<br/>
Five little Indian Boys going in for law;<br/>
One got in <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Chancery" title="Court of Chancery">Chancery</a> and then there were four.<br/>
<br/>
Four little Indian Boys going out to sea;<br/>
A <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_herring_(narrative)" title="Red herring (narrative)">red herring</a> swallowed one and then there were three.<br/>
<br/>
Three little Indian Boys walking in the zoo;<br/>
A big bear hugged one and then there were two.<br/>
<br/>
Two little Indian Boys sitting in the sun;<br/>
One got frizzled up and then there was one.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. <br/>

He went out and hanged himself and then there were none.</p>

<p>Over the next two days, Mrs Rogers, the cook, dies from a lethal dose of <a href="/wiki/Chloral_hydrate" title="Chloral hydrate">chloral hydrate</a>, a sleeping medication ("one overslept himself") put into her brandy, and then General MacArthur is found dead from a blow to the back of his skull with a <a href="/wiki/Lifebuoy" title="Lifebuoy">life preserver</a>, having predicted earlier to Vera Claythorne that none of them would leave the island alive ("one said he'd stay there"). In each case the death matches the rhyme and is accompanied by another broken figurine. Three of the men—Armstrong, Blore and Lombard—search the island but there seems nowhere on the bare rock for a stranger to hide, and the bad weather would not allow any boats to visit. They decide that one of the remaining visitors must be a <a href="/wiki/Insanity" title="Insanity">mad</a> killer. To the puzzlement of the guests a skein of grey wool from Miss Brent and a red oilsilk shower curtain turn up missing. The next morning, Thomas Rogers, the butler, is found dead in the woodshed, having been bludgeoned with an axe ("one chopped himself in halves"), and later that day the fifth death occurs when Emily Brent is killed by an injection of potassium cyanide ("a <a href="/wiki/Bumblebee" title="Bumblebee">bumblebee</a> sting") after being drugged with chloral hydrate.</p>
<p>Justice Wargrave suggests they lock up any potential weapons, including Dr. Armstrong's medical equipment and his own sleeping pills. They use two locks, giving one key to <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">soldier of fortune</a> Philip Lombard and the other to ex-policeman William Blore. Lombard admits to bringing a <a href="/wiki/Revolver" title="Revolver">revolver</a> to the island and initially refuses to turn it over until the others force him to do so. When he goes to get it, he seems genuinely confounded that it is missing. To stay safe, they agree that only one person will leave the others at any given time. Ex-governess Vera Claythorne screams after her face brushes against <a href="/wiki/Seaweed" title="Seaweed">seaweed</a> which has been hung from a ceiling hook in her darkened room, causing the others to run upstairs to help. Once upstairs they realize Wargrave is not with them and rush back downstairs. He is found sitting dressed in the missing shower curtain and wearing the wool skein in mockery of <a href="/wiki/Court_dress" title="Court dress">judicial robes</a> with the mark of a gunshot through the <a href="/wiki/Forehead" title="Forehead">forehead</a> ("one got into <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Chancery" title="Court of Chancery">Chancery</a>"). Armstrong confirms the death, and Lombard appears confounded when he discovers that his gun has now been returned to his room. That night, Blore hears someone sneaking out of the house and catches a glimpse of someone in the moonlight. Realizing Armstrong is missing, Blore, Vera and Lombard decide that he must be the killer. The next morning, the three try to signal the mainland and remain in the relative safety of the beach. However, Blore eventually goes to the house for food, and is later found under Vera's bedroom window, his skull crushed by a heavy bear-shaped clock ("a big bear hugged one") dropped from the window, and shortly afterwards, shocking Lombard and Vera, the corpse of Armstrong—who has drowned—washes ashore, his absence now appearing to have been designed to mislead everyone as to the identity of the true killer ("a <a href="/wiki/Red_herring" title="Red herring">red herring</a> swallowed one").</p>
<p>Vera and Lombard, the last two survivors, both now believe each other to be the killer, shock and panic overcoming reason. She persuades Lombard to help her drag Armstrong's body away from the tideline, as a pretext to get hold of his gun, which she does. When Lombard makes a sudden move towards her, she fires, managing to shoot him through the heart, killing him instantly (an early version of the rhyme "<a href="/wiki/Ten_Little_Injuns" title="Ten Little Injuns">Ten Little Injuns</a>" contained the verse "One shot the other and then there was One"). She returns, relieved, to the house, which she notes does not "feel like an empty house", decides she is not hungry and ascends to her room. There, in a dreamlike <a href="/wiki/Dissociation_(psychology)" title="Dissociation (psychology)">disoriented</a> <a href="/wiki/Altered_state_of_consciousness" title="Altered state of consciousness">state</a> after shooting Lombard, she finds a hanging noose and chair arranged beneath it in her room. Feeling the presences of Cyril, the boy she allowed to drown, as well as that of her former lover, Hugo, her lover and the boy's uncle, she places the noose around her neck and kicks away the chair ("he went and hanged himself, and then there were none").<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bookrags_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-bookrags-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In an epilogue, the investigating police officer discusses the mystery with his Assistant Commissioner at <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard" title="Scotland Yard">Scotland Yard</a>. There are no clues as to the owner, nor from any of the visitors' diaries (which broadly agree about the sequence of events on the island up to the final day on the deaths from Marston to Wargrave). However, the situation they find apparently contradicts any theory they can suggest. Vera's fingerprints on Lombard's pistol and the clock that killed Blore coming from Vera's room point to Vera as "U.N. Owen", yet the chair Vera kicked away is found neatly set against the wall. Lombard's revolver was not found on or near his person. Armstrong's body has been dragged above where any tide could have taken it, and it is inconceivable that Blore could or would have dropped the clock on himself. The inclement weather, combined with the distance from the mainland, would have prevented anyone else from entering or exiting the island before the first boats arrived after the weekend. They deduce someone must have been alive after the deaths of Vera, Lombard, Armstrong and Blore. The novel ends with the investigators thoroughly confounded by the apparently impossible situation they must unravel.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Postscript_by_the_killer">Postscript by the killer</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Postscript by the killer">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In a postscript, a fishing ship picks up a bottle (containing a written confession) inside its trawling nets, which is sent to <a href="/wiki/Scotland_Yard" title="Scotland Yard">Scotland Yard</a> and explains the events, but has been left to chance whether it will be discovered. It is signed by Justice Wargrave, who states he has, since childhood, relished causing suffering, but in adulthood he determined that such pain be meted out only to the guilty. Upon hearing of a chance remark of possible suspects having gotten away with murder and having learned he was terminally ill with a relatively short time to live, he decided to commit murder himself, locating ten individuals who had knowingly or intentionally caused the deaths of others but escaped earthly justice; however, not every case was murder (i.e. Emily Brent, Marston). By using a stock phrase he managed to trick other people into remarking about possible murder suspects with the exceptions of Marston (whom he choose as a victim because of his amoral and cavalier attitude) and Armstrong (whom he had heard had committed <a href="/wiki/Negligent_homicide" title="Negligent homicide">negligent homicide</a> because of drink during an anti drinking lecture). He lured nine of them to the island he had purchased with the help of one Isaac Morris, and pretended to be a tenth visitor like them. Experienced in observation, he confirmed all were guilty by their reactions to the phonograph recording. His tenth killer (and first chronological victim) was his sole <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/go-between" title="wikt:go-between">go-between</a> and contact, Morris, who had been responsible for the death of the daughter of Wargrave's friends from a drug overdose. Morris, a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypochondriac" title="Hypochondriac">hypochondriac</a>, constantly complained of maladies, both real and imagined. For this reason, he accepted a cocktail of sleeping medication given to him by the judge, who promised they would cure his upsets, however proved fatal.</p>
<p>Wargrave first killed those he considered less culpable, saving the "prolonged mental strain and fear" for the more cold-blooded perpetrators, slipping potassium cyanide into the drink of Marston (whom he felt was <a href="/wiki/Amorality" title="Amorality">amoral</a>) and chloral hydrate into the drink of Mrs. Rogers (who acted largely under the influence of her domineering husband). After <a href="/wiki/Club_(weapon)" title="Club (weapon)">bludgeoning</a> General MacArthur with a life preserver and Rogers, the butler, with an axe, the judge used the remaining chloral hydrate as a sedative in Miss Brent's coffee before injecting her with potassium cyanide when she was left alone in the kitchen, using one of Dr Armstrong's syringe. He then played upon Armstrong, who trusted the judge, whom he persuaded to go along with a plan whereby the judge would <a href="/wiki/Faked_death" title="Faked death">fake being shot dead</a>, but would, actually, remain alive and thus be able to investigate who was the "real killer". After Wargrave's "death" was "confirmed" by Armstrong, the judge met with his dupe secretly that night along the rocks overlooking the sea and pushed him off the cliff where he drowned, at a location where the body would wash ashore with the tide. After dropping the clock on Blore, he correctly surmised that Vera and Lombard would turn on each other but that she would be more than a worthy opponent and manage to turn the tables on the latter. At which point he placed the noose and chair in her room as a "psychological experiment" – which, combined with her "hypnotic" posttraumatic state from shooting Lombard, he (again correctly) anticipated would lead her to hang herself.</p>
<p>After her death, Wargrave describes how he returned the chair back against the wall, and wrote his confession, admitting a "pitiful human need" for recognition, which he will throw into the sea <a href="/wiki/Message_in_a_bottle" title="Message in a bottle">in a bottle</a> before shooting himself. He states that he will fasten a rubber cord to Lombard's gun, using a handkerchief to prevent leaving any of his own fingerprints. If this plan works as intended, the gun will recoil after firing the fatal shot through the forehead, leaving him exactly as described in the others' diaries, with the gun fallen far from his own hand. The cloth on the floor should "not attract any attention", he notes. He concludes that when the police arrive, "they will find ten dead bodies – and an unsolved mystery – on Soldier Island".</p>
<p>Wargrave points out three clues by which the police could perhaps identify him as the killer, but surmises correctly that the police will not figure them out:</p>
<ol>
<li>Wargrave was the only "innocent" guest, since the man he sentenced to death—despite strong public belief in his innocence—was found after his execution to have been unquestionably <i>guilty</i> of the crime for which he was hanged. Therefore paradoxically the only <i>innocent</i> guest had to be "the" murderer!</li>
<li>The "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Red_herring_(narrative)" title="Red herring (narrative)">red herring</a>" line in the poem suggests that Armstrong was tricked into his death, and the elderly judge would have been the person most likely for Armstrong to trust;</li>
<li>The gunshot wound on Wargrave's forehead is symbolic. It would appear similar to <a href="/wiki/Curse_and_mark_of_Cain" title="Curse and mark of Cain">the mark God placed</a> upon <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cain" title="Cain">Cain</a> {the first <i>murderer</i>} in the Bible as a sign after he killed his brother <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Abel" title="Abel">Abel</a>.</li>
</ol>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Characters">Characters</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Characters">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The following details of the characters are based on the original novel. Backstories, backgrounds and names vary with differing international adaptations, based on the rules, censorship, cultural norms, etc.</p>
<ul>
<li><b>Anthony James Marston</b> killed two young children (John and Lucy Combes) while driving recklessly, for which he felt no real remorse nor did he accept any personal responsibility, complaining only that his driving license had been suspended as a result. He was the first island victim, poisoned with <a href="/wiki/Potassium_cyanide" title="Potassium cyanide">potassium cyanide</a> slipped into his drink while the guests were listening to the fateful gramophone recording.</li>
<li><b>Mrs Ethel Rogers</b>, the cook/housekeeper and Thomas Rogers' wife, described as pale and ghostlike woman who walks in mortal fear. She was dominated by her bullying husband, who withheld the medicine of their former employer (an elderly spinster, Miss Jennifer Brady) to collect an inheritance they knew she had left them in her will. Mrs Rogers was haunted by the crime for the rest of her life, and was Owen's second victim, dying in her sleep peacefully from an overdose of <a href="/wiki/Chloral_hydrate" title="Chloral hydrate">chloral hydrate</a> in her brandy.</li>
<li><b>General John Gordon Macarthur</b>, a retired <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a> war hero, who sent his late wife's lover (a younger officer, Arthur Richmond) to his death by assigning him to a mission where it was practically guaranteed he would not survive. Leslie Macarthur had mistakenly put the wrong letters in the envelopes on one occasion when she wrote to both men at the same time. The general fatalistically accepts that no one will leave the island alive, which he tells Vera Claythorne. Shortly thereafter, he is bludgeoned while sitting along the shore with a <a href="/wiki/Lifebuoy" title="Lifebuoy">life preserver</a>.</li>
<li><b>Thomas Rogers</b>, the butler and Ethel Rogers' husband. He dominated his weak-willed wife and they killed their former elderly employer by withholding her medicine, causing the woman to die from heart failure and inheriting the money she bequeathed them in her will. He was killed when bludgeoned with an axe as he cut firewood in the woodshed.</li>
<li><b>Emily Caroline Brent</b>, a rigid, repressed elderly spinster holding harsh moralistic principles. She accepted the vacation on Soldier Island largely due to financial constraints. Years earlier, she had dismissed her young maid, Beatrice Taylor, for becoming pregnant out of wedlock. Beatrice, who had already been rejected by her parents for the same reason, drowned herself in a river, which Miss Brent considered an even worse sin. She refuses to discuss the matter with the gentlemen, but later confides what happened to Vera Claythorne, who tells the others shortly before Miss Brent is found dead herself. Having been sedated with <a href="/wiki/Chloral_hydrate" title="Chloral hydrate">chloral hydrate</a> in her coffee, leaving her disoriented, she was left alone in the kitchen and injected in the neck with potassium cyanide with one of Dr Armstrong's hypodermic syringes (the "bee sting"). Right before her own murder, due to the chloral hydrate she has ingested, she has a lurid daydream about Beatrice and imagines hearing the girl's footsteps (they are actually the footsteps of the murderer).</li>
<li><b>Dr Edward George Armstrong</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Harley_Street" title="Harley Street">Harley Street</a> doctor, responsible for the death of a patient, Louisa Mary Clees, after he operated on her while drunk, many years earlier. Armstrong foolishly trusts Wargrave, and, while rendezvousing with the judge on a rocky cliff, is pushed into the sea and drowns. His body goes missing for a while, leading the others to believe he is the killer, but his corpse washes ashore expeditiously at the end of the novel, leading to the climax.</li>
<li><b>William Henry Blore</b>, a retired <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Police_inspector" title="Police inspector">police inspector</a> and now a private investigator, accused of falsifying his testimony in court for a bribe from a criminal gang too dangerous to double-cross, which resulted in an innocent man, James Landor, being convicted and sentenced to life imprisonment. Landor, who had a wife and daughter, died in prison. Blore arrives using the alias "Davis" and claiming to have arrived from South Africa, as he was instructed to do by Isaac Morris, who hired him for "security" work, but is confronted about his true name which was revealed on the gramophone recording, and he acknowledges his true identity. He denies the accusation against him from the gramophone recording but later privately admits the truth to Lombard. His skull was crushed by a bear-shaped clock dropped from Vera's bedroom window onto the terrace below.</li>
<li><b>Philip Lombard</b>, a <a href="/wiki/Mercenary" title="Mercenary">soldier of fortune</a>. Literally down to his last square meal, he comes to the island with a loaded revolver, as suggested by Isaac Morris. Lombard is accused of causing the deaths of a number of East African tribesmen, after stealing their food and leaving them to starve. He, along with Marston, are the only guests to openly and immediately confirm that the accusations against them are true; neither feels remorse. Lombard fulfilled the ninth referenced verse of the rhyme, shot to death on the beach by Vera, who believed him to be the murderer. Of all the "guests" he is the only one to theorize that "U.N.Owen" is Justice Wargrave, but ironically he is also "tricked" by the Judge and the Doctor into circumstances leading to his own "execution".</li>
<li><b>Vera Elizabeth Claythorne</b>, a cool, efficient, resourceful former teacher and governess, who has taken mostly secretarial jobs since her last job as a governess ended in the death of her charge, Cyril Hamilton, whom she intentionally allowed to swim out to sea – as the child had wanted to do but had theretofore been denied as too dangerous – and drown. She did this so her lover, Cyril's uncle Hugo Hamilton, could become the family heir, inherit the estate and marry her, which had been their original plan before Cyril's birth changed things. She swam out to sea to "save" Cyril to make it seem he had disobeyed her – as she had consistently told him it was too dangerous – but knowing she would not arrive in time. Hugo, however, who loved his nephew, abandoned her after he somehow sensed what she had done. After shooting Lombard in what she believed was self-defense, she returns to the house, relieved she has survived. When she goes to her room, she finds a readied noose, complete with chair beneath it, suspended from a hook hanging from the ceiling. In a combination of both latent guilt for her crime and in a post-traumatic state, she adjusts the noose round her neck and kicks the chair away, apparently fulfilling the rhyme's final verse ("One little Soldier Boy left all alone; He went out and hanged himself and then there were none").</li>
<li><b>Justice Lawrence John Wargrave</b>, a retired judge, known as a "<a href="/wiki/Hanging_judge" title="Hanging judge">hanging judge</a>" for liberally awarding the <a href="/wiki/Capital_punishment_in_the_United_Kingdom" title="Capital punishment in the United Kingdom">death penalty</a> in different murder cases, and is revealed at the end to be the killer on the island. Finding himself with <a href="/wiki/Terminal_illness" title="Terminal illness">only a short time to live</a>, he creates a game in which, as island owner "U. N. Owen" (a <a href="/wiki/Homonym" title="Homonym">homonym</a> of "Unknown") he entices various people who have caused death and escaped justice, to a secret location, in order to be a murderer himself, and to kill them in a way that would leave an almost-unsolvable <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Murder_mystery" title="Murder mystery">murder mystery</a>. In the story proper, he appears to be one of the ten visitors, and is accused in turn on the gramophone recording, of <a href="/wiki/Judicial_murder" title="Judicial murder">judicial murder</a> as a result of giving biased summation and jury directions leading to a <a href="/wiki/Hanging" title="Hanging">hanging</a> which was widely believed at the time to be a deliberate <a href="/wiki/Miscarriage_of_justice" title="Miscarriage of justice">miscarriage of justice</a> on his part. Wargrave fakes his own death as the sixth murder on the island, with Armstrong's help, creating an enormous <a href="/wiki/Red_herring" title="Red herring">red herring</a> that fools everyone, and then kills Armstrong once the doctor's verdict of his death is accepted by the other visitors. After Vera Claythorne's death, Wargrave rearranges some furniture in the house, writes his confession which he <a href="/wiki/Message_in_a_bottle" title="Message in a bottle">throws in the sea</a>, and shoots himself in accordance with the others' diaries, using a rubber cord to throw the gun far from him, ensuring that his death will match their descriptions.</li>
<li><b>Sir Thomas Legge</b> and <b>Inspector Maine</b>, two detectives who discuss the case in the epilogue of the book, but are unable to solve the mystery.</li>
<li><b>Isaac Morris</b>, a lawyer hired by "Mr Owen" (Wargrave) to purchase the island on his behalf, and who is deceased before the story begins. He told the locals to ignore distress signals for a week. He also arranged for the financially desperate Lombard to come to the island armed, and to meet "Mr Owen" for a later payment of 100 <a href="/wiki/Guinea_(British_coin)" title="Guinea (British coin)">guineas</a> (105 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/GBP" title="GBP">GBP</a>). Like the guests on the island, Morris is responsible for someone's death. Through his narcotics dealings, he caused the addiction and suicide of a young woman, who just happened to be the daughter of friends of Justice Wargrave. The detectives discuss the death of Morris, from an overdose of sleeping medication, as does Wargrave's confession. Manipulated by his hypochondria, and to help with his "gastric juices", Morris trusted "Mr Owen" sufficiently to accept the latter's lethal cocktail of pills, assured they would improve his health. Morris is actually the first victim chronologically, dying before Marston.</li>
<li><b>Fred Narracott</b>, the boatman who delivered the guests to the island. After doing so he does not appear again in the story, although Inspector Maine notes it was Narracott who, sensing something seriously amiss, returned to the island as soon as the weather allowed, before he was supposed to, and found the bodies.</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_and_book_title_history">Publication and book title history</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Publication and book title history">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:202px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:And_Then_There_Were_None_First_Edition_Cover_1939.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="295" data-file-width="200" height="295" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/4/4a/And_Then_There_Were_None_First_Edition_Cover_1939.jpg" width="200"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:And_Then_There_Were_None_First_Edition_Cover_1939.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of first UK 1939 edition by Stephen Bellman with original title</div>

<p>The novel was originally published in 1939 and early 1940 almost simultaneously, in <a href="/wiki/Great_Britain" title="Great Britain">Great Britain</a> and the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a>. In Great Britain it was originally published under the title <i>Ten Little Niggers</i>, in book and <a href="/wiki/Serialization" title="Serialization">newspaper serialised</a> formats. The serialisation was in 23 parts in the <i><a href="/wiki/Daily_Express" title="Daily Express">Daily Express</a></i> from Tuesday, 6 June to Saturday, 1 July 1939. All of the instalments carried an illustration by "Prescott" with the first instalment having an illustration of <a href="/wiki/Burgh_Island" title="Burgh Island">Burgh Island</a> in Devon which inspired the setting of the story. The serialised version did not contain any chapter divisions.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The book retailed for seven <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Shillings" title="Shillings">shillings</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sixpence_(British_coin)" title="Sixpence (British coin)">sixpence</a> (£0.375 in the <a href="/wiki/%C2%A3sd" title="£sd">pre-decimal</a> currency of that time).</p>
<p>In the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> it was published under the title <i>And Then There Were None</i>, again in both book and serial formats. Both of the original US publications changed the title from that originally used in the UK, due to the extreme offensiveness of the word <a href="/wiki/Racism_in_the_United_States" title="Racism in the United States">in US culture</a>, where it was more widely understood as a <a href="/wiki/Loaded_language" title="Loaded language">racially loaded</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_slur" title="Ethnic slur">insult</a> compared to contemporary UK culture, and because of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_slur" title="Ethnic slur">pejorative connotations</a> of the original <a href="/wiki/Blackface" title="Blackface">blackface</a> rhyme. The serialized version appeared in the <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Saturday_Evening_Post" title="Saturday Evening Post">Saturday Evening Post</a></i> in seven parts from 20 May (Volume 211, Number 47) to 1 July 1939 (Volume 212, Number 1) with illustrations by Henry Raleigh, and the book was published in January 1940 by <a href="/wiki/Dodd,_Mead_and_Company" title="Dodd, Mead and Company">Dodd, Mead and Company</a> for $2.00.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-CC_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-CC-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pendergast_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-pendergast-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-US_6-1"><a href="#cite_note-US-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_and_title:_English_language">Publication and title: English language</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Publication and title: English language">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In the original UK novel all references to "Indians" or "Soldiers" were originally "Nigger", including the island's name, the pivotal rhyme found by the visitors, and the ten figurines.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-pendergast_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-pendergast-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> (In Chapter 7, Vera Claythorne becomes hysterical at the thought of "our black brothers," which is understandable only in the context of the original name.) The word "<a href="/wiki/Nigger" title="Nigger">Nigger</a>" was already <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Ethnic_slur" title="Ethnic slur">racially offensive</a> in the United States by the start of the 20th century, and therefore the book's first US edition and first serialization changed the title to <i>And Then There Were None</i> and removed all references to the word from the book, as did the 1945 motion picture.</p>
<p>The book and its adaptations have since been released under various new names since the original publication, including <i>Ten Little Indians</i> (1946 play, <a href="/wiki/Broadway_theatre" title="Broadway theatre">Broadway</a> performance and 1964 <a href="/wiki/Paperback" title="Paperback">paperback</a> book) – this title was also later deemed offensive, in this case to <a href="/wiki/Indigenous_peoples_of_the_Americas" title="Indigenous peoples of the Americas">Native Americans</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> – <i>Ten Little Soldiers</i>, and the most widely used today,<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (October 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> <i>And Then There Were None</i>.</p>
<p>UK editions continued to use the work's original title until the 1980s; the first UK edition to use the alternative title <i>And Then There Were None</i> appeared in 1985 with a reprint of the 1963 Fontana Paperback.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Publication_and_title:_non-English_languages">Publication and title: non-English languages</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Publication and title: non-English languages">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The original title (<i>Ten Little Niggers</i>) still survives in a few foreign-language versions of the novel, such as the <a href="/wiki/Bulgarian_language" title="Bulgarian language">Bulgarian</a> title <i>Десет малки негърчета</i>, and was used in other languages for a time, for example in the <a href="/wiki/Dutch_language" title="Dutch language">Dutch</a> publication until the 18th edition of 1994.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup> The title <i>Ten Little Negroes</i> continues to be commonly used in foreign-language versions, for example in Spanish, Greek, Serbian, Romanian,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Romanian_lang_16-0"><a href="#cite_note-Romanian_lang-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> French<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-French_lang_17-0"><a href="#cite_note-French_lang-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> and Hungarian, as well as a 1987 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Russian_film" title="Russian film">Russian film</a> adaptation <i><a href="/wiki/Desyat_Negrityat" title="Desyat Negrityat">Десять негритят (Desyat Negrityat)</a></i>. In 1999, the <a href="/wiki/Slovak_National_Theatre" title="Slovak National Theatre">Slovak National Theatre</a> showed the play under its original title but then changed the name to <i>A napokon nezostal už nik</i> (And Then There Were None) mid-run,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> while the Italian translation is titled <i>Dieci piccoli indiani</i> (<i>Ten Little Indians</i>).</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_significance_and_reception">Literary significance and reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Literary significance and reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>And Then There Were None</i> is one of Agatha Christie's best-known mysteries, widely considered her <a href="/wiki/Masterpiece" title="Masterpiece">masterpiece</a> and described by her as the most difficult of her books to have written.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harpercollins2009_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-harpercollins2009-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Writing for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Times_Literary_Supplement" title="The Times Literary Supplement">The Times Literary Supplement</a></i> of 11 November 1939, Maurice Percy Ashley stated, "If her latest story has scarcely any detection in it there is no scarcity of murders... There is a certain feeling of monotony inescapable in the regularity of the deaths which is better suited to a serialized newspaper story than a full-length novel. Yet there is an ingenious problem to solve in naming the murderer," he continued. "It will be an extremely astute reader who guesses correctly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Many other reviews were also complimentary; in <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times_Book_Review" title="The New York Times Book Review">The New York Times Book Review</a></i> (25 February 1940), Isaac Anderson detailed the set-up of the plot up to the point where "the voice" accuses the ten "guests" of their past crimes or sins, which have all resulted in the deaths of other human beings, and then said, "When you read what happens after that you will not believe it, but you will keep on reading, and as one incredible event is followed by another even more incredible you will still keep on reading. The whole thing is utterly impossible and utterly fascinating. It is the most baffling mystery that Agatha Christie has ever written, and if any other writer has ever surpassed it for sheer puzzlement the name escapes our memory. We are referring, of course, to mysteries that have logical explanations, as this one has. It is a tall story, to be sure, but it could have happened."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Such was the quality of Christie's work on this book that many compared it to her 1926 novel <i><a href="/wiki/The_Murder_of_Roger_Ackroyd" title="The Murder of Roger Ackroyd">The Murder of Roger Ackroyd</a></i>. For instance, an unnamed reviewer in the <i><a href="/wiki/Toronto_Star" title="Toronto Star">Toronto Daily Star</a></i> of 16 March 1940 said, "Others have written better mysteries than Agatha Christie, but no one can touch her for ingenious plot and surprise ending. With <i>And Then There Were None</i>... she is at her most ingenious and most surprising... is, indeed, considerably above the standard of her last few works and close to the <i>Roger Ackroyd</i> level."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Other critics laud the use of plot twists and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Surprise_ending" title="Surprise ending">surprise endings</a>. Maurice Richardson wrote a rhapsodic review in <i><a href="/wiki/The_Observer" title="The Observer">The Observer</a>'</i>s issue of 5 November 1939 which began, "No wonder Agatha Christie's latest has sent her publishers into a <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/Vatic" title="wikt:Vatic">vatic</a> trance. We will refrain, however, from any invidious comparisons with <i>Roger Ackroyd</i> and be content with saying that <i>Ten Little Niggers</i> is one of the very best, most genuinely bewildering Christies yet written. We will also have to refrain from reviewing it thoroughly, as it is so full of shocks that even the mildest revelation would spoil some surprise from somebody, and I am sure that you would rather have your entertainment kept fresh than criticism pure." After stating the set-up of the plot, Richardson concluded, "Story telling and characterisation are right at the top of Mrs Christie's baleful form. Her plot may be highly artificial, but it is neat, brilliantly cunning, soundly constructed, and free from any of those red-herring false trails which sometimes disfigure her work."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-vuyqxh_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-vuyqxh-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Robert_Barnard" title="Robert Barnard">Robert Barnard</a>, a recent critic, concurred with the reviews, describing the book as "Suspenseful and menacing detective-story-cum-thriller. The closed setting with the succession of deaths is here taken to its logical conclusion, and the dangers of ludicrousness and sheer reader-disbelief are skillfully avoided. Probably the best-known Christie, and justifiably among the most popular."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The original name of the mystery (<i>Ten Little Niggers</i>) has long been abandoned as offensive in English-speaking countries and a number of others. Some critics have opined that Christie's original title and the setting on "Nigger Island" (later changed to "Indian Island" and "Soldier Island", variously) are perhaps integral to the work. These aspects of the novel, argues Alison Light, "could be relied upon automatically to conjure up a thrilling 'otherness', a place where revelations about the 'dark side' of the English would be appropriate."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Light_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-Light-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> Unlike novels such as <i><a href="/wiki/Heart_of_Darkness" title="Heart of Darkness">Heart of Darkness</a></i>, "Christie's location is both more domesticated and privatised, taking for granted the construction of racial fears woven into psychic life as early as the nursery. If her story suggests how easy it is to play upon such fears, it is also a reminder of how intimately tied they are to sources of pleasure and enjoyment."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Light_23-1"><a href="#cite_note-Light-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In the "Binge!" article of <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> Issue #1343-44 (26 December 2014–3 January 2015), the writers picked <i>And Then There Were None</i> as an "EW favorite" on the list of the "Nine Great Christie Novels".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Adaptations">Adaptations</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Adaptations">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>And Then There Were None</i> has had more adaptations than any other single work of <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie" title="Agatha Christie">Agatha Christie</a>. They often used Christie's alternative ending from her <a href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_None_(play)" title="And Then There Were None (play)">1943 stage play</a>, with the setting often being changed to locations other than an island.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Stage">Stage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Stage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 1943, Agatha Christie <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_None_(1943_play)" title="And Then There Were None (1943 play)">adapted the story</a> for the stage. In the process of doing so, she and the producers agreed that audiences might not flock to such a grim tale and it would not work well dramatically as there would be no one left to tell the tale. Thus, she reworked the ending for Lombard and Vera to be innocent of the crimes of which they were accused, survive, and fall in love with each other. Some of the names were also changed with General Macarthur becoming General McKenzie, most likely due to the same surnamed real-life <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/General_Douglas_MacArthur" title="General Douglas MacArthur">General Douglas MacArthur</a> playing a prominent role in the ongoing <a href="/wiki/World_War_II" title="World War II">World War II</a>. On 14 October 2005, a new version of the play, written by Kevin Elyot and directed by Steven Pimlott, opened at the <a href="/wiki/Gielgud_Theatre" title="Gielgud Theatre">Gielgud Theatre</a> in London. For this version, Elyot returned to the original story and restored the original downbeat ending in which Lombard and Vera both die.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Dundee_Repertory_Theatre" title="Dundee Repertory Theatre">Dundee Repertory Theatre Company</a> was given special permission to restore the original ending of the novel. The company first performed a stage adaptation of the novel in 1944 under its original title.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>There have been numerous film adaptations of the novel, some adapted or comedic; at present only one of these – <a href="/wiki/Stanislav_Govorukhin" title="Stanislav Govorukhin">Stanislav Govorukhin</a>'s 1987 Russian adaptation <i><a href="/wiki/Desyat_Negrityat" title="Desyat Negrityat">Desyat' negrityat</a></i> (<i><span lang="ru" xml:lang="ru">Десять негритят</span></i>, Eng: "Ten Little Negroes") – keeps intact Christie's grim storyline and ending. The first cinema adaptation was <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Clair" title="René Clair">René Clair</a>'s <a href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_None_(1945_film)" title="And Then There Were None (1945 film)">successful 1945 US production</a> in 1945, followed in 1965 by <a href="/wiki/George_Pollock_(director)" title="George Pollock (director)">George Pollock</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Ten_Little_Indians_(1965_film)" title="Ten Little Indians (1965 film)">second cinema adaptation</a>; Pollock had previously handled four Miss Marple films starring <a href="/wiki/Margaret_Rutherford" title="Margaret Rutherford">Margaret Rutherford</a>. Another significant production includes the <a href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_None_(1974_film)" title="And Then There Were None (1974 film)">first English-language colour version</a> (<a href="/wiki/Peter_Collinson_(film_director)" title="Peter Collinson (film director)">Peter Collinson</a>, 1974).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Television">Television</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Television">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Several variations of the original novel were adapted for television. For instance, there were two different British adaptions, the BBC adaption in 1949<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-RT-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> and ITV adaptation in 1959.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> In addition, there was an American version, <i>Ten Little Indians</i>, directed by Paul Bogart, Philip F. Falcone, Leo Farrenkopf and Dan Zampino, with the screenplay by Philip H. Reisman Jr., that was a truncated TV adaptation of the play. A <a href="/wiki/West_Germany" title="West Germany">West German</a> adaptation, <i>Zehn kleine Negerlein</i>, was directed by Hans Quest for <a href="/wiki/ZDF" title="ZDF">ZDF</a> in 1969.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In 1970, Pierre Sabbagh directed <i>Dix petits nègres</i> for the French television adaptation. In 1974, Jean Fayyad directed the television series "10 Little Niggars" in Arabic for the National Lebanese Television. The adaptation to television was orchestrated by Latifeh Moultaka. The series was a great success. In 2014, the Lebanese channel MTV Lebanon revived the television series as "عشرة عبيد صغار" or "Ten Little Servants". In <a href="/wiki/Cuba" title="Cuba">Cuba</a>, the novel was adapted in 1981 in a black and white six parts series starring Yolanda Ruiz, Miguel Navarro and Fernando Robles in the Vera Claythorne, Philip Lombard and Justice Wargrave roles.</p>
<p>The CBS television show <i><a href="/wiki/Harper%27s_Island" title="Harper's Island">Harper's Island</a></i> was loosely modelled on the book; however, there are 25 characters instead of 10, and the body count at at the end differs.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2013)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>The ninth season premiere of <i><a href="/wiki/Family_Guy" title="Family Guy">Family Guy</a></i> began with an hour-long parody, "<a href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_Fewer" title="And Then There Were Fewer">And Then There Were Fewer</a>;" the titles and much of the episode's plot is a parody of the mystery.</p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/Mathnet" title="Mathnet">Mathnet</a></i> on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Square_One_TV" title="Square One TV">Square One TV</a> had a story arc, "The Case of the Mystery Weekend", based on this story, with a surprise ending. <sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Please_clarify" title="Wikipedia:Please clarify"><span title="The text near this tag may need clarification or removal of jargon. (June 2013)">clarification needed</span></a></i>]</sup> In February 2014, the <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> announced it had commissioned a <a href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_None_(2015_film)" title="And Then There Were None (2015 film)">film of the same name</a> based on the novel.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>A seventh-season episode of the US show <a href="/wiki/Psych" title="Psych">Psych</a>, titled "100 Clues," follows the novel's basic structure when Shawn and Gus are invited to a remote estate by an aging rock star whom they helped incriminate years ago.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Radio">Radio</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Radio">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The BBC broadcast "Ten Little Niggers" adapted by <a href="/wiki/Ayton_Whitaker" title="Ayton Whitaker">Ayton Whitaker</a> as a Monday Matinee on the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Home_Service" title="BBC Home Service">Home Service</a> on 27 December 1947 and as <a href="/wiki/Saturday_Night_Theatre" title="Saturday Night Theatre">Saturday Night Theatre</a> on the <a href="/wiki/BBC_Light_Programme" title="BBC Light Programme">Light Programme</a> on 29 December.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-RT_26-1"><a href="#cite_note-RT-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On 13 November 2010, as part of its <i>Saturday Play</i> series, <a href="/wiki/BBC_Radio_4" title="BBC Radio 4">BBC Radio 4</a> broadcast a 90-minute adaptation written by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Joy_Wilkinson&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Joy Wilkinson (page does not exist)">Joy Wilkinson</a>. The production was directed by <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Mary_Peate&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Mary Peate (page does not exist)">Mary Peate</a> and featured, among others, <a href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Whitehead" title="Geoffrey Whitehead">Geoffrey Whitehead</a> as Justice Wargrave, <a href="/wiki/Lyndsey_Marshal" title="Lyndsey Marshal">Lyndsey Marshal</a> as Vera Claythorne, <a href="/wiki/Alex_Wyndham" title="Alex Wyndham">Alex Wyndham</a> as Philip Lombard, <a href="/wiki/John_Rowe_(actor)" title="John Rowe (actor)">John Rowe</a> as Dr. Armstrong, and <a href="/wiki/Joanna_Monro" title="Joanna Monro">Joanna Monro</a> as Emily Brent. In this production, which is extremely faithful to the novel, the rhyme is "Ten Little Soldier Boys".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2012)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Other_media">Other media</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Other media">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/The_Adventure_Company" title="The Adventure Company">The Adventure Company</a> released the video game <i><a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie:_And_Then_There_Were_None" title="Agatha Christie: And Then There Were None">Agatha Christie: And Then There Were None</a></i> in 2005, the first in a series of <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie_(video_game_series)" title="Agatha Christie (video game series)">PC games based on Christie novels</a>. In February 2008, it was ported to the <a href="/wiki/Wii" title="Wii">Wii</a> console. The identity of the murderer is not that of the killer in the original book. The game player assumes the role of Fred Naracott, who is stranded with the others when his boat is scuttled. This allows for alternate, more successful endings in which Naracott survives and is able to prevent the final murders. <i>And Then There Were None</i> was released by <a href="/wiki/HarperCollins" title="HarperCollins">HarperCollins</a> as a <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a> adaptation on 30 April 2009, adapted by François Rivière and illustrated by Frank Leclercq.</p>
<p>A computer <a href="/wiki/Adventure_game" title="Adventure game">adventure game</a> based on the novel uses the title "Ten Little Sailor Boys".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/HarperCollins" title="HarperCollins">HarperCollins</a></i> released a <a href="/wiki/Graphic_novel" title="Graphic novel">graphic novel</a> adaptation of the book in 2009.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-harpercollins2009_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-harpercollins2009-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Peká Editorial released a <a href="/wiki/Board_game" title="Board game">board game</a> based on the book, created by Judit Hurtado and Fernando Chavarría, and illustrated by Esperanza Peinado.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-29"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parodies.2C_references_and_related_works">Parodies, references and related works</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=And_Then_There_Were_None&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Parodies, references and related works">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The 1933 <a href="/wiki/Sono_Art-World_Wide_Pictures" title="Sono Art-World Wide Pictures">K.B.S. Productions</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a></i> film, <i><a href="/wiki/A_Study_in_Scarlet_(1933_film)" title="A Study in Scarlet (1933 film)">A Study in Scarlet</a></i>, predates the publication of <i>Ten Little Indians</i> and follows a strikingly similar plot;<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> it includes a scene where Holmes is shown a card with the hint: "Six little Indians ....bee stung one and then there were five"; the film predates the novel by six years. Though it is a <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> movie, the movie bears no resemblance to <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Conan_Doyle" title="Arthur Conan Doyle">Arthur Conan Doyle</a>'s original <a href="/wiki/A_Study_in_Scarlet" title="A Study in Scarlet">story of the same name</a>. In this case, the rhyme refers to "Ten Little Fat Boys". The author of the movie's screenplay, <a href="/wiki/Robert_Florey" title="Robert Florey">Robert Florey</a>, "doubted that [Christie] had seen <i>A Study in Scarlet</i>, but he regarded it as a compliment if it had helped inspire her".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Several parodies have been made. As early as autumn 1942, "World's Finest Comics" (#7, Fall Issue) had a Superman story by Jerry Siegel and Joe Shuster called "The Eight Doomed Men" which used Christie's basic structure and even borrowed a number of her victim's backgrounds, although Superman intervened to rescue half of the intended victims and the killer's motivation was changed to specific revenge. Siegel and Shuster anticipated the 1966 film by moving the locale to a mountain cabin and tossed in a Zeppelin-like dirigible – one location not yet used in adaptations of the story. Another parody, the 1976 Broadway musical <i><a href="/wiki/Something%27s_Afoot" title="Something's Afoot">Something's Afoot</a></i>, stars <a href="/wiki/Tessie_O%27Shea" title="Tessie O'Shea">Tessie O'Shea</a> as a female sleuth resembling <a href="/wiki/Miss_Marple" title="Miss Marple">Miss Marple</a>. <i>Something's Afoot</i> takes place in a remote English estate, where six guests have been invited for the weekend. The guests, as well as three servants and a young man who claims to have wandered innocently onto the estate, are then murdered one by one, several in full view of the audience, with the murderer's surprise identity revealed at the end. For an encore, the murdered cast members perform a song, "I Owe It All to Agatha Christie".<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (May 2014)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<p>In television, the story was spoofed in the 1966 <i><a href="/wiki/Get_Smart" title="Get Smart">Get Smart</a></i> episode "Hoo Done It", which featured guest star <a href="/wiki/Joey_Forman" title="Joey Forman">Joey Forman</a> as "Detective Harry Hoo", a parody of <a href="/wiki/Charlie_Chan" title="Charlie Chan">Charlie Chan</a>. An episode of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Spider-Man_and_his_Amazing_Friends" title="Spider-Man and his Amazing Friends">Spider-Man and his Amazing Friends</a></i> titled "7 Little Superheroes" is, being a children's show, a murder-free adaptation of the story. The <i><a href="/wiki/Remington_Steele" title="Remington Steele">Remington Steele</a></i> episode "Steele Trap" and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Adventures_of_Brisco_County,_Jr." title="The Adventures of Brisco County, Jr.">The Adventures of Brisco County, Jr.</a></i> episode "Bounty Hunters Convention" were other television episodes inspired by the story. In the 1967 <i><a href="/wiki/The_Avengers_(TV_series)" title="The Avengers (TV series)">The Avengers</a></i> adaption "The Superlative Seven"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/John_Steed" title="John Steed">John Steed</a> is invited to a costume party aboard a chartered aeroplane. The aeroplane is being flown by remote control. Steed and the six other fancy-dressed guests, who are specialists in various combat styles, eventually land on a deserted island where they are informed that one of them is a trained assassin trying to kill them all. When the first murder is committed, Steed observes "Looks as though his back was broken". To which an off-screen protagonist responds, over a speaker, "Quite right, Mr Steed. And then there were six".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-33"><a href="#cite_note-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/CSI:Crime_Scene_Investigation" title="CSI:Crime Scene Investigation">CSI:Crime Scene Investigation</a></i> based an episode of the show's second season under the same name focusing on a <a href="/wiki/Gang" title="Gang">gang</a> of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Armed_robbery" title="Armed robbery">armed robbers</a> stealing from casinos outside the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Las_Vegas_metropolitan_area" title="Las Vegas metropolitan area">Las Vegas metropolitan area</a> with each criminal killing a member of the gang to keep more of the proceeds.</p>
