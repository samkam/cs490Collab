<p><i><b>The Name of the Rose</b></i> (<a href="/wiki/Italian_language" title="Italian language">Italian</a>: <span lang="it" xml:lang="it"><i>Il nome della rosa</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/German_language" title="German language">German</a>: <span lang="de" xml:lang="de"><i>Der Name der Rose</i></span>, <a href="/wiki/French_language" title="French language">French</a>: <span lang="fr" xml:lang="fr"><i>Le nom de la rose</i></span>) is a 1986 film directed by <a href="/wiki/Jean-Jacques_Annaud" title="Jean-Jacques Annaud">Jean-Jacques Annaud</a>, based on <a href="/wiki/The_Name_of_the_Rose" title="The Name of the Rose">the book of the same name</a> by <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Eco" title="Umberto Eco">Umberto Eco</a>. <a href="/wiki/Sean_Connery" title="Sean Connery">Sean Connery</a> is the <a href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> friar <a href="/wiki/William_of_Baskerville" title="William of Baskerville">William of Baskerville</a> and <a href="/wiki/Christian_Slater" title="Christian Slater">Christian Slater</a> is his apprentice Adso of <a href="/wiki/Melk" title="Melk">Melk</a>, who are called upon to solve a deadly mystery in a <a href="/wiki/Middle_Ages" title="Middle Ages">medieval</a> abbey.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Franciscan" title="Franciscan">Franciscan</a> friar <a href="/wiki/William_of_Baskerville" title="William of Baskerville">William of Baskerville</a> and his novice, Adso of <a href="/wiki/Melk" title="Melk">Melk</a> (narrating as an old man), arrive at an early 14th century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Benedictine" title="Benedictine">Benedictine</a> abbey in Northern Italy. A mysterious death has occurred ahead of an important theological Church conference. William, known for his deductive and analytic mind, confronts the worried Abbot and gains permission to investigate the death – a young <a href="/wiki/Illuminated_manuscript" title="Illuminated manuscript">illuminator</a> appears to have committed suicide. Over the next few days, several other bizarre deaths occur, and the two gradually discover that everything is not what it seems in the abbey.</p>
<p>William and Adso also make the acquaintance of Salvatore, a demented hunchback who speaks gibberish in various languages, and his handler and protector, Remigio da Varagine who, as events prove, also has a shady past. William quickly deduces from Salvatore's <a href="/wiki/Penitenziagite" title="Penitenziagite">speech</a> that he had once been a member of <a href="/wiki/Dulcinian" title="Dulcinian">a heretical sect</a> and infers that Salvatore and Remigio may have been involved in the killings. Meanwhile, Adso encounters a beautiful semi-<a href="/wiki/Feral_child" title="Feral child">feral</a> peasant girl who has apparently sneaked into the abbey to trade sexual favours for food; she seduces him, and he falls in love with her.</p>
<p>Investigating and keen to head off accusations of <a href="/wiki/Demonic_possession" title="Demonic possession">demonic possession</a>, the protagonists discover and explore a labyrinthine library in the abbey's forbidden principal tower. William is astonished to find that it is "one of the greatest libraries in all Christendom," containing dozens of works by Classical masters such as <a href="/wiki/Aristotle" title="Aristotle">Aristotle</a>, thought to have been lost for centuries. William deduces that the library is kept hidden because such advanced knowledge, coming from pagan philosophers, is difficult to reconcile with Christianity. It becomes clear that the only remaining copy of Aristotle's <i>Second Book of <a href="/wiki/Poetics_(Aristotle)" title="Poetics (Aristotle)">Poetics</a></i> is somehow related to the deaths. He further deduces that all of those who died had read the book.</p>
<p>His investigations are curtailed by the arrival of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Bernardo_Gui" title="Bernardo Gui">Bernardo Gui</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Inquisition" title="Inquisition">Inquisition</a>, summoned for the conference and keen to prosecute those he deems responsible for the deaths. The two men clashed in the past, and the zealous inquisitor has no time for theories outside his own. Salvatore and the girl are found fighting over a black cockerel while in the presence of a black cat. Gui presents this as irrefutable proof that they are in league with Satan and tortures Salvatore into confessing. Salvatore, Remigio, and the girl are dragged before a tribunal, where Gui intimidates the Abbot into concurring with his judgment of heresy. But William, also "invited" by Gui to serve on the panel of judges, refuses to confirm the accusations of murder, pointing out that the murderer could read Greek, a skill that Remigio doesn't possess. Gui resorts to extracting a confession from Remigio by the threat of torture, and clearly plans to take care of William for good.</p>
<p>When another monk succumbs like the others, William and Adso ascend the forbidden library, and come face to face with the Venerable Jorge, the most ancient denizen of the abbey, with the book, which describes comedy and how it may be used to teach. Believing laughter and jocularity to be instruments of the Devil, Jorge has poisoned the pages to stop the spread of what he considers dangerous ideas: those reading it would ingest the poison as they licked their fingers to aid in turning pages. Confronted, Jorge throws over a candle, starting a blaze that quickly engulfs the library. William insists that Adso flee, as he manages to collect an inadequate armload of invaluable books to save; the volume of <i>Poetics</i>, Jorge, and the rest of the library are lost.</p>
<p>Meanwhile, Salvatore and Remigio have been burned at the stake. The girl has been slated for the same fate but local peasants take advantage of the chaos of the library fire to free her and turn on Gui. Gui attempts to flee but they throw his wagon off a cliff, to his death. William and Adso later take their leave of the Abbey. On the road, Adso is stopped by the girl, silently appealing for him to stay with her, but Adso continues on with William. In his closing narration, a much older Adso reflects that he never regretted his decision, and that the girl was the only earthly love of his life, yet he never learned her name.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Production">Production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Rocca_calascio.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="3264" data-file-width="2448" height="333" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Rocca_calascio.jpg/250px-Rocca_calascio.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Rocca_calascio.jpg/375px-Rocca_calascio.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/e5/Rocca_calascio.jpg/500px-Rocca_calascio.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:252px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Castel_del_monte02.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="768" data-file-width="1024" height="188" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Castel_del_monte02.jpg/250px-Castel_del_monte02.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Castel_del_monte02.jpg/375px-Castel_del_monte02.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Castel_del_monte02.jpg/500px-Castel_del_monte02.jpg 2x" width="250"/></a>

<a href="/wiki/Castel_del_Monte,_Apulia" title="Castel del Monte, Apulia">Castel del Monte, Apulia</a>, set of the library of the abbey</div>

<p>Director Jean-Jacques Annaud once told Umberto Eco that he was convinced the book was written for only one person to direct, that is to say himself. He felt personally intrigued by the project, among other things because of a lifelong fascination with medieval churches and a great familiarity with Latin and Greek.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Annaud spent four years preparing the film, traveling throughout the United States and Europe, searching for the perfect multiethnic cast with interesting and distinctive faces. He resisted suggestions to cast Sean Connery for the part of William because he felt that the character, who was already an amalgam of <a href="/wiki/Sherlock_Holmes" title="Sherlock Holmes">Sherlock Holmes</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/William_of_Occam" title="William of Occam">William of Occam</a>, would become too overwhelming with "<a href="/wiki/James_Bond" title="James Bond">007</a>" added.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Later, after Annaud failed to find another actor he liked for the part, he was won over by Connery's reading, but Eco was dismayed by the casting choice and <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> pulled out, as Connery's career was then in a slump.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd_2-2"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Christian Slater was cast through a large-scale audition of teenage boys.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd_2-3"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> For the wordless scene in which the Girl seduces Adso, Annaud allowed Valentina Vargas to lead the scene without his direction. Annaud did not explain to Slater what she would be doing in order to elicit a more authentic performance from the actors.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd_2-4"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The exterior and some of the interiors of the monastery seen in the film were constructed as a replica on a hilltop outside Rome, and ended up being the biggest exterior set built in Europe since <i>Cleopatra</i>. Many of the interiors were shot at <a href="/wiki/Eberbach_Abbey" title="Eberbach Abbey">Eberbach Abbey</a>, Germany. Most props, including period illuminated manuscripts, were produced specifically for the film.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dvd_2-5"><a href="#cite_note-dvd-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reception">Reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The film did poorly at the box office in the United States, playing at only 176 theatres and grossing only $7.2 million in return on a $17 million budget.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mojo_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-mojo-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> However, it was popular in many parts of Europe and had a worldwide gross of over $77 million. It received generally positive reviews from American and Italian critics, with review aggregator <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> giving it a 76% approval rating.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Awards">Awards</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Name_of_the_Rose_(film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Awards">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The film was awarded the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/C%C3%A9sar_award" title="César award">César</a> for best foreign film.</li>

Sean Connery for best actor, and <a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Hasso_von_Hugo&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Hasso von Hugo (page does not exist)">Hasso von Hugo</a> won Best Make Up Artist.</li>
</ul>
