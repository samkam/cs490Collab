<h1 class="entry-title">Top 100 Children’s Novels #8: Anne of Green Gables by L.M. Montgomery</h1>
<div class="post-info"><span class="date published time" title="2012-06-18T04:02:44+00:00">June 18, 2012</span>  By <span class="author vcard"><span class="fn"><a class="fn n" href="http://blogs.slj.com/afuse8production/author/elizabethbird/" rel="author" title="Elizabeth Bird">Elizabeth Bird</a></span></span> </div> <div class="entry-content">
<p><a href="http://blogs.slj.com/afuse8production/files/2012/06/AnneGreenGables1.jpg"><img alt="" class="alignright size-medium wp-image-13940" height="271" src="http://blogs.slj.com/afuse8production/files/2012/06/AnneGreenGables1-222x300.jpg" title="AnneGreenGables1" width="200"/></a>#8 Anne of Green Gables by L.M. Montgomery (1908)<br/>
182 points</p>
<p><em>Oh how I wanted to be Anne Shirley growing up! I could relate to her so much-I was imaginative and had a temper to match, so I always felt as though Anne was a kindred spirit. And who wouldn’t want to end up with Gilbert Blythe?? This is a series that whenever I would read them, I would find myself in a “reading fog”.  I would stop reading and have to remember that I wasn’t on Prince Edward Island with Anne and Diana. It always seemed like such a magical place and I wished for those books to be real.  A friend said it best when she told me “there’s always a Anne book for every stage of life.” I think that’s what makes them timeless.</em> – Sarah (<a href="http://www.greenbeanteenqueen.com/" target="_blank">Green Bean Teen Queen</a>)</p>
<p><em>Anne took this skinny, awkward, mousy-haired suburban lass from the age of bell bottoms and sunset-print polyester shirts and dropped her into a world of Victorian charm.  A world of puffed sleeves, bosom friends, strolls down wooded lanes, and unbridled imagination. I must have reread Gilbert rescuing Anne from under the bridge a million times.   Oh, the transforming power of literature on a young romantic soul.  Anne, how I dreamed of being you</em>. – DaNae Leu</p>
<p><em>L. M. Montgomery’s books are the sort of books I reread every few years just to feel that life is good</em>. – Sondra Eklund</p>
<p>L.M. Montgomery, to my mind,  single-handedly destroys the notion that authors give themselves  initials as their first names so as to throw off potential male readers  who wouldn’t want a book penned by a woman.  Is there any book in this  world girlier than <em>Anne of Green Gables</em>?  Or, for that matter,  any other of Ms. Montgomery’s works?  Be that as it may be, tis a fine  novel for both the boy and girl set.  Aside from<em> Pippi Longstocking</em>, there’s no other literary redhead of quite the same tomboyish aspects as our Anne.</p>
<p>How it came to be:  In <em>100 Best Books for Children</em> by Anita Silvey we learn that when Ms. Montgomery began writing the book she,  “first intended the story to be a mere seven chapters long, ideal for a  serial treatment in a Sunday school paper.”  That plan quickly fell by  the wayside and so she submitted it to several publishers.  It was  rejected multiple times, and according to <em>What Katy Read</em>, after  she got four rejections in a row, “Montgomery put the manuscript in an  old hat-box, intending at some later date to cut it back to its original  proportions. But she changed her mind when she rediscovered the  forgotten work in the winter of 1906, and decided to try it out once  more.”  So it reached L.C. Page and Company.  They offered her “either  an outright fee of $500 or a royalty of 9 cents a book.”  Thank the  heavens above she went with the royalty.  Her first royalty check =  $1730.  The book was an instant hit.</p>
<p>Obviously the publisher wanted sequels and she obliged, though she would say that the, “<em>freshness</em> of the <em>idea</em> was gone . . . I simply <em>built</em> it. Anne, grown-up, couldn’t be made as quaint and unexpected as the child Anne.” Seven books would follow, but they never quite lived up to the first.</p>
<p>Book #1 remains hugely beloved.  Indeed in December 2009 a first edition of this book <a href="http://www.theguardian.pe.ca/index.cfm?sid=310104&amp;sc=98">sold at auction</a> for $37,500.  This smashed the previous child vintage children’s novel  record of a mere $24,000.  Sotheby’s also auctioned off the book in  2005, but that sale was marred slightly by the fact that they referred  to the title as “a beloved <em>American</em> children’s book.” One must assume that the Canadians were NOT pleased.</p>
<p>There haven’t been any sequels by other folks, partly because  Montgomery was clever enough to write them herself.  There was, however,  a relatively recent prequel.  In conjunction with Anne’s 100th birthday, Budge  Wilson wrote <em>Before Green Gables</em>.  It met with mixed reviews, though many folks liked it.  It has, however, largely been forgotten since its publication.</p>
<ul>
<li>I do believe you can <a href="http://activerain.com/blogsview/407207/100-years-of-anne-of-green-gables-">visit</a> Lucy Maud Montgomery’s house if you like.</li>
</ul>

<p>Periodically the book gets filmed.  Not as often as <em>Little Women</em> or anything, but continually just the same.  First there was the 1919  version.  Not on YouTube, obviously, but you can listen to <a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=zFTuNL2Q1ew">the theme</a> if you really want to.  Back in 1934 there was this version, directed by George Nichols Jr.:</p>
<p>http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Lw499WSH7aM&amp;feature=embed</p>
<p>Then came a 1956 version, but  that’s probably best left forgotten.  No, the Anne that is undeniably  the best there is, bar none, came in 1985.  It was produced for  television, brilliantly cast, and when people of my generation think of  the book it’s hard not to conjure up Megan Follows’ face.</p>
