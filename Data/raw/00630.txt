<p><i><b>Music Box</b></i> is the third <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">studio album</a> by American recording artist <a href="/wiki/Mariah_Carey" title="Mariah Carey">Mariah Carey</a>. It was released by <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a> on August 31, 1993, in North America. The album comprises <a href="/wiki/Ballad" title="Ballad">ballads</a> primarily co-written by Carey and <a href="/wiki/Walter_Afanasieff" title="Walter Afanasieff">Walter Afanasieff</a>, with whom she had previously worked on <i><a href="/wiki/Emotions_(Mariah_Carey_album)" title="Emotions (Mariah Carey album)">Emotions</a></i> (1991), and a few urban dance tracks. During the course of the album's development, Carey wanted to broaden her audience, choosing a more pop/R&amp;B oriented sound. During this time frame, they experimented with different <a href="/wiki/Organ_(music)" title="Organ (music)">organs</a> and other musical instruments, leading the album's sound away from her more contemporary previous efforts. Two unused tracks from the album sessions were released as <a href="/wiki/A-side_and_B-side" title="A-side and B-side">B-sides</a>: "Do You Think of Me" and "Everything Fades Away".</p>
<p>In order to successfully take the album in a new direction, Carey and Afanasieff sought out new and innovative producers, as well as some from Carey's previous releases. <a href="/wiki/Babyface_(musician)" title="Babyface (musician)">Kenneth "Babyface" Edmonds</a> first began working with Carey on <i>Music Box</i>, where he helped produce some of the album's softer and more melodious tracks, as well as being part of the songwriting process. Additional writers and producers were <a href="/wiki/Robert_Clivill%C3%A9s" title="Robert Clivillés">Robert Clivillés</a> and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/David_Cole_(producer)" title="David Cole (producer)">David Cole</a> (a pair also known as <a href="/wiki/C%2BC_Music_Factory" title="C+C Music Factory">C+C Music Factory</a>) and <a href="/wiki/Daryl_Simmons" title="Daryl Simmons">Daryl Simmons</a>. While the album featured a range of different talented music producers, the bulk of the songwriting was done by Carey and her writing partner, Walter Afanasieff. In future projects, they would continue writing material for Carey's albums, until her 1999 release <i><a href="/wiki/Rainbow_(Mariah_Carey_album)" title="Rainbow (Mariah Carey album)">Rainbow</a></i>, where he is absent from the writing credits.</p>
<p>Four singles were released from the album. The first three, "<a href="/wiki/Dreamlover_(song)" title="Dreamlover (song)">Dreamlover</a>", "<a href="/wiki/Hero_(Mariah_Carey_song)" title="Hero (Mariah Carey song)">Hero</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Without_You_(Badfinger_song)#Mariah_Carey_version" title="Without You (Badfinger song)">Without You</a>", became worldwide chart-topping singles, the latter becoming Carey's highest charting international single of her career. "Without You" became Carey's first number-one single in most European markets and reached the top three in the United States. To promote <i>Music Box</i>, Carey embarked on the short but successful <a href="/wiki/Music_Box_Tour" title="Music Box Tour">Music Box Tour</a> which traveled to select cities in North America. Carey was nominated for <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award_for_Best_Female_Pop_Vocal_Performance" title="Grammy Award for Best Female Pop Vocal Performance">Best Female Pop Vocal Performance</a> for "Dreamlover" at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/36th_Grammy_Awards" title="36th Grammy Awards">1994 Grammy Awards</a> and received the same nomination for "<a href="/wiki/Hero_(Mariah_Carey_song)" title="Hero (Mariah Carey song)">Hero</a>" at the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/37th_Grammy_Awards" title="37th Grammy Awards">1995 Grammy Awards</a>.</p>
<p>After its release, <i>Music Box</i> received generally mixed reviews from music critics. The album faced criticism regarding Carey's more mellow and lazy tone in comparison to her previous work. Despite some unfavorable reviews, the album became a commercial success, topping the <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> and becoming Carey's first number-one album in Australia, France, Germany, the Netherlands, Switzerland and the United Kingdom. The <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA) certified it <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">diamond</a>, for shipments of ten million copies across the United States. <i>Music Box</i> remains Carey's highest seller with 32 million copies sold worldwide and is one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_albums_worldwide" title="List of best-selling albums worldwide">best-selling albums</a> of all time.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Background">Background</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_Box_(Mariah_Carey_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Background">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: right; clear: right; margin: 0.5em 0 0.8em 1.4em; width:30%; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9; padding:8px">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"Mariah and Dave did this loop thing, and it was new to us pop producers at the time. Their version of 'Dreamlover' was missing a lot of stuff. The spirit of the song was up but it wasn't hitting hard enough. [My work on the track] put a whole different shade of colors into it."</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: left;">—Afanasieff on "Dreamlover" before and after he worked on it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>In 1988, Carey was discovered by <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Mottola" title="Tommy Mottola">Tommy Mottola</a>, CEO of <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Records" title="Columbia Records">Columbia Records</a>, and was promptly signed to the label. Carey's <a href="/wiki/Mariah_Carey_(album)" title="Mariah Carey (album)">self-titled</a> debut <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Studio_album" title="Studio album">studio album</a>, released the following year, focused on re-recording and mastering several songs she had already written in high school alongside classmate, <a href="/wiki/Ben_Margulies" title="Ben Margulies">Ben Margulies</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-nickson-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Aside from the seven songs taken from Carey's demo tape, four other tracks were written and produced by the former and an array of famed record producers. The album was complimented by critics, who called it a mature debut, full of various genre influences ranging from <a href="/wiki/Pop_music" title="Pop music">pop</a>, <a href="/wiki/Contemporary_R%26B" title="Contemporary R&amp;B">R&amp;B</a> and <a href="/wiki/Soul_music" title="Soul music">soul</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson2_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nickson2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> The album became a commercial success, selling fifteen million units globally.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bet_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-bet-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> While making a strong impact on pop music, Carey became interested in altering her sound, and deviating from pop music for her second studio effort, <i><a href="/wiki/Emotions_(Mariah_Carey_album)" title="Emotions (Mariah Carey album)">Emotions</a></i> (1991).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson2_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-nickson2-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Following the success of her debut, Columbia allowed her to take more control over her musical departure, enabling her to change her genre infusions, melodies and production.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Writing_and_development">Writing and development</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_Box_(Mariah_Carey_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Writing and development">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>With Carey in the captain's chair, having more control than she had on any other album, she took the album in a new direction, alongside Afanasieff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> For Carey's third studio effort, she enrolled the help of a range of songwriters, as well as record producers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-2"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Aside from Afanasieff, <a href="/wiki/Babyface_(musician)" title="Babyface (musician)">Kenneth "Babyface" Edmonds</a>, a man who would collaborate with Carey often in the near future, also took part in the project.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-3"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Babyface, who helped produce much of the album, also co-wrote a track with Carey titled "<a href="/wiki/Never_Forget_You" title="Never Forget You">Never Forget You</a>", a song that was released as a B-side to "Without You" exclusively in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-4"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The album, which consisted mostly of slower ballads (with the exception of "Dreamlover" and "Now That I Know"), contained collaborations with some carry-on producers and writers from <i><a href="/wiki/Emotions_(Mariah_Carey_album)" title="Emotions (Mariah Carey album)">Emotions</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-5"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Of these were <a href="/wiki/C%2BC_Music_Factory" title="C+C Music Factory">Clivillés &amp; Cole</a> (of C+C Music Factory), who co-wrote the track "Now That I Know", a danceable pop ballad, which used similar formulas and synthesizers from tracks on <i>Emotions</i>. Another writer–producer that worked on the album was <a href="/wiki/Dave_Hall_(record_producer)" title="Dave Hall (record producer)">David Hall</a>, who with Carey wrote the U.S. single "Dreamlover".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-6"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>During the album's recording, Carey worked with several different musicians and producers, aside from <a href="/wiki/Walter_Afanasieff" title="Walter Afanasieff">Walter Afanasieff</a>, the only hold over from her debut.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson3_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-nickson3-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> On the album's first track "<a href="/wiki/Dreamlover_(song)" title="Dreamlover (song)">Dreamlover</a>", Carey worked with <a href="/wiki/Dave_Hall_(record_producer)" title="Dave Hall (record producer)">Dave Hall</a> throughout the song's entire production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-7"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson6_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-nickson6-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> In order to help with some of the song's arrangements, Mottola enrolled the help of <a href="/wiki/Walter_Afanasieff" title="Walter Afanasieff">Walter Afanasieff</a>, who took on the completed track and transformed it into a more commercial hit.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-8"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><i>Emotions</i> contained influences from mostly 1950s, 1960s and 1970s balladry and <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel</a>, as well as her continued work of R&amp;B and soul. The album, while praised by some as more mature and raw, failed to reach the critical or commercial heights of her debut effort, while selling far less and failed to introduce Carey into a different market.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson4_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-nickson4-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> Following these events, Columbia decided to try to market Carey in a similar fashion to her debut, only having her produce a more commercial and radio-friendly album. Their plans were to tone down Carey's vocals, and soften the album's production, leaving a more contemporary pop/R&amp;B record.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson5_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-nickson5-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Agreeing to the change, Carey and Afanasieff began writing and recording material for her third studio effort, <i>Music Box</i>.</p>
<p>One of the noticeable differences from <i>Music Box</i> and Carey's previous albums was its sound. The album was described by Afanasieff as a softer and more pop-oriented album, "filling the songs with air", and allowing far more space in the overall sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-9"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Another noticeable change was in the album's production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-10"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> When <i>Mariah Carey</i> was released, critics took notice of its "overly produced" and "studio perfect" quality, where in comparison, <i>Emotions</i> maintained a "raw, live sound."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-11"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Music Box</i> however, fell in between the two, a decision made by Carey during the album's production.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-12"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> She would layer each track with live backing vocals, so not to sound too overly produced, but still kept the inclusion of musical synthesizers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-13"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to Marc Shapiro, <i>Music Box</i> reflected signs of Carey's vocal maturity, as well as representing an album she was truly proud of.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's first single "<a href="/wiki/Dreamlover_(song)" title="Dreamlover (song)">Dreamlover</a>" was described as a "slight piece of pop fluff," representing a more commercial side to Carey than the "more ambitious," "<a href="/wiki/Vision_of_Love" title="Vision of Love">Vision of Love</a>". Critics believed the song's chart performance was due to its summer release, as people were still looking for a "not-too-heavy" and more diverse sound.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-1"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The song's composition was described as "mid <a href="/wiki/Tempo" title="Tempo">tempo</a> and mildly dance-able," with Carey's voice being called "perpetually happy," like a "little-girl voice."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-14"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Songs">Songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_Box_(Mariah_Carey_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="quotebox" style="float: left; clear: left; margin: 0.5em 1.4em 0.8em 0; width:294px; padding: 6px; border: 1px solid #aaa; font-size: 88%; background-color: #F9F9F9;">
<div class="" style="position: relative; text-align: left;">
<div>
<p>"It's not that I'm experimenting with lower notes. I actually think my natural voice is low. My speaking voice is low, you know what I mean? And I'm really comfortable singing in my lower register..., I sing from the heart. Whatever the music makes me feel at the time, I go into the studio to sing a song, that's what it's going to do. Some people like it, some people don't. But it's just a part of my voice and that's it."</p>
</div>
</div>
<div style="text-align: left;">—Carey, on her vocals on <i>Music Box</i>, as well as her voice in general<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick5_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-nick5-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></div>
</div>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Hero_(Mariah_Carey_song)" title="Hero (Mariah Carey song)">Hero</a>", the album's second single, was one of Carey's most inspirational ballads at the time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-15"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The song was described as "a lush ballad", with Carey making use of her impressive, "lower <a href="/wiki/Alto" title="Alto">alto</a> register."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick6_11-0"><a href="#cite_note-nick6-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> As one of the more emotional tracks on the album, "Hero" built emotion, verse through verse, where the lyrics and melody finally "broke through."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick6_11-1"><a href="#cite_note-nick6-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Anytime_You_Need_a_Friend" title="Anytime You Need a Friend">Anytime You Need a Friend</a>" was another pop ballad in which Carey would, "let her voice roam free", a feature critics felt lacked on the album.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick6_11-2"><a href="#cite_note-nick6-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The song featured "rough and low vocals", as well as some glimpses of Carey's upper <a href="/wiki/Vocal_register" title="Vocal register">registers</a>. As with most of the songs on <i>Music Box</i>, the lyrics boasted a positive message, and it was the only song on the album to feature traces of <a href="/wiki/Gospel_music" title="Gospel music">gospel-inspired vocals</a> throughout the chorus.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick6_11-3"><a href="#cite_note-nick6-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album's title track, "Music Box", was another ballad Carey wrote with Afanasieff.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick6_11-4"><a href="#cite_note-nick6-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The song was described as one of Carey's more difficult <a href="/wiki/Musical_composition" title="Musical composition">compositions</a>, due to its "softness". The song requires a great deal of <a href="/wiki/Legato" title="Legato">legato</a>, to keep "the tunes softness and sweetness, without resorting to volume." Carey's vocals on the track are defined as "soft and controlled," managing to maintain the delicate balance in a manner that seems effortless, floating easily over the keyboard and the shimmer of the guitar.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick6_11-5"><a href="#cite_note-nick6-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Lyrically, due to the song's message of "commitment and promise," and the "tinkling music-box line played on the synthesizer," the track gives the sensation of a wedding vow recital.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick6_11-6"><a href="#cite_note-nick6-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> "Never Forget You" is a slow song, further connecting it to the song's message of "lamenting the loss of love, in a very tender way." The song contains keyboard notes that hover over the verses and allow Carey to indulge in her backing vocals.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick6_11-7"><a href="#cite_note-nick6-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> It was described by Nickson as a "stand out track," one that could have easily become a hit single, "with an appeal that would have easily transcended generational barriers."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick6_11-8"><a href="#cite_note-nick6-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>Upon release, the album received mixed reviews from critics.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Ron_Wynn" title="Ron Wynn">Ron Wynn</a> from <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Allmusic" title="Allmusic">Allmusic</a> gave the album 4 out of 5 stars, Roc Wynn of Allmusic said that Carey's "octave-leaping" voice was downplayed in favor of the demonstration of "her ability to sing softly and coolly."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_12-2"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Although favorably stating that Carey lowered the volume on her vocals, he said that the energy had declined, with the exception of personality-injected songs like "Hero" and "Dreamlover." Overall, however, he claimed the album's "different" approach was wise.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-allmusic_12-3"><a href="#cite_note-allmusic-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Ashley S. Battel from <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> gave the album a positive review, writing, "While Carey tones down the predominance of her tremendous vocal range throughout much of this release, there is no question that she remains the driving force behind yet anoth[e]r collection of heavy-rotation Top-40 successes."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-bbissue_13-1"><a href="#cite_note-bbissue-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/David_Browne" title="David Browne">David Browne</a> from <i><a href="/wiki/Entertainment_Weekly" title="Entertainment Weekly">Entertainment Weekly</a></i> gave the album a mixed review.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew_15-1"><a href="#cite_note-ew-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> He said that due to Carey's lower energy level, her voice no longer soars above the backup chorus, instead it only "drip[s] over them like syrup instead of overpowering them; she lets the melodies speak for themselves.".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew_15-2"><a href="#cite_note-ew-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Browne awarded the album an unenthusiastic score of C+.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ew_15-3"><a href="#cite_note-ew-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Stephen Holden of <i><a href="/wiki/Rolling_Stone" title="Rolling Stone">Rolling Stone</a></i> complimented Carey's vocals, but found her lyrics "made up entirely of pop and soul clichés" and stated "<i>Music Box</i> is so precisely calculated to be a blockbuster that its impact is ultimately a little unnerving".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rs_17-1"><a href="#cite_note-rs-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> However, in his review for <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, Holden wrote that the album "takes aim at the commercial pop mainstream with an even sharper focus" than Carey's previous albums, while noting "If huge ballads like 'Hero,' 'Anytime You Need a Friend' and a gospel-flavored remake of Harry Nilsson's 1971 hit, 'Without You,' traffic in pop cliches, they are the top-of-the-line in generic 90's pop".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYtimes_16-1"><a href="#cite_note-NYtimes-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> In his consumer guide for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Village_Voice" title="The Village Voice">The Village Voice</a></i>, critic <a href="/wiki/Robert_Christgau" title="Robert Christgau">Robert Christgau</a> gave the album a "dud" rating,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rc_14-1"><a href="#cite_note-rc-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> indicating "a bad record whose details rarely merit further thought".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> In a retrospective review, <i><a href="/wiki/The_Rolling_Stone_Album_Guide" title="The Rolling Stone Album Guide">The New Rolling Stone Album Guide</a></i> (2004) noted a "soft-rock slump" and stated "While there's nothing wrong with Carey singing relatively straight for 10 songs, there is a sore lack of power here: baleful soaring ballads, pop optimism along the lines of smiling through the tears and believing in yourself (or him, or Him), and hardly a decent tune in the lot".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rsguide_18-1"><a href="#cite_note-rsguide-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Commercial_performance">Commercial performance</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Music_Box_(Mariah_Carey_album)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Commercial performance">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><i>Music Box</i> entered the US <a href="/wiki/Billboard_200" title="Billboard 200"><i>Billboard</i> 200</a> at number two, with 174,000 copies sold.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> In its fifteenth week after release, the album topped the chart and enjoyed its highest sales week in December, selling 295,000 copies in its first week atop, 395,000 the following week and peaking with 505,000 copies sold in year's final week. It stayed at the top for eight non-consecutive weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> It remained in the top ten for thirty-one weeks and on the <i>Billboard</i> 200 for 128 weeks (more than two years, longer than any other of her albums), re-entering the chart three times.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> The album also reached number one on <a href="/wiki/Top_R%26B/Hip-Hop_Albums" title="Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums">Top R&amp;B/Hip-Hop Albums</a>. <i>Music Box</i> was the second-best selling album in the United States in 1994, only behind <a href="/wiki/Ace_of_Base" title="Ace of Base">Ace of Base</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Happy_Nation#The_Sign" title="Happy Nation">The Sign</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> In the United States, <i>Music Box</i> became Carey's best-selling album at the time, being certified <a href="/wiki/RIAA_certification" title="RIAA certification">diamond</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">RIAA</a>, denoting shipments of ten million copies. As of October 2013, the album has sold 7,277,000 copies in the US according to Nielsen Soundcan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcsales_24-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcsales-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The album became a success in Canada, peaking at number two on the charts and being certified seven-times <a href="/wiki/List_of_music_recording_certifications" title="List of music recording certifications">platinum</a> by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Canadian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Canadian Recording Industry Association">Canadian Recording Industry Association</a> (CRIA). <i>Music Box</i> became Carey's best-seller in Europe, topping the charts in Australia, Germany, Switzerland and the United Kingdom. In Germany, it became her highest-charting album, spending eighty weeks on the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Media_Control_Charts" title="Media Control Charts">German Albums Chart</a>, eleven of which were spent at number one. Germany's sales of the album were also very impressive, with a certification of double-platinum by the <a href="/wiki/International_Federation_of_the_Phonographic_Industry" title="International Federation of the Phonographic Industry">International Federation of the Phonographic Industry</a> (IFPI), for shipments of one million copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-mcgerman_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-mcgerman-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-German_certifications_database_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-German_certifications_database-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> In Australia, the album was certified twelve-times platinum by the <a href="/wiki/Australian_Recording_Industry_Association" title="Australian Recording Industry Association">Australian Recording Industry Association</a> (ARIA), denoting shipments of 840,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kent_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-kent-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The album finished at number one on the (ARIA) 1994 End of Year Chart.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-28"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-aus_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-aus-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> In the United Kingdom, <i>Music Box</i> remains Carey's highest seller, topping the <a href="/wiki/UK_Albums_Chart" title="UK Albums Chart">UK Albums Chart</a> and being certified five-times platinum. It became a million-selling album in France, receiving a diamond certification and topping the <a href="/wiki/Syndicat_National_de_l%27%C3%89dition_Phonographique" title="Syndicat National de l'Édition Phonographique">French Album Charts</a>. Sales in France have been estimated at 1,418,100.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Les_Albums_Diamant_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-Les_Albums_Diamant-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> In Brazil, "Music Box" remains one of the best-selling albums by an international artist, selling o 800,000 copies.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Brazil_sales_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Brazil_sales-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> "Music Box" finished number twenty-three on the all time album chart in the Netherlands, where it was certified six-times platinum.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-dutch_32-0"><a href="#cite_note-dutch-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Dutch_Certifications_to_Music_Box_33-0"><a href="#cite_note-Dutch_Certifications_to_Music_Box-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In Asia, <i>Music Box</i> became one of the best-selling albums of 1994, selling 2,200,000 copies in Japan alone.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tvcarey_34-0"><a href="#cite_note-tvcarey-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mariahnumberone_35-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mariahnumberone-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup> The album's sales continued to skyrocket in Asia, topping sales of 1,000,000 in South Korea and 320,000 copies in Taiwan as of 1994.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tvcarey_34-1"><a href="#cite_note-tvcarey-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-skcarey_36-0"><a href="#cite_note-skcarey-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup> In Singapore and Hong Kong, sales reached 110,000 and 80,000 copies respectively.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-tvcarey_34-2"><a href="#cite_note-tvcarey-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Music Box</i> has sold 32 million copies worldwide, and is one of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_albums_worldwide" title="List of best-selling albums worldwide">best-selling albums</a> of all time.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-betMB_37-0"><a href="#cite_note-betMB-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-yahoo_38-0"><a href="#cite_note-yahoo-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-soul_39-0"><a href="#cite_note-soul-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>"<a href="/wiki/Dreamlover_(song)" title="Dreamlover (song)">Dreamlover</a>" was released as the album's first single on July 27, 1993.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro_40-0"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> The single debuted at number thirteen on the <i><a href="/wiki/Billboard_(magazine)" title="Billboard (magazine)">Billboard</a></i> <a href="/wiki/Billboard_Hot_100" title="Billboard Hot 100">Hot 100</a>, and due to its massive airplay, the song reached the top of the Hot 100, staying there for eight weeks.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro_40-1"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard_Hot_100_41-0"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard_Hot_100-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> <i>Dreamlover</i> topped the charts in Canada, and reached the top-ten in many other countries.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-42"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup> The song was certified <a href="/wiki/Music_recording_sales_certification" title="Music recording sales certification">platinum</a> by the <a href="/wiki/Recording_Industry_Association_of_America" title="Recording Industry Association of America">Recording Industry Association of America</a> (RIAA), denoting shipments of one million copies, as well as receiving a gold certification in Australia and New Zealand.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-kent_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-kent-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-43"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-scapolo_44-0"><a href="#cite_note-scapolo-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup> The music video for "Dreamlover" featured "summer scenery," with scenes of Carey swimming in a pool by a waterfall, lying in a bed of sunflowers, as well as singing in front of hip-hop dancers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-16"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> The video tried to capture, a "home video" feel, adding to the song's subtle and airy nature, something that only helped the song dominate the charts<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nickson7_1-17"><a href="#cite_note-nickson7-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Hero_(Mariah_Carey_song)" title="Hero (Mariah Carey song)">Hero</a>" served as the album's second single, and was released on October 19, 1993. Music critics praised the song, calling it her most directly inspirational song since "<a href="/wiki/Make_It_Happen_(Mariah_Carey_song)" title="Make It Happen (Mariah Carey song)">Make It Happen</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nick6_11-9"><a href="#cite_note-nick6-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> The song topped the charts in the United States and the top-five in many other major music markets.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard_Hot_100_41-1"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard_Hot_100-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-45"><a href="#cite_note-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> The music video for "Hero," featured footage from Carey's concert at <a href="/wiki/Proctor%27s_Theatre_(Schenectady,_New_York)" title="Proctor's Theatre (Schenectady, New York)">Proctor's Theatre</a>, as was done for Carey's following music video for "Without You."</p>
<p>"<a href="/wiki/Without_You_(Badfinger_song)#Mariah_Carey_version" title="Without You (Badfinger song)">Without You</a>", the album's third single, became the biggest international hit of Carey's career. "Without You" reached number three in the United States, but experienced its real success throughout Europe.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard_Hot_100_41-2"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard_Hot_100-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> The song became Carey's first number one chart topper in France, Germany, Sweden, Switzerland and the United Kingdom, propelling the sales of <i>Music Box</i> throughout the world.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-46"><a href="#cite_note-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup> Carey brought the song renewed popularity, through her highly successful adaptation. It became one of the "fifty most-played songs of the year" and even outsold <a href="/wiki/Harry_Nilsson" title="Harry Nilsson">Harry Nilsson</a>'s version, receiving many awards and recognition.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-47"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> "<a href="/wiki/Anytime_You_Need_a_Friend" title="Anytime You Need a Friend">Anytime You Need a Friend</a>", the album's final single, experienced success on the charts, reaching the top-ten in many countries, as well as peaking at number twelve in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Billboard_Hot_100_41-3"><a href="#cite_note-Billboard_Hot_100-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> It became the second single in <i>Music Box</i> to perform better throughout Europe than in the U.S.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Mccharts2_48-0"><a href="#cite_note-Mccharts2-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p>In order to promote the album, Carey embarked on her first-headlining tour. Originally, Carey had not planned to tour due to stage fright, a feat she followed during her previous album releases.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-2"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> However, after the continued success of <i>Music Box</i> and persuasion from <a href="/wiki/Tommy_Mottola" title="Tommy Mottola">Tommy Mottola</a>, Carey agreed to tour, supporting her current and previous albums.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-3"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> As Carey was not physically or emotionally ready for an extensive tour, six shows were dated, each with many days in between, in order to give Carey's voice time to rest.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-4"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> An extra performance, taking place at <a href="/wiki/Proctor%27s_Theatre_(Schenectady,_New_York)" title="Proctor's Theatre (Schenectady, New York)">Proctor's Theatre</a> was planned, where Carey would film the one-hour special, <i><a href="/wiki/Here_Is_Mariah_Carey" title="Here Is Mariah Carey">Here Is Mariah Carey</a></i>, to be released during the Christmas season.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-5"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>When the tickets went on sales, the shows did not sell-out instantly; but were selling at a healthy pace.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-6"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> On the opening night of Carey's tour, she sang at the <a href="/wiki/Miami_Arena" title="Miami Arena">Miami Arena</a>. The show was only about two-thirds full, something that worried Carey's management, as it was the tour's opening night. However, Carey did not seem to mind, and began the show with high spirits.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-7"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> The first show in Miami received scathing reviews, with critics "ripping her opening-night performance to shreds." The following shows were more favorable to Carey, not only were her succeeding shows sold-out, they also won rave-reviews.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-8"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Regarding Carey's show in New York, <a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a> wrote the following remarks regarding Carey's voice and performance on the tour:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>"Beyond any doubt, Ms. Carey's voice is no studio concoction. Her range extends from a rich, husky alto to dog-whistle high notes; she can linger over sensual turns, growl with playful confidence, syncopate like a scat singer. Although rock concerts aren't known for precise intonation, she sang with startlingly exact pitch."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytreview_49-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytreview-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>Aside from touring for the first time in her career, Carey visited various American and European television programs, where she performed different singles from the album. In late 1993, Carey appeared on <a href="/wiki/The_Arsenio_Hall_Show" title="The Arsenio Hall Show">The Arsenio Hall Show</a>, where she performed both "<a href="/wiki/Dreamlover_(song)" title="Dreamlover (song)">Dreamlover</a>" and "<a href="/wiki/Hero_(Mariah_Carey_song)" title="Hero (Mariah Carey song)">Hero</a>." Other performances Carey staged during 1993 included, "Hero" on <a href="/wiki/The_Jay_Leno_Show" title="The Jay Leno Show">The Jay Leno Show</a> and "Dreamlover" on her first visit to <a href="/wiki/Top_of_the_Pops" title="Top of the Pops">Top of the Pops</a>. Throughout 1994, Carey continued the album's promotion, performing "<a href="/wiki/Without_You_(Badfinger_song)#Mariah_Carey_version" title="Without You (Badfinger song)">Without You</a>" on a second visit to "Top of the Pops," as well as visiting France, Germany, Japan, Spain and Sweden. In the midst of her "Music Box Tour," Carey had already begun working with Walter Afanasieff on her holiday album <i><a href="/wiki/Merry_Christmas_(Mariah_Carey_album)" title="Merry Christmas (Mariah Carey album)">Merry Christmas</a></i>, which was to be released during the Christmas season of the following year.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-9"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Additionally, Carey and Affanasieff reportedly already began experimenting with different ideas and musical components for her 1995 studio album, <i><a href="/wiki/Daydream_(Mariah_Carey_album)" title="Daydream (Mariah Carey album)">Daydream</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-shapiro3_9-10"><a href="#cite_note-shapiro3-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
