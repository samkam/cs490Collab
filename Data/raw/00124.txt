<p style="text-align: center;"><img alt="jd-salinger-catcher-in-the-rye-book-cover-slice" class="aligncenter size-full wp-image-147592" height="200" src="http://cdn.collider.com/wp-content/uploads/jd-salinger-catcher-in-the-rye-book-cover-slice.jpg" title="jd-salinger-catcher-in-the-rye-book-cover-slice" width="600"/></p>
<p>If we’re lucky, <strong><em>Catcher in the Rye</em></strong> will never be made into a movie.  It’s managed to avoid that fate for over sixty years, and hopefully it will continue to do so.  Not everything <em>needs</em> to be a movie, and I say that as someone who loves movies.  We live in an age where something always had to be translated into other things, and whoever owns the rights happily takes the money that translation provides.  <strong>J.D. Salinger</strong> was not one of those people and he never sold the rights for <em>Catcher in the Rye</em>.  Producers will continue to hunger for the opportunity to turn one of the greatest novels of all-time into something that can be viewed in under two hours, but Salinger wasn’t just being stodgy when he refused to give up the rights.  In a letter he wrote in 1957, he listed his reasons for why he couldn’t see <em>Catcher in the Rye</em> as a play or a motion picture.  Hit the jump for his arguments.</p>
<p><span id="more-147583"></span><img alt="catcher-in-the-rye-book-cover" class="size-full wp-image-147594 alignright" height="550" src="http://cdn.collider.com/wp-content/uploads/catcher-in-the-rye-book-cover.jpg" style="margin: 5px;" title="catcher-in-the-rye-book-cover" width="356"/>The letter popped up online about two years ago, but for those who didn’t see it, Cameron Crowe <a href="http://www.whosay.com/cameroncrowe/photos/135034" target="_blank">posted a partial copy</a> on his website yesterday.  In the letter, Salinger lists the following reasons why he doesn’t think <em>Catcher in the Rye</em> can be translated to the stage or screen:</p>
<ul>
<li>It’s a      “novelistic” novel.       Salinger talks about the asides of Holden’s thoughts like      “gasoline rainbows in street puddles, his philosophy or way of      looking at cowhide suitcases and empty toothpaste cartons—in a way, his      thoughts.”</li>
<li>The      “immeasurably risky business of using actors.”  Salinger couldn’t envision a child      actress playing Phoebe or a young actor playing Holden because even if      they had “X”, they wouldn’t know what to do with it.</li>
<li>No      director could get the necessary performances from these actors.</li>
</ul>
<p>Salinger particularly couldn’t see the book being done as a stage play due to the medium’s technical limitations.</p>
<p>One could argue that Salinger simply didn’t have the imagination to consider what could be done with his novel, and it’s impossible to know what he would say about a film adaptation based on how far the medium has come since 1957 (he died in 2010 and no recorded comment on a <em>Catcher in the Rye</em> movie has been found past his letter).  But those arguments are beside the point.  To be honest, Salinger could have simply responded, “Fuck you.  That’s why,” and it would be a respectable answer.  We can dream about how such-and-such would make a good movie or TV show, but the original dreamer should always have the final say.  He or she doesn’t owe us an explanation, but I commend Salinger for providing his rationale to someone who asked for it.</p>
<p>Crowe published part of the letter on his website.  Here’s the full copy [via <a href="http://www.momentsintime.com/J%20D%20Salinger.htm" target="_blank">Moments in Time</a>]:</p>
<p style="text-align: center;"><a href="http://cdn.collider.com/wp-content/uploads/jd-salinger-catcher-in-the-rye-letter-rejection.jpg" target="_blank"><img alt="jd-salinger-catcher-in-the-rye-letter-rejection" class="aligncenter size-thumbnail wp-image-147595" height="600" src="http://cdn.collider.com/wp-content/uploads/jd-salinger-catcher-in-the-rye-letter-rejection-475x600.jpg" title="jd-salinger-catcher-in-the-rye-letter-rejection" width="475"/></a></p>

<div class="dsq-comment-body" id="dsq-comment-body-689598">
<div class="dsq-comment-message" id="dsq-comment-message-689598"><p>There seems to be a misunderstanding- a few have commented that “Catcher in the Rye”  could be made into a feature film. Yes, obviously if you have a camera anything can be made into a film- its a question of the quality of the film that is debatable.</p>
<p>I would argue a resounding “NO”, no it would not make for a decent film. Nor a decent TV series, mini series or a stage play. Because the book is more or less a long soliloquy (yes, I remember he’s technically talking to a psychologist)- but I say soliloquy because the character rambling in great detail about the events of a weekend, his emotions, opinions….. its not set up as a conversation and there aren’t various points of view. The closest film that could resemble the narrative style of Catcher in the Rye, might be “Ferris Buellers Day Off”- because Bueller is revealing what he’s thinking directly at the camera (at us) and breaks off to talk to his friends or run around Chicago. But Bueller isn’t a complex character, he’s the guy having a good time; Holden Caulfield is a far more complicated character.</p>
<p>On the flip side of the argument- would I want to read a novel adaptation of “Ferris Bueller’s Day Off”? No, and I doubt most people would ask “why, what’s the point in that?”. So why would people want to see a film adaptation of “Catcher in the Rye” – again, why and what’s the point in that?</p>
