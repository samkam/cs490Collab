<p><i><b>The Da Vinci Code</b></i> is a 2003 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mystery_(fiction)" title="Mystery (fiction)">mystery</a>-<a href="/wiki/Detective_fiction" title="Detective fiction">detective</a> novel written by <a href="/wiki/Dan_Brown" title="Dan Brown">Dan Brown</a>. It follows <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Symbology" title="Symbology">symbologist</a> <a href="/wiki/Robert_Langdon" title="Robert Langdon">Robert Langdon</a> and cryptologist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sophie_Neveu" title="Sophie Neveu">Sophie Neveu</a> after a murder in the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Louvre_Museum" title="Louvre Museum">Louvre Museum</a> in Paris, when they become involved in a battle between the <a href="/wiki/Priory_of_Sion" title="Priory of Sion">Priory of Sion</a> and <a href="/wiki/Opus_Dei" title="Opus Dei">Opus Dei</a> over the possibility of Jesus having been married to <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>. The title of the novel refers, among other things, to the finding of the first murder victim in the Grand Gallery of the Louvre, naked and posed like <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci</a>'s famous drawing, the <i><a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a></i>, with a cryptic message written beside his body and a <a href="/wiki/Pentagram" title="Pentagram">pentagram</a> drawn on his chest in his own blood.</p>
<p>The novel explores an alternative religious history, whose central plot point is that the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merovingian" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> <a href="/wiki/List_of_French_monarchs" title="List of French monarchs">kings of France</a> were descended from <a href="/wiki/Jesus_bloodline" title="Jesus bloodline">the bloodline of Jesus Christ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>, ideas derived from Clive Prince's <i><a href="/wiki/The_Templar_Revelation" title="The Templar Revelation">The Templar Revelation</a></i> (1997) and books by Margaret Starbird. The book also refers to <i><a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Blood_and_the_Holy_Grail" title="The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail">The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail</a></i> (1982) though Dan Brown has stated that it was not used as research material.</p>
<p><i>The Da Vinci Code</i> provoked a popular interest in speculation concerning the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a> legend and Mary Magdalene's role in the <a href="/wiki/History_of_Christianity" title="History of Christianity">history of Christianity</a>. The book has, however, been extensively denounced by many <a href="/wiki/List_of_Christian_denominations" title="List of Christian denominations">Christian denominations</a> as an attack on the Roman Catholic Church, and consistently criticized for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Inaccuracies_in_The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="Inaccuracies in The Da Vinci Code">its historical and scientific inaccuracies</a>. The novel nonetheless became a worldwide <a href="/wiki/List_of_best-selling_books" title="List of best-selling books">bestseller</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> that sold 80 million copies as of 2009<sup class="plainlinks noprint asof-tag update" style="display:none;"><a class="external text" href="//en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code&amp;action=edit">[update]</a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and has been translated into 44 languages. Combining the detective, <a href="/wiki/Thriller_(genre)" title="Thriller (genre)">thriller</a> and <a href="/wiki/Conspiracy_fiction" title="Conspiracy fiction">conspiracy fiction</a> genres, it is Brown's second novel to include the character <a href="/wiki/Robert_Langdon" title="Robert Langdon">Robert Langdon</a>: the first was his 2000 novel <i><a href="/wiki/Angels_%26_Demons" title="Angels &amp; Demons">Angels &amp; Demons</a></i>. In November 2004, <a href="/wiki/Random_House" title="Random House">Random House</a> published a Special Illustrated Edition with 160 illustrations. In 2006, a <a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code_(film)" title="The Da Vinci Code (film)">film adaptation</a> was released by <a href="/wiki/Sony" title="Sony">Sony's</a> <a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot_summary">Plot summary</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot summary">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p><a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a> curator and <a href="/wiki/Priory_of_Sion" title="Priory of Sion">Priory of Sion</a> Grand Master <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jacques_Sauni%C3%A8re" title="Jacques Saunière">Jacques Saunière</a> is fatally shot one night at the museum by an albino Catholic monk named <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Silas_(The_Da_Vinci_Code)" title="Silas (The Da Vinci Code)">Silas</a>, who is working on behalf of someone he knows only as the Teacher, who wishes to discover the location of the "keystone", an item crucial to the search for the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a>. After Saunière's body is discovered in the pose of the <a href="/wiki/Vitruvian_Man" title="Vitruvian Man">Vitruvian Man</a>, the police summon Harvard Professor Robert Langdon, who is in town on business. Police Captain Bezu Fache tells him that he was summoned to help the police decode the cryptic message Saunière left during the final minutes of his life. The message includes a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Fibonacci_sequence" title="Fibonacci sequence">Fibonacci sequence</a> out of order. Langdon explains to Fache that Saunière was a leading authority on the subject of goddess artwork and that the pentacle Saunière drew in his own blood represents an allusion to the goddess and not "devil worship", as Fache says.</p>
<p>A police <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cryptographer" title="Cryptographer">cryptographer</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sophie_Neveu" title="Sophie Neveu">Sophie Neveu</a>, secretly explains to Langdon that she is Saunière's estranged granddaughter, and that Fache thinks Langdon is the murderer, because her grandfather's message said "PS Find Robert Langdon", which she says Fache had erased prior to Langdon's arrival. Neveu is troubled by memories of her grandfather's involvement in a secret pagan group. However, she understands that her grandfather intended Langdon to decipher the code, which she and Langdon find leads them to a <a href="/wiki/Safe_deposit_box" title="Safe deposit box">safe deposit box</a> at the Paris branch of the Depository Bank of Zurich. Neveu and Langdon escape from the police and visit the bank. In the safe deposit box they find the keystone: a <a href="/wiki/Cryptex" title="Cryptex">cryptex</a>, a cylindrical, hand-held vault with five concentric, rotating dials labeled with letters. When these are lined up correctly, they unlock the device. If the cryptex is forced open, an enclosed vial of vinegar ruptures and dissolves the message inside the cryptex, which was written on papyrus. The box containing the cryptex contains clues to its password.</p>
<p>Langdon and Neveu take the keystone to the house of Langdon's friend, Sir Leigh Teabing, an expert on the Holy Grail. There, Teabing explains that the Grail is not a cup, but a tomb containing the bones of Mary Magdalene. The trio then flees the country on Teabing's private plane, on which they conclude that the proper combination of letters spell out Neveu's given name, "SOFIA." Opening the cryptex, they discover a smaller cryptex inside it, along with another riddle that ultimately leads the group to the tomb of <a href="/wiki/Isaac_Newton" title="Isaac Newton">Isaac Newton</a> in <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>.</p>
<p>During the flight to Britain, Neveu reveals the source of her estrangement from her grandfather, ten years earlier. Arriving home unexpectedly from university, Neveu clandestinely witnesses a spring fertility rite conducted in the secret basement of her grandfather's country estate. From her hiding place, she is shocked to see her grandfather having sex with a woman at the center of a ritual attended by men and women who are wearing masks and chanting praise to the goddess. She flees the house and breaks off all contact with Saunière. Langdon explains that what she witnessed was an ancient ceremony known as <a href="/wiki/Hieros_gamos" title="Hieros gamos">Hieros gamos</a> or "sacred marriage".</p>
<p>By the time they arrive at <a href="/wiki/Westminster_Abbey" title="Westminster Abbey">Westminster Abbey</a>, Teabing is revealed to be the Teacher for whom Silas is working. Teabing wishes to use the Holy Grail, which he believes is a series of documents establishing that <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a> married <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a> and bore children, in order to ruin the <a href="/wiki/Holy_See" title="Holy See">Vatican</a>. He compels Langdon at gunpoint to solve the second cryptex's password, which Langdon realizes is "APPLE." Langdon secretly opens the cryptex and removes its contents before destroying it in front of Teabing. Teabing is arrested by Fache, who by now knows that Langdon was innocent. Bishop Aringarosa, realizing that Silas has been used to murder innocent people, rushes to help the police find him. When the police find Silas hiding in an Opus Dei Center, he assumes that they are there to kill him, and he rushes out, accidentally shooting Bishop Aringarosa. Bishop Aringarosa survives but is informed that Silas was found dead later from a bullet wound.</p>
<p>The final message inside the second keystone leads Neveu and Langdon to <a href="/wiki/Rosslyn_Chapel" title="Rosslyn Chapel">Rosslyn Chapel</a>, whose docent turns out to be Neveu's long-lost brother, whom Neveu had been told died as a child in the car accident that killed her parents. The guardian of Rosslyn Chapel, Marie Chauvel Saint Clair, is Neveu's long-lost grandmother. It is revealed that Neveu is a descendant of Jesus Christ and Mary Magdalene. The Priory of Sion hid her identity to protect her from possible threats to her life.</p>
<p>The real meaning of the last message is that the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Grail</a> is buried beneath the small <a href="/wiki/Pyramid" title="Pyramid">pyramid</a> directly below the <a href="/wiki/La_Pyramide_Invers%C3%A9e" title="La Pyramide Inversée">inverted glass pyramid</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Louvre" title="Louvre">Louvre</a>. It also lies beneath the "Rose Line," an allusion to "Roslyn." Langdon figures out this final piece to the puzzle in the last pages of the book, but he does not appear inclined to tell anyone about this.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Secret_of_the_Holy_Grail">Secret of the Holy Grail</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Secret of the Holy Grail">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Da_Vinci_The_last_supper_detail_Da_Vinci_code.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="259" data-file-width="461" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Da_Vinci_The_last_supper_detail_Da_Vinci_code.jpg/220px-Da_Vinci_The_last_supper_detail_Da_Vinci_code.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Da_Vinci_The_last_supper_detail_Da_Vinci_code.jpg/330px-Da_Vinci_The_last_supper_detail_Da_Vinci_code.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/1/1c/Da_Vinci_The_last_supper_detail_Da_Vinci_code.jpg/440px-Da_Vinci_The_last_supper_detail_Da_Vinci_code.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>In the novel Leigh Teabing explains to <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sophie_Neveu" title="Sophie Neveu">Sophie Neveu</a> that the figure at the right hand of Jesus in Leonardo da Vinci's painting of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Last_Supper_(Leonardo)" title="The Last Supper (Leonardo)">"The Last Supper"</a> is not the <a href="/wiki/John_the_Apostle" title="John the Apostle">apostle John</a>, but actually <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>. Leigh Teabing says that the absence of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalice_(cup)" title="Chalice (cup)">chalice</a> in Leonardo's painting means Leonardo knew that Mary Magdalene was the actual <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a> and the bearer of Jesus' blood. Leigh Teabing goes on to explain that this idea is supported by the shape of the letter "V" that is formed by the bodily positions of Jesus and Mary, as "V" is the symbol for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_feminine" title="Sacred feminine">sacred feminine</a>. The absence of the Apostle John in the painting is explained by knowing that John is also referred to as "<a href="/wiki/Disciple_whom_Jesus_loved" title="Disciple whom Jesus loved">the Disciple Jesus loved</a>", <a href="/wiki/Gospel_of_Philip#Mary_Magdalene" title="Gospel of Philip">code</a> for <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene#Assertions_about_Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>. The book also notes that the <a href="/wiki/Color_scheme" title="Color scheme">color scheme</a> of their garments are inverted: Jesus wears a red tunic with royal blue cloak; Mary Magdalene wears the opposite.</p>
<p>According to the novel, the secrets of the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a>, as kept by the <a href="/wiki/Priory_of_Sion" title="Priory of Sion">Priory of Sion</a> are as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>The Holy Grail is not a physical chalice, but a woman, namely <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>, who carried the <a href="/wiki/Jesus_bloodline" title="Jesus bloodline">bloodline of Christ</a>.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Old_French" title="Old French">Old French</a> expression for the Holy Grail, <i>San gréal</i>, actually is a play on <i>Sang réal</i>, which literally means "royal blood" in Old French.</li>
<li>The Grail relics consist of the documents that testify to the bloodline, as well as the actual bones of <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>.</li>
<li>The Grail relics of <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a> were hidden by the <a href="/wiki/Priory_of_Sion" title="Priory of Sion">Priory of Sion</a> in a secret crypt, perhaps beneath <a href="/wiki/Rosslyn_Chapel" title="Rosslyn Chapel">Rosslyn Chapel</a>.</li>
<li>The Church has suppressed the truth about Mary Magdalene and the Jesus bloodline for 2000 years. This is principally because they fear the power of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sacred_feminine" title="Sacred feminine">sacred feminine</a> in and of itself and because this would challenge the primacy of <a href="/wiki/Saint_Peter" title="Saint Peter">Saint Peter</a> as an apostle.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a> was of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Royal_Descent" title="Royal Descent">royal descent</a> (through the Jewish <a href="/wiki/Benjamin" title="Benjamin">House of Benjamin</a>) and was the wife of Jesus, of the <a href="/wiki/David" title="David">House of David</a>. That she was a prostitute was <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Slander" title="Slander">slander</a> invented by the <a href="/wiki/Catholic_Church" title="Catholic Church">Church</a> to obscure their true relationship. At the time of the <a href="/wiki/Crucifixion" title="Crucifixion">Crucifixion</a>, she was pregnant. After the Crucifixion, she fled to <a href="/wiki/Gaul" title="Gaul">Gaul</a>, where she was sheltered by the Jews of <a href="/wiki/Marseille" title="Marseille">Marseille</a>. She gave birth to a daughter, named <a href="/wiki/Saint_Sarah" title="Saint Sarah">Sarah</a>. The bloodline of Jesus and Mary Magdalene became the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Merovingian" title="Merovingian">Merovingian</a> dynasty of France.</li>
<li>The existence of the bloodline was the secret that was contained in the documents discovered by the <a href="/wiki/Crusades" title="Crusades">Crusaders</a> after they conquered <a href="/wiki/Jerusalem" title="Jerusalem">Jerusalem</a> in 1099 (see <a href="/wiki/Kingdom_of_Jerusalem" title="Kingdom of Jerusalem">Kingdom of Jerusalem</a>). The <a href="/wiki/Priory_of_Sion" title="Priory of Sion">Priory of Sion</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Knights_Templar" title="Knights Templar">Knights Templar</a> were organized to keep the secret.</li>
</ul>
<p>The secrets of the Grail are connected, according to the novel, to <a href="/wiki/Leonardo_da_Vinci" title="Leonardo da Vinci">Leonardo da Vinci's</a> work as follows:</p>
<ul>
<li>Leonardo was a member of the Priory of Sion and knew the secret of the Grail. The secret is in fact revealed in <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Last_Supper" title="The Last Supper">The Last Supper</a></i>, in which no actual chalice is present at the table. The figure seated next to Christ is not a man, but a woman, his wife <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>. Most reproductions of the work are from a later alteration that obscured her obvious female characteristics.</li>
<li>The <a href="/wiki/Androgyny" title="Androgyny">androgyny</a> of the <a href="/wiki/Mona_Lisa" title="Mona Lisa">Mona Lisa</a> reflects the sacred union of male and female implied in the holy union of Jesus and Mary Magdalene. Such parity between the cosmic forces of masculine and feminine has long been a deep threat to the established power of the Church. The name <i>Mona Lisa</i> is actually an anagram for "Amon L'Isa", referring to the father and mother gods of <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Egyptian_religion" title="Ancient Egyptian religion">Ancient Egyptian religion</a> (namely <a href="/wiki/Amun" title="Amun">Amun</a> and <a href="/wiki/Isis" title="Isis">Isis</a>).</li>
</ul>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Reaction">Reaction</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Reaction">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Sales">Sales</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Sales">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><i>The Da Vinci Code</i> was a major success in 2003 and was outsold only by <a href="/wiki/J._K._Rowling" title="J. K. Rowling">J. K. Rowling</a>'s <i><a href="/wiki/Harry_Potter_and_the_Order_of_the_Phoenix" title="Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix">Harry Potter and the Order of the Phoenix</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Historical_inaccuracies">Historical inaccuracies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Historical inaccuracies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Criticism_of_The_Da_Vinci_Code" title="Criticism of The Da Vinci Code">Criticism of The Da Vinci Code</a></div>
<p>The book generated criticism when it was first published for inaccurate description of core aspects of Christianity and descriptions of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Western_art_history" title="Western art history">European art</a>, history, and architecture. The book has received mostly negative reviews from Catholic and other Christian communities.</p>
<p>Many critics took issue with the level of research Brown did when writing the story. <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/New_York_Times" title="New York Times">New York Times</a></i> writer Laura Miller characterized the novel as "based on a notorious hoax", "rank nonsense", and "bogus", saying the book is heavily based on the fabrications of <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Plantard" title="Pierre Plantard">Pierre Plantard</a>, who is asserted to have created the Priory of Sion in 1956.</p>
<p>Critics accuse Brown of distorting and fabricating history. For example, Marcia Ford wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>Regardless of whether you agree with Brown's conclusions, it's clear that his history is largely fanciful, which means he and his publisher have violated a long-held if unspoken agreement with the reader: Fiction that purports to present historical facts should be researched as carefully as a nonfiction book would be.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faithfulreader1_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-faithfulreader1-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p><a href="/wiki/Richard_Abanes" title="Richard Abanes">Richard Abanes</a> wrote:</p>
<blockquote class="templatequote">
<p>The most flagrant aspect... is not that Dan Brown disagrees with Christianity but that he utterly warps it in order to disagree with it... to the point of completely rewriting a vast number of historical events. And making the matter worse has been Brown's willingness to pass off his distortions as ‘facts' with which innumerable scholars and historians agree.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-faithfulreader1_4-1"><a href="#cite_note-faithfulreader1-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<p>The book opens with the claim by Dan Brown that "The Priory of Sion – a French <a href="/wiki/Secret_society" title="Secret society">secret society</a> founded in 1099 – is a real organization". This assertion is broadly disputed. Some critics claim that the <a href="/wiki/Priory_of_Sion" title="Priory of Sion">Priory of Sion</a> was a hoax created in 1956 by <a href="/wiki/Pierre_Plantard" title="Pierre Plantard">Pierre Plantard</a>. The author also claims that "all descriptions of artwork, architecture, documents... and secret rituals in this novel are accurate", but this claim is disputed by numerous academic scholars expert in numerous areas.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Dan Brown himself addresses the idea of some of the more controversial aspects being fact on his web site, stating that the "FACT" page at the beginning of the novel mentions only "documents, rituals, organization, artwork and architecture", but not any of the ancient theories discussed by fictional characters, stating that "Interpreting those ideas is left to the reader". Brown also says, "It is my belief that some of the theories discussed by these characters may have merit" and "the secret behind The Da Vinci Code was too well documented and significant for me to dismiss."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2003, while promoting the novel, Brown was asked in interviews what parts of the history in his novel actually happened. He replied "Absolutely all of it." In a 2003 interview with CNN's <a href="/wiki/Martin_Savidge" title="Martin Savidge">Martin Savidge</a> he was again asked how much of the historical background was true. He replied, "99% is true... the background is all true". Asked by <a href="/wiki/Elizabeth_Vargas" title="Elizabeth Vargas">Elizabeth Vargas</a> in an <a href="/wiki/ABC_News" title="ABC News">ABC News</a> special if the book would have been different if he had written it as non-fiction he replied, "I don't think it would have."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2005, UK TV personality <a href="/wiki/Tony_Robinson" title="Tony Robinson">Tony Robinson</a> edited and narrated a detailed rebuttal of the main arguments of Dan Brown and those of <a href="/wiki/Michael_Baigent" title="Michael Baigent">Michael Baigent</a>, <a href="/wiki/Richard_Leigh_(author)" title="Richard Leigh (author)">Richard Leigh</a> and <a href="/wiki/Henry_Lincoln" title="Henry Lincoln">Henry Lincoln</a>, who authored the book <i><a href="/wiki/The_Holy_Blood_and_the_Holy_Grail" title="The Holy Blood and the Holy Grail">Holy Blood, Holy Grail</a></i>, in the program <i>The Real Da Vinci Code</i>, shown on <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/British_television" title="British television">British TV</a> <a href="/wiki/Channel_4" title="Channel 4">Channel 4</a>. The program featured lengthy interviews with many of the main protagonists cited by Brown as "absolute fact" in <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>. Arnaud de Sède, son of <a href="/wiki/G%C3%A9rard_de_S%C3%A8de" title="Gérard de Sède">Gérard de Sède</a>, stated categorically that his father and Plantard had made up the existence of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Prieur%C3%A9_de_Sion" title="Prieuré de Sion">Prieuré de Sion</a>, the cornerstone of the <a href="/wiki/Jesus_bloodline" title="Jesus bloodline">Jesus bloodline</a> theory: "frankly, it was piffle".</p>
<p>The earliest appearance of this theory is due to the 13th-century <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cistercian" title="Cistercian">Cistercian</a> monk and chronicler <a href="/wiki/Peter_of_Vaux_de_Cernay" title="Peter of Vaux de Cernay">Peter of Vaux de Cernay</a> who claimed it was part of <a href="/wiki/Catharism" title="Catharism">Catharist</a> belief that the earthly <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jesus_Christ" title="Jesus Christ">Jesus Christ</a> had a relationship with <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a>, described as his <a href="/wiki/Concubinage" title="Concubinage">concubine</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> The program <i>The Real Da Vinci Code</i> also cast doubt on the <a href="/wiki/Rosslyn_Chapel" title="Rosslyn Chapel">Rosslyn Chapel</a> association with the Grail and on other related stories, such as the alleged landing of <a href="/wiki/Mary_Magdalene" title="Mary Magdalene">Mary Magdalene</a> in France.</p>
<p>According to <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>, the Roman Emperor <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Constantine_I" title="Constantine I">Constantine I</a> suppressed <a href="/wiki/Gnosticism" title="Gnosticism">Gnosticism</a> because it portrayed Jesus as purely human. The novel's argument is as follows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> Constantine wanted Christianity to act as a unifying religion for the <a href="/wiki/Roman_Empire" title="Roman Empire">Roman Empire</a>. He thought Christianity would appeal to <a href="/wiki/Paganism" title="Paganism">pagans</a> only if it featured a <a href="/wiki/Demigod" title="Demigod">demigod</a> similar to pagan heroes. According to the <a href="/wiki/Gnostic_Gospels" title="Gnostic Gospels">Gnostic Gospels</a>, Jesus was merely a human prophet, not a demigod. Therefore, to change Jesus' image, Constantine destroyed the <a href="/wiki/Gnostic_Gospels" title="Gnostic Gospels">Gnostic Gospels</a> and promoted the gospels of Matthew, Mark, Luke, and John, which portray Jesus as divine or semidivine.</p>
<p>But Gnosticism did not portray Jesus as merely human.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chapterfiftyfive_10-0"><a href="#cite_note-chapterfiftyfive-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> All Gnostic writings depict Christ as purely divine, his <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Human_anatomy" title="Human anatomy">human body</a> being a mere illusion (see <a href="/wiki/Docetism" title="Docetism">Docetism</a>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Gnostic sects saw Christ this way because they regarded matter as evil, and therefore believed that a divine spirit would never have taken on a material body.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-chapterfiftyfive_10-1"><a href="#cite_note-chapterfiftyfive-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Literary_criticism">Literary criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Literary criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The book received both positive and negative reviews from critics, and it has been the subject of negative appraisals concerning its portrayal of history. Its writing and historical accuracy were reviewed negatively by <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorker_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorker-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Salon.com" title="Salon.com">Salon.com</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Maclean%27s" title="Maclean's">Maclean's</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Janet_Maslin" title="Janet Maslin">Janet Maslin</a> of <i>The New York Times</i> said, "it concisely conveys the kind of extreme enthusiasm with which this riddle-filled, code-breaking, exhilaratingly brainy thriller can be recommended. That word is wow. The author is Dan Brown (a name you will want to remember). In this gleefully erudite suspense novel, Mr. Brown takes the format he has been developing through three earlier novels and fine-tunes it to blockbuster perfection."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>David Lazarus of <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_San_Francisco_Chronicle" title="The San Francisco Chronicle">The San Francisco Chronicle</a></i> said, "This story has so many twists – all satisfying, most unexpected – that it would be a sin to reveal too much of the plot in advance. Let's just say that if this novel doesn't get your pulse racing, you need to check your meds."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>While interviewing <a href="/wiki/Umberto_Eco" title="Umberto Eco">Umberto Eco</a> in a 2008 issue of <i><a href="/wiki/The_Paris_Review" title="The Paris Review">The Paris Review</a></i>, Lila Azam Zanganeh characterized <i>The Da Vinci Code</i> as "a bizarre little offshoot" of Eco's novel, <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Foucault%E2%80%99s_Pendulum" title="Foucault’s Pendulum">Foucault’s Pendulum</a></i>. In response, Eco remarked, "Dan Brown is a character from Foucault's Pendulum! I invented him. He shares my characters’ fascinations—the world conspiracy of Rosicrucians, Masons, and Jesuits. The role of the Knights Templar. The hermetic secret. The principle that everything is connected. I suspect Dan Brown might not even exist."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The book appeared on a 2010 list of 101 best books ever written, which was derived from a survey of more than 15,000 Australian readers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Salman_Rushdie" title="Salman Rushdie">Salman Rushdie</a> said during a lecture, "Do not start me on 'The Da Vinci Code'. A novel so bad that it gives bad novels a bad name."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-19"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stephen_Fry" title="Stephen Fry">Stephen Fry</a> has referred to Brown's writings as "complete loose stool-water" and "arse gravy of the worst kind".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-20"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> In a live chat on June 14, 2006, he clarified, "I just loathe all those book[s] about the <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a> and <a href="/wiki/Freemasonry" title="Freemasonry">Masons</a> and Catholic conspiracies and all that botty-dribble. I mean, there's so much more that's interesting and exciting in art and in history. It plays to the worst and laziest in humanity, the desire to think the worst of the past and the desire to feel superior to it in some fatuous way."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-21"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Stephen_King" title="Stephen King">Stephen King</a> likened Dan Brown's work to "Jokes for the John", calling such literature the "intellectual equivalent of <a href="/wiki/Kraft_Dinner" title="Kraft Dinner">Kraft Macaroni and Cheese</a>".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-22"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i>, while reviewing the movie based on the book, called the book "Dan Brown's best-selling primer on how not to write an English sentence".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorkTimes_23-0"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorkTimes-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker" title="The New Yorker">The New Yorker</a></i> reviewer <a href="/wiki/Anthony_Lane" title="Anthony Lane">Anthony Lane</a> refers to it as "unmitigated junk" and decries "the crumbling coarseness of the style".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NewYorker_12-1"><a href="#cite_note-NewYorker-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Linguist <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Geoffrey_Pullum" title="Geoffrey Pullum">Geoffrey Pullum</a> and others posted several entries critical of Dan Brown's writing, at <a href="/wiki/Language_Log" title="Language Log">Language Log</a>, calling Brown one of the "worst prose stylists in the history of literature" and saying Brown's "writing is not just bad; it is staggeringly, clumsily, thoughtlessly, almost ingeniously bad".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Roger_Ebert" title="Roger Ebert">Roger Ebert</a> described it as a "potboiler written with little grace and style", although he said it did "supply an intriguing plot".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_25-0"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> In his review of the film <i><a href="/wiki/National_Treasure_(film)" title="National Treasure (film)">National Treasure</a></i>, whose plot also involves ancient conspiracies and treasure hunts, he wrote: "I should read a potboiler like <i>The Da Vinci Code</i> every once in a while, just to remind myself that life is too short to read books like <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Ebert_25-1"><a href="#cite_note-Ebert-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Lawsuits">Lawsuits</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: Lawsuits">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Author <a href="/wiki/Lewis_Perdue" title="Lewis Perdue">Lewis Perdue</a> alleged that Brown plagiarized from two of his novels, <i>The Da Vinci Legacy</i>, originally published in 1983, and <i>Daughter of God</i>, originally published in 2000. He sought to block distribution of the book and film. However, <a href="/wiki/George_B._Daniels" title="George B. Daniels">Judge George Daniels</a> of the <a href="/wiki/United_States_District_Court_for_the_Southern_District_of_New_York" title="United States District Court for the Southern District of New York">US District Court in New York</a> ruled against Perdue in 2005, saying that "A reasonable average lay observer would not conclude that The Da Vinci Code is substantially similar to Daughter of God" and that "Any slightly similar elements are on the level of generalized or otherwise unprotectable ideas."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-26"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Perdue appealed, the 2nd US Circuit Court of Appeals upheld the original decision, saying Mr. Perdue's arguments were "without merit".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-27"><a href="#cite_note-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In early 2006, Baigent and Leigh filed suit against Brown's publishers, Random House. They alleged that significant portions of <i>The Da Vinci Code</i> were plagiarized from <i>Holy Blood, Holy Grail</i>, violating their copyright.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrialBBC_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-TrialBBC-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Brown confirmed during the court case that he named the principal Grail expert of his story Leigh Teabing, an anagram of "Baigent Leigh", after the two plaintiffs. In reply to the suggestion that Henry Lincoln was also referred to in the book, since he has medical problems resulting in a severe limp, like the character of Leigh Teabing, Brown stated he was unaware of Lincoln's illness and the correspondence was a coincidence.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrialWashPost_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-TrialWashPost-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Because Baigent and Leigh had presented their conclusions as historical research, not as fiction, Justice Peter Smith, who presided over the trial, deemed that a novelist must be free to use these ideas in a fictional context, and ruled against Baigent and Leigh. Smith also hid <a href="/wiki/Smithy_code" title="Smithy code">his own secret code</a> in his written judgement, in the form of seemingly random italicized letters in the 71-page document, which apparently spell out a message. Smith indicated he would confirm the code if someone broke it.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrialMSNBC_30-0"><a href="#cite_note-TrialMSNBC-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup> Baigent and Leigh appealed, unsuccessfully, to the <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Appeal_of_England_and_Wales" title="Court of Appeal of England and Wales">Court of Appeal</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-TrialWashPost_29-1"><a href="#cite_note-TrialWashPost-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In April 2006 Mikhail Anikin, a Russian scientist and art historian working as a senior researcher at the <a href="/wiki/Hermitage_Museum" title="Hermitage Museum">Hermitage Museum</a> in St Petersburg, stated the intention to bring a lawsuit against Dan Brown, maintaining that he was the one who coined the phrase used as the book's title and one of the ideas regarding the Mona Lisa used in its plot. Anikin interprets the Mona Lisa to be an Christian allegory consisting of two images, one of Jesus Christ that comprises the image's right half, one of the Virgin Mary that forms its left half. According to Anikin, he expressed this idea to a group of experts from the Museum of Houston during a 1988 <a href="/wiki/Ren%C3%A9_Magritte" title="René Magritte">René Magritte</a> exhibit at the Hermitage, and when one of the Americans requested permission to pass it along to a friend Anikin granted the request on condition that he be credited in any book using his interpretation. Anikin eventually compiled his research into <i>Leonardo Da Vinci or Theology on Canvas</i>, a book published in 2000, but <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>, published three years later, makes no mention of Anikin and instead asserts that the idea in question is a "well-known opinion of a number of scientists."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-31"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-32"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Parodies">Parodies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Parodies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<ul>
<li>The book was parodied by <a href="/wiki/Adam_Roberts_(British_writer)" title="Adam Roberts (British writer)">Adam Roberts</a> and Toby Clements with the books <i><a href="/wiki/The_Va_Dinci_Cod" title="The Va Dinci Cod">The Va Dinci Cod</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/The_Asti_Spumante_Code" title="The Asti Spumante Code">The Asti Spumante Code</a></i>, respectively, both in 2005.</li>
<li>A 2005 <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Telemovie" title="Telemovie">telemovie</a> spin-off of the Australian television series <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Kath_%26_Kim_(Australian_TV_series)" title="Kath &amp; Kim (Australian TV series)">Kath &amp; Kim</a></i> parodied the film version as <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Da_Kath_and_Kim_Code" title="Da Kath and Kim Code">Da Kath and Kim Code</a></i> in 2005.</li>
<li>The 2006 <a href="/wiki/BBC" title="BBC">BBC</a> program <i><a href="/wiki/Dead_Ringers_(comedy)" title="Dead Ringers (comedy)">Dead Ringers</a></i> parodied <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>, calling it the "Da <a href="/wiki/Rolf_Harris" title="Rolf Harris">Rolf Harris</a> Code".</li>
<li>South African <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Political_cartoonist" title="Political cartoonist">political cartoonist</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Zapiro" title="Zapiro">Zapiro</a> published a 2006 book collection of his strips entitled <i>Da Zuma Code</i>, which parodies the former <a href="/wiki/Vice_president" title="Vice president">deputy president</a> <a href="/wiki/Jacob_Zuma" title="Jacob Zuma">Jacob Zuma</a>.</li>
<li>A 2006 independent film named <i><a href="/wiki/The_Norman_Rockwell_Code" title="The Norman Rockwell Code">The Norman Rockwell Code</a></i> parodied the book and film. Instead of that of a curator in the Louvre, the murder is that of a curator at the <a href="/wiki/Norman_Rockwell_Museum" title="Norman Rockwell Museum">Norman Rockwell Museum</a> in <a href="/wiki/Stockbridge,_Massachusetts" title="Stockbridge, Massachusetts">Stockbridge, Massachusetts</a>.</li>
<li><a href="/wiki/The_DiCaprio_Code" title="The DiCaprio Code">The DiCaprio Code</a>, a 2006, seven-part animated series by <a href="/wiki/Movies.com" title="Movies.com">Movies.com</a> and Scrapmation.</li>
<li>The book was parodied in the 2007 <i><a href="/wiki/South_Park" title="South Park">South Park</a></i> episode "<a href="/wiki/Fantastic_Easter_Special" title="Fantastic Easter Special">Fantastic Easter Special</a>" and <a href="/wiki/Robert_Rankin" title="Robert Rankin">Robert Rankin</a>'s novel <i>The Da-da-de-da-da Code</i>.</li>
<li>The characters Lucy and Silas are parodied in the 2007 film <i><a href="/wiki/Epic_Movie" title="Epic Movie">Epic Movie</a></i>, which begins with a scene similar to the opening of <i>The Da Vinci Code</i>, with Silas chasing the orphan Lucy.</li>
<li><i>Szyfr Jana Matejki</i> (<i><a href="/wiki/Jan_Matejko" title="Jan Matejko">Jan Matejko</a>'s Cipher</i>) is a 2007 Polish parody by <a href="/wiki/Dariusz_Rekosz" title="Dariusz Rekosz">Dariusz Rekosz</a>. A sequel, <i>Ko(s)miczna futryna: Szyfr Jana Matejki II</i> (<i>Co[s]mic Door-frame: Jan Matejko's Cipher II</i>), was released in 2008. The main character is inspector Józef Świenty, who tries to solve The Greatest Secret of Mankind (<i>Największa Tajemnica Ludzkości</i>) – parentage of <a href="/wiki/Piast_dynasty" title="Piast dynasty">Piast dynasty</a>.</li>
<li>The book was parodied in the 2008 <i><a href="/wiki/American_Dad!" title="American Dad!">American Dad!</a></i> episode "Black Mystery Month", in which <a href="/wiki/Stan_Smith_(American_Dad!)" title="Stan Smith (American Dad!)">Stan Smith</a> searches for the controversial truth that <a href="/wiki/Mary_Todd_Lincoln" title="Mary Todd Lincoln">Mary Todd Lincoln</a> invented peanut butter.</li>
<li>In 2008, it was parodied in the second series of <i><a href="/wiki/That_Mitchell_and_Webb_Look" title="That Mitchell and Webb Look">That Mitchell and Webb Look</a></i> as "The Numberwang Code", a trailer for a <a href="/wiki/Fictional_film" title="Fictional film">fictional film</a> based on <a href="/wiki/That_Mitchell_and_Webb_Look#Recurring_sketches" title="That Mitchell and Webb Look">a recurring sketch</a> on the show.</li>
<li>The book's plot is parodied in "<a href="/wiki/The_Duh-Vinci_Code" title="The Duh-Vinci Code">The Duh-Vinci Code</a>", an episode of the animated TV series <i><a href="/wiki/Futurama" title="Futurama">Futurama</a></i>.</li>
<li>The book was parodied in the <i><a href="/wiki/Mad_(TV_series)" title="Mad (TV series)">Mad</a></i> episode "Da Grinchy Code / Duck", in which the greatest movie minds try to solve the mystery of the <a href="/wiki/Grinch" title="Grinch">Grinch</a>.</li>
<li>The book's theme of conspiracy theories is parodied in the 2007 <a href="/wiki/MC_Solaar" title="MC Solaar">MC Solaar</a> single "Da Vinci Claude".</li>
</ul>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Film">Film</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=The_Da_Vinci_Code&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Film">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/The_Da_Vinci_Code_(film)" title="The Da Vinci Code (film)">The Da Vinci Code (film)</a></div>
<p><a href="/wiki/Columbia_Pictures" title="Columbia Pictures">Columbia Pictures</a> adapted the novel to film, with a screenplay written by <a href="/wiki/Akiva_Goldsman" title="Akiva Goldsman">Akiva Goldsman</a>, and <a href="/wiki/Academy_Awards" title="Academy Awards">Academy Award</a> winner <a href="/wiki/Ron_Howard" title="Ron Howard">Ron Howard</a> directing. The film was released on May 19, 2006, and stars <a href="/wiki/Tom_Hanks" title="Tom Hanks">Tom Hanks</a> as <a href="/wiki/Robert_Langdon" title="Robert Langdon">Robert Langdon</a>, <a href="/wiki/Audrey_Tautou" title="Audrey Tautou">Audrey Tautou</a> as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Sophie_Neveu" title="Sophie Neveu">Sophie Neveu</a>, and Sir <a href="/wiki/Ian_McKellen" title="Ian McKellen">Ian McKellen</a> as Leigh Teabing. During its opening weekend, moviegoers spent an estimated $77 million in America, and $224 million worldwide.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-37"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
