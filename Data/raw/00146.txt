<p><i><b>Lolita</b></i> is a <a href="/wiki/1997_in_film" title="1997 in film">1997</a> <a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_France" title="Cinema of France">French</a><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/-" title="-">-</a><a href="/wiki/Cinema_of_the_United_States" title="Cinema of the United States">American</a> <a href="/wiki/Drama_film" title="Drama film">drama film</a> written by <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Schiff" title="Stephen Schiff">Stephen Schiff</a> and directed by <a href="/wiki/Adrian_Lyne" title="Adrian Lyne">Adrian Lyne</a>. It is the second <a href="/wiki/Film_adaptation" title="Film adaptation">screen adaptation</a> of <a href="/wiki/Vladimir_Nabokov" title="Vladimir Nabokov">Vladimir Nabokov</a>'s <a href="/wiki/Lolita" title="Lolita">novel of the same name</a> and stars <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Irons" title="Jeremy Irons">Jeremy Irons</a> as Humbert Humbert and <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Swain" title="Dominique Swain">Dominique Swain</a> as Dolores "Lolita" Haze, with supporting roles by <a href="/wiki/Melanie_Griffith" title="Melanie Griffith">Melanie Griffith</a> as Charlotte Haze, and <a href="/wiki/Frank_Langella" title="Frank Langella">Frank Langella</a> as Clare Quilty.</p>
<p>The film had considerable difficulty finding an American distributor<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and premiered in <a href="/wiki/Europe" title="Europe">Europe</a> before being released in America, where it was met with much controversy. The film was picked up in the United States by <a href="/wiki/Showtime_(TV_network)" title="Showtime (TV network)">Showtime</a>, a <a href="/wiki/Cable" title="Cable">cable</a> network, before finally being released theatrically by <a href="/wiki/The_Samuel_Goldwyn_Company" title="The Samuel Goldwyn Company">The Samuel Goldwyn Company</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Black_4-0"><a href="#cite_note-Black-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The performances by Irons and Swain impressed audiences, but, although praised by some critics for its faithfulness to Nabokov's narrative, the film received a mixed critical reception in the United States. Following its theatrical release, the film was distributed on <a href="/wiki/VHS" title="VHS">VHS</a> and <a href="/wiki/DVD" title="DVD">DVD</a>, both now out of print, by <a href="/wiki/Path%C3%A9" title="Pathé">Pathé</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Plot">Plot</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lolita_(1997_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Plot">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In 1947, Humbert Humbert (<a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Irons" title="Jeremy Irons">Jeremy Irons</a>), a European professor of <a href="/wiki/French_literature" title="French literature">French literature</a>, travels to the <a href="/wiki/United_States" title="United States">United States</a> to take a teaching position in <a href="/wiki/New_Hampshire" title="New Hampshire">New Hampshire</a>. He rents a room in the home of widow Charlotte Haze (<a href="/wiki/Melanie_Griffith" title="Melanie Griffith">Melanie Griffith</a>), largely because he sees her <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Adolescent" title="Adolescent">adolescent</a> daughter Dolores (<a href="/wiki/Dominique_Swain" title="Dominique Swain">Dominique Swain</a>), also called "Lo", while touring the house. Obsessed from boyhood with girls of this age (whom he calls "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Nymphet" title="Nymphet">nymphets</a>"), Humbert is immediately smitten with Lo and marries Charlotte just to be near her.</p>
<p>Charlotte finds Humbert's secret diary and discovers his preference for her daughter. Furious, Charlotte runs out of the house, when she is struck by a car and killed. Her death frees Humbert to pursue a sexual relationship with Lo, whom he nicknames "<a href="/wiki/Lolita_(term)" title="Lolita (term)">Lolita</a>". Humbert and Lo then travel the country, staying in various <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Motor_inn" title="Motor inn">motels</a> before eventually settling in the college town of Beardsley, where Humbert takes a teaching job. However, Lo's increasing boredom with Humbert, as well as her growing desire for independence, fuels a constant tension between them. Humbert's desperate affections for Lo are also rivaled by another man, playwright Clare Quilty (<a href="/wiki/Frank_Langella" title="Frank Langella">Frank Langella</a>), who has been pursuing Lo since the beginning of their travels. Lo eventually runs off with Quilty, whose name Humbert doesn't know, and he searches for them unsuccessfully.</p>
<p>Three years later, Humbert receives a letter from Lo asking for money. Humbert visits Lo, who is now married and pregnant. Humbert asks her to run away with him, but she refuses. He relents and gives her a substantial amount of money. Lo also reveals to Humbert how Quilty actually tracked young girls and took them to Pavor Manor, his home in Parkington, to exploit them for <a href="/wiki/Child_pornography" title="Child pornography">child pornography</a>. Quilty abandoned her after she refused to be in one of his films.</p>
<p>After his visit with Lo, Humbert tracks down Quilty and kills him. After being chased by the police, Humbert is arrested and sent to prison. He dies in November 1950, and Lo dies the next month during childbirth.</p>

<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="History_of_the_screenplay">History of the screenplay</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lolita_(1997_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: History of the screenplay">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The first screen adaptation of <a href="/wiki/Lolita_(1962_film)" title="Lolita (1962 film)"><i>Lolita</i> (1962)</a> was written by Nabokov and directed (after revisions) by <a href="/wiki/Stanley_Kubrick" title="Stanley Kubrick">Stanley Kubrick</a>.</p>
<p>The screenplay for the 1997 version, more faithful to the text of the novel than the earlier motion picture, is credited to <a href="/wiki/Stephen_Schiff" title="Stephen Schiff">Stephen Schiff</a>, a writer for <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_New_Yorker_(magazine)" title="The New Yorker (magazine)">The New Yorker</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Vanity_Fair_(magazine)" title="Vanity Fair (magazine)">Vanity Fair</a></i>, and other magazines. He was hired to write it as his first movie script, after the film's producers had rejected screenplays commissioned from more experienced screenwriters and directors <a href="/wiki/James_Dearden" title="James Dearden">James Dearden</a> (<i><a href="/wiki/Fatal_Attraction" title="Fatal Attraction">Fatal Attraction</a></i>), <a href="/wiki/Harold_Pinter" title="Harold Pinter">Harold Pinter</a>, and <a href="/wiki/David_Mamet" title="David Mamet">David Mamet</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Gale_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-Gale-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Hudgins_6-0"><a href="#cite_note-Hudgins-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>According to Schiff, "Right from the beginning, it was clear to all of us that this movie was not a 'remake' of Kubrick's film. Rather, we were out to make a new adaptation of a very great novel." Schiff stated that "Some of the filmmakers involved actually looked upon the Kubrick version as a kind of 'what not to do'" and quipped that Kubrick's film should have been called <i>Quilty</i>, due to the prominent role of that character.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-SchiffCRS_8-0"><a href="#cite_note-SchiffCRS-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (June 2009)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Release">Release</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lolita_(1997_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Release">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Critical_reception">Critical reception</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lolita_(1997_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Critical reception">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The film received mixed to positive reviews, with an approval rating of 67% at <a href="/wiki/Rotten_Tomatoes" title="Rotten Tomatoes">Rotten Tomatoes</a> and an average score of 46/100 at <a href="/wiki/Metacritic" title="Metacritic">Metacritic</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-rottentomatoes_9-0"><a href="#cite_note-rottentomatoes-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup> Many critics evaluated the film highly and appreciated aspects of it, though some tended to qualify their positive comments. For example, <a href="/wiki/James_Berardinelli" title="James Berardinelli">James Berardinelli</a> praised the performances of the two leads, <a href="/wiki/Jeremy_Irons" title="Jeremy Irons">Jeremy Irons</a> and <a href="/wiki/Dominique_Swain" title="Dominique Swain">Dominique Swain</a>, but he considered <a href="/wiki/Melanie_Griffith" title="Melanie Griffith">Melanie Griffith</a>'s performance weak, "stiff and unconvincing"; he considered the film better when she no longer appeared in it and concluded: "Lolita is not a sex film; it's about characters, relationships, and the consequences of imprudent actions. And those who seek to brand the picture as immoral have missed the point. Both Humbert and Lolita are eventually destroyed—what could be more moral? The only real controversy I can see surrounding this film is why there was ever a controversy in the first place."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The film was <i><a href="/wiki/The_New_York_Times" title="The New York Times">The New York Times</a></i> "Critics Pick" on 31 July 1998, with its critic Caryn James championing it and saying, "Rich beyond what anyone could have expected, the film repays repeated viewings...it turns Humbert's madness into art."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-James_12-0"><a href="#cite_note-James-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Writer and director <a href="/wiki/James_Toback" title="James Toback">James Toback</a> lists it in his picks for the 10 finest films ever made, but he rates the original film higher.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Toback_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-Toback-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Commenting on differences between the novel and the film, Charles Taylor observes that "[f]or all of their vaunted (and, it turns out, false) fidelity to Nabokov, Lyne and Schiff have made a pretty, gauzy <i>Lolita</i> that replaces the book's cruelty and comedy with manufactured lyricism and mopey romanticism".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Taylor_14-0"><a href="#cite_note-Taylor-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Extending Taylor's observation, Keith Phipps concludes: "Lyne doesn't seem to get the novel, failing to incorporate any of Nabokov's black comedy—which is to say, <i>Lolita'</i>s heart and soul."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Phipps_15-0"><a href="#cite_note-Phipps-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Box_office">Box office</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Lolita_(1997_film)&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: Box office">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Due to the difficulty in securing a distributor, the film had a very limited theatrical run in order to qualify for awards.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-nytimes.com_3-1"><a href="#cite_note-nytimes.com-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Accordingly <i>Lolita</i> only took in a gross income of $19,492 in its opening weekend. Since the final domestic gross income was $1,147,784<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-numbers_2-1"><a href="#cite_note-numbers-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> on a $62,000,000 budget, the film was considered a <a href="/wiki/Box_office_bomb" title="Box office bomb">flop</a> at the box office.</p>

<li id="cite_note-Hudgins-6"><span class="mw-cite-backlink"><b><a href="#cite_ref-Hudgins_6-0">^</a></b></span> <span class="reference-text">Hudgins observes: "During our 1994 interview, Pinter told [Steven H.] Gale and me that he had learned his lesson after the revisions imposed on his script for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Handmaid%27s_Tale_(film)" title="The Handmaid's Tale (film)">The Handmaid's Tale</a></i>, which he has decided not to publish. When his script for <i><a href="/wiki/The_Remains_of_the_Day_(film)#Background_and_production_notes" title="The Remains of the Day (film)">Remains of the Day</a></i> was radically revised by the <a href="/wiki/James_Ivory_(director)" title="James Ivory (director)">James Ivory</a>–<a href="/wiki/Ismail_Merchant" title="Ismail Merchant">Ismail Merchant</a> partnership, he refused to allow his name to be listed in the credits"; Hudgins adds: "We did not see Pinter's name up in lights when Lyne's <i>Lolita</i> finally made its appearance in 1998. Pinter goes on in the March 13 [1995] letter [to Hudgins] to state that 'I have never been given any reason at all as to why the film company brought in another writer,' again quite similar to the equally ungracious treatment that he received in the <i>Remains of the Day</i> situation. He concludes that though he never met Nabokov, 'indeed I knew "Lolita" very well and loved it.' " (125). Hudgins also observes that Schiff was brought in after the efforts by Dearden (October 21, 1991), Pinter (September 26, 1994), and Mamet (March 10, 1995) and that Schiff "has no previous scripts to his credit" (124).</span></li>
