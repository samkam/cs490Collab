<h1 class="firstHeading" id="firstHeading" lang="en"><i>Hieros gamos</i></h1>
<div class="mw-body-content" id="bodyContent">
<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
<div id="contentSub"></div>

<p><i><b>Hieros gamos</b></i> or <b>Hierogamy</b> (<a href="/wiki/Ancient_Greek" title="Ancient Greek">Greek</a> <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἱερὸς γάμος</span>, <span lang="grc" xml:lang="grc">ἱερογαμία</span> "holy marriage") refers to a <a href="/wiki/Sexual_ritual" title="Sexual ritual">sexual ritual</a> that plays out a <a href="/wiki/Marriage" title="Marriage">marriage</a> between a <a href="/wiki/God_(male_deity)" title="God (male deity)">god</a> and a <a href="/wiki/Goddess" title="Goddess">goddess</a>, especially when enacted in a symbolic <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> where human participants represent the deities.</p>
<p>The notion of <i>hieros gamos</i> does not presuppose actual performance in ritual, but is also used in purely symbolic or <a href="/wiki/Mythology" title="Mythology">mythological</a> context, notably in <a href="/wiki/Alchemy" title="Alchemy">alchemy</a> and hence in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Jungian_psychology" title="Jungian psychology">Jungian psychology</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Hinduism">Hinduism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hieros_gamos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Hinduism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Hinduism" title="Hinduism">Hinduism</a>, <a href="/wiki/Devadasi" title="Devadasi">Devadasi</a> tradition (देवदासी / ದೇವದಾಸಿ; "servant of god") is a religious tradition in which girls are "married" and dedicated to a deity (<a href="/wiki/Deva_(Hinduism)" title="Deva (Hinduism)">deva</a> or <a href="/wiki/Devi" title="Devi">devi</a>) or to a <a href="/wiki/Temple" title="Temple">Hindu temple</a> and includes performance aspects such as those that take place in the temple as well as in the courtly and mujuvani [Telugu] or home context. Originally, in addition to this and taking care of the temple and performing rituals, these women learned and practiced Sadir, <a href="/wiki/Odissi" title="Odissi">Odissi</a> and other classical Indian artistic traditions and enjoyed a high social status. Though traditionally, they carry out dances in the praise of lord, they also evolved into Sacred Marriage over time.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Ancient_Near_East">Ancient Near East</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hieros_gamos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Ancient Near East">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Barton_Cylinder" title="Barton Cylinder">Barton Cylinder</a> and <a href="/wiki/Sacred_prostitution" title="Sacred prostitution">Sacred prostitution</a></div>
<p>Sacred prostitution was common in the <a href="/wiki/Ancient_Near_East" title="Ancient Near East">Ancient Near East</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> as a form of "Sacred Marriage" or hieros gamos between the king of a <a href="/wiki/Sumer" title="Sumer">Sumerian</a> city-state and the High Priestess of <a href="/wiki/Inanna" title="Inanna">Inanna</a>, the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Mesopotamian_mythology" title="Mesopotamian mythology">Sumerian goddess</a> of love, fertility, and warfare. Along the <a href="/wiki/Tigris" title="Tigris">Tigris</a> and <a href="/wiki/Euphrates" title="Euphrates">Euphrates</a> rivers there were many shrines and temples dedicated to Inanna. The temple of Eanna, meaning "house of heaven"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> in <a href="/wiki/Uruk" title="Uruk">Uruk</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> was the greatest of these. The temple housed <a href="/wiki/Nad%C4%ABtu" title="Nadītu">Nadītu</a>, priestesses of the goddess. The high priestess would choose for her bed a young man who represented the shepherd <a class="mw-disambig" href="/wiki/Dumuzid" title="Dumuzid">Dumuzid</a>, consort of Inanna, in a hieros gamos or sacred marriage, celebrated during the annual <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Duku" title="Duku">Duku</a> ceremony, just before Invisible Moon, with the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Autumn_Equinox" title="Autumn Equinox">autumn Equinox</a><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> (Autumnal Zag-mu Festival).</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Greek_mythology">Greek mythology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hieros_gamos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Greek mythology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Greek_mythology" title="Greek mythology">Greek mythology</a> the classic instance is the wedding of <a href="/wiki/Zeus" title="Zeus">Zeus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hera" title="Hera">Hera</a> celebrated at the <a href="/wiki/Heraion_of_Samos" title="Heraion of Samos">Heraion of Samos</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> and doubtless its architectural and cultural predecessors. Some scholars<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> would restrict the term to reenactments, but most accept its extension to real or simulated union in the promotion of fertility: such an ancient union of <a href="/wiki/Demeter" title="Demeter">Demeter</a> with <a href="/wiki/Iasion" title="Iasion">Iasion</a>, enacted in a thrice-plowed furrow, a primitive aspect of a sexually-active Demeter reported by <a href="/wiki/Hesiod" title="Hesiod">Hesiod</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> is sited in <a href="/wiki/Crete" title="Crete">Crete</a>, origin of much early Greek myth. In actual <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cult_(religion)" title="Cult (religion)">cultus</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/Walter_Burkert" title="Walter Burkert">Walter Burkert</a> found the Greek evidence "scanty and unclear": "To what extent such a sacred marriage was not just a way of viewing nature, but an act expressed or hinted at in <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> is difficult to say"<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> the best-known <a href="/wiki/Ritual" title="Ritual">ritual</a> example surviving in classical Greece is the <i>hieros gamos</i> enacted at the <a href="/wiki/Anthesteria" title="Anthesteria">Anthesteria</a> by the wife of the <i><a href="/wiki/Archon_basileus" title="Archon basileus">Archon basileus</a></i>, the "Archon King" in Athens, originally therefore the queen of Athens, with <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a>, presumably represented by his priest or the <i><a href="/wiki/Basileus" title="Basileus">basileus</a></i> himself, in the <a href="/wiki/Archon_basileus" title="Archon basileus">Boukoleion</a> in the <a href="/wiki/Agora" title="Agora">Agora</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The brief fertilizing mystical union engenders <a href="/wiki/Dionysus" title="Dionysus">Dionysus</a>, and doubled unions, of a god and of a mortal man on one night, result, through <a href="/wiki/Telegony_(pregnancy)" title="Telegony (pregnancy)">telegony</a>, in the semi-divine nature of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Greek_hero" title="Greek hero">Greek heroes</a> such as <a href="/wiki/Theseus" title="Theseus">Theseus</a> and <a href="/wiki/Heracles" title="Heracles">Heracles</a> among others.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Tantric_Buddhism">Tantric Buddhism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hieros_gamos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Tantric Buddhism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tantric_Buddhism" title="Tantric Buddhism">Tantric Buddhism</a> of <a href="/wiki/Nepal" title="Nepal">Nepal</a>, <a href="/wiki/Bhutan" title="Bhutan">Bhutan</a>, <a href="/wiki/India" title="India">India</a> and <a href="/wiki/Tibet" title="Tibet">Tibet</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Yab-yum" title="Yab-yum">yab-yum</a> is a ritual of the male <a href="/wiki/Deity" title="Deity">deity</a> in union with his female <a class="extiw" href="//en.wiktionary.org/wiki/consort" title="wikt:consort">consort</a>. The symbolism is associated with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Anuttarayoga_tantra" title="Anuttarayoga tantra">Anuttarayoga tantra</a> where the male figure is usually linked to compassion (<i><span class="Unicode" style="white-space:normal; text-decoration: none" title="International Alphabet of Sanskrit Transliteration">karuṇā</span></i>) and skillful means (<i><a href="/wiki/Upaya" title="Upaya">upāya-kauśalya</a></i>), and the female partner to 'insight' (<i>prajñā</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Yab-yum is generally understood to represent the primordial (or mystical) union of wisdom and compassion.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<p><a href="/wiki/Maithuna" title="Maithuna">Maithuna</a> is a <a href="/wiki/Sanskrit" title="Sanskrit">Sanskrit</a> term used in <a href="/wiki/Tantra" title="Tantra">Tantra</a> most often translated as <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tantric_sexuality" title="Tantric sexuality">sexual union</a> in a ritual context. It is the most important of the five makara and constitutes the main part of the Grand Ritual of Tantra variously known as <a href="/wiki/Panchamakara" title="Panchamakara">Panchamakara</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Panchatattva" title="Panchatattva">Panchatattva</a>, and Tattva Chakra.</p>
<p>The symbolism of union and polarity is a central teaching in <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Tantric_Buddhism" title="Tantric Buddhism">Tantric Buddhism</a>, especially in Tibet. The union is realized by the practitioner as a mystical experience within one's own body.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Alchemy_and_Jungian_psychology">Alchemy and Jungian psychology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hieros_gamos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Alchemy and Jungian psychology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Lapis_philosophorum" title="Lapis philosophorum">Lapis philosophorum</a> and <a href="/wiki/Holy_Grail" title="Holy Grail">Holy Grail</a></div>

<p>The hieros gamos is one of the themes that <a href="/wiki/Carl_Jung" title="Carl Jung">Carl Jung</a> dealt with, in his book <i><a href="/wiki/Symbols_of_Transformation" title="Symbols of Transformation">Symbols of Transformation</a></i>. In the novel <i>Hieros Gamos (Sacred Union) - A Confession</i> (2013), author Lia Cacciari, inspired by Jung's thematic exploration of the hieros gamos as an alchemical union of opposites, tells a story of feuding medieval siblings that culminates in an incestuous union.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Wicca">Wicca</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Hieros_gamos&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Wicca">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="hatnote">Further information: <a href="/wiki/Sex_magic" title="Sex magic">Sex magic</a></div>
<p>In <a href="/wiki/Wicca" title="Wicca">Wicca</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Rite" title="Great Rite">Great Rite</a> is a ritual based on the Hieros Gamos. It is generally enacted symbolically by a dagger being placed point first into a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Chalice_(cup)" title="Chalice (cup)">chalice</a>, the action symbolizing the union of the male and female divine in the Hieros gamos. In <a href="/wiki/British_Traditional_Wicca" title="British Traditional Wicca">British Traditional Wicca</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Great_Rite" title="Great Rite">Great Rite</a> is sometimes carried out in actuality by the High Priest and High Priestess.</p>
