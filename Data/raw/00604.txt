<p><b>Björn Kristian Ulvaeus</b> (<small>Swedish pronunciation: </small><span class="IPA" title="Representation in the International Phonetic Alphabet (IPA)"><a href="/wiki/Help:IPA_for_Swedish" title="Help:IPA for Swedish">[bjœːɳ ɵlˈveːɵs]</a></span>; born 25 April 1945; credited as <b>Björn Ulvæus</b>) is a <a href="/wiki/Sweden" title="Sweden">Swedish</a> songwriter, producer, a former member of the Swedish musical group <a href="/wiki/ABBA" title="ABBA">ABBA</a> (1972–82), and co-composer of the musicals <i><a href="/wiki/Chess_(musical)" title="Chess (musical)">Chess</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kristina_fr%C3%A5n_Duvem%C3%A5la" title="Kristina från Duvemåla">Kristina från Duvemåla</a></i>, and <i><a href="/wiki/Mamma_Mia!" title="Mamma Mia!">Mamma Mia!</a></i>. He co-produced the film <i><a href="/wiki/Mamma_Mia!_(film)" title="Mamma Mia! (film)">Mamma Mia!</a></i> with fellow ABBA member and close friend <a href="/wiki/Benny_Andersson" title="Benny Andersson">Benny Andersson</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Biography">Biography</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bj%C3%B6rn_Ulvaeus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Biography">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bj%C3%B6rn_Ulvaeus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Ulvaeus was born in <a href="/wiki/Gothenburg" title="Gothenburg">Gothenburg</a>, but as a child he moved with his family to <a href="/wiki/V%C3%A4stervik" title="Västervik">Västervik</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> Ulvaeus studied business and law at <a href="/wiki/Lund_University" title="Lund University">Lund University</a> after doing his <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Armed_Forces" title="Swedish Armed Forces">military service</a> with <a href="/wiki/Stand-up_comedy" title="Stand-up comedy">stand-up comedian</a> Magnus Holmström.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Career">Career</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bj%C3%B6rn_Ulvaeus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Career">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="thumb tleft">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Hootenanny_Singers.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="277" data-file-width="381" height="160" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Hootenanny_Singers.jpg/220px-Hootenanny_Singers.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/thumb/2/2f/Hootenanny_Singers.jpg/330px-Hootenanny_Singers.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/en/2/2f/Hootenanny_Singers.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Hootenanny_Singers.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Björn Ulvaeus with <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/The_Hootenanny_Singers" title="The Hootenanny Singers">The Hootenanny Singers</a></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>Prior to gaining international recognition with ABBA, Ulvaeus was a member of the Swedish folk-<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Schlager" title="Schlager">schlager</a> band <a href="/wiki/Hootenanny_Singers" title="Hootenanny Singers">Hootenanny Singers</a>, early known as the "West Bay Singers", who had an enormous following in <a href="/wiki/Scandinavia" title="Scandinavia">Scandinavia</a>. While on the road in southern Sweden in 1966, they encountered the <a href="/wiki/Hep_Stars" title="Hep Stars">Hep Stars</a>, and Ulvaeus quickly became friends with the group’s keyboard player, <a href="/wiki/Benny_Andersson" title="Benny Andersson">Benny Andersson</a>. The two musicians shared a passion for songwriting, and each found a composing partner in the other. On meeting again that summer, they composed their first song together: "Isn't It Easy To Say", a song soon to be recorded by Andersson's group. The two continued teaming up for music, helping out each other's bands in the recording studio, and adding guitar or keyboards respectively to the recordings. In 1968, they composed two songs together: "A Flower In My Garden", recorded by <a href="/wiki/Hep_Stars" title="Hep Stars">Hep Stars</a>, and their first "real" hit "Ljuva Sextiotal", for which <a href="/wiki/Stig_Anderson" title="Stig Anderson">Stig Anderson</a> wrote lyrics. The latter, a cabarét-style ironic song about the 1960s, was submitted for the 1969 Swedish heats for <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Eurovision" title="Eurovision">Eurovision Song Contest</a>, but was rejected; it was later recorded by diva <a href="/wiki/Brita_Borg" title="Brita Borg">Brita Borg</a>. Another hit came in 1969 with "Speleman", also recorded by Hep Stars.</p>
<p>While filming a nostalgic schlager special for television in March 1969, Björn met an eighteen-year-old singer-songwriter <a href="/wiki/Agnetha_F%C3%A4ltskog" title="Agnetha Fältskog">Agnetha Fältskog</a>. Benny had himself met his future spouse, a 23-year-old jazz and schlager vocalist <a href="/wiki/Anni-Frid_Lyngstad" title="Anni-Frid Lyngstad">Anni-Frid Lyngstad</a>, only weeks before.</p>
<p>Björn Ulvaeus continued recording and touring with Hootenanny Singers to great acclaim while working as in-house producer at <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Polar_Record_Company" title="Polar Record Company">Polar Record Company</a> (headed by future manager <a href="/wiki/Stig_Anderson" title="Stig Anderson">Stig Anderson</a>), with Benny as his new partner. The twosome produced records by other artists and continued writing songs together. Polar artist Arne Lamberts Swedish version of "A Flower In My Garden" ("<i>Fröken Blåklint</i>") was one of Björn &amp; Benny's first in-house productions. In December 1969, they recorded the new song "She's My Kind Of Girl", which became their first single as a duo. It was released in March 1970, giving them a minor hit in Sweden (and by chance a top-ten hit in Japan two years later).</p>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Hootenanny_Singers" title="Hootenanny Singers">Hootenanny Singers</a> entered <a href="/wiki/Svensktoppen" title="Svensktoppen">Svensktoppen</a>, the Swedish radio charts, in 1970 with "Omkring Tiggarn Från Luossa", a cover of an old folk-schlager song. It remained on the charts for 52 consecutive weeks, a record which endured until 1990; the song was produced by Björn and Benny, and had Ulvaeus's solo vocal and Benny's piano.</p>
<p>After ABBA, Ulvaeus, together with Andersson, created the musicals <i><a href="/wiki/Chess_(musical)" title="Chess (musical)">Chess</a></i>, <i><a href="/wiki/Kristina_fr%C3%A5n_Duvem%C3%A5la" title="Kristina från Duvemåla">Kristina från Duvemåla</a></i> (based on <i><a href="/wiki/The_Emigrants_(novels)" title="The Emigrants (novels)">The Emigrants</a></i> novels by Swedish writer <a href="/wiki/Vilhelm_Moberg" title="Vilhelm Moberg">Vilhelm Moberg</a>), and <i><a href="/wiki/Mamma_Mia!" title="Mamma Mia!">Mamma Mia!</a></i> (based on ABBA songs).</p>
<p>Together with Andersson, Ulvaeus was nominated for the <a href="/wiki/Drama_Desk_Award" title="Drama Desk Award">Drama Desk Award</a> in the category "Outstanding Music" (for the musical <i>Chess</i>), and for a <a href="/wiki/Tony_Award" title="Tony Award">Tony Award</a> in a category "Best Orchestrations" (for musical <i>Mamma Mia!</i>). Original cast recordings of both musicals were nominated for a <a href="/wiki/Grammy_Award" title="Grammy Award">Grammy Award</a>.</p>
<p>For the 2004 semi-final of the <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest" title="Eurovision Song Contest">Eurovision Song Contest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Istanbul" title="Istanbul">Istanbul</a>, <a href="/wiki/Turkey" title="Turkey">Turkey</a>, thirty years after <a href="/wiki/ABBA" title="ABBA">ABBA</a> had won the <a href="/wiki/Eurovision_Song_Contest_1974" title="Eurovision Song Contest 1974">1974 contest</a> in <a href="/wiki/Brighton" title="Brighton">Brighton</a>, <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/UK" title="UK">UK</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> Ulvaeus appeared briefly in a special comedy video made for the interval act, entitled "Our Last Video". Each of the four members of the group appeared briefly in <a href="/wiki/Cameo_appearance" title="Cameo appearance">cameo</a> roles, as did others such as <a href="/wiki/Cher" title="Cher">Cher</a> and <a href="/wiki/Rik_Mayall" title="Rik Mayall">Rik Mayall</a>. The video was not included in the official DVD release of the Eurovision Contest, but was issued as a separate DVD release. It was billed as the first time the four had worked together since the group split. In fact, they each filmed their appearances separately.</p>
<p>Ulvaeus also shared with Andersson "The Special International <a href="/wiki/Ivor_Novello" title="Ivor Novello">Ivor Novello</a> Award" from the <a href="/wiki/British_Academy_of_Songwriters,_Composers_and_Authors" title="British Academy of Songwriters, Composers and Authors">British Academy of Songwriters, Composers and Authors</a>, "The Music Export Prize" from the Swedish Ministry of Industry and Trade (2008),<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> and "Lifetime Achievement Award" from the Swedish Music Publishers Association (SMFF).</p>
<p>On 15 April 2013, it was officially announced by the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/EBU" title="EBU">EBU</a> and the <a href="/wiki/Sveriges_Television" title="Sveriges Television">SVT</a> that Björn along with the ex-<a href="/wiki/ABBA" title="ABBA">ABBA</a> member, <a href="/wiki/Benny_Andersson" title="Benny Andersson">Benny Andersson</a> and the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Swedish_people" title="Swedish people">Swedish</a> <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/DJ" title="DJ">DJ</a> and <a href="/wiki/Record_producer" title="Record producer">record producer</a>, <a href="/wiki/Avicii" title="Avicii">Avicii</a>, have composed the anthem for the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/2013_Eurovision_Song_Contest" title="2013 Eurovision Song Contest">2013 Eurovision Song Contest</a>. The song was performed for the first time in the Final on 18 May.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Personal_life">Personal life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bj%C3%B6rn_Ulvaeus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Personal life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On 6 July 1971, Ulvaeus married <a href="/wiki/Agnetha_F%C3%A4ltskog" title="Agnetha Fältskog">Agnetha Fältskog</a>; the marriage resulted in two children: <a href="/wiki/Linda_Ulvaeus" title="Linda Ulvaeus">Linda Elin Ulvaeus</a> (born 23 February 1973), and Peter Christian Ulvaeus (born 4 December 1977). In July 1980, Björn and Agnetha divorced, although they both agreed to remain in ABBA. Ulvaeus then married music journalist Lena Kallersjö on 6 January 1981. This marriage produced two daughters: Emma (born in 1982) and Anna (born in 1986).</p>
<p>Ulvaeus and Kallersjö currently live in <a href="/wiki/Stockholm" title="Stockholm">Stockholm</a>. From 1984 to 1990 they resided in the UK, where Ulvaeus founded an IT business with his brother.</p>
<p>He is also one of four people (including <a href="/wiki/Per_Gessle" title="Per Gessle">Per Gessle</a>) who own NoteHeads, a Swedish company which publishes the <a href="/wiki/Scorewriter" title="Scorewriter">music notation program</a> <a href="/wiki/Igor_Engraver" title="Igor Engraver">Igor Engraver</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Ulvaeus is a member of the <a href="/wiki/International_Humanist_and_Ethical_Union" title="International Humanist and Ethical Union">International Humanist and Ethical Union</a>'s Swedish member organisation <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Humanisterna" title="Humanisterna">Humanisterna</a>, and was awarded their annual prize, <i>Hedenius-priset</i>, in 2006. Ulvaeus describes himself as a "freethinker", rather than an <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Atheist" title="Atheist">atheist</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 2008, Ulvaeus revealed that he suffered from severe <a href="/wiki/Long-term_memory" title="Long-term memory">long-term memory</a> loss and now no longer remembers many important events in his life. He now has little memory of some of the critical events in his professional history and has resorted to images of his past and even <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Hypnotism" title="Hypnotism">hypnotism</a> to try to bring back his memory.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> However, in a 2009 interview, he stated that reports of his memory loss were "hugely exaggerated".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><i><a href="/wiki/The_Guardian" title="The Guardian">The Guardian</a></i> called him Sweden's "highest-profile cash-free campaigner", explaining that "after his son was robbed several years ago, Ulvaeus became an evangelist for the electronic payment movement, claiming that cash was the primary cause of crime and that 'all activity in the <a href="/wiki/Black_market" title="Black market">black economy</a> requires cash'".<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup> He has reportedly been living cash-free for more than a year, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/ABBA_The_Museum" title="ABBA The Museum">ABBA The Museum</a> has operated cash-free since it opened in May 2013.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Tax_vindication">Tax vindication</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Bj%C3%B6rn_Ulvaeus&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Tax vindication">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The <a href="/wiki/Swedish_Tax_Agency" title="Swedish Tax Agency">Swedish Tax Agency</a> accused Björn Ulvaeus of failing to pay 90 million <a href="/wiki/Swedish_krona" title="Swedish krona">kronor</a> (US$12.8 million) in <a href="/wiki/Back_taxes" title="Back taxes">back taxes</a> for eight years ending in 2005. The agency claimed that he "<a href="/wiki/Money_laundering" title="Money laundering">laundered</a>" his music <a href="/wiki/Royalties" title="Royalties">royalty</a> income through institutions in several foreign countries. Ulvaeus paid the taxes as a precautionary measure during the 2½-year dispute. In October 2008, the county administrative court decided the case in Ulvaeus’ favour, ruling that he never owed any of the 90 million kronor.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-13"><a href="#cite_note-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
