<p><b>"Ten Little Indians"</b> is a US American <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Children%27s_rhyme" title="Children's rhyme">children's rhyme</a>. It has a <a href="/wiki/Roud_Folk_Song_Index" title="Roud Folk Song Index">Roud Folk Song Index</a> number of 13512. The word Indian refers to <a href="/wiki/Native_Americans_in_the_United_States" title="Native Americans in the United States">Native Americans</a>.</p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Lyrics">Lyrics</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ten_Little_Indians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Lyrics">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Negrastrakarnir.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="500" data-file-width="890" height="124" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Negrastrakarnir.jpg/220px-Negrastrakarnir.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Negrastrakarnir.jpg/330px-Negrastrakarnir.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/e/ec/Negrastrakarnir.jpg/440px-Negrastrakarnir.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Negrastrakarnir.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Cover of <i>Negrastrákarnir</i>, an Icelandic version of the song published in 1922</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>The modern lyrics are:</p>
<dl>
<dd>One little, two little, three little Indians</dd>
<dd>Four little, five little, six little Indians</dd>
<dd>Seven little, eight little, nine little Indians</dd>
<dd>Ten little Indian boys.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Ten little, nine little, eight little Indians</dd>
<dd>Seven little, six little, five little Indians</dd>
<dd>Four little, three little, two little Indians</dd>
<dd>One little Indian boy.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Opie1997_1-0"><a href="#cite_note-Opie1997-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<p>The song sometimes begins with a repeated verse, "<a href="/wiki/John_Brown_(abolitionist)" title="John Brown (abolitionist)">John Brown</a> met a little Indian" before entering the well-known verses.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Origins">Origins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ten_Little_Indians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Origins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The original piece, then called "<a href="/wiki/Ten_Little_Injuns" title="Ten Little Injuns">Ten Little Injuns</a>", was written by songwriter <a href="/wiki/Septimus_Winner" title="Septimus Winner">Septimus Winner</a> in 1868 for a <a href="/wiki/Minstrel_show" title="Minstrel show">minstrel show</a> and was much more elaborate:</p>
<dl>
<dd>Ten little Injuns standin' in a line,</dd>
<dd>One toddled home and then there were nine;</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Nine little Injuns swingin' on a gate,</dd>
<dd>One tumbled off and then there were eight.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Eight little Injuns gayest under heav'n.</dd>
<dd>One went to sleep and then there were seven;</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Seven little Injuns cuttin' up their tricks,</dd>
<dd>One broke his neck and then there were six.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Six little Injuns all alive,</dd>
<dd>One kicked the bucket and then there were five;</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Five little Injuns on a cellar door,</dd>
<dd>One tumbled in and then there were four.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Four little Injuns up on a spree,</dd>
<dd>One got fuddled and then there were three;</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Three little Injuns out on a canoe,</dd>
<dd>One tumbled overboard and then there were two.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Two little Injuns foolin' with a gun,</dd>
<dd>One shot t'other and then there was one;</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>One little Injun livin' all alone,</dd>
<dd>He got married and then there were none.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Opie1997_1-1"><a href="#cite_note-Opie1997-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Derivative_songs">Derivative songs</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ten_Little_Indians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Derivative songs">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:172px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Frank_Green_TLN_1869.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="1260" data-file-width="1076" height="199" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Frank_Green_TLN_1869.jpg/170px-Frank_Green_TLN_1869.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Frank_Green_TLN_1869.jpg/255px-Frank_Green_TLN_1869.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/f5/Frank_Green_TLN_1869.jpg/340px-Frank_Green_TLN_1869.jpg 2x" width="170"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Frank_Green_TLN_1869.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Book cover by Frank Green, 1869</div>
</div>
</div>
<p>It is generally thought that this song was adapted, possibly by Frank J. Green in 1869, as "Ten Little <a href="/wiki/Nigger" title="Nigger">Niggers</a>", though it is possible that the influence was the other way round, with "Ten Little Niggers" being a close reflection of the text that became "Ten Little Indians". Either way, "Ten Little Niggers" became a standard of the <a href="/wiki/Blackface" title="Blackface">blackface</a> minstrel shows.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-2"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> It was sung by <a href="/wiki/Christy%27s_Minstrels" title="Christy's Minstrels">Christy's Minstrels</a> and became widely known in Europe, where it was used by <a href="/wiki/Agatha_Christie" title="Agatha Christie">Agatha Christie</a> in her novel of the same name. The novel was later retitled <i><a href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_None" title="And Then There Were None">And Then There Were None</a></i> (1939), and remains one of her most famous works, about ten killings on a remote island.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-3"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Variants of this song have been published widely as children's books; what the variants have in common is 'that they are about dark-skinned boys who are always children, never learning from experience'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> For example, it had been published in Holland by 1913; in Denmark by 1922 (in <i>Börnenes billedbog</i>); in Iceland in 1922 (as <i>Negrastrákarnir</i>); and in Finland in the 1940s (in <i>Kotoa ja kaukaa: valikoima runosatuja lapsille</i> and <i>Hupaisa laskukirja</i>).<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-5"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> The <a href="/wiki/Bengali_language" title="Bengali language">Bengali</a> poem <i><a class="new" href="/w/index.php?title=Haradhon_er_Dosti_Chhele&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" title="Haradhon er Dosti Chhele (page does not exist)">Haradhon er Dosti Chhele</a></i> (Haradhon's Ten Sons) is also inspired from <i>Ten Little Indians</i>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ten_Little_Indians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Because this song, and even the original term <i>Indians</i>, have become politically sensitive, modern versions for children often use "soldier boys" or "teddy bears" as the objects of the rhyme.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> The unaltered republication of the 1922 Icelandic version in 2007 by the Icelandic publisher Skrudda caused considerable debate in Iceland, with a strong division between people who saw the book as racist and people who saw it as 'a part of funny and silly stories created in the past'.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-7"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup> In Kristín Loftsdóttir's assessment of the debate,</p>
<dl>
<dd>
<dl>
<dd>Some of the discussions focusing on the republishing of the <i>Ten Little Negroes</i> can be seen as colonial nostalgia in the sense that they bring images of more "simple" times when such images were not objected to. As such, these public discourses seek to separate Icelandic identity from past issues of racism and prejudice. Contextualising the publication of the nursery rhyme in 1922 within European and North American contexts shows, however, that the book fitted very well with European discourses of race, and the images show similarity to caricatures of black people in the United States.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
</dd>
</dl>
<p>The republishing of the book in Iceland triggered a number of parodies or rewritings: and <i>Tíu litlír kenjakrakkar</i> ('Ten little prankster-children') by Sigrún Eldjárn and <a href="/wiki/%C3%9E%C3%B3rarinn_Eldj%C3%A1rn" title="Þórarinn Eldjárn">Þórarinn Eldjárn</a>; <i>10 litlir sveitastrákar</i> ('Ten little country-boys') by Katrín J. Óskarsdóttir and Guðrún Jónína Magnúsdóttir; and <i>Tíu litlir bankastrákar</i> ('Ten little banker-boys') by <a href="/wiki/%C3%93ttar_M._Nor%C3%B0fj%C3%B6r%C3%B0" title="Óttar M. Norðfjörð">Óttar M. Njorðfjörð</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The following version of the song was included in the first film version of <i><a href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_None_(1945_film)" title="And Then There Were None (1945 film)">And Then There Were None</a></i> (1945), which largely took Green's lyrics and replaced the already sensitive word "nigger" with "indian" (in some versions "soldiers"):</p>
<dl>
<dd>Ten little Indian boys went out to dine;</dd>
<dd>One choked his little self and then there were nine.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Nine little Indian boys sat up very late;</dd>
<dd>One overslept himself and then there were eight.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Eight little Indian boys travelling in <a href="/wiki/Devon" title="Devon">Devon</a>;</dd>
<dd>One said he'd stay there and then there were seven.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Seven little Indian boys chopping up sticks;</dd>
<dd>One chopped himself in halves and then there were six.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Six little Indian boys playing with a <a href="/wiki/Beehive" title="Beehive">hive</a>;</dd>
<dd>A <a href="/wiki/Bumblebee" title="Bumblebee">bumblebee</a> stung one and then there were five.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Five little Indian boys going in for law;</dd>
<dd>One got in <a href="/wiki/Court_of_Chancery" title="Court of Chancery">Chancery</a> and then there were four.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Four little Indian boys going out to sea;</dd>
<dd>A <a href="/wiki/Red_herring" title="Red herring">red herring</a> swallowed one and then there were three.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Three little Indian boys walking in the zoo;</dd>
<dd>A big bear hugged one and then there were two.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>Two little Indian boys playing in the sun;</dd>
<dd>One got all frizzled up and then there was one.</dd>
</dl>
<dl>
<dd>One little Indian boy left all alone;</dd>
<dd>He went out and hanged himself and then there were none.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></dd>
</dl>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Popular_culture">Popular culture</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ten_Little_Indians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Popular culture">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The Ten Little Indians are guests of <a href="/wiki/Old_King_Cole" title="Old King Cole">Old King Cole</a> in the 1933 <a href="/wiki/Old_King_Cole_(film)" title="Old King Cole (film)">Disney cartoon</a> of the same name. They perform a catchy dance which inspires the other nursery rhyme characters to join in.</p>
<p>The rock musical <i><a href="/wiki/Bloody_Bloody_Andrew_Jackson" title="Bloody Bloody Andrew Jackson">Bloody Bloody Andrew Jackson</a></i> includes a much darker song called "Ten Little Indians" that is modeled after this nursery rhyme.</p>
<p>The opening sequence of <a href="/wiki/Blackstone_(TV_series)" title="Blackstone (TV series)"><i>Blackstone</i></a> on <a href="/wiki/Aboriginal_Peoples_Television_Network" title="Aboriginal Peoples Television Network">APTN</a> features a version of the song.</p>
<p>The novel by Agatha Christie <i><a href="/wiki/And_Then_There_Were_None" title="And Then There Were None">And Then There Were None</a></i> is titled after the last line of the derivative minstrel song. Its original title was <i>Ten Little Niggers</i>. The present title is the title under which it was published in America, changed for reasons of cultural sensitivity.</p>
<p><a href="/wiki/Ten_Little_Indians_(The_Beach_Boys_song)" title="Ten Little Indians (The Beach Boys song)">"Ten Little Indians"</a> is a 1962 single by <a href="/wiki/The_Beach_Boys" title="The Beach Boys">the Beach Boys</a>, also present on their debut album <i><a href="/wiki/Surfin%27_Safari" title="Surfin' Safari">Surfin' Safari</a></i>.</p>
<p>In England's Mickey Mouse Annual No. 6, the song was adapted into the comic "10 Little Mickey Kids", which is considered to possibly be the most violent Disney Comic ever. It depicted 10 little mouse babies who meet an unfortunate end until there are only two left.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The opening song on <a href="/wiki/Harry_Nilsson" title="Harry Nilsson">Harry Nilsson</a>'s album <a href="/wiki/Pandemonium_Shadow_Show" title="Pandemonium Shadow Show">Pandemonium Shadow Show</a> is an adaption of "Ten Little Indians," though <a href="/wiki/Ten_Little_Indians_(Harry_Nilsson_song)" title="Ten Little Indians (Harry Nilsson song)">Ten Little Indians (Harry Nilsson song)</a> is about the ten commandments.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Notes">Notes</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Ten_Little_Indians&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Notes">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
