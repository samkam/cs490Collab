<p><b>Joseph Bruce Ismay</b> (12 December 1862 – 17 October 1937<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-1"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup>) was an English businessman who served as chairman and managing director of the <a href="/wiki/White_Star_Line" title="White Star Line">White Star Line</a> of steamships. He came to international attention as the highest-ranking White Star official among the 712 survivors (out of 2,208 passenger and crew on board) of the maiden voyage of his company's newest ocean liner, the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Titanic" title="RMS Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-NYT_obit_2-0"><a href="#cite_note-NYT_obit-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Early_life">Early life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._Bruce_Ismay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: Early life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>Ismay was born in Crosby, Lancashire. He was the son of <a href="/wiki/Thomas_Henry_Ismay" title="Thomas Henry Ismay">Thomas Henry Ismay</a> (7 January 1837 – 23 November 1899) and Margaret Bruce (13 April 1837 – 9 April 1907), daughter of <a href="/wiki/Ship-owner" title="Ship-owner">ship-owner</a> Luke Bruce.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-ET_3-0"><a href="#cite_note-ET-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup> Thomas Ismay was the senior partner in Ismay, Imrie and Company and the founder of the White Star Line.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-4"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>a<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSL_5-0"><a href="#cite_note-WSL-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> The younger Ismay was educated at <a href="/wiki/Elstree_School" title="Elstree School">Elstree School</a> and <a href="/wiki/Harrow_School" title="Harrow School">Harrow</a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-6"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup> then tutored in <a href="/wiki/France" title="France">France</a> for a year. He was apprenticed at his father's office for four years, after which he toured the world. He then went to <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York City</a> as the company representative, eventually rising to the rank of agent.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChirnside2004144_7-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChirnside2004144-7"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On 4 December 1888, Ismay married Julia Florence Schieffelin (5 March 1867 – 31 December 1963), daughter of George Richard Schieffelin and Julia Matilda Delaplaine of <a href="/wiki/New_York_City" title="New York City">New York</a>, with whom he had five children:<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-8"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<ul>
<li>Margaret Bruce Ismay (29 December 1889 – 15 May 1967), who married George Ronald Hamilton Cheape (1881–1957) in 1912</li>
<li>Henry Bruce Ismay (3 April 1891 – 1 October 1891)</li>
<li>Thomas Bruce Ismay (18 February 1894 – 27 April 1954<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-9"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup>), who married Jane Margaret Seymour</li>
<li>Evelyn Constance Ismay (17 July 1897 – 9 August 1940), who married <a href="/wiki/Basil_Sanderson,_1st_Baron_Sanderson_of_Ayot" title="Basil Sanderson, 1st Baron Sanderson of Ayot">Basil Sanderson</a> (1894–1971) in 1927</li>
<li>George Bruce Ismay (6 June 1902 – 30 April 1943), who married Florence Victoria Edrington in 1926.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-10"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></li>
</ul>
<p>In 1891, Ismay returned with his family to the United Kingdom and became a partner in his father's firm, Ismay, Imrie and Company. In 1899, Thomas Ismay died, and Bruce Ismay became head of the family business. Ismay had a head for business, and the White Star Line flourished under his leadership. In addition to running his ship business, Ismay also served as a director of several other companies. In 1901, he was approached by Americans who wished to build an international shipping conglomerate, which agreed to merge his firm into the <a href="/wiki/International_Mercantile_Marine_Co." title="International Mercantile Marine Co.">International Mercantile Marine Company</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSL_5-1"><a href="#cite_note-WSL-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="As_the_chairman_of_White_Star_Line">As the chairman of White Star Line</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._Bruce_Ismay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: As the chairman of White Star Line">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>After the death of his father on 23 November 1899,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-11"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-12"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Bruce Ismay succeeded him as the chairman of White Star Line. He decided to build four ocean liners to surpass the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Oceanic_(1899)" title="RMS Oceanic (1899)">RMS <i>Oceanic</i></a> built by his father: the ships were dubbed the <a href="/wiki/Big_Four_(White_Star_Line)" title="Big Four (White Star Line)">Big Four</a>: <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMS_Celtic" title="RMS Celtic">RMS <i>Celtic</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/RMS_Cedric" title="RMS Cedric">RMS <i>Cedric</i></a>, <a href="/wiki/RMS_Baltic_(1903)" title="RMS Baltic (1903)">RMS <i>Baltic</i></a>, and <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMS_Adriatic_(1907)" title="RMS Adriatic (1907)">RMS <i>Adriatic</i></a>. These vessels were designed more for luxury and for speed than safety.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEChirnside200411_13-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEChirnside200411-13"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In 1902, Ismay negotiated the sale of the White Star Line to <a href="/wiki/J.P._Morgan_%26_Co." title="J.P. Morgan &amp; Co.">J.P. Morgan &amp; Co.</a>, which was organising the formation of <a href="/wiki/International_Mercantile_Marine_Co." title="International Mercantile Marine Co.">International Mercantile Marine Company</a>, an Atlantic shipping combine which absorbed several major American and British lines. IMM was a holding company that controlled subsidiary operating corporations. Morgan hoped to dominate transatlantic shipping through interlocking directorates and contractual arrangements with the railroads, but that proved impossible because of the unscheduled nature of sea transport, American antitrust legislation, and an agreement with the British government.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-14"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup> White Star Lines became one of the IMM operating companies and, in February 1904, Ismay became president of the IMM, with the support of Morgan.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-15"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="RMS_Titanic">RMS <i>Titanic</i></span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._Bruce_Ismay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: RMS Titanic">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Titanic_the_sinking.jpg"><img alt="" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="395" data-file-width="600" height="145" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Titanic_the_sinking.jpg/220px-Titanic_the_sinking.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Titanic_the_sinking.jpg/330px-Titanic_the_sinking.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/4f/Titanic_the_sinking.jpg/440px-Titanic_the_sinking.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>
<div class="thumbcaption">
<div class="magnify"><a class="internal" href="/wiki/File:Titanic_the_sinking.jpg" title="Enlarge"></a></div>
Illustration of the sinking of the <i>Titanic</i></div>
</div>
</div>
<p>In 1907, Ismay met <a href="/wiki/William_Pirrie,_1st_Viscount_Pirrie" title="William Pirrie, 1st Viscount Pirrie">Lord Pirrie</a> of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Harland_%26_Wolff" title="Harland &amp; Wolff">Harland &amp; Wolff</a> shipyard to discuss White Star's answer to the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Lusitania" title="RMS Lusitania">RMS <i>Lusitania</i></a> and the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Mauretania_(1906)" title="RMS Mauretania (1906)">RMS <i>Mauretania</i></a>,<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-16"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>b<span>]</span></a></sup> the recently unveiled marvels of their chief competitor, <a href="/wiki/Cunard_Line" title="Cunard Line">Cunard Line</a>. Ismay's new type of ship would not be as fast as their competitors, but it would have huge <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Steerage" title="Steerage">steerage</a> capacity and luxury unparalleled in the history of ocean-going steamships. The latter feature was largely meant to attract the wealthy and the prosperous middle class. To accommodate the luxurious features Ismay ordered the number of lifeboats reduced from 48 to 16, the latter being the minimum allowed by the <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a>, based on the Titanic's projected tonnage.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-17"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-18"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> Three ships of the Olympic Class were planned and built; the second was the RMS <i>Titanic</i>, which began its maiden voyage from <a href="/wiki/Southampton" title="Southampton">Southampton</a> to New York City, on 10 April 1912. The first and third ships of this class were the <a href="/wiki/RMS_Olympic" title="RMS Olympic">RMS <i>Olympic</i></a> and <a href="/wiki/HMHS_Britannic" title="HMHS Britannic">HMHS <i>Britannic</i></a>.</p>
<p>Ismay occasionally accompanied his ships on their maiden voyages, and this was the case with the <i>Titanic</i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-WSL_5-2"><a href="#cite_note-WSL-5"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> During the voyage, Ismay talked with either (or possibly both) chief engineer Joseph Bell or captain Edward Smith about a possible test of speed if time permitted.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPiouffre2009111.E2.80.93112_19-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPiouffre2009111.E2.80.93112-19"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup> When the ship hit an <a href="/wiki/Iceberg" title="Iceberg">iceberg</a> 400 miles south of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Grand_Banks" title="Grand Banks">Grand Banks of Newfoundland</a> and started sinking on the night of 14 April 1912, Ismay was rescued in Collapsible Lifeboat C.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-canotc_20-0"><a href="#cite_note-canotc-20"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup> He testified that as the ship was in her final moments, he turned away, unable to watch his creation sink beneath the waters of the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/North_Atlantic" title="North Atlantic">North Atlantic</a>. He was taken aboard the Cunard liner <a href="/wiki/RMS_Carpathia" title="RMS Carpathia">RMS <i>Carpathia</i></a> and arrived in New York on 18 April. Ismay later testified at the <i>Titanic</i> disaster inquiry hearings held by both the <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/U.S._Senate" title="U.S. Senate">U.S. Senate</a> (chaired by Senator <a href="/wiki/William_Alden_Smith" title="William Alden Smith">William Alden Smith</a>) the following day, and the British <a href="/wiki/Board_of_Trade" title="Board of Trade">Board of Trade</a> (chaired by <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/John_Charles_Bigham,_1st_Viscount_Mersey" title="John Charles Bigham, 1st Viscount Mersey">Lord Mersey</a>) a few weeks later.</p>
<p>After being picked up by the <i>Carpathia</i>, Ismay was led to the cabin belonging to the ship's doctor, Frank Mcgee. He gave <a href="/wiki/Arthur_Rostron" title="Arthur Rostron">Captain Rostron</a> a message to send to White Star's New York office: "Deeply regret advise you <i>Titanic</i> sank this morning after collision with iceberg, resulting in serious loss of life. Full particulars later." Ismay did not leave Dr. Mcgee's cabin for the entire journey, ate nothing solid, and was kept under the influence of opiates.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPiouffre2009207_21-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPiouffre2009207-21"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPiouffre2009209_22-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPiouffre2009209-22"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> Fellow survivor <a href="/wiki/Jack_Thayer" title="Jack Thayer">Jack Thayer</a> visited Ismay to try to console him.</p>
<blockquote>
<p>[Ismay] was staring straight ahead, shaking like a leaf. Even when I spoke to him, he paid absolutely no attention. I have never seen a man so completely wrecked. — <a href="/wiki/Jack_Thayer" title="Jack Thayer">Jack Thayer</a>, First Class passenger on <i>Titanic</i></p>
</blockquote>
<p>When he arrived in New York, Ismay was hosted by Philip Franklin, vice president of the company. He also received a summons to appear before a Senate committee headed by Republican Senator <a href="/wiki/William_Alden_Smith" title="William Alden Smith">William Alden Smith</a>.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Criticism">Criticism</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._Bruce_Ismay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Criticism">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>After the disaster, Ismay was savaged by both the American and the British press for deserting the ship while women and children were still on board. Some papers called him the "Coward of the <i>Titanic</i>" or "J. Brute Ismay" and suggested that the White Star flag be changed to a yellow liver. Some ran negative cartoons depicting him deserting the ship. The writer <a href="/wiki/Ben_Hecht" title="Ben Hecht">Ben Hecht</a>, then a young newspaperman in <a href="/wiki/Chicago" title="Chicago">Chicago</a>, wrote a scathing poem contrasting the actions of Capt. Smith and Ismay. The final verse reads: "To hold your place in the ghastly face of death on the sea at night is a seaman's job, but to flee with the mob, is an owner's noble right."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-23"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Some maintain Ismay followed the "<a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Women_and_children_first_(protocol)" title="Women and children first (protocol)">women and children first</a>" principle, having assisted many women and children himself. Ismay's actions were defended in the official British inquiry, which found "Mr. Ismay, after rendering assistance to many passengers, found "C" collapsible, the last boat on the starboard side, actually being lowered. No other people were there at the time. There was room for him and he jumped in. Had he not jumped in he would merely have added one more life, namely, his own, to the number of those lost."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-24"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Ismay had boarded Collapsible C with first-class passenger William Carter; both said they did so after there were no more women and children near that particular lifeboat. Carter's own behaviour and reliability, however, were criticised by <a href="/wiki/Lucile_Carter" title="Lucile Carter">Mrs. Carter</a>, who sued him for divorce in 1914; she testified Carter had left her and their children to fend for themselves after the crash and accused him of "cruel and barbarous treatment and indignities to the person."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-25"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup> London society ostracised Ismay and labelled him a coward. On 30 June 1913, Ismay resigned as president of International Mercantile Marine and chairman of the White Star Line, to be succeeded by Harold Sanderson.</p>
<p>Ismay announced during the United States Inquiry that all the vessels of the International Mercantile Marine Company would be equipped with lifeboats in sufficient numbers for all passengers.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-FOOTNOTEPiouffre2009257_26-0"><a href="#cite_note-FOOTNOTEPiouffre2009257-26"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup> Following the inquiry, Ismay and the surviving officers of the ship returned to England aboard <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/RMS_Adriatic_(1907)" title="RMS Adriatic (1907)">RMS <i>Adriatic</i></a>.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Titanic_Controversy"><i>Titanic</i> Controversy</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._Bruce_Ismay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Titanic Controversy">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>During the congressional investigations, some passengers testified that during the voyage they heard Ismay pressuring <a href="/wiki/Edward_Smith_(sea_captain)" title="Edward Smith (sea captain)">Captain Edward J. Smith</a> to go faster, in order to arrive in New York ahead of schedule and generate some free press about the new liner. The 2000 book <i>The White Star Line: An Illustrated History</i> by Paul Louden-Brown states that this was unlikely, and that Ismay's record does not support the notion that he had any motive to do so.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-THS_27-0"><a href="#cite_note-THS-27"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Writing on the BBC News magazine website, Rosie Waites reports that Ismay was widely vilified in America after the sinking of the <i>Titanic</i>, due to the hostility of the press controlled by <a href="/wiki/William_Randolph_Hearst" title="William Randolph Hearst">William Randolph Hearst</a>, who had fallen out with Ismay. Waites writes "Ismay was almost universally condemned in America, where the Hearst syndicated press ran a vitriolic campaign against him, labelling him 'J Brute Ismay'. It published lists of all those who died but in the column of those saved it had just one name - Ismay's."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_28-0"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Following from the Hearst press depiction of Ismay, Waites writes that every subsequent film about the <i>Titanic</i> has depicted Ismay as a villain including the <a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1943_film)" title="Titanic (1943 film)">Nazi Titanic film</a>, where Ismay is depicted as "a power-mad Jewish businessman", <i><a href="/wiki/A_Night_to_Remember_(1958_film)" title="A Night to Remember (1958 film)">A Night To Remember</a></i>, <a href="/wiki/James_Cameron" title="James Cameron">James Cameron's</a> <i><a href="/wiki/Titanic_(1997_film)" title="Titanic (1997 film)">Titanic</a></i>, and <a href="/wiki/Julian_Fellowes" title="Julian Fellowes">Julian Fellowes'</a> TV miniseries <i><a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Titanic_(2012_TV_miniseries)" title="Titanic (2012 TV miniseries)">Titanic</a></i>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_28-1"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup> Louden-Brown, consultant to the Cameron film, has stated that he thought this characterisation of Ismay was unfair and he tried to challenge this. Louden-Brown said "Apart from being told, under no circumstances are we prepared to adjust the script, one thing they also said is 'this is what the public expect to see'."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_28-2"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p><a href="/wiki/John_Bigham,_1st_Viscount_Mersey" title="John Bigham, 1st Viscount Mersey">Lord Mersey</a>, who led the 1912 <a href="/wiki/British_Wreck_Commissioner%27s_inquiry_into_the_sinking_of_the_RMS_Titanic" title="British Wreck Commissioner's inquiry into the sinking of the RMS Titanic">British inquiry into the sinking of the <i>Titanic</i></a>, concluded that Ismay had helped many other passengers before finding a place for himself on the last lifeboat to leave the starboard side. Mersey stated "Had he not jumped in he would merely have added one more life, namely, his own, to the number of those lost."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-BBC_28-3"><a href="#cite_note-BBC-28"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Later_life">Later life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=J._Bruce_Ismay&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Later life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<div class="thumb tright">
<div class="thumbinner" style="width:222px;"><a class="image" href="/wiki/File:Bruce_Ismay_grave_Putney_Vale_2014.jpg"><img alt="A fenced patch of grass in a cemetery with four headstones of different shapes" class="thumbimage" data-file-height="2667" data-file-width="2669" height="220" src="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Bruce_Ismay_grave_Putney_Vale_2014.jpg/220px-Bruce_Ismay_grave_Putney_Vale_2014.jpg" srcset="//upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Bruce_Ismay_grave_Putney_Vale_2014.jpg/330px-Bruce_Ismay_grave_Putney_Vale_2014.jpg 1.5x, //upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/3/3b/Bruce_Ismay_grave_Putney_Vale_2014.jpg/440px-Bruce_Ismay_grave_Putney_Vale_2014.jpg 2x" width="220"/></a>

<p>Ismay kept a low profile following the <i>Titanic</i> disaster. He lived part of the year in a large cottage, Costelloe Lodge, near <a href="/wiki/Casla" title="Casla">Costelloe</a> in <a href="/wiki/Connemara" title="Connemara">Connemara</a>, <a href="/wiki/Irish_Free_State" title="Irish Free State">Ireland</a>. Paul Louden-Brown, in his history of the White Star Line, writes that Ismay continued to be active in business, and that much of his work was for The Liverpool &amp; London Steamship Protection &amp; Indemnity Association Limited, a company founded by his father. According to Louden-Brown: "Hundreds of thousands of pounds were paid out in insurance claims to the relatives of the <i>Titanic'</i>s victims; the misery created by the disaster and its aftermath dealt with by Ismay and his directors with great fortitude, this, despite the fact that he could easily have shirked his responsibilities and resigned from the board. He stuck with the difficult task and during his twenty-five year chairmanship hardly a page of the company's minutes does not contain some mention of the <i>Titanic</i> disaster."<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-THS_27-1"><a href="#cite_note-THS-27"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Ismay maintained an interest in maritime affairs. He inaugurated a cadet ship called <i>Mersey</i> used to train officers for <a href="/wiki/Merchant_Navy_(United_Kingdom)" title="Merchant Navy (United Kingdom)">Britain's Merchant Navy</a>, donated £11,000 to start a fund for lost seamen, and in 1919 gave £25,000 (£1,002,419 as of 2015)<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-inflation-UK_29-0"><a href="#cite_note-inflation-UK-29"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> to set up a fund to recognise the contribution of merchant mariners in <a href="/wiki/World_War_I" title="World War I">World War I</a>.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-30"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>His health declined in the 1930s, following a diagnosis of <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Diabetes" title="Diabetes">diabetes</a>, which worsened in early 1936, when the illness resulted in amputation of part of his right leg. He returned to England a few months later, settling in a small house on the <a href="/wiki/Wirral_Peninsula" title="Wirral Peninsula">Wirral</a> across the <a href="/wiki/River_Mersey" title="River Mersey">River Mersey</a> from <a href="/wiki/Liverpool" title="Liverpool">Liverpool</a>. J. Bruce Ismay died in <a href="/wiki/Mayfair" title="Mayfair">Mayfair</a>, London, on 17 October 1937, of a <a class="mw-redirect" href="/wiki/Cerebral_thrombosis" title="Cerebral thrombosis">stroke</a> at the age of 74. His funeral was held on 21 October 1937, and he is buried in <a href="/wiki/Putney_Vale_Cemetery" title="Putney Vale Cemetery">Putney Vale Cemetery</a>, London.<sup class="reference" id="cite_ref-Kerrigan_1998_p285_31-0"><a href="#cite_note-Kerrigan_1998_p285-31"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> He was survived by his wife, Florence Schieffelin. After his death, she renounced her British subject status in order to restore her American citizenship on 14 November 1949. Julia Florence Ismay, <i>née</i> Schieffelin, died 31 December 1963, aged 92, in <a href="/wiki/Kensington" title="Kensington">Kensington</a>, London.</p>
